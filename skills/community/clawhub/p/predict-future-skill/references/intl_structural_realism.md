# International Relations — Structural Realism (Waltz & Mearsheimer)

The dominant explanatory paradigm of international politics. Predicts **state
behavior from the structure of the international system** — not from leaders'
personalities, ideology, or domestic politics. Two canonical variants:

- **Defensive realism** — Kenneth Waltz, *Theory of International Politics*
  (1979). States are **security maximizers**; they seek *enough* power to be
  safe and then stop, because over-expansion provokes balancing.
- **Offensive realism** — John Mearsheimer, *The Tragedy of Great Power
  Politics* (2001). States are **power maximizers**; because intentions are
  unknowable and the cost of being weak is extinction, the only safe amount of
  power is **regional hegemony**.

Use this reference for the **structural "outside view"** of great-power behavior:
the slow, system-level pressures that constrain what states can plausibly do
over a multi-year horizon — regardless of who is in office.

## The five bedrock assumptions

Both variants share the same starting axioms (Mearsheimer 2001, ch. 2; built on
Waltz 1979):

1. **Anarchy** — no central authority sits above states; there is no 911 to call.
2. **Offensive capability** — every great power can inflict some harm on others.
3. **Uncertainty of intentions** — a state can never be *certain* another won't
   use that capability, today or after a future leadership change.
4. **Survival is the primary goal** — autonomy is the precondition for every
   other end.
5. **States are rational actors** — they choose strategies (instrumentally) to
   maximize survival.

From these, three behaviors follow: **fear, self-help, and power-maximization.**
The tragedy: even purely security-seeking states are pushed into competition and
sometimes war. No one has to be evil for the system to produce conflict.

## Core predictive mechanisms

### 1. Distribution of power sets the board

Forecast from **polarity** and **relative capabilities**, not rhetoric.

- **Bipolar** systems (e.g. Cold War) — relatively stable; two blocs, clear lines.
- **Balanced multipolar** — more fluid; miscalculation risk rises.
- **Unbalanced multipolar** (a potential hegemon present) — most dangerous;
  others must actively organize against the aspirant.

Count latent power (population + wealth) and military power. The state with a
growing share of relative power is the one whose behavior will most change the
system.

### 2. Balancing vs. buck-passing

When a threatening state rises, others can:

- **Balance** — build arms (internal) or form alliances (external) against it.
- **Buck-pass** — hang back and hope a *different* state pays the cost of
  checking the threat.

Mearsheimer's key prediction: states **prefer buck-passing** in multipolarity
*except* when facing a genuine potential hegemon, where balancing becomes
unavoidable. Geography matters — the **"stopping power of water"** means great
powers rarely achieve *global* hegemony; oceans protect and limit. So expect
*regional* hegemons that work to prevent peer competitors in other regions.

### 3. Waltz vs. Mearsheimer — pick by "how much power is enough?"

| | Defensive (Waltz) | Offensive (Mearsheimer) |
|---|---|---|
| State goal | Security (status quo when secure) | Maximum relative power → hegemony |
| Expansion | Self-defeating; provokes balancing | Rational whenever cost-effective |
| Best forecast for | Why balances re-form; restraint | Why rising powers push outward; great-power rivalry |

Use **both as competing hypotheses** on the same question. If they diverge,
that divergence *is* the forecast's key uncertainty (e.g. "Will China seek
regional hegemony aggressively, or settle once secure?").

## Worked example — the rise of China

Mearsheimer's standing prediction (from 2001 onward): a wealthy China will try to
**dominate Asia** the way the U.S. dominates the Western Hemisphere, and the U.S.
will work to **contain** it; regional states (Japan, India, Vietnam) will tend to
**balance** with the U.S. rather than buck-pass, because a potential hegemon is
present. This is a structural, falsifiable call independent of any leader's
stated intentions — and the kind of multi-year trajectory this reference is for.

## Limits — when NOT to lean on realism

- **Short-horizon, single-event** questions (a specific summit outcome, a vote) —
  structure is too coarse; use a bargaining model instead.
- **Domestic-politics-driven** outcomes (regime survival, coalition deals) —
  realism brackets these out by design.
- **Cooperation under strong institutions / economic interdependence** — realism
  systematically under-weights these (a known critique from liberal
  institutionalism and constructivism). Flag it rather than ignore it.

## How to apply in this skill

1. **State the structure first**: name the relevant great powers, the polarity,
   and the trend in *relative* power. This is your structural base rate.
2. **Run both variants as separate hypotheses.** Ask of the question: what does
   defensive realism predict? What does offensive realism predict? Report both;
   do not silently merge.
3. **Predict via balancing/buck-passing/geography**, not intentions or
   personalities. Discount leaders' stated motives; weight capabilities and
   position.
4. **Give a qualitative call + horizon + falsifiable triggers**, e.g. "Over
   3–5 years, expect intensified U.S.–China security competition (~high
   confidence structurally); falsified if a credible binding security
   institution forms or relative power gap stops narrowing."
5. **Name realism's blind spots explicitly** (institutions, economics, domestic
   politics) so the forecast isn't overconfident on cooperation questions.
6. Treat new evidence (a new alliance, an arms build-up) as confirming or
   weakening the structural read, not as a fresh story.
