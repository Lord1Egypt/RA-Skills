# Legal Baselines by Market

Your warranty is **additive** to statutory consumer rights, not a replacement. These notes summarize the floor you cannot go below in major markets. This is general information, not legal advice — confirm with counsel in each market before publishing.

## United States

- **Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act**: any written warranty must be designated as "Full" or "Limited." Most consumer brands offer "Limited."
- A "Full" warranty requires free remedy within a reasonable time, no time limit on implied warranties, and the consumer can choose between repair, replace, or refund after a reasonable repair attempt fails. Most brands cannot afford "Full" terms.
- A "Limited" warranty must clearly state what is covered, the duration, and the steps to claim.
- State laws may impose additional implied warranties of merchantability and fitness for purpose. You can disclaim these in writing for "as-is" sales but not in conjunction with a written warranty.
- The warranty document must be available **before purchase** for items over $15.

## United Kingdom

- **Consumer Rights Act 2015**: goods must be of satisfactory quality, fit for purpose, and as described, for **up to 6 years** from purchase (5 in Scotland).
- The first 30 days the buyer has a short-term right to reject for a full refund.
- After 30 days and within 6 months, defects are presumed to have existed at delivery — burden is on the seller to prove otherwise.
- After 6 months, burden shifts to the buyer.
- Your warranty cannot displace these rights. State this explicitly.

## European Union

- **Directive 2019/771 on the sale of goods**: 2-year statutory legal guarantee minimum across the EU.
- During the first year, defects are presumed to have existed at delivery (some member states extend this to 2 years).
- The consumer can require repair or replacement first; refund only if those fail.
- Member states can extend (e.g., Sweden has 3 years; the Netherlands uses "expected lifespan").
- Warranty terms must be in the buyer's language and presented before purchase.

## Australia

- **Australian Consumer Law (ACL)**: consumer guarantees apply regardless of the seller's stated warranty. Goods must be of acceptable quality, fit for purpose, match description, and have spare parts and repair facilities reasonably available.
- There is no fixed time limit; the standard is "reasonable expected life" of the product.
- The mandatory consumer guarantee notice must be displayed alongside any voluntary warranty, in specific wording.

## Canada

- Provincial law governs (e.g., Ontario Consumer Protection Act). Implied warranty of merchantability applies. Quebec adds a "legal warranty" of "reasonable life" similar to Australia.

## Practical implications

- **Never claim your warranty replaces consumer rights.** Always say "in addition to" or "without prejudice to."
- **In the EU, "lifetime" cannot be shorter than 2 years.** A 1-year voluntary warranty is undermined by the statutory 2 years.
- **Disclose pre-purchase.** Warranty hidden in T&C only is not enforceable in some jurisdictions.
- **Local language required.** Don't ship a German customer an English-only warranty.
- **Authorized seller language**: if you only honor warranties from authorized sellers, this is enforceable in most markets, but you must publish a list and police the secondary market.
- **Translation versions**: put a "controlling version" clause naming one language version as the legal authority for disputes.
