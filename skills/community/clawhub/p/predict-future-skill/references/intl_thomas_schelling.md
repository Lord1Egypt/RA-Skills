# International Relations — Schelling Strategy of Conflict & Coercion

Thomas Schelling reframed conflict as **bargaining**: even bitter adversaries
share a common interest in avoiding the worst mutual outcome, so most conflict is
a negotiation conducted with threats. Key works: *The Strategy of Conflict*
(1960) and *Arms and Influence* (1966). Nobel Prize in Economics 2005 (with Robert
Aumann) "for having enhanced our understanding of conflict and cooperation
through game-theory analysis."

Use this reference for **crisis, deterrence, and escalation** questions —
"Will the threat work? Will this escalate? Will deterrence hold?" — i.e. the
**strategic-interaction layer** between two (or few) adversaries.

## Core concepts (the forecasting toolkit)

### 1. Deterrence vs. compellence

- **Deterrence** — threaten punishment to keep an adversary from *starting* an
  action; preserves the status quo. Passive, open-ended in time, easier.
- **Compellence** — use threats/force to make an adversary *start* or *stop*
  doing something; changes the status quo. Active, time-bound, visible
  compliance required — **harder**, because the other side must be *seen* to back
  down.

First classify which one is in play. Compellence demands far stronger signaling
and usually has a deadline; predict a lower success rate than deterrence, all else
equal.

### 2. Credible commitment ("the power to bind oneself")

A threat is only as good as its credibility. Counter-intuitively, a player gets
*stronger* by **removing their own options** — burning bridges, tying hands,
delegating to automatic responses, staking reputation publicly. Ask: **what has
each side done to make backing down impossible?** The side that has more
visibly locked itself in holds the stronger position.

- *Uncertain* retaliation can be more credible/efficient than *certain*
  retaliation; the *capability to retaliate* matters more than the ability to
  resist the initial attack.

### 3. The threat that leaves something to chance / brinkmanship

When a threat to do something deliberately is *not* credible (because carrying it
out is suicidal), states instead **manipulate shared risk**: deliberately create
a situation that *could* spiral out of anyone's full control, making the
adversary share the danger. **Brinkmanship** = competition in raising that risk
until one side yields. Escalation only works if **each rung of the ladder is
itself credible.**

### 4. Focal points (Schelling points)

In the absence of communication, parties converge on an outcome that is
**uniquely salient** — a natural line (a river, a round number, a prior
precedent, a status-quo boundary). Use focal points to predict **where** a tacit
settlement or ceasefire line will land, and which thresholds (e.g. "no nukes,"
"no crossing border X") function as bright lines whose violation triggers sharp
escalation.

## Worked logic — a coercion question

To forecast "Will state A's threat compel state B to stop action X?":

1. **Classify**: deterrence or compellence? (Compellence → discount success.)
2. **Credibility of A's threat**: has A tied its hands / staked reputation / set a
   deadline / pre-positioned force? Or is it cheap talk?
3. **Cost to B of complying**: does backing down threaten B's regime survival or
   core interest? High cost → B resists even credible threats.
4. **Is there a face-saving focal point** that lets B comply without humiliation?
   Its presence or absence strongly shifts the odds.
5. **Escalation ladder**: is each rung credible, or does A have to jump straight
   to an incredible (suicidal) top rung? Gaps in the ladder predict bluff-calling.
6. **Shared-risk dynamics**: could the crisis spiral beyond either side's
   control? Higher autonomous risk raises both the danger and the coercive power
   of the side more willing to tolerate it.

Output a qualitative probability the coercion succeeds, plus the **observable
triggers** (a mobilization, a public ultimatum, a crossed red line) that would
update it.

## Limits

- Assumes adversaries are **strategically rational** and reading each other's
  signals; misperception, domestic noise, and organizational accidents can
  dominate real crises (pair with a misperception/decision lens if that's the
  crux).
- Best for **two-party / few-party** confrontations; poor for diffuse, many-actor
  policy outcomes (use the bargaining-model reference there).
- Says little about the **long-run structural** trajectory (use realism for that).

## How to apply in this skill

1. **Classify the interaction** (deterrence vs. compellence) explicitly — it sets
   the baseline difficulty.
2. **Score credibility on both sides**: list the concrete commitment devices each
   has used (hands tied, reputation staked, deadlines, automaticity).
3. **Map the escalation ladder** and check each rung for credibility; flag any
   gap where a threat becomes a bluff.
4. **Locate focal points / red lines** that anchor a likely settlement or whose
   breach triggers escalation.
5. **Give a qualitative call + horizon + falsifiable triggers**, e.g. "Deterrence
   likely holds (~70%) absent a credible compellent deadline; falsified if A
   issues a public ultimatum with pre-positioned force and no face-saving exit
   for B."
6. Keep the analysis **standalone**; if it disagrees with the realism or
   bargaining-model forecasts, present the tension rather than averaging it away.
