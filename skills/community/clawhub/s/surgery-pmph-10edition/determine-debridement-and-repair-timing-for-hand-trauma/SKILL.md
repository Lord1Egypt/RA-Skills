# Determine Debridement and Repair Timing for Hand Trauma

Use this skill to guide surgical decision-making for open hand wounds by evaluating the injury’s time window, contamination status, and tissue integrity.

## Prerequisites
- Known time of injury
- Wound not yet infected
- Surgical capability available

## Constraints
- Do **not** apply to wounds with established infection or necrotic tissue

## Procedure

1. **Evaluate Time Since Injury**
   - **≤6–8 hours**: Proceed with thorough debridement and consider primary repair.
   - **>8 hours**: Bacterial load increases significantly; primary closure is generally contraindicated.

2. **If Within 6–8 Hours (Primary Repair Window)**
   - Perform complete debridement to convert contaminated wound to clean.
   - Repair critical structures immediately:
     - Vascular injuries affecting perfusion
     - Tendons, nerves, bones, and joints (reduce/dislocate fractures promptly)

3. **If >12 Hours or High-Risk Conditions Present**
   Indications for delayed/secondary repair include:
   - Injury >12 hours old
   - Heavy contamination (e.g., soil, debris, crush mechanism)
   - Extensive soft tissue damage
   - Lack of surgical resources or expertise
   - Choose between:
     - **Delayed repair**: ~3 weeks post-injury
     - **Secondary repair**: ~12 weeks post-injury

4. **For High-Risk Wounds (Contaminated or Late Presentation)**
   - Remove gross debris and necrotic tissue only.
   - Apply moist saline dressings, negative pressure wound therapy (NPWT), or irrigation systems.
   - Reassess in 3–5 days before definitive repair.

> **Key principle**: Bacteria typically do not invade deep tissues within 8 hours—early debridement prevents infection.

> **Trigger context**: Use this skill immediately upon encountering any open hand injury to determine whether to proceed with immediate reconstruction or adopt a staged approach.
