# Payment Terms Guide

Payment terms are pricing. A 60-day term at 12% working capital cost is worth 2 percentage points of margin. Quote and track accordingly.

## The cost of a day of float

Working-capital cost of a day of receivables:

```
cost_per_day = invoice_$ × annual_wc_rate / 365
```

Example: $10,000 invoice, 12% working-capital rate:

```
cost_per_day = $10,000 × 0.12 / 365 = $3.29 per day
```

Net-30 costs you $98.63. Net-60 costs $197.26. Net-90 costs $295.89 per $10,000 invoice. A customer asking for Net-90 is asking you for a ~3% discount that doesn't show up on the price sheet.

## Standard terms to offer

| Term | When to offer | Cost assumption (12% WC) |
|---|---|---|
| Prepay | All new accounts | 0% |
| Credit card | New accounts up to $2,500 order | ~2.9% merchant fee |
| Net-15 | Established accounts that qualify | 0.5% |
| Net-30 | Default after 2 clean invoices | 1.0% |
| Net-60 | Top 10% of accounts, with credit review | 2.0% |
| Net-90 | Only if margin supports it AND with early-pay discount | 3.0% |

## Early-pay discount

Offer **2/10 Net-30**: pay in 10 days, take 2% off the invoice; otherwise pay in full by day 30. The effective APR of the 2% discount over the 20 extra days is ~36%, which is much better than your working-capital rate. Any retailer that isn't broke will take it.

## Credit review basics for a new account

Ask for:

1. Business license and resale certificate.
2. Years in business and any corporate parent.
3. Three trade references (suppliers they have been paying).
4. Bank reference (optional but useful for larger accounts).
5. D&B number if available.

For orders over $10k, run a credit check through a trade-credit service (Creditsafe, Experian BOP, D&B). Costs ~$50–$150 per check; worth it for any first order that exceeds a week of working capital.

## Trade-credit insurance

For orders over $25k on Net-60+ terms, trade credit insurance (Atradius, Coface, Allianz Trade) typically costs 0.2–0.5% of insured receivables and lets you extend terms to customers you would otherwise need to reject. Worth investigating at ~$1M annual wholesale revenue.

## Red flags when extending terms

- Sudden request for much longer terms after months of fine Net-30.
- Round-number orders that double or triple prior volume.
- Requests to change payment destination or billing name.
- Consistent 10+ days past due with no explanation.
- Retailer's own supplier references decline to confirm payment history.

Any two of these should trigger a credit review before accepting the next order.

## Collections process

1. **Day 1 post-due**: automated email reminder.
2. **Day 7**: personal email from the rep, friendly.
3. **Day 15**: phone call from AR, firm.
4. **Day 30**: credit hold on new orders, certified letter.
5. **Day 60**: collections agency or small-claims filing, depending on amount.

Do not ship additional product to an account that is 30+ days past due. This seems obvious; it is the most common mistake brands make.

## Factoring — when it makes sense

Selling invoices to a factoring company advances cash at 85–95% of invoice value immediately, in exchange for a 1–3% fee. Makes sense when:

- You are growing faster than your working capital allows.
- You have a concentrated retailer base (one big account delaying payment starves the business).
- The effective cost (1–3%) is less than your cost of equity or growth capital.

Disclose factoring to retailers in advance; some have clauses requiring notice.
