# Macro Analysis Framework — Reference Guide

Methodology for Module 1: Macro Analysis. Used for assessing the macro environment and scenario-building in equity research.

---

## 1. Global & Domestic GDP Environment

**What to evaluate:**
- Are the company's end markets in expansion, deceleration, or recession?
- Is global growth accelerating or decelerating? (IMF World Economic Outlook is a key reference)
- For US-focused companies: GDP growth, ISM Manufacturing & Services PMI, Retail Sales, Industrial Production, Business Fixed Investment trends
- For companies with significant international exposure: growth and policy cycles in key regions (US, EU, China, emerging markets)

**Signals more relevant for equities:**
| GDP Phase | Implication (Equity Lens) |
|-----------|--------------------------|
| Strong expansion (threshold depends on economy and cycle) | Cyclical / growth styles tend to outperform |
| Moderate growth | High-quality "compounder" companies tend to outperform |
| Decelerating growth | Defensive styles outperform; margins and multiples under pressure |
| Recession / significant downturn | Risk appetite declines: cash flow and balance sheet quality become paramount |

---

## 2. Interest Rate Environment & Central Bank Policy

**Key dimensions:**
- **Rate direction**: Hiking, pausing, or cutting? For equities, "direction and expectations gap" often matters more than the absolute level.
- **Real rates**: Nominal rates minus inflation. Elevated real rates typically compress growth stock valuations (higher discount rates); low/negative real rates favor risk assets.
- **Yield curve shape**: Term spreads (10Y–2Y, 10Y–1Y, Treasury curve slope) reflect growth and liquidity expectations; inversion is often viewed as a recession/downside risk signal, but needs interpretation within the prevailing policy framework.
- **Federal Reserve & policy signals**: Fed Funds Rate, discount window, quantitative easing/tightening, reverse repo operations, forward guidance; FOMC statements, press conferences, dot plot, and meeting minutes.

**Sector sensitivity to rates (indicative; adjust for current cycle and valuation structure):**
| Sector | Rate Sensitivity | Reason |
|--------|------------------|--------|
| Tech / high-growth | High / inverse | Longer "duration" — future cash flows more sensitive to discount rate |
| Utilities | Medium-high / inverse | Debt and dividend yield sensitive to risk-free rate |
| Financials (banks) | Directional, structure-dependent | Net interest margin (NIM) and asset-liability duration mismatch |
| Real Estate / REITs | High / inverse | Financing costs and cap rate adjustments |
| Consumer staples | Low | More defensive; pricing power partially offsets |
| Energy | Low / cycle-dependent | Commodity cycle dominates; rates are secondary |

## 3. Inflation & Pricing Dynamics

**Evaluate from two angles:**
1. **Cost-side inflation**: Are raw materials, energy, labor, and logistics costs rising? Can the company pass through increases?
2. **Demand-side pricing**: Does the company have pricing power? Does inflation erode consumers' real purchasing power — what is the demand elasticity for the company's products?

**Key indicators:**
- CPI (headline and core), PPI, PCE deflator
- Wage and employment data (especially important for labor-intensive industries): Nonfarm Payrolls, Average Hourly Earnings, Employment Cost Index
- Supply chain stress and freight rates (e.g., freight indices, delivery times — sector-specific)

**Margin impact framework:**
- Companies with **pricing power** (brands, scarcity, inelastic demand) are more likely to maintain or expand margins during inflationary periods
- **Commodity-exposed, thin-margin** businesses (retail, distribution, airlines) are more prone to margin squeeze
- **High labor cost** businesses (professional services, certain healthcare services) are more exposed to wage inflation

---

## 4. Industry Tailwinds & Headwinds

**Structural tailwinds (long-term trends, 5–10+ years):**
- Digital transformation & cloud adoption → Technology
- AI & automation penetration → Semiconductors, Software
- Energy transition → Renewables, EVs, batteries, grid infrastructure, storage
- Aging population → Healthcare, pharmaceuticals, medical devices
- Supply chain reshoring / friend-shoring → Advanced manufacturing, defense, critical materials
- E-commerce & last-mile delivery penetration → Logistics, warehousing, fulfillment tech

**Cyclical headwinds to track:**
- Inventory destocking cycles
- Consumer spending and housing-related sector swings
- Tightening credit conditions impacting CapEx
- Wage inflation in low-margin industries

**Assessment framework:**
Classify each relevant trend as:
- **Strong tailwind**: long-term, accelerating, directly beneficial to the company
- **Moderate tailwind**: broadly favorable to the sector; company is a partial beneficiary
- **Neutral**: trend has limited impact or offsetting effects
- **Moderate headwind**: creates pressure but manageable
- **Structural headwind**: poses a long-term threat to the business model

---

## 5. Supply Chain Factors

**Questions to answer:**
- Is the company dependent on a single supplier or single-region supply concentration?
- Does the company disclose supply chain diversification (e.g., "China+1", nearshoring, domestic alternatives)?
- Are there commodity / critical raw material inputs subject to significant price volatility or geopolitical risk?
- Where do inventory levels sit relative to historical ranges (destocking / restocking / overstocked)?

**Data sources:** Company annual/quarterly reports risk factors and MD&A sections, investor presentations and earnings call transcripts, SEC filings (10-K, 10-Q, 8-K), and industry supply chain tracking data (combine public information with third-party data sources such as Bloomberg, FactSet, and sector-specific reports).

---

## 6. Regulatory & Geopolitical Environment

**Regulatory risk categories (examples):**
| Category | Examples | Affected Sectors |
|----------|----------|------------------|
| Antitrust | Sherman Act, Clayton Act, FTC/DOJ enforcement, merger challenges | Tech platforms, telecom, healthcare |
| Data & Privacy | CCPA/CPRA (California), state privacy laws, proposed federal legislation | Tech, fintech, healthcare |
| Financial Regulation | Dodd-Frank, Basel III/IV, capital adequacy, stress testing, CFPB | Banks, insurance, brokerages |
| Environmental Compliance | EPA standards, IRA clean energy provisions, carbon reporting rules | Energy, chemicals, industrials |
| Healthcare Pricing | IRA drug price negotiation, PBM reform, Medicaid/Medicare policy | Pharma, biotech, healthcare services |
| Export Controls & Sanctions | Entity List, CHIPS Act restrictions, BIS regulations, OFAC sanctions | Semiconductors, defense, advanced tech |

**Geopolitical risk factors:**
- US-China trade and tech friction: tariffs, export controls, technology restrictions
- Impact of geopolitical conflicts on energy and commodity prices
- Shipping lane disruptions and regional instability affecting freight rates and delivery
- Emerging market political risks: currency/ capital controls, nationalization, sanctions

**Macro Module Scoring Guide:**
- Score of 5: Policy and liquidity environment supportive (e.g., rate cuts or easing expectations strengthening), growth improving, industry tailwinds clear, external uncertainty low
- Score of 1: Rate hikes or tightening expectations, clear demand deterioration, commodity cost shock, compounded by significant regulatory/compliance risk events
