## Overview — Common Law vs Civil Law {#overview}

The world's legal systems broadly fall into two families, with significant implications for how construction contracts are interpreted, claims are assessed, and disputes are resolved.

### Common Law

- **Source of law:** Primarily judge-made law through court decisions (precedent/case law), supplemented by statutes
- **Doctrine of precedent (stare decisis):** Courts are bound by prior decisions of higher courts
- **Contract interpretation:** Courts look at the express terms, implied terms, and surrounding circumstances; extensive case law on interpretation principles
- **Flexibility:** Judges can develop the law incrementally through new decisions
- **Key feature:** Adversarial court system — parties present their cases, judge decides

### Civil Law

- **Source of law:** Primarily written codes and statutes (codified law)
- **Role of precedent:** Court decisions are persuasive but generally not binding in the same way
- **Contract interpretation:** Closer to the written code provisions; gap-filling through code principles rather than case law
- **Codification:** Rights, obligations, and remedies are systematically set out in codes
- **Key feature:** Inquisitorial system — judge takes a more active role in investigating facts

## Impact on Construction Contracts {#impact-on-contracts}

### Contract Interpretation

| Aspect | Common Law | Civil Law |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| **Primary source** | Express contract terms + implied terms | Written code provisions + contract terms |
| **Gap-filling** | Implied terms (business efficacy, officious bystander tests); case law | Code provisions fill gaps automatically |
| **Ambiguity** | Contra proferentem; surrounding circumstances (factual matrix) | Literal interpretation of code; good faith principle |
| **Good faith** | Generally no implied duty (except some jurisdictions, e.g. Australia) | Fundamental principle — parties must act in good faith |
| **Parol evidence** | Generally excluded (written contract prevails) | More receptive to extrinsic evidence of intent |
| **Unfair terms** | Limited judicial intervention (freedom of contract) | Courts may strike down unconscionable terms more readily |

### Standard Forms Across Systems

| Form | Origin System | Used In |
|------|--------------|---------|
| **FIDIC** | Swiss (civil law origin) | International — both common and civil law jurisdictions |
| **NEC** | English (common law) | UK, international (growing adoption) |
| **PSSCOC** | Singapore (common law) | Singapore public sector |
| **SIA** | Singapore (common law) | Singapore private sector |
| **AIA / ConsensusDocs** | US (common law) | United States |
| **JCT** | English (common law) | UK, former Commonwealth |
| **CCAG** | French (civil law) | France, Francophone countries |
| **VOB** | German (civil law) | Germany, German-influenced markets |

## Impact on Claims & Disputes {#impact-on-claims}

### Claims Assessment

| Aspect | Common Law | Civil Law |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| **Precedent** | Prior court decisions are binding/persuasive — cite relevant cases | Precedent is persuasive but not formally binding |
| **Time-bars** | Strictly enforced (see *Multiplex v Honeywell*) | May be moderated by good faith principles |
| **Causation** | Detailed causation analysis required; concurrent delay case law | Code provisions on causation; less granular case law |
| **Quantum** | Extensive methods developed through case law (Eichleay, Hudson, Emden) | Quantum often assessed by court-appointed experts |
| **Expert evidence** | Party-appointed experts; hot-tubbing in some jurisdictions | Court-appointed experts more common |
| **Discovery/disclosure** | Broad disclosure obligations | More limited document production |

### Dispute Resolution

| Aspect | Common Law | Civil Law |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| **Adjudication** | Statutory in many jurisdictions (UK, SG, AU, MY) | Less common; not typically statutory |
| **Arbitration** | Well-established; party autonomy; institutional rules (ICC, SIAC, LCIA) | Well-established; often ICC; court involvement varies |
| **Mediation** | Growing; voluntary or court-ordered | Growing; mandatory in some jurisdictions (Italy, France) |
| **Enforcement** | New York Convention (arbitral awards); direct court enforcement | New York Convention; enforcement procedures vary |
| **Appeals** | Limited grounds (arbitration); broader in litigation | Broader appeal rights in some civil law courts |

## FIDIC Across Legal Systems {#fidic-across-systems}

FIDIC contracts originated in Switzerland (civil law) but are widely used across both legal traditions. Key considerations:

### In Common Law Jurisdictions
- Clause interpretation guided by case law (e.g. *Obrascon Huarte Lain v AG* [2015] EWHC 1918 — interpretation of FIDIC Sub-Clause 20.1 notice requirements)
- Time-bar provisions strictly enforced
- Engineer/Employer's Representative decisions subject to judicial review on reasonableness
- Implied terms may supplement express FIDIC provisions

### In Civil Law Jurisdictions
- Code provisions may override or supplement FIDIC terms
- Good faith obligations apply even where FIDIC is silent
- Mandatory local law provisions cannot be contracted out of
- Court-appointed experts may re-assess Engineer's determinations
- Some civil law jurisdictions require FIDIC to be adapted to local codes (e.g. UAE, China)

### Practical Tip
When using FIDIC internationally, always check:
1. **Governing law** — which legal system applies?
2. **Mandatory local provisions** — what can't be contracted out of?
3. **Particular Conditions** — how has the standard form been modified for local law?
4. **Dispute forum** — where will disputes be heard, and under what rules?
5. **Enforcement** — can awards/judgments be enforced in the relevant jurisdictions?

## Practical Implications {#practical-implications}

### For Claims Practitioners

**Working in common law jurisdictions:**
- Research case law thoroughly — precedent strengthens arguments
- Strict compliance with notice provisions (time-bars are real)
- Prepare detailed expert reports with methodology transparency
- Expect broad document disclosure obligations
- Use established quantum methods (cite the cases that support them)

**Working in civil law jurisdictions:**
- Study the applicable code provisions carefully — they fill gaps
- Good faith arguments carry weight (both ways)
- Court-appointed experts may override party experts
- Document production may be more limited
- Code interpretation principles differ from common law rules

**Working across both systems (international projects):**
- Identify the governing law FIRST — it determines everything
- Don't assume common law interpretation principles apply to civil law contracts (or vice versa)
- Check for mandatory local law provisions that cannot be overridden by contract
- Consider the enforcement jurisdiction for disputes
- FIDIC's Sub-Clause 1.4 (governing law) is critical — never leave it blank
