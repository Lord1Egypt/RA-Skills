# Concurrent Delay — Strategic Approach

Concurrent delay occurs when two or more independent delay events — typically one caused by the Contractor and one by the Employer/Authority — overlap in time and both are alleged to affect the completion date.

It is one of the most complex and disputed areas in construction claims.

---

## Why Concurrent Delay Matters Strategically

- It directly impacts **Extension of Time** entitlement and **liquidated damages** exposure
- It affects **prolongation cost claims**
- Different jurisdictions apply **different approaches**, creating strategic risk if not handled correctly
- Poor handling of concurrent delay is a common reason claims are **reduced or rejected** in adjudication and arbitration

---

## Common Approaches

### Common Law Jurisdictions

Approaches vary and may include:
- **Dominant cause** — which delay had the greater impact?
- **Apportionment** — split responsibility between the parties
- **First-in-time / prevention principle** — the first cause takes precedence

The applicable approach must be checked against the governing law and contract terms.

### Civil Law Jurisdictions

- More reliance on **good faith and fairness**
- Tribunals may look at overall impact and whether one party's actions prevented the other from mitigating delay
- Less rigid adherence to a single analysis method

---

## Strategic Recommendations — 7-Dimension Framework

### 1. Situation Assessment & Risk Assessment

- Identify all delaying events early and classify them as **Employer-caused**, **Contractor-caused**, or **neutral**
- Analyse periods of potential concurrency and document them
- Assess the risk of the other side arguing concurrency to reduce EOT or impose liquidated damages

### 2. Argument Strategy

- **Primary:** Use strong factual evidence to show Employer delay events were on the critical path and that Contractor delays did not cause overall delay
- **Fallback:** Argue for apportionment or prevention/good faith reasoning if full EOT is not accepted
- **Avoid** weak global arguments — be specific about which events caused which delay

### 3. Disclosure Control

- Be careful with full programme disclosure if it shows Contractor-caused delays — seek legal advice on what must be disclosed
- Clearly separate Employer-caused delay from Contractor-caused delay in submissions

### 4. Response Architecture

- **Proactively** address potential concurrency arguments rather than waiting for the other side to raise them
- Use clear diagrams or summaries showing critical path and overlapping periods

---

## Practical Tips

- Maintain detailed and updated programmes throughout the project
- Use a consistent delay analysis method and be prepared to justify it
- In international arbitration, be ready to explain which approach should apply based on governing law and contract
- Consider engaging a delay expert early if concurrency is likely to be a major issue
