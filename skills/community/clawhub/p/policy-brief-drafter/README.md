# Policy Brief Drafter

**Platforms:** Claude · Openclaw · Codex
**Domain:** Public Policy

## Purpose

Turns legislative text, regulatory dockets, academic studies, expert interviews, and stakeholder notes into a 2–4 page decision-maker policy brief targeted at a specific audience and a specific decision. Produces an evidence-tied options analysis with a single recommendation, a risks-and-trade-offs section, a source map, a decision-window flag, and a talking-points appendix.

## When to Use

- Government affairs analyst preparing a brief for an internal executive or board on a pending bill, rule, or hearing
- Think-tank researcher converting a long report into a 2–4 page brief for legislators or regulators
- Advocacy or trade-association staff turning a coalition position into a written brief for a member meeting or regulatory comment-letter campaign
- Agency staffer producing a decision memo for a principal ahead of a vote, comment deadline, or hearing
- Communications team needing a brief plus talking points for an executive testifying or speaking at a panel

## What It Does

**Phase 1: Audience and Ask**
1. Forces clarification of (a) the specific decision-maker(s), (b) the exact decision being asked of them, and (c) the decision-window date — refuses to draft without all three
2. Identifies the brief type (informational, advocacy with a clear ask, or comparative options memo) and the disclosure posture (internal-only, member-distribution, public-record)

**Phase 2: Evidence Intake and Source Discipline**
3. Ingests source material in batches with per-item tagging: primary (statute, regulation, court decision, hearing transcript, official data series), peer-reviewed, gray literature, advocacy/industry, news/opinion, expert interview (attributed / on background / off the record)
4. Routes any quantitative claim to a sourced statement; flags claims that cannot be sourced as "unsourced — drop or downgrade to qualitative"
5. Surfaces material counter-evidence and dissent — refuses to write a one-sided brief without explicitly noting that opposing positions exist

**Phase 3: Options Analysis**
6. Builds an options table (typically 3–5 options including status quo and the recommended option) scored against the audience's stated criteria (e.g., cost, equity, feasibility, legal authority, political viability, timeline)
7. Names the recommended option and the falsifiable conditions under which the recommendation would change

**Phase 4: Brief Draft and Talking Points**
8. Produces a 2–4 page brief: executive summary, problem statement, background, options analysis, recommendation, risks and trade-offs, evidence-source map, decision-window flag
9. Appends a talking-points sheet (≤10 lines) and a likely-questions Q&A set for the audience
10. Runs a self-check rubric (audience named, ask named, decision-window present, every quantitative claim cited, counter-evidence acknowledged, no unattributed quotation, no fabricated source, disclosure posture honored)

## Output

A 2–4 page DRAFT policy brief with an options table, named recommendation, risks/trade-offs, evidence-source map, decision-window flag, and a separate talking-points + Q&A appendix.

## Notes

The skill writes briefs — it does not predict legislative outcomes, give legal advice, recommend specific lobbying contacts, or take positions on contested partisan questions on its own. Every quantitative claim must be tied to a user-supplied source; the skill never invents citations or URLs. Confidential coalition strategy, draft regulatory comments, and member positions shared in session are excluded from tool calls, examples, and external searches.
