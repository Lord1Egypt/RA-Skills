# Delay Analysis Methods — Strategic Overview

Delay analysis is the process of investigating the causes and effects of delays on a construction project. The method chosen can significantly impact the strength of an Extension of Time or prolongation claim.

---

## Main Delay Analysis Methods

### As-Planned vs As-Built

Compares the original baseline programme with actual progress.

- **Strengths:** Simple, easy to understand
- **Weaknesses:** Can be challenged if the baseline was unrealistic
- **Best for:** Simple delay scenarios with a clear baseline

### Time Impact Analysis (TIA)

Adds delay events one by one to an updated programme to show the impact at the time they occurred.

- **Strengths:** Generally considered robust and widely accepted
- **Weaknesses:** Requires good contemporaneous programme records; can be complex
- **Best for:** Complex projects with multiple delay events

### Collapsed As-Built / But-For Analysis

Removes delay events from the as-built programme to show what the completion date would have been "but for" those events.

- **Strengths:** Uses actual as-built data
- **Weaknesses:** Retrospective; can be challenged on assumptions about what "would have" happened
- **Best for:** Post-completion analysis

### Windows Analysis

Divides the project into time windows and analyses delays within each window.

- **Strengths:** Useful for complex projects with multiple issues; can isolate periods of delay
- **Weaknesses:** Complex; window boundaries can be disputed
- **Best for:** Large, complex projects with multiple overlapping events

---

## Method Comparison

| Method | Complexity | Reliance on Programme | Timing | Acceptance |
|--------|------------|----------------------|--------|------------|
| As-Planned vs As-Built | Low | Baseline + As-built | Post-completion | Moderate |
| Time Impact Analysis | High | Updated programmes | Prospective | High |
| Collapsed As-Built | Medium | As-built | Retrospective | Moderate-High |
| Windows Analysis | High | Updated programmes | Either | High |

---

## Strategic Considerations — Common Law vs Civil Law

### Common Law

- Often a strong preference for methods that demonstrate **causation at the time the delay occurred** (e.g. TIA)
- Tribunals may expect a clear link between specific events and critical path impact

### Civil Law

- The approach may be more **flexible and pragmatic**
- Tribunals may focus on overall fairness, good faith, and whether the claimed events genuinely impacted progress
- Analysis must still be logical and supported by contemporaneous records

---

## Strategic Recommendations

1. Choose a method appropriate to the **project complexity** and **quality of available records**
2. Be **consistent** — changing methods mid-claim without good reason damages credibility
3. In common law jurisdictions, be prepared to defend the method by reference to established practice
4. In civil law or international arbitration, focus on demonstrating overall impact and fairness while still providing clear analysis
5. **Maintain and update the programme contemporaneously** — this is the foundation of credible delay analysis
