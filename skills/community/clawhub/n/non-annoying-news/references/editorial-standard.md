# Editorial Standard

## Goal

Create a compact personal newspaper that gives the reader the substance of selected sources without requiring them to open every link.

A good issue is useful. A great issue has an editorial spine: the reader can tell why these stories belong together now.

## Source handling

- Start with sources supplied or intentionally saved by the user: URLs, pasted text, files, bookmarks, X/Twitter bookmarks, browser reading lists/bookmarks, read-later items, feed items, or notes.
- Treat saved/bookmarked items as strong intent signals, not verified facts.
- For each candidate, capture: title, URL, author/publisher if visible, date if visible, signal type, core claim, evidence, practical consequence, and uncertainty.
- If linked content is inaccessible, say so and only use what is visible. Never imply that inaccessible material was read.
- Prefer primary sources for facts. Use social posts as signals unless the post itself is the news/source.
- Preserve the user's reason for saving an item when visible through notes, tags, highlights, folder names, or prompt context.

## Selection and issue shape

Before writing, decide:

1. What is the lead story and why does it lead?
2. Which 2–4 themes connect the issue?
3. Which saved signals are duplicates, weak, inaccessible, or better as side notes?
4. What should the reader understand by the end that they did not understand at the start?
5. Which source boundaries must be visible?

Do not simply summarize in input order. Rank for reader value, novelty, evidence strength, and configured user intent.

## Article test

Every article must pass these questions before layout:

1. Would a reader understand the topic without seeing the source list, bookmark, or thread?
2. Is it clear what happened, what is claimed, or what changed?
3. Is the origin of the information visible without sounding like a bibliography dump?
4. Is there a concrete mechanism, argument, number, example, or practical consequence?
5. Is the evidence limit stated plainly?
6. Does the article earn its space on the page?
7. Can the article be summarized as one clear kitchen-call sentence?

If any answer is no, rewrite before rendering.

## Tone

- Newspaper/magazine voice: clear, compressed, concrete.
- Light editorial judgment is welcome; lazy hype is not.
- Avoid influencer phrasing, LinkedIn language, and AI boilerplate.
- Do not write about the source as a post unless that is the news. Write about the subject.
- Avoid generic labels: "interesting", "potentially relevant", "worth watching", "food for thought".
- Use strong nouns and verbs. Replace vague abstractions with specific mechanisms, numbers, names, and consequences.
- Do not apologize inside the issue for source limitations. State boundaries cleanly in source notes or copy.

## Article anatomy

A regular article should include:

- **Lead:** the concrete news/claim in one or two sentences.
- **Body:** mechanism, evidence, example, or model.
- **Relevance:** why the reader should care now.
- **Boundary:** what remains uncertain, inaccessible, disputed, or unproven.
- **Source note:** concise origin/link.

## Issue components

Use a mix of:

- lead article: the most consequential or connective story;
- feature articles: substantial explanations;
- side-rail briefs: short but concrete consequences, counterpoints, numbers, definitions, or evidence notes;
- radar items: compact items that are useful but not lead-worthy;
- source-boundary notes: only where they help trust, not as filler.

## Length targets

Tune to layout, but start here:

- Front-page lead article: 1,600–2,200 characters.
- Side-column brief: 260–460 characters.
- Inside-page article: 650–950 characters.
- Radar/short item: 450–750 characters.
- Cross-cutting analysis box: 3–6 concrete points or 500–900 characters.

Do not add filler to hit a length. Add context, mechanism, consequence, counterpoint, or evidence only when it improves understanding.

## Proofreading gate

Before rendering, read the issue as if the reader has not seen any source. Check:

- Does the issue have a memorable thesis or shape?
- Are the headlines informative rather than cute?
- Does each article explain the mechanism, not just the existence of a link?
- Are saved/bookmarked signals respected and not dismissed without reason?
- Are uncertainty and access limits visible?
- Does any paragraph sound generic enough to fit any topic? Rewrite it.
