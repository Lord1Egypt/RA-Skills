# Expert Engagement Tips

Experts — including delay analysts, quantum experts, and technical experts — can significantly strengthen complex claims, but they must be engaged strategically.

---

## When to Engage Experts

- Complex delay analysis is required (critical path or concurrent delay analysis)
- Quantum is large or difficult to calculate
- Technical issues are central to the dispute (design, workmanship, ground conditions)
- The other side has already engaged experts
- The matter is heading to adjudication, arbitration, or litigation

---

## Best Practices

1. **Engage experts early** — do not wait until the last minute
2. **Choose experts with relevant experience** in your jurisdiction and contract type
3. **Provide clear instructions** and all relevant documents
4. **Ensure independence and objectivity** — the expert's duty is to the tribunal, not the party
5. **Ask for clear methodology** and stated assumptions in the report
6. **Consider joint experts** — agreed by both parties — where beneficial
7. **Budget for expert costs** — they can be significant in arbitration

---

## Types of Expert

| Expert Type | Typical Scope | When Needed |
|-------------|---------------|-------------|
| **Delay Analyst** | Critical path analysis, concurrent delay, programme analysis | EOT and prolongation claims |
| **Quantum Expert** | Cost assessment, formula calculations, loss quantification | Large or complex quantum claims |
| **Technical Expert** | Design review, workmanship assessment, defects analysis | Technical disputes |
| **Programming Expert** | Programme review, baseline assessment, progress analysis | Complex programme disputes |

---

## Expert Reports — What Tribunals Expect

- Clear statement of qualifications and experience
- Independence declaration
- Stated methodology and assumptions
- Clear opinion on disputed issues
- Logical structure linking evidence to conclusions
- Awareness of limitations and alternative interpretations

---

## Common Mistakes in Expert Engagement

- Engaging too late (expert cannot properly review the records)
- Choosing an expert without relevant jurisdiction experience
- Providing incomplete or selective documentation
- Allowing the expert to become an advocate rather than independent analyst
- Not discussing methodology before the report is produced
