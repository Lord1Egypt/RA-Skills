# Techniques: Debiasing and Better Decision-Making

## The Premortem

The single most practical debiasing technique in the book. Before making a significant decision:

1. Imagine your plan has been executed and has failed spectacularly
2. Write down 3-5 specific reasons why it failed
3. Work backward from each failure to identify what could go wrong
4. Build safeguards into your plan

**Why it works**: It bypasses overconfidence by making failure feel real. It also legitimizes dissenting opinions — everyone is imagining failure, so no one is being "negative."

> **Case: The planning team** (Part 3, Chapter 23): Kahneman introduced the premortem to a team that was overly confident about a project launch. The team imagined it had failed and listed reasons: competitor release, technical debt, key person departure. Two of the three reasons actually materialized — and the team had built contingencies for each.

## The Outside View

When estimating time, cost, or probability:

1. **Select a reference class** — What category does this project/decision belong to?
2. **Get the base rate** — What is the average outcome for this class?
3. **Predict from the outside** — Based on the base rate, what is the most likely outcome?
4. **Adjust only with strong evidence** — Only deviate from the base rate when you have specific evidence that your case is different.

**The mental shift**: Instead of "This is my project, and here's why it's special," ask "What happens to projects like this?"

## The Adversarial Collaboration

When you need to test a belief:

1. Find someone who disagrees with you
2. Agree on what evidence would settle the disagreement
3. Both of you gather evidence — including evidence that would disconfirm your own view
4. Write a joint conclusion

**The goal**: Not to win the argument, but to converge on a better estimate.

## The Decision Audit

For every major decision, run this checklist:

- [ ] Am I anchored by an irrelevant number?
- [ ] Am I overestimating because of the availability of a vivid example?
- [ ] Am I ignoring the base rate?
- [ ] Have I run a premortem?
- [ ] Am I loss-averse (valuing avoidance over gain)?
- [ ] Am I framing the decision differently if I change the reference point?
- [ ] Would I make the same decision if someone else proposed it?

## The Focusing Illusion — "Nothing in life is as important as you think it is while you're thinking about it"

When evaluating a decision, check whether you're over-weighting the factor that happens to be top of mind. Ask: "If I change this one factor, how much would my overall satisfaction actually change?"

> **Case: The California happiness study** (Part 5, Chapter 36): People in the Midwest thought people in California were happier. They weren't. The focusing illusion made weather seem more important to happiness than it actually was. When people in California were asked about their happiness, they didn't think about the weather at all.
> **Key takeaway**: Whatever you're focused on right now seems disproportionately important. Step back. Consider other factors.

## The Peak-End Rule Protocol

When evaluating past experiences (for future planning):

1. Identify the peak (the most intense moment, positive or negative)
2. Identify the ending (how did it conclude?)
3. Ask: "Would I make the same decision if I knew the peak and ending were misleading?"

**Example**: A long vacation with a bad last day might be remembered as a bad vacation even though 90% was great. Don't let the peak-end rule distort future planning.
