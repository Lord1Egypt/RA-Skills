# §1 Calendar Systems & Seasonal Rhythms

*Source: E.A. Sherwood, "Calendar of the Ancient Celts and Germans." Posted by Дмитрий Архиватор on Velya forum.*

This section provides the **calendrical and seasonal framework** within which runic practice historically operated. Understanding how ancient Celts and Germans measured time, celebrated seasonal festivals, and structured their year is essential for: timing rune rituals accurately, understanding seasonal correspondences in the Anglo-Saxon Rune Poem, working with lunar vs. solar cycles in divination, and connecting rune magic to its proper cultural-temporal context.

---

## FUNDAMENTAL PRINCIPLE: NIGHT PRECEDES DAY

Both Celts and Germans counted time by **nights, not days**. This is not merely a convention — it reflects a cosmological principle where darkness precedes light, the unknown gives birth to the known, and the feminine (night) precedes the masculine (day).

**Celtic evidence:**
- Caesar records that Gauls counted by nights, not days
- Old Irish expressions: "three nights," "nine nights," "the year before this evening"
- "Oidche samain" = "the night of Samhain" = the night BEFORE November 1st
- "Oidche sathuirn" = "the night of Saturday" = the night connecting Friday to Saturday
- "Night" was understood as "night together with half the light" of the following day

**Germanic evidence:**
- All Germanic peoples counted by nights — the day began at sunset
- This principle survives in modern English "fortnight" (fourteen nights = two weeks)
- Before the 7-day week, Germans counted by 14-night periods

**Implication for rune practice:** Ritual work traditionally begins at nightfall. The most powerful time for rune magic is the night, particularly the transitional nights at seasonal boundaries. When the Anglo-Saxon Rune Poem speaks of "day" (Dagaz), it is the daylight portion that follows the night — the breakthrough that emerges from darkness.

## THE TWO-SEASON YEAR

The oldest division in both Celtic and Germanic tradition is **two seasons: Summer and Winter**. The three-season and four-season divisions came later under Roman influence.

### Celtic Two-Season Year

| Season | Celtic Term | Meaning | Period |
|--------|------------|---------|--------|
| Summer | *samo* (Old Celtic), *sam/samrad* (Irish), *hâf* (Welsh) | Summer | May 1 – October 31 |
| Winter | *gimo/gaimo* (Old Celtic), *gemred* (Irish) | Winter | November 1 – April 30 |

The year began with **winter** (Samhain, November 1). Summer and winter were each considered "half-years" (*hanter blwydyn* in Welsh). The two half-years were not equal: 7 winter months and 5 summer months were counted, yet both were treated as "half-years."

### Germanic Two-Season Year

| Season | Norse Term | English Cognate | Period |
|--------|-----------|----------------|--------|
| Summer | *sumar* | summer | mid-April – mid-October |
| Winter | *vetr* | winter | mid-October – mid-April |

The Germanic year also began with **winter**. Each half-year (*misseri*) was further divided into beginning, middle, and end. The dividing line was the equinox, called *jafndœgr* (Old Norse), *efenniht* (Anglo-Saxon), *ebennath* (Old High German) — literally "equal night."

**Implication for rune practice:** The two-season framework connects directly to the rune row's structure. The first aett (Freyja's) aligns with the generative, growing forces of summer; the second aett (Heimdall's) with the disruptive, transformative forces of the winter half. The third aett (Tyr's) represents the social and ancestral order that must be maintained through both halves.

## THE FOUR GREAT CELTIC SEASONAL FESTIVALS

These four festivals, preserved in Irish tradition, governed the Celtic ritual year. They are significant for rune practice because they mark the **power points** of the annual cycle — times when the boundaries between worlds thin, making divination and magical work especially potent.

### 1. SAMHAIN (November 1) — The Beginning

- **Meaning:** "Union," "joining together"
- **Nature:** The night that united the earthly world of humans and the otherworldly realm of the dead (*sid*)
- **Significance:** The Celtic New Year. Neither belonging to the old year nor the new — a time **between** years
- **Practice:** Druids burned offerings on bonfires. Cattle were driven between two fires for purification and protection through the coming year
- **Dual nature:** Samhain was viewed from two sides — dark (the dead walk) and light (the new year begins)
- **Later adaptation:** Became All Saints' Day (November 1) and Halloween (October 31)

**Rune correspondence:** This is the time of **Hagalaz** (disruptive transformation), **Eihwaz** (death/transition), and **Perthro** (the veil between worlds, fate). The Perthro Principle — "this cannot be known" — is most active during Samhain. Divinations performed at Samhain carry special weight but also special uncertainty.

### 2. IMBOLC (February 1) — Purification

- **Meaning:** "Lustration," purification after winter
- **Significance:** Anchored the poles of the calendar year — beginning of winter (Samhain) and beginning of summer (Beltane)
- **Nature:** A threshold of emergence — the first stirrings of spring within winter's depths
- **Later adaptation:** Replaced by St. Brigid's Day, then Candlemas (Presentation of the Lord)

**Rune correspondence:** This is the time of **Berkano** (birch, new growth within containment), **Nauthiz** (constraint that contains hidden potential), and **Isa** (the ice that is beginning to thin). Imbolc is the Isa-in-reverse: hidden development preceding rebirth.

### 3. BELTANE (May 1) — The Bright Fire

- **Meaning:** "Bright fire" or "fire of Bel"
- **Dedicated to:** The god Lugh (corresponding to Roman Mercury)
- **Significance:** Marked the beginning of the light half of the year. The date of the greatest gatherings of druids
- **Practice:** Great bonfires lit on hilltops. Cattle driven to summer pastures
- **Later adaptation:** May Day celebrations

**Rune correspondence:** This is the time of **Sowilo** (the sun's victory), **Dagaz** (breakthrough, the day dawns), and **Wunjo** (joy, harmony). The first aett's energies — Fehu, Uruz, Kenaz — are at their peak.

### 4. LUGHNASA (August 1) — The Harvest Assembly

- **Meaning:** "Assembly in honor of Lugh" or "Lugh's marriage"
- **Significance:** Lugh as fertility deity distributing his wealth. The festival of autumn and harvest
- **Historical note:** In Roman Gaul, replaced by the Concilium Galliarum at Lyon, honoring the Emperor instead of Lugh
- **Nature:** A celebration of abundance, but also acknowledgment that the light half is ending

**Rune correspondence:** This is the time of **Jera** (harvest, fruits of labor), **Fehu** (abundance, wealth), and **Gebo** (exchange, giving back). The transition from Jera to Hagalaz — from harvest to the first hints of disruption.

## THE COLIGNY CALENDAR — CELTIC LUNAR SYSTEM

Discovered in 1897 near Lyon, France, the Coligny calendar is a bronze plaque inscribed with a 5-year lunisolar cycle — the most detailed surviving Celtic calendar.

### Structure
- **62 months** arranged in 5 years of 12 months each, plus 2 intercalary months
- Year begins with *samon* (summer) and ends with *cantlos*
- Months alternate between 30-day (*mat* = "good/complete") and 29-day (*anm* = "incomplete")

### Month Names of the Coligny Calendar

| # | Name | Days | Season |
|---|------|------|--------|
| 1 | Samon | 30 | Summer half begins |
| 2 | Dumann | 29 | |
| 3 | Ruiros | 30 | |
| 4 | Anagantios | 29 | |
| 5 | Ogron | 30 | Beginning of cold? |
| 6 | Cutios | 30 | |
| 7 | Giamon | 29 | Winter half begins |
| 8 | Simiuisonn | 30 | |
| 9 | Equos | 30* | *Marked "anm" but has 30 days |
| 10 | Elembiu | 29 | |
| 11 | Edrini | 30 | |
| 12 | Cantlos | 29 | |

**Key features:**
- Each month divided into two halves by the full moon: "bright" half (new moon to full) and "dark" half (full to new moon)
- The second half marked **ATENOUX** ("great night" / "night of the full moon")
- Days marked as favorable (+I), ordinary (I+I), or unfavorable (II+)
- The word *diuertomu* at the end of incomplete months = "turning," "return" of the new moon
- Intercalary month (*giallos*) inserted before *giamon* (end of summer half)

**Favorable and Unfavorable Days:** The Coligny calendar explicitly marks certain days as favorable (+I), ordinary (I+I), or unfavorable (II+). This is the Celtic equivalent of electional astrology — choosing the right day for rituals, business, or divination.

**The "Great Days" Tradition:** The first 12 days of January "fix the character of the time" that will be reflected in the 12 months of the coming year. Each day corresponds to a month. This Breton tradition (*des gourdeziou*) parallels the intercalary days of the Coligny calendar, where each added day bears the name of one of the 30 months.

## GERMANIC CALENDAR SYSTEMS

### The Old Norse/Icelandic Calendar

The most detailed Germanic calendar, combining indigenous practice with Julian adjustments.

**Basic structure:**
- Year of 364 days = 52 seven-day weeks
- Two *misseri* (half-years): summer (26 weeks + 2 days) and winter (25 weeks + 5 days)
- Summer begins on a **Thursday**; winter begins on a **Saturday**
- Each month = 30 days, with 4 "extra nights" (*aukanætr*) added to the 3rd summer month

| 3 | Selmánaðr / Sólmánaðr | 3rd summer | Jun 8–14 → | "Seal month" / "Sun month" |
| 4 | Heyannir / Miðsumar | 4th summer | Jul 13–20 → | "Hay harvest" / "Midsummer" |
| 5 | Kornskurðarmánaðr | 5th summer | Aug 12–19 → | "Grain-reaping month" |
| 6 | Haustmánaðr | 6th summer | Sep 11–18 → | "Autumn month" |

**Leap year adjustment:** Every 7th year, an extra week (*sumarauki* = "summer increase") was added at the end of summer to keep the calendar aligned with the solar year.

### The Anglo-Saxon Calendar (Bede, 8th century)

The Venerable Bede records the Anglo-Saxon calendar system — the most relevant for the Anglo-Saxon Rune Poem used in rune practice.

**Structure:**
- Lunar cycle: 12-month common year, 13-month leap year
- Year began December 25 with **Modraneht** ("Night of the Mothers") — a deeply significant festival predating Yule
- Two months called *giuli* (December and January) — the "Yule months" framing the winter solstice
- Leap year called *thrilidus* ("three-lida") because it added a third *lida* month

**Key insights for rune practice:**
- **Blōtmónaþ** (November) = the month of sacrifices, when livestock were slaughtered before winter. This corresponds to the Samhain season and is an especially potent time for rune work involving sacrifice (Eihwaz, Gebo), death/ancestors (Othala, Eihwaz), and divination (Perthro).
- **Winterfylleþ** (October) = "winter full moon" — the exact moment winter begins, determined by the full moon. This connects to the practice of timing rituals by lunar phases.
- **Hāligmónaþ** (September) = "holy month" — sacred gatherings before the onset of winter.
- **Éosturmónaþ** (April) = named after the goddess Éostre (Easter), a spring renewal festival.
- **Modraneht** (December 25) = "Night of the Mothers" — the beginning of the Anglo-Saxon year, a night dedicated to the maternal divine, connected to the disir and ancestral feminine powers.

## RUNIC CALENDORS (RÎMSTOCK)

The **runic calendar** (*rîmstock* or *primstav*) was a practical application of runic writing to timekeeping, spreading through Northern Europe from the 13th century.

**Form:** Wooden sticks, bone or metal disks with runic signs inscribed on them, marking the days of the year with their ecclesiastical and astronomical significance.

**Calendar structure:**
- Year beginning aligned to either the start of the Norse summer half-year (April 14) or the winter half-year (October 14)
- Some runic calendars preserve the three-season division of the year
- The runic inscriptions marked important feast days, agricultural tasks, and auspicious/inauspicious periods

**Significance for rune practice:** The rîmstock demonstrates that runes were not only used for divination and magic but served as a practical timekeeping system. The same symbols used to read fate were also used to mark time — reinforcing the deep connection between the runic alphabet and the cyclical nature of existence.

## STONE CIRCLES AS CALENDORS

The neolithic stone circles of Britain (Avebury, Stonehenge, etc.) served as calendrical devices connecting lunar and solar cycles.

### Avebury

**Structure:**
- Outer circle: 99 stone slabs (3–4m high, 1.5m thick)
- Two inner circles: northern = 29 stones, southern = 30 stones
- Innermost circle: 12 stones

**Calendrical interpretation:**
- 29 and 30 stones = days in empty (29) and full (30) lunar months
- 12 stones = months in a solar year
- 99 total stones = number of months after which the beginning of the month and year coincide again

**The 8-year cycle:**
- 49 months × 29 days = 1,421 days
- 50 months × 30 days = 1,500 days
- Total: 99 months = 2,921 days
- 8 solar years = 2,922 days
- Difference: only 1 day — corrected by a single extra stone placed before the southern circle

**Sacred number 13:** Germanic peoples associated the number 13 with the 13th intercalary month needed to align lunar and solar years. The number carried a sacerdotal quality: suns/stars with 13 rays, circles divided into 13 segments. This is NOT originally "unlucky" but rather **sacred and transformative** — the extra, the liminal, the month that doesn't belong to either year.

## WEEKDAY NAMES AND NORSE GOD ASSOCIATIONS

The 7-day week reached the Germanic north via Gaul in the 3rd–4th centuries CE. Germanic peoples replaced the Roman planetary deities with their own, creating the weekday names that survive in English today.

### Norse Weekday Names and Rune Correspondences

| Day | Old Norse | God | Rune Connection |
|-----|-----------|-----|-----------------|
| Sunday | Sunnudagr | Sunna (Sun) | **Sowilo** — the sun's victory, life force |
| Monday | Mánadagr | Máni (Moon) | **Laguz** — lunar flow, intuition, hidden knowledge |
| Tuesday | Týrsdagr | Týr | **Tiwaz** — justice, sacrifice, warrior spirit |
| Wednesday | Óðinsdagr | Óðinn | **Ansuz** — divine wisdom, communication, Odin's breath |
| Thursday | Þórsdagr | Þórr | **Thurisaz** — protection, force, the hammer/thorn |
| Friday | Frjádagr | Freyja/Freyr | **Fehu** / **Berkano** — love, fertility, wealth |
| Saturday | Laugardagr | (No Norse god — "washing day") | **Laguz** — water, purification, the flow |

**Note:** Saturday (*laugardagr*) retains its name from the ancient practice of weekly ritual washing — not from any deity. The Romans associated it with Saturn, but no Germanic equivalent was found. This day of purification connects to Laguz (water, cleansing) rather than any warrior or sovereign deity.

## LUNAR VS. SOLAR YEAR — THE FUNDAMENTAL TENSION

Both Celtic and Germanic calendars began as **lunar** systems and gradually incorporated **solar** elements under Roman and Christian influence. This transition is encoded in mythology:

**Celtic:** Myths of gods representing lunar and solar cults in struggle. Lugh as a lunisolar hero who fathers Cú Chulainn, a purely solar hero — symbolizing the transition from lunar to solar dominance.

**Germanic:** The tension between summer and winter, light and dark, is itself the lunar-solar dynamic. Summer is solar (long days, Sowilo's domain), winter is lunar (long nights, Laguz's domain).

**Calendrical reconciliation:**
- Celts: 3 solar years = 37 lunar months (difference only 3 days)
- Germans: 8 solar years = 99 lunar months (difference only 1 day)
- Both systems used intercalary months/days to bring lunar and solar cycles into alignment

**Implication for rune practice:** The **full moon** is the critical moment in the traditional calendar. The Coligny calendar divides each month at the full moon (ATENOUX = "great night"). All four Celtic festivals are timed to lunar phases. For rune work:
- **New moon** → best for beginnings, sowing intentions (Fehu, Berkano, Dagaz)
- **First quarter** → growth, action, building (Uruz, Kenaz, Tiwaz)
- **Full moon** → culmination, power, divination, maximum energy (Sowilo, Jera, Perthro)
- **Last quarter** → release, letting go, preparation for renewal (Eihwaz, Hagalaz, Nauthiz)

## ORIENTATION AND COSMIC DIRECTION

The Celtic orientation system encoded calendrical and metaphysical meaning:

| Direction | Celtic Term | Meaning | Season/Time |
|-----------|-----------|---------|-------------|
| North | *ichtar* | Low, left | Winter, night, the dead, dark gods |
| South | *tûas* | High, right | Summer, day, the living, bright gods |

**The sun's movement as yearly cycle:**
- Daytime: Sun travels "south" (upward) east to west → bright gods rule → world of the living
- Nighttime: Sun travels "north" (downward) west to east → dark gods rule → world of the dead

**Practical consequence:** The Celts turned **right** (sunwise / deasil) for favorable incantations. Entrances to Gallic towns were positioned so wagons had to turn right upon entering. This "sunwise" direction is preserved in folk magic throughout the Celtic and Germanic world.

**Rune practice connection:** When drawing runes in air, tracing bindrunes, or moving during ritual — moving **sunwise** (clockwise / deasil) invokes favorable forces; moving **widdershins** (counter-clockwise) invokes the darker, ancestral, or banishing forces.

## 1. The Nature of Runes as a Closed System of Principles

*Source: t9798 — "Жертвоприношение Одина. Как появились руны?" (Odin's Sacrifice. How Runes Appeared?), based on Anton Platonov's "Практический курс рунического искусства" (Practical Course of Runic Art)*

A critical distinction that every rune worker must understand: **twenty-four individual runes and the twenty-four-rune Futhark as a system are different things.** Each individual rune, having divine origin and being a sacred sign, possesses a certain reserve of magical force and the ability to connect us with a particular egregor or deity. But the totality of the runes, together with their forces and connections, forms a magical system possessing its own powerful egregor and connected to specific Forces of the World.

This concept — the **"closed associative system of principles"** — means that using only these principles and no others, one can describe any possible situation and any scenario of its development. Comparable systems include:
- The 22 Major Arcana of the Tarot
- The 22 letters of the sacred Hebrew alphabet (foundation of Kabbalah)
- The 64 hexagrams of the Chinese I Ching

**Any of these systems can be applied for mantic purposes** — they are systems in whose signs an answer to any question can be formulated. In the process of receiving an answer, each sign opens a particular door into the world of associations, on the basis of which the diviner, using experience, makes the prediction.

On a more subtle level, such systems serve as the basis for more or less precise descriptions of the world. Just as the Hebrew alphabet underlies Kabbalah, and the eight trigrams of the I Ching participate in many Chinese philosophical constructions, the runes form the structural framework for understanding and interacting with the Norse cosmos.

**Key implication for practice:** A person who wishes to work with runes — whether in mantic or practical application — must forge their own path to the doors into the world of associations and images opened by the runes, must feel the archetypal images and mythologems corresponding to each rune. There is no shortcut to this; it requires direct experiential engagement with each rune's symbolic universe.

### What Odin Actually Received

According to Platonov's analysis, Odin undoubtedly knew the runic signs before his nine-night ordeal on the tree. The signs themselves have a "mundane" origin, traceable to North Italic alphabets. **What the sacrifice brought was knowledge of the runes as sacred signs** — their power and how to use it. The revelation gave Odin the Art of seeing the Tree of Runes, the capacity to perceive the interconnections of the entire runic system as a living whole rather than merely a collection of individual characters.

## 2. The Norns: Deep Practitioner Perspective

*Source: t10745 — "Работа с норнами, или Жизнь в доме Великих Матерей" (Working with the Norns, or Life in the House of the Great Mothers), from Raven Kaldera's "Книга йотунов" (Book of Jotuns), translated by Anna Blaze*

This section provides experiential, practitioner-level knowledge about the Norns that goes far beyond the standard mythological descriptions found in encyclopedias.

### The First Secret of the Norns: Cosmic Movement

The Norns are the force that maintains all existence in motion. **If this movement stops, everything dies: people, gods, giants, even the stars.** Without movement there is no life. Even within our bodies, at the cellular level, everything moves ceaselessly — developing, being reborn, dying. This is the first and greatest secret of the Norns.

### Demands of the Norns

Those who work with the Norns are required to possess **absolute independence**. You must clearly understand that ultimately you answer only to yourself and depend only on your own decisions; you must be able to work alone, by your own free will, without outside help. It is impossible to summon the Norns for help in a moment of need. You may appeal to them as much as you like, but they will give you nothing except advice about what paths exist for you and what may — or may not — open in the future. The Norns are not valkyries; they do not intervene directly in the lives and fates of mortals.

### Urðr (Урд) — Past, Foundation, the Deep Memory

Urðr commands the greatest reverence of the three Norns. In her we find strength, support, and understanding of what has happened to us. All of humanity as a species needs to understand its past — we spend lifetimes studying the history of our world, our religion, our own personality. The power of Urðr lies in knowledge of everything that has passed into the past, and in this respect she is akin to a goddess of death. Everything that passes into the past goes to her.

But this is far from exhausting her essence. **Originally, Urðr was the sole Norn, uniting all three hypostases within herself**, but over time, as the worlds grew larger and older, she needed help — and so Verðandi and Skuld came into being.

The concept of **"Urdic consciousness" (урдическое сознание)** is the state Odin entered when he gained the runes at the foot of Yggdrasil — the ability to read the chronicles stored in the Well of Urðr, on the Tree, and in the threads by which the Norns record all that occurs. Through his self-sacrifice, Odin opened for humans and other living beings a channel of access to the runes and gifted them writing — a fundamentally new method of storing information.

### Verðandi (Верданди) — The Paradox of the Present Moment

Verðandi is perhaps the least discussed and least understood of the Norns. She lacks Urðr's might and Skuld's mystery. Yet the very moment in which we live — the "fraction of an instant," the "moment of the present" that Buddhists urge us to inhabit — this is the truth of Verðandi. **It is impossible to measure, impossible to bottle, impossible to fully comprehend.** And this IS the consciousness of Verðandi.

To perceive the moment of the present without looking forward or backward is a truly great gift. **It is precisely in this instant that the ability to create change lies** — to rewrite one's own wyrd, to forge one's own path. If we root too deeply in the past or peer too far into the future, we create predetermination that robs us of choice. But if we can accept the present and live in a single moment, a path opens free from the constraints of what was and what will be.

This does not mean ignoring past and future. On the contrary — if you are fully immersed in the present, consequences are felt instantly. **Working with Verðandi means working with the paradox of eternity.** Living in the consciousness of this paradox grants access to the power of the present instant, which is critically important both in ordinary daily life and in magical practice. Without a clear perception of the present, it is impossible to act, impossible to take the next step.

### Skuld (Скульд) — Duty, Transition, and Inevitability

Skuld is the most formidable of the Norns. Through the lens of Greek mythology she appears as the cruel goddess who severs the threads of our lives, but her work is far more complex. **Skuld governs duty and obligation**, and she not only cuts but also marks threads that must change or break. She governs not only the time of death but other turning points — the birth of a child, a change of profession, meetings and partings with loved ones.

Verðandi merely shows us the path we are currently on; **Skuld marks the points of transition**, destroying the old way of life and creating a new one. She studies the chronicles of Urðr, examines the work of Verðandi, and then marks on the canvas those places where the pattern or color must change, where one thread must end and another begin.

We fear Skuld because she studies the structures of Wyrd and dictates necessary changes — and we generally do not like abrupt change. **Skuld is the path of inevitability and the one who rules the ending of every path.**

Yet working with her brings remarkable gifts. Like Skaði, the third Norn is interested not so much in what comes after death as in the act of dying itself. By marking the point of death on the tapestry of fate, she thereby proclaims change and transition — for by the law of conservation of energy, energy is eternal: it disappears nowhere and arises from nowhere. It is continuous, and Skuld is part of that continuity. **Transforming the energy of ending into a new beginning, Skuld infuses her own essence into the continuously creating world. This is her paradox.**

### Practical Work with the Norns

- **Offerings:** Green tea, milk (goat's milk preferred, but natural whole cow's milk also works), fresh bread. Rye bread and caraway baked goods are less favored. Beer and alcohol are less effective.
- **Meditative practice:** Meditate daily at the same time (evenings preferred). Maintain clarity of consciousness so that the objectivity of the Norns' energy may enter your home and mind.
- **Craft work:** Embroidery, sewing — or ideally spinning and weaving if possible. In meditation, open your consciousness and allow the answer about what pattern you should work on — this is crucial, for the pattern given to you will teach you much.
- **Research paths:** The cult of the Matrons (see H.R. Ellis Davidson), the nature of huldr forest spirits. These can lead directly to the Norns' door.
- **Sanctuary work:** The Norns' sanctuaries are not for mass worship — they are more like Buddhist monasteries, places of inner transformation and understanding. Those who work there need clarity of mind, the greatest possible impartiality, and the capacity to accept suffering and death's inevitability.

## 3. Odin as Breath-Giver (Податель Дыхания)

*Source: t9816 — "Северная традиция: боги, обычаи, обряды и праздники. Один (Водан)" by Galina Krasskova, translated by Anna Blaze*

### The Breath That Gives and Takes Life

Odin as Breath-Giver is intimately connected to Odin as Father of Runes and Seeker of Knowledge. Many of his heiti (byname) indicate the power of the spoken word and concentrated breath: Ómi ("Loud-Voiced"), Hvedrung ("Roarer"), Viðrimnir ("Shouting Against"), Galdraföðr ("Father of Galdr"), Gjöllnir ("Screamer"), Eiðud ("Ever-Thundering").

**In magical practice, speech calls named things from non-existence.** A significant part of runic magic consisted of special chants and singing of runes (galdr — literally "to sing" or "to croak/caw"), through which the magician directed the embodiment of their intent into reality or, sometimes, summoned spirits and brought them under control. Thus, to speak is to give life; and in the Scandinavian pantheon, this ability belongs to the chief of all gods.

Odin breathed life into the first humans — man and woman. **Our life is the interval between Odin's exhale and inhale:** he breathes us forth, and at our mortal moment he draws us back in. Odin does not merely grant us life — he marks its beginning and its end with his breath. This understanding transforms the meaning of every breath in ritual and meditative practice.

### Odin's Hunger and Thirst for Knowledge

"Woden, id est furor" — "Woden is fury" (Adam of Bremen, 11th century). True, but even more so, Woden is thirst and hunger. From the written sources of the tradition, Odin ceaselessly craves knowledge. To gain wisdom, knowledge, and understanding, he will go to any lengths, not stopping even before torment and suffering. The Hávamál verses about the self-sacrifice on Yggdrasil are the most famous example, but there are others:

- The competition with Vafþrúðnir (wager: the loser's head)
- The sacrifice of his eye at Mímir's well
- The seduction of Gunnlöð for the mead of poetry (three nights of love for three sips)

**Odin is hunger and thirst that impel one to go ever further and deeper.** This is the core of his nature, and it is the model for all who seek runic wisdom — the work is never finished, the understanding is never complete, there is always another depth to plumb.

### Odin as Healer

Three sources preserve the tradition of Odin as healer:

**The Second Merseburg Charm** — Wodan heals a horse's dislocated leg:
> Phol and Wodan rode to the forest.
> Then Baldur's horse twisted its foot.
> Then Sinthgunt sang charms, Sunna her sister.
> Then Frija sang charms, Volla her sister.
> Then Wodan sang charms, as well as he knew how:
> Bone to bone, blood to blood, joint to joint — so be they glued.

**The Nine Herbs Charm** (from the Old English Lacnunga):
> Woden took nine glory-twigs,
> He smote the serpent so that it flew into nine parts...
> These nine herbs were created by the wise Lord of the one eye,
> The holy one in heaven, when he hung on the tree,
> And sent them into all nine worlds...

**The Second Runic Spell** (Hávamál stanza 140):
> I know a second — it is useful for healing.

These three sources establish that Odin's healing power flows directly from the same ordeals that gave him runic wisdom — the self-sacrifice on the tree and the mastery of sacred speech. Healing is not a separate domain but an extension of the same principle: **the breath that creates can also restore.**

### Odin's Names and Their Significance

Over 150 heiti are known for Odin. Each characterizes one facet of his activity. Key name-groups include:

- **Fury and Ecstasy:** Óðinn ("Fury"), Wōden, Wōdan, Wuotan (all from óðr = fury, passion, ecstasy, inspiration)
- **Wisdom and Knowledge:** Fimbulþýr ("Mighty God"), Sanngetall ("Truth-Finder"), Óski ("Wish-Fulfiller")
- **War and Death:** Valföðr ("Father of the Slain"), Herblindi ("Army-Blinder"), Hnikud ("Overthrower"), Valgautr ("God of the Fallen"), Bǫðgæðir ("Battle-Inciter")
- **Deception and Change:** Svipall ("Changeable"), Grímnir ("Masked"), Bǫlverkr ("Evil-Doer"), Glapsviðr ("Seducer")
- **Breath and Speech:** Ómi, Galdraföðr, Gjöllnir
- **Stranger and Wanderer:** Gangleri ("Wayfarer"), Hárbarðr ("Grey-Beard")

The sheer number of names indicates not just respect or fear but the recognition that **Odin can assume many forms and manifest in the most diverse guises.** He frequently hides behind the mask of a simple wanderer, traveling through all nine worlds in this guise, gathering knowledge, implementing his plans, and communicating with mortals.

## 4. The Wild Hunt (Дикая Охота)

*Sources: t2057 — comprehensive cross-cultural analysis by Адэлиниэль (Shellir); t363 — Helga_Falko's addition on Odin's Wild Hunt from "Mythology of the North" and Helen Guerber's "Myths of Northern Europe"*

### The Five Universal Features

1. **The Hunt appears in the dark season**, when life retreats before winter's breath — around Samhain for Celts, during Yuletide for Germanic peoples.
2. **Led by beings connected to the Otherworld** — gods of the dead (Odin/Herevin), those who have crossed between worlds (King Herla, King Arthur), or those who cannot find rest.
3. **The Hunt comes from the direction of death** — from the west or north (cf. the Sluagh in Scottish belief).
4. **To look upon the Hunt is fatal** — those who see it die, disappear, are torn apart by hounds, or are doomed to ride with it forever.
5. **The Hunt is an omen of disaster** — war, plague, famine. If it passes over a house, misfortune enters; if many see it, a great catastrophe is coming.

### Germanic vs. Celtic Dimensions

**Germanic (Wodan's Hunt / Asgardrèy):** Encountering the Hunt is not unconditionally bad. Those who meet it in fields and forests (not under a roof) and find the courage to echo Wodan's hunting cry will have a portion of his prey thrown to them, which by sunlight turns to gold and silver. Fields over which Asgardrèy is seen will yield double harvest. German tales also feature the Hunt punishing sinners and unbelievers.

**Celtic (Cŵn Annwn / Hounds of Annwn):** More closely resembles cavalcades of the sídhe. The Hunt appears at Samhain because this festival divides the year into the time belonging to humans and the time belonging to the Otherworld. The risk is being swept away and never returning home. Celtic tales feature sídhe guardians who warn of impending disaster — by appearance, by wailing, or by direct speech.

### The Hounds

Germanic traditions have little specialized dog-lore — hounds follow Wodan's Hunt rather than lead it. Celtic traditions have an elaborate system: white-red hounds of Annwn, enormous green hill-hounds, coal-black fire-eyed or eyeless Grims (guardians of the boundary between living and dead, omens of death).

### The Prey

Prey of the Wild Hunt is seldom mentioned. In Germanic tradition it is typically a boar or deer; in Celtic, a deer or hare. All are sacrificial animals symbolizing rebirth and often associated with dying-and-rising gods. The Christian overlay of "lost souls of the unbaptized" as prey is a later addition.

### Historical Roots

By Afanasyev's analysis, the Wild Hunt is mythologized perception of autumn/winter storms — Wodan's prey is beast-shaped clouds, his whip is lightning, the wind supports his horse's hooves. By Chris Kershaw and Otto Höfler, the Hunt preserves echoes of archaic Männerbünde (warrior brotherhoods) — bands of beast-warriors who hunted sacrificial prey. Surviving fragments of similar customs exist in Britain in the form of autumn men's games and processions near the equinox.

### Regional Variations

| Region | Name | Leader |
|--------|------|--------|
| Germany | Wodans Jagd / Wilde Heer | Wodan |
| Germany | — | Frau Holda/Holle |
| Norway | Oskorei | Odin |
| Denmark/Sweden | Odensjakt | Odin |
| England | Wild Hunt | Herne the Hunter / Gabriel's Hounds |
| Wales | Cŵn Annwn | Gwyn ap Nudd |
| Scotland | Arthur's Hunt / Unseelie Court | — |
| Ireland | Hounds of Finn mac Cumhal | Finn |
| France | Mesnée d'Hellequin | Arlequin |
| Normandy | — | The Devil |
| America | — | Headless Horseman |

### Connection to Rune Work

The Wild Hunt represents the **liminal moment when the veil between worlds thins and the dead ride.** For the rune worker, this is the time of greatest access to ancestral wisdom and the deeper layers of the Wyrd — but also the time of greatest danger. The same principle that makes the Norns' knowledge more accessible during the dark season also makes the consequences of error more severe. The Hunt is a reminder that some forces cannot be controlled, only navigated with courage and respect.

## 5. Ratatosk: The Archetype of the Diviner

*Source: t9183 — "РАТАТОСК-БЕЛКА, ОБИТАЮЩИХ НА СТВОЛЕ ИГГДРАСИЛЬ" (Ratatosk — Squirrel Living on the Trunk of Yggdrasil)*

### The Connecting Link

Ratatosk (Old Norse ratatǫskr, "bore-tooth" — a kenning for squirrel) is mentioned only once in the Eddic corpus, in Grímnismál stanza 32, yet the image carries profound significance for runic practice.

Ratatosk runs ceaselessly up and down the trunk of Yggdrasil, carrying words between the eagle (or the giant Hræsvelgr) at the crown and the dragon Níðhöggr at the roots. The distance between them is too great for direct communication, so Ratatosk serves as the necessary intermediary.

### Ratatosk as Human Archetype

H.R. Ellis Davidson notes that Ratatosk's etymology suggests the squirrel gnaws the World Tree, embodying the idea of change and transformation of the surrounding world. More significantly, **Ratatosk is a manifest image of the mediator between the esoteric concepts of "above" and "below."**

Researcher John Lindow sees in this an allegory for the human condition: dwelling in Midgard, the "middle world," humans are literally between the "higher" world of Asgard and the "lower" world of Helheim. The existential idea of duality genuinely brings Ratatosk's image close to the metaphorical understanding of human fate — constantly tossed between dualistic principles (good and evil, egoism and altruism, conscience and greed).

**For the rune worker, Ratatosk is the archetype of the diviner** — the one who dwells on the trunk (the manifest world) but carries messages between the upper (divine wisdom) and the lower (chthonic knowledge). The diviner, like Ratatosk, is never at rest, always moving between realms of meaning, translating the ineffable into the speakable.

Yet Ratatosk also embodies the **tragic dimension of mediation** — forever in transit, never fully belonging to either realm, gnawing at the very structure that supports all worlds. This is the cost of the intermediary role: the diviner's own roots in any single perspective are shallower than those who dwell entirely in one world.

## 6. The Einherjar: Warriors of Valhalla

*Source: t10754 — "Эйнхерии Вальхаллы" (Einherjar of Valhalla)*

### Who Are the Einherjar?

The einherjar (singular: einheri) are the greatest warriors who fell bravely in battle. The etymology of "einherjar" remains unknown — no linguistic theory can explain the origin and meaning of the term.

Only warriors who died in battle could become einherjar — a living warrior could not be enrolled among Valhalla's inhabitants. After glorious death on the battlefield, the fallen went either to Odin or to Freyja, who divided the souls of the slain equally between them. Those taken by Freyja went to her hall, Fólkvangr. The Eddic texts give no clear criteria for how this selection is made.

### The Number 432,000

Through each of Valhalla's 540 doors, 800 einherjar will march when Odin leads them out at Ragnarök — totaling **432,000** warriors. This number, drawn from Völuspá, may not represent Valhalla's capacity limit but rather the prophesied host for the Last Battle.

### The Cycle of Battle and Feast

The einherjar fight each other daily in Valhalla's courtyard, dealing mortal wounds — but every wound heals, and at the sound of the horn calling them to feast, they are whole again, bearing no grudges. They eat the flesh of Sæhrímnir (the wondrous boar who revives each morning after being consumed each evening) and drink mead from the udder of the goat Heiðrún.

### Connection to the Wild Hunt and Warrior Brotherhoods

Many researchers associate the einherjar with the **Hjadningar** (warriors of the eternally warring kings Hedin and Högni), whose daughter Hild — a valkyrie — resurrects the fallen each night to continue the battle. This directly parallels the einherjar's daily cycle.

The einherjar are also compared to the **Harii** mentioned by Tacitus — and the Wild Hunt (army of the dead riding through the sky under Wodan's leadership) is likely derived from early medieval legends about the einherjar.

Otto Höfler hypothesizes that the myths of Valhalla's warriors contain references to **secret warrior brotherhoods of ancient Scandinavia** — though this theory remains unconfirmed.

**For rune work:** The einherjar's cycle of death-and-revival, battle-and-feast, directly parallels the runic principle that transformation requires destruction of the old form. The number 432,000 connects to numerological systems within runic practice (4+3+2 = 9, Odin's number).

## 7. Hrafnagaldr Óðins: Odin's Raven Charm

*Source: t9741 — "Предваряющая песнь (Вороново Заклинание Одина)" (The Fore-Song / Odin's Raven Charm), translation by N.V. Topchiy*

### Overview

Hrafnagaldr Óðins (Forspjallsljóð) is an Eddic poem typically dated to the 16th century, of unknown authorship. It is uncertain whether the text that has come down to us is complete. Written in fornyrðislag meter with initial alliteration linking each pair of lines.

### The Poem's Narrative

The poem describes events that some scholars connect to the eve of Ragnarök: something happens to the waters of the Well of Urðr, Iðunn changes form, Odin sends messengers to a prophetess to learn the fates of the world, they return without answer, the gods at feast reach no decision, and in the morning Heimdallr sounds his horn — the sound that should begin the Last Battle.

Yet this is clearly not the Ragnarök of Völuspá: the dark elves, dwarves, and dead peacefully go to rest in the morning, and the poem ends with the rising of Sól (the Sun) — not her destruction. The mood is peaceful, as though we see not the Last Battle itself but its habitual, annually repeated earthly reflection in the alternation of day and night.

### Key Stanzas for Rune Workers

**Stanza 1** — the cosmic hierarchy of knowing:
> Allfather can, alfs will understand,
> Vanir know, Norns will show,
> Giantesses give birth, humans endure,
> Thursar desire, valkyries crave.

This stanza establishes that **different beings have different modes of knowing** — and the Norns' way (to show/reveal) is distinct from Odin's way (to be able), from the elves' way (to understand), and from human knowing (to endure/bear). The rune worker occupies the human position: knowledge through endurance, through bearing what is revealed.

**Stanza 5** — Mímir's hidden knowledge:
> Not forever stand neither Sun nor firmament,
> The air ceases not to flow with poison;
> In the most glorious hidden spring of Mímir
> Was knowledge; is that enough for you?

The rhetorical "is that enough for you?" challenges the reader — even the knowledge of Mímir's well may not suffice, for the cosmos itself is not eternal. This is the same unsettling wisdom Odin gained from Mímir's well: **the knowledge that even the gods are doomed.**

### Significance for Divination

Hrafnagaldr Óðins dramatizes the **failure of prophecy** — the gods send messengers to learn the future, and they return without answer. This is the mythological precedent for the **Perthro Principle** ("this cannot be known") and for the Wyrd Rune's appearance in readings. Sometimes the very act of seeking an answer yields only silence, and that silence is itself the oracle.

## 8. Creation of the World and the Sun-Moon Myth

*Sources: t9192 — "Создание мира, страна великанов и Асгард"; t8089 — "Как появились Солнце и Луна"*

### The Creation Narrative (Condensed)

In the beginning was only Ginnungagap, the yawning void. To its north lay Niflheim (realm of mist and cold); to its south, Múspellheimr (realm of fire). When the ice of the Élivágar rivers (flowing from the spring Hvergelmir) approached the fire of Múspellheimr, the melting ice was quickened by sparks — and from this union of fire and ice rose Ymir, the first being.

From Ymir's left armpit emerged a boy and girl; from his feet came the six-headed Thrúðgelmir. Simultaneously, the cow Auðumla formed from the melting ice, feeding Ymir with her four rivers of milk while herself licking the ice — freeing Búri on the third day. Búri's son Borr married the giantess Bestla, and their three sons were Odin, Vili, and Vé.

The brothers slew Ymir and made the world from his body: his blood became the sea, his flesh the earth (Midgard), his skull the sky, his bones the mountains, his hair the trees, his teeth the stones, his brain the clouds. The four dwarves — Norðri, Suðri, Austri, Vestri — hold up the sky at its four corners.

From trees on the seashore, the gods made the first humans: Ask (ash) and Embla (alder/elm). Odin gave them breath, Vili gave them reason, Vé gave them warmth and good complexion.

### Sól and Máni: The Sun and Moon

When the world was yet dark, Odin and his brothers took fire from Múspellheimr and made the sun and moon — their finest creations. But they could not decide who should drive them across the sky.

At that time, a man named Mundilfäri had a son and daughter of extraordinary beauty. In his pride, he named his daughter Sól (Sun) and his son Máni (Moon), declaring that even the gods could create nothing more beautiful. Seeking to enhance his line further, he arranged for Sól to marry a youth named Glen ("Shining") whose face glowed like a star.

For this arrogance, Odin punished Mundilfäri by placing Sól and Máni in the sky to drive the very chariots they were named after — so all could see whether their beauty could outshine what the gods had made. Sól drives the sun-chariot with two white horses, shielded by a great round shield and cooled by bellows; Máni drives the moon-chariot by night.

**For rune work:** This myth establishes the principle that **naming carries power and consequence** — to name a child after a cosmic force is to bind that child to the force's service. The same principle operates in runic practice: to speak a rune's name is to invoke its power, and to inscribe it is to commit to its service.

## 9. Odin's Full Attributes and the Wild Hunt (Extended)

*Source: t363 — "Бог Один" (God Odin), from "Mythology of the North" and H.A. Guerber's "Myths of Northern Europe"*

### Odin's Complete Iconography

Odin appears as a tall, powerful man of about fifty, with either dark curling hair or a long gray beard and bald head. He wears a gray robe with a blue hood, and a wide blue cloak with gray flecks — symbolizing sky and clouds. His attributes:

- **Gungnir** — the sacred spear that never misses; oaths sworn on its point can never be broken
- **Draupnir** — the ring of fertility, which produces eight identical rings every ninth night
- **Hliðskjálf** — the high seat from which all nine worlds can be observed (shared only with Frigg)
- **Huginn and Muninn** (Thought and Memory) — the two ravens who fly out each morning and return at evening to whisper all they've seen
- **Geri and Freki** (Greedy and Ravenous) — the two wolves whom Odin feeds by hand while himself subsisting only on sacred mead
- **Sleipnir** — the eight-legged gray horse, swiftest of all steeds

### The Wild Hunt (Asgardrèy)

As god of the wind, Odin was imagined riding furiously through the air on Sleipnir. The ancient Scandinavian riddle: "Who are those two riding to the thing? Between them they have three eyes, ten legs, and one tail" — the answer being Odin on Sleipnir.

When people heard the wind howling, they cried out in superstitious terror, imagining they saw Odin riding past with a cavalcade of spirits on snorting horses accompanied by baying hounds. **Meeting the Wild Hunt presaged disasters such as war or plague.** Those who mocked the procession would be seized by the whirlwind and carried off; those who greeted it respectfully would be rewarded with gifts that fell from under the horses' tails and turned to gold by morning.

**Odin preferred to hunt in autumn and winter**, especially between Christmas and Twelfth Night. Peasants would leave the last sheaf or a measure of grain as food for his horse. Even after Christianization, Northern European peoples still feared approaching storms, declaring it was the "Wild Hunt" riding through the sky.

In different countries, the same pagan source manifested under different names: in Germany as Wütendes Heer or Oskorei, in England as Herlathing (after King Herla), in Northern France as Hunt of Hel (after the death goddess), and in the Middle Ages as Cain's Hunt or Herod's Hunt.

## 10. Icelandic Magical Tradition: Fiölkyngi, Galdrastafir, and the Principle of Deception

*Source: t362 — "Мистерии севера" (Mysteries of the North), by Амон Ра (Amon Ra), based on lectures by L. Korablev (Сигурд)*

Iceland's pre-Christian Tradition was practically never interrupted. A Christian who believes in elves (эльфы/альвы) and trolls (тролли) is considered perfectly normal even today. Ásatrú is the second official church in Iceland. The last death sentence for witchcraft was revoked by the Danish king in 1690.

### The Foundational Principle: Deception, Glamour, and Cipher

**All magic rests on deception, glamour, and cipher (обман, морок, шифр).** A sorcerer sent a magical storm that dissipated as soon as people discovered who sent it and named his name. Nearly all sorcery recipes instruct that runes (and everything connected to them) must be placed secretly (подкладывать тайно) and worn secretly (носить тайно). An entire book could be written about methods of encrypting the runes themselves — which are already a form of cipher — and composing galdrastafir (which are also encrypted messages).

**For rune work:** This principle illuminates why galdrastafir exist as an encrypted layer above the runic system, and why runic inscriptions historically employed cipher techniques (aett-and-position encoding, branch/twig runes, etc.). The secrecy is not mere concealment — it is functionally necessary. Once the sorcerer's identity was known, his working dissolved. This has direct implications for how rune workers should approach the crafting and deployment of inscribed formulas.

### The Three-Part Structure of Icelandic Witchcraft

**Part 1: Fiölkyngi** ("много-знание" / "much-knowledge") — the general term for Icelandic sorcery, encompassing:

1. **Runes (rúnar):** The foundation of all runic images are the runes of speech (málrúnar — руны речи), meaning the ordinary Icelandic fuþork of 16 runes or the 27 neo-Icelandic runes, which also have different variant scripts.
2. **Galdr (гальдр):** Incantation chanting / пропевание заклинаний. Many spells were written in runes on paper or carved on runic sticks (рунические палочки) and worn on the person or placed secretly against an enemy.
3. **Töfrabrögð (тофрабрёгд):** Spirit management and sorcery techniques.

The terms "rune" and "galdr" vary in meaning across manuscripts; sometimes either word simply means "sorcery in general." Sorcery techniques, runes, and spells are most often used simultaneously.

### Cipher Runes and Galdrastafir

Manuscripts typically contain sorcery staves (galdrastafir — гальдрставы) and sorcery images (galdrmyndir — гальдрмунды), with application recipes and sometimes incantations.

**Cipher method:** A figure or rune is taken, and to the right of it, strokes indicate the rune's number within the ætt; to the left, the ætt number. (Korablev's books detail this system.) Cipher-runes are named after their shape: bag-runes (belg-rúnar — мешок-руны), fish-runes (fisk-rúnar — руны-рыбы), or after the country where they are used.

Galdrastafir also incorporate: circle, cross, lattice/hatch, square, and the Icelandic sign of Thor's Hammer (swastika). In later staves, alchemical signs and Latin letters appear. In late-period staves, magical symbols from many traditions with no relation to Iceland appear.

**Crucial principle: Every stave, image, or runic alphabet has its own name and description, but is never deciphered/decoded (никогда не расшифровывается).** The encryption is permanent — the meaning is known only to the practitioner who created or received it.

### Gender Division of Magic

In the Icelandic tradition, galdr is considered **male magic** because it was written/literature-based (grimoires were widespread for the practice of fiölkyngi), while seiðr, transmitted only orally, is **female magic** — because women at that time generally could not read or write. The full treatment of the galdr/seiðr distinction, including the paradox of Óðinn practicing "unmanly" seiðr and the three types of female practitioners (gyðja, völva, seiðkona), is in `runic-magic.md` §1–§2.

### Spirit Communication

- **Elves/Alfar and the Hidden People (сокрытый народ):** Believed to live in hills, stones, and fields. "Going to the Hidden People" is a separate art, and those who have visited and learned magic from them are considered the most powerful sorcerers.
- **Necromancy:** Communicating with one's own dead (relatives) was likely considered normal, while using others' dead was regarded as bad/black magic. The dead were primarily used for divination/prophesy (прорецания), for which special runes may have been carved. Ghost-messengers (призраки-посылы) are evidenced by many galdrastafir and counter-techniques.
- **Draugr (драуги):** The undead/un-quiet dead who trouble the living, sometimes resembling poltergeists. Sorcerers and skalds could calm/pacify draugr through their sorcery.
- **Land spirits (духи земли):** One sorcerer bound the spirits of a territory to torment his enemy until that enemy left Norway. Vikings removed the "terrible heads" (ужасные хари) from their ships' prows when approaching home shores — a sign of respect for local spirits.

### Power-Skalds (kraftaskáld)

Skalds were feared and respected in all times, including the present. The art of the power-skald lies in composing short vísur-spells (висы-заклинания) rich in kennings (кеннинги) with special internal rhyme. The presence of kennings — which do not immediately reveal what the skald is talking about — amplifies this type of sorcery several times over. Skaldic styles are described in the Younger Edda (Младшая Эдда), but this knowledge is of limited use without language proficiency. They are sometimes called defenders of the people (народные защитники) because they protected ordinary people from evil sorcerers with their vísur.

**For rune work:** The power-skald tradition establishes that **encoding meaning within meaning** — the kenning principle — amplifies magical effect. This is the same principle that makes cipher runes and galdrastafir more potent than plain inscriptions: the layer of obfuscation is not decorative but functional, concentrating and directing the magical force through the additional cognitive work required to penetrate the surface.

## 11. Valkyries — Odin's Warrior Maidens

*Source: t6369 — "Валькирии - воительницы Одина" (Valkyries — Odin's Warrior Maidens), by Lafty (Practicing Master)*

### Definition and Etymology

From Old Icelandic, **valkyria** translates as "she who picks/chooses the slain" (подбирающая убитых). They are immortal warrior maidens in the service of Odin.

### Physical Appearance — Debunking the Winged Myth

**Contrary to popular belief, valkyries were NOT winged.** The Eddic texts state that the warrior maidens rode winged horses (крылатые кони), which enabled them to travel between Asgard and Midgard. The wings belong to the horses, not the valkyries themselves. They were depicted as stately, fair-haired maidens (статные и светловолосые девы) in shining armor (сверкающие доспехи). Their bare swords emit radiance, and the manes of their steeds drip with heavenly moisture (небесная влага). Their armor and weapons shone so brightly that the ancient Scandinavians believed this brilliance causes the Northern Lights (северное сияние).

### Key Names and Numbers

Total valkyries: 13 (by one version) or 9 (by another). Three are singled out as the most important:

- **Gunnr (Гунн)** — "battle" (битва)
- **Róta (Рота)** — "she who sows confusion/turmoil" (сеющая смятение)
- **Skuld (Скульд)** — "duty/obligation" (долг) — the same Skuld who is the third Norn

These three specifically decide how a battle ends and who perishes. **Skuld's dual role as both valkyrie and Norn** establishes a direct link between the warrior-death aspect of fate and the broader weaving of wyrd. This connection means that any rune reading touching on themes of duty, obligation, or inevitable transition carries Skuld's valkyrie aspect as well as her Norn aspect.

### Valkyries Do NOT Fight

Despite being called warrior maidens, valkyries do not participate in battles themselves. The Eddic texts contain no episodes of valkyries fighting anyone. Their role is to choose and collect the dead, not to wield weapons in combat.

### Valkyries in Valhalla — Cup-Bearers, Not Pleasure-Givers

In Gylfaginning, valkyries also serve in Valhalla. They do NOT pleasure the einherjar, as many assume. Their role is specifically to ensure the cups of the fallen warriors are always full — they are cup-bearers/attendants (валькирии подносят мёд эйнхериям), not concubines.

### The Alaisiagae — Roman-Era Precursors

During the Roman Empire, valkyries were apparently known as the Alaisiagae (алайсиаги). From Proto-Germanic, alaidsaigae translates as "those who inspire terror" (наводящие ужас) or alternatively "all-conquering" (всепобеждающие). The cult of the Alaisiagae was widespread among warriors; they were venerated as companions of Odin and believed to bring death and chaos to enemies. Depictions of the Alaisiagae are found on Hadrian's Wall (northern England). The Celts also worshipped the Alaisiagae. Many scholars believe the cult of the Alaisiagae later transformed into the cult of the valkyries.

### Sigrdrífa (Сигрдива) — The Valkyrie Who Disobeyed Odin

The famous valkyrie Sigrdrífa appears in Sigrdrífumál. She made the wrong choice in a duel between Agnar and Hjalm-Gunnarr. Gunnarr was more worthy of victory and was chosen by Odin himself, but Sigrdrífa had feelings for Agnar and chose him instead. Odin's punishment: he put Sigrdrífa to sleep, stripped her of the ability to participate in battles, and gave her the possibility of marriage — implying a full-fledged valkyrie could not have a permanent husband. Many researchers believe the image of Sigrdrífa later transformed into Brynhildr (Брюнхильда), the beloved of Sigurd, which later formed the basis of the Nibelungenlied.

**Þrúðr (Труд)**, whose name means "strength/power" (сила), was the daughter of Thor and Sif — a valkyrie who is also the embodiment of Thor's own force.

## 12. Ragnarök: The Death of the Gods — Detailed Narrative

*Source: t10359 — "Гибель богов" (The Death of the Gods), by Helga_Falko, drawing on Helen Guerber's "Myths of Northern Europe," R. B. Anderson's "Viking Tales of the North," J. Jones's "Valhalla," and the Elder Edda*

### The Core Doctrine: Mortality of the Gods

A defining feature of Germanic-Scandinavian mythology is the belief that the gods are mortal. The Æsir had a beginning; therefore they must have an end. Since the gods originated from both gods and giants, they were imperfect and doomed to death. Like humans, they had to die so their souls could achieve immortality. The entire mythology is framed as a dramatic work with a climax, a tragic end, and punishment and reward in equal measure.

The gods gradually accommodated the presence of evil among them, embodied by Loki. They passively followed his advice, allowing him to draw them into schemes from which they could extricate themselves only by sacrificing their values. Eventually Loki gained such power that he dared to deprive them of their most precious possession — the purity and innocence embodied by Baldr the Bright. The gods realized too late how malignant this spirit was, and too late banished him to earth.

### Fimbulwinter (Фимбульветер)

Sól and Máni grew pale with terror and drove their chariots with trembling hands, glancing fearfully at the pursuing wolves ready to devour them. When the smile left their faces, the earth grew dark and cold, and the harsh "giant's winter" Fimbulwinter began. Snow fell from all sides, evil winds blew from the north, and the entire earth was covered in thick ice. This severe winter lasted three years, and then another three years — nothing joyful remained, and crimes multiplied until people entirely forgot compassion.

### The Wolves of Fenrir

In the dark thickets of Járnviðr (Ярнвид — the Iron Forest), the giantess Angrboda raised the offspring of Fenrir: Hati ("hater"), Sköll ("deceit"), and Managarm ("moon dog"), feeding them the bones of murderers and adulterers. They grew quickly, then chased Sól and Máni and eventually swallowed them, flooding the earth with blood dripping from their jaws.

### The Battle of Vígríd — Combat Pairings

- **Fenrir vs. Óðinn:** The wolf grew larger every moment until his gaping jaws spanned the entire space between heaven and earth, and then the monstrous beast threw himself upon the father of gods and swallowed him.
- **Freyr vs. Surtr:** Freyr fought with all his might, but Surtr with his flaming sword dealt him a mortal wound.
- **Heimdallr vs. Loki:** Heimdallr initially gained the upper hand against his sworn enemy, but ultimately fell dead.
- **Týr vs. Garm:** Both killed each other.
- **Þórr vs. Jörmungandr:** Thor killed the serpent with Mjöllnir, then stepped back nine paces and drowned in the venom flowing from the dying monster's jaws.

### Víðarr's Vengeance

Víðarr stepped on Fenrir's lower jaw, grabbed the upper jaw with his hands, and ripped Fenrir's jaws apart with one mighty pull — avenging his father Odin.

### The Rebirth

After Surtr's all-consuming fire, the earth is reborn. From the grove of Hoddmímir emerge two people: the woman Líf (Лив) and the man Lífþrasir (Ливтрасир), who had hidden there when Surtr set the world on fire, nourishing themselves on morning dew.

The surviving gods return to Iðavöllr: Váli and Víðarr, Thor's sons Móði and Magni (who saved Mjöllnir from the fire), Hœnir returning from exile among the Vanir, and the radiant Baldr with his brother Höðr — the brothers reconcile and will live in complete harmony. On Iðavöllr they find the golden game-pieces (тавлеи золотые) that the Æsir once used for amusement.

Gimlé, the highest divine hall, survived the fire, its golden roof outshining the sun. Two additional halls exist for beings who lack free will: Sindri's hall in the mountains of Níða for dwarves (who drink foaming beer), and Brímir's hall in Ókólnir ("not cold") for giants.

### Christian Influence Note

It is possible that Scandinavian skalds borrowed elements of Christian doctrine during six centuries of contact with Christians. A stanza was added to the Edda asserting that a new God, whose name must not be spoken, will come to rule in Gimlé. Sinners will be banished to Náströnd, while the benevolent will enter Gimlé.

**For rune work:** The Ragnarök narrative provides the eschatological framework for understanding the Norns' weaving and the cyclical nature of Norse time. The combat pairings are mythological correspondences for rune combinations — each pairing embodies a fundamental cosmic tension. The nine steps Thor takes before falling from the serpent's venom echo the sacred number 9 (Odin's number). The rebirth — with Líf and Lífþrasir emerging from Hoddmímir's grove — parallels the runic principle that destruction is a prerequisite for renewal (Hagalaz → Jera → Dagaz).

## 13. Thurses, Vættes, Dwarves, and Their Kin — Norwegian Folklore

*Source: t10079 — "Турсы, ваетты, карлики и им подобные" (Thurses, Vættes, Dwarves and Their Kin), by Helga_Falko, from Norwegian folklore*

### Tusser / Thurses / Trolls

Human-sized, with regular features but pale or blue color (бледного или синего цвета). Inhabited mountain ridges and hills. In ancient times they were so numerous that no Christian could live in Norway until Christians began entering into mixed marriages with trolls. They had houses, churches, personal belongings, and fine cattle that grazed at night under women-guards or black dogs. Their life began when the sun set. They particularly favored young women and would abduct young virgins into the mountains. They liked human children and would swap them for their own less beautiful offspring (changelings).

**Protective measures against changelings:** Cross the child (make the sign of the cross) or place any iron object of any shape in the cradle. **Iron as apotropaic** against supernatural beings is a major Norwegian folk practice.

### Jutuls (Ютулы) — Mountain Giants

Big and strong, inhabiting the highest mountains, possessing great wealth and treasures. Evil by nature — they hate churches and the sound of bells, and are greedy for Christian blood. During storms, the jutul shakes off the snow and the clanking of pots and kettles can be heard as his wife Givri/Giogra (from Old Norse gygr/gyfr — giantess) cooks his food. They bear the most signs of deep antiquity of all Northern supernatural beings. Correspond to the jötnar and risar of Old Norse myth — enemies of the gods, with Thor as their most dangerous enemy.

### Legend: The Jutul of Hestmandø and the Maiden of Lekoe

A jutul on Hestmandø loved a maiden on Lekoe in Nummedal — a haughty maiden highly skilled in all types of magic. She not only rejected the jutul but turned all his messengers to stone (these stones still exist). Enraged, the jutul drew his bow; his arrow pierced the mountain Torgehat (the hole is 600 feet high, still visible). Because the arrow was slightly delayed, it fell at the maiden's feet rather than striking her. Because both used magic against each other, they both turned to stone and remain condemned to gaze at each other until Judgment Day. Even today, Northerners rarely sail past without tipping their hats to the Maiden of Lekoe.

### Legend: The Jutul's Bridge

A jutul from Elsrudkolle courted a hulder on Engerkolle across the sea. He built a bridge so he wouldn't arrive wet. As soon as the rising sun discovered his work, the bridge shattered — confirming the rule that the works of jötnar cannot survive daylight.

**For rune work:** These legends establish the principle that **jötun/giant magic is powerful but bound by cosmic constraints** — it cannot endure daylight, it turns its wielder to stone when pitted against equal magic, and it is ultimately subordinate to the cosmic order the Æsir represent. The iron apotropaic is directly relevant to protective rune work, and the chthonic beings correspond to specific runes (Thurisaz for thurses, Eihwaz for underworld forces).

## 14. Danish Troll Legends and the Hidden Peoples

*Source: t10123 — "Тролли, народ курганов, или горный народ, народы эльфов и гномов" (Trolls, the Barrow People, or the Mountain People, the Peoples of Elves and Dwarves), by Helga_Falko, from Danish folklore*

### Three Origin Traditions for Trolls

1. **Fallen Angels (Jutland):** When God cast the fallen angels from heaven, those who landed on hills and barrows became the Barrow People (народ курганов) / Mountain People / Hill People. Those who fell on heather moors became elves of the heather moors. Those who fell into dwelling houses became nisser (household spirits).

2. **Eve's Unwashed Children:** Eve hid unwashed children from God; he declared they would be hidden from humanity forever. Their descendants became all the subterranean peoples (подземные народцы).

3. **Rabbinical Tradition:** During Adam's 130-year curse, his children were only shedim (demons).

### Key Folklore Observations for Rune Work

- **St. John's Night / Midsummer (Иванова ночь):** Critical liminal time when mountains open on red pillars and troll-treasure is visible — echoes of Norse cosmological liminality. At Fibjerg-Bakke, the entire mountain rises on red pillars, with merriment and singing inside, and trolls moving enormous chests of money.
- **Yule (Святки):** Another liminal period when trolls expose their wealth — dangerous to approach.
- **Cross as apotropaic:** Trolls/elves cannot touch anything marked with a Christian cross. In the Holmby beer-borrowing legend, the troll could take no keg that had a cross on it. This reflects the Christian overlay on older Norse protective practices.
- **Eddaic connection explicitly stated:** The magical smiths of Danish legend are identified as descendants of Eddaic dwarves/gnomes (dvergar). They can be commissioned: place iron on the mountain with a silver shilling and name the required item — by morning it is ready.
- **North side ritual protocol:** Knocking three times from the north side to summon trolls for borrowing (the Gamtofte tradition). This spatial orientation practice echoes the ritual significance of cardinal directions in Norse cosmology.
- **Reciprocity with hidden peoples:** Borrowing and returning items is a major theme. Consequences of breaking terms are severe (death), while honoring them brings prosperity. This directly parallels the Gebo principle — gifts demand gifts in return.
- **Shapeshifting:** Trolls can turn invisible and transform into animals (yellow cat in the Knurremurre legend).
- **The changeling luck-theft:** Placing a newborn between newlyweds who haven't said the Lord's Prayer transfers all the luck intended for the new family to the child. This is a distinctly Norse/Germanic folk-magical concept of luck-transfer.
- **Headless horse:** The elf-smith riding a headless horse between workplaces echoes Wild Hunt / Odinic motifs.

## 15. Swedish Folk Legends: Odin, Thor, and Surviving Pagan Customs

*Source: t10095 — "Народные легенды Швеции" (Folk Legends of Sweden), by Helga_Falko, from Benjamin Thorp's "Nordic Mythology"*

### Modern Legends About Odin

- On Gotland and especially in Småland, legends about Odin the Old (Один Старый) are still told.
- In Blekinge, the old custom of leaving a sheaf of grain for Odin's horses survived into modern times — a direct continuation of sacrificial offering.
- **Kettil Runske and Odin's rune-staves:** The place Kettilsås got its name from a man named Kettil Runske, who stole Odin's rune-staves (:runekaflar — the Old Norse term for rune-staves). These were the staves with runes to which Odin tied his oxen and dogs. Kettil also stole a mermaid (русалка) who had come to Odin's aid. **This is a significant mythological reference to rune-staves as tangible objects of power belonging to Odin** — the :runekaflar are not abstract symbols but physical artifacts that can be stolen and that confer power over Odin's own animals and supernatural helpers.
- **The golden ship of Runemade:** Treasures captured by Odin at the Battle of Bråvalla were being sent to Valhalla on a golden ship that sank at Runemade near Nikelberg.
- **Odin's swallow:** The black stork (Ardea nigra) was anciently called Odin's swallow (ласточка Одина).
- **Odin guarding the rye field:** A priest named Peter Dagson, living near Troyenborg, sowed rye. When it sprouted, Odin descended from the hills every evening — of enormous stature, carrying a spear — and prevented everyone from entering or leaving all night, repeating this every night until the rye was harvested.

### Modern Legends About Thor

- Thor, like Odin, came to the North with settlers from Asia and Asgard. He battled the local population — jättar (giants), trolls, and bergsboar (mountain dwellers).
- **Torviggar (Thor's wedges):** Smooth wedge-shaped stones found in the earth, believed to be stones Thor threw at trolls.
- **Aerolites as Thor's playthings:** Meteorites found in many places belong to Thor — typically small but extraordinarily heavy. Few modern people can lift them.
- **Thor's beetle (Торов жук):** On Gotland, Thor was honored more than other gods. A beetle (Scarabaeus stereorarius) was named Thor's beetle. An old tradition holds: if a person finds this beetle lying on its back, they should turn it onto its belly, and the beetle will forgive seven sins — because in pagan times, **Thor was considered the mediator between man and the All-Father (Всеотец)** and the higher power.
- **Christian demonization:** When Christianity spread, Thor's beetle was renamed tordjävul / tordjävle (Thor-devil), still used in Sweden today.
- **The Greenland sailor legend:** Sailors from Bohuslän met an old pagan on the Greenland shore who identified himself as Thorir Brak. He asked about Thorsby ("Thor's village") and the standing stones and altar of Glosshed near Ulfveberg. He revealed: "In the great mound near Ulfvesberg, my entire kin is buried, and at the altar in Glosshed we worshipped the gods." He promised a fair wind if the stones and altar remained intact. **This legend preserves the memory of actual pagan worship sites named after Thor.**

### Yule Customs — Surviving Pagan Sacrificial Practice

- Sacrifices to Odin were accompanied by games and dances. In some places on Gotland, such games are still performed.
- **The sacrificial boar (sonargǫltr):** The boar sacrificed to Freya at Yule. In Sweden the custom survived with a baked loaf shaped like a pig — the Julgalt (йульская краюха). Kept until spring, then crushed in a vessel; what remains is partly given to plowmen, partly mixed with barley and fed to plow-horses — to ensure an abundant harvest. Verelius records this in his notes to Hervarar saga.
- **Etymology of Yule:** Connected to the circular movement of the sun (круговое движение солнца). The period uniting the waning and waxing halves of the year is called Julamot. The new year began with the shortest day — Modernatt (Mother's Night).

**For rune work:** The Swedish legends provide crucial evidence of **continuous pagan practice surviving under Christian overlay** — Thor's beetle forgiving sins, the Yule boar loaf as substitution for animal sacrifice, the standing stones and altars still being maintained. The :runekaflar reference is the only known folk tradition that treats Odin's runes as physical staves (rather than abstract knowledge), which has implications for understanding the material culture of runic practice. Thor's role as mediator between humanity and the All-Father parallels the Algiz rune's function as the connecting channel between human and divine.
