## The Anti-Patterns of Discipline

### Anti-Pattern 1: The Talking Trap
**The mistake:** Explaining, reasoning, and arguing with a child who is already in Stop behavior. "I told you we can't have candy before dinner because it will ruin your appetite and then you won't eat healthy food and then..."

**Why it fails:** Every word you give them is ammunition. The child learns that misbehavior triggers a conversation, which means attention (even negative attention). 1-2-3 Magic removes the talking channel entirely.

### Anti-Pattern 2: The Emotion Trap
**The mistake:** Getting angry, raising your voice, showing frustration. The child learns: "I know how to push Mom's buttons."

**Why it fails:** Emotional reactions are deeply reinforcing to attention-seeking children. If screaming = a big reaction, they will scream more. The No Emotion rule is not about suppressing feelings — it's about withholding the reinforcement of misbehavior.

### Anti-Pattern 3: Over-Explaining Consequences
**The mistake:** "If you don't stop that, you're going to get a 1! Do you hear me? I'm going to count you!" This is not counting — it's threatening.

**Why it fails:** A threat is an invitation to a power struggle. A count is a statement of fact. "That's 1" is not a threat. It's an observation that the child can use to self-correct.

### Anti-Pattern 4: Playing Referee
**The mistake:** Trying to determine who started it, who hit first, who said what. This transforms you from parent to judge — and the children keep escalating because the judge might rule in their favor.

**Why it fails:** You will never know the full story. Both children learn that fighting gets your undivided attention. The fix: count both. 90% of the time, both are contributing.

### Anti-Pattern 5: Resentment After Discipline
**The mistake:** Holding a grudge after counting. Continuing to lecture. "And you'd better be good when you come out of that timeout!"

**Why it fails:** After the consequence, the slate is clean. Holding resentment teaches children that punishment isn't about learning — it's about parental anger. The break ends, and they rejoin the family with a clean start.

### Anti-Pattern 6: Inconsistency
**The mistake:** Using counting sometimes but not others. Counting for a behavior today but ignoring it tomorrow.

**Why it fails:** Inconsistent discipline is worse than no discipline — it creates intermittent reinforcement, which is the strongest schedule for maintaining behavior. If a slot machine only pays sometimes, you keep pulling the lever. If counting only happens sometimes, the child keeps testing.
