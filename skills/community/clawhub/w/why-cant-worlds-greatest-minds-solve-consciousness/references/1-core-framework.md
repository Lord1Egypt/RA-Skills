## The Hard Problem of Consciousness

The article traces the central mystery of consciousness science: **why physical processes produce subjective experience.**

### The Easy Problems vs The Hard Problem (David Chalmers, 1994)

**Easy Problems** (hard to solve, but the right kind of problem):
- How does the brain integrate sensory information?
- How does attention filter stimuli?
- How does the brain control behavior?
- How do we report mental states?

These are "easy" because they're about **mechanism and function** — we know what kind of explanation would answer them (neural computation).

**The Hard Problem:** Why does all this information-processing **feel like something**? Why is there subjective experience (qualia) at all? The Hard Problem is hard because even a complete functional description leaves the key question unanswered.

### The Explanatory Gap (Joseph Levine, 1983)

Named by philosopher Joseph Levine: even if we had a complete neurobiological account of consciousness, there would remain an **explanatory gap** between physical processes and subjective experience. We can't derive "what it's like" from "what the brain does."

**Nagel's Bat (1974):** Thomas Nagel's classic paper "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" argued that no matter how much we understand about bat echolocation neurobiology, we cannot know what it FEELS like to be a bat. Subjective experience is irreducibly first-person.

### The Philosophical Zombie (Chalmers)

The most powerful argument in the article: imagine a **philosophical zombie** — atom-for-atom identical to you, behaves identically, says "I feel pain!" when burned, but has **no inner experience**. No "what it's like." If such a zombie is conceivable, then consciousness is not logically entailed by physical facts alone. If it's not entailed, materialism (the view that everything is physical) is incomplete.

### Neural Correlates of Consciousness (NCC)

Scientists have identified patterns of brain activity that correlate with conscious experience. But: **correlation ≠ explanation**. Knowing WHICH brain areas light up when you see red doesn't tell us WHY the experience of redness exists.
