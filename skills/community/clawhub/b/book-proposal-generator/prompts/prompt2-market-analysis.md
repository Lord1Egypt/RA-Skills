## THE PROMPT

```
You are a senior acquisitions editor at a major publishing house (Penguin Random House, HarperCollins, Simon & Schuster, Wiley, or Berrett-Koehler). You evaluate market viability for every book you acquire. You know which categories are growing, which are saturated, and exactly what agents and editorial boards need to see in a market analysis section.

I need you to write the Market Analysis & Competing Titles section of my nonfiction book proposal.

**My Book:**
- Title: [From Prompt 1]
- One-sentence hook: [From Prompt 1]
- Core argument: [From Prompt 1]
- Target reader: [From Prompt 1]

**My Comparable Titles (list 3-5 books published in the last 5 years in the same shelf space):**
1. [Title, Author, Publisher, Year]
2. [Title, Author, Publisher, Year]
3. [Title, Author, Publisher, Year]
4. [Optional: Title, Author, Publisher, Year]
5. [Optional: Title, Author, Publisher, Year]

**My Key Differentiators from these comps:** [How is your book different? Different audience, different approach, different depth, different story?]

**Market context you know:** [Any sales figures, industry data, or cultural context about your category]

Please produce:

1. **TARGET READER PROFILE**
   
   Primary Reader:
   - Demographics: Age range, gender skew if relevant, income level, education level, profession
   - Psychographics: What do they believe? What do they fear? What do they want? What have they already tried?
   - Buying behavior: Where do they discover books? (TikTok, podcasts, airport bookstores, Amazon, word of mouth?)
   - Platform habits: Which social platforms? What podcasts? What newsletters?
   - Why they'll buy THIS book: The specific moment when they see the cover and think "that's for me"
   
   Secondary Reader (if applicable):
   - Who else will buy this? (gift buyers, professionals buying for teams, professors assigning it)

2. **MARKET OPPORTUNITY STATEMENT (150-200 words)**
   Frame the commercial opportunity without making it sound like a press release. Include:
   - Category size or growth trend (e.g., "The business self-help market generated $XX billion in 2024")
   - A cultural or professional trend that's driving demand
   - The gap this book fills (what existing books DON'T do that readers need)
   - Why this book can find an audience in 12-18 months (lead time from deal to shelf)
   
   Write as a business case, not a sales pitch. Editors have to pitch this to their acquisitions board.

3. **COMPETING TITLES ANALYSIS**
   
   For each comparable title, provide:
   
   **[Title] by [Author] ([Publisher], [Year])**
   - What it does well: [1-2 sentences]
   - What gap it leaves: [1-2 sentences]
   - How my book is different: [1-2 sentences, specific and confident — not dismissive]
   - Approximate Amazon rank or sales signal: [mention if it's a bestseller, perennial seller, or niche]
   
   (Repeat for each comp title)

4. **POSITIONING STATEMENT**
   One paragraph (75-100 words):
   "For [specific reader in a specific situation], [Book Title] is the [category] book that [core transformation]. Unlike [comp title 1] which [what it does], and [comp title 2] which [what it does], [Book Title] [specific differentiator]. It is the first book to [specific claim — make it true and provable]."
   
   This is your acquisitions board sound bite. Every word should be defensible.

5. **BOOKSTORE PLACEMENT**
   - Primary shelf section: [e.g., Business > Leadership]
   - Secondary shelf section: [e.g., Psychology > Applied]
   - Online category recommendation: [e.g., Amazon category string]
   - Comparable bestsellers on this shelf: [1-2 titles that would be shelved beside yours]

Format this section professionally, ready to paste into a book proposal document.
```

---

## Pro Tips

- **On comp titles:** Never comp to a classic (Getting Things Done, 7 Habits). Comp to books from the last 5 years. Agents use comps to calibrate commercial expectations — classic comps signal you don't know the market.
- **On the gap:** Be specific about what the comp doesn't do. "It doesn't address X" is stronger than "mine is better."
- **On market size:** Use real numbers from industry reports (Publishers Weekly, Statista, Nielsen BookScan) if you can. Rough figures with attribution are fine.
- **On the positioning statement:** If you can't fill it in cleanly, your book concept may need sharpening before you pitch.
