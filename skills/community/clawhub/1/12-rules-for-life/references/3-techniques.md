> Source: 12 Rules for Life by Jordan B. Peterson, Rules 1, 4, 6, 8, 10

# Techniques: Applying the 12 Rules

## The Posture Audit (Rule 1)

Stand in front of a mirror. Check your posture:
- Are your shoulders forward or back?
- Is your head tilted down or up?
- Are your feet planted or shifting?

**Exercise**: For one day, practice standing up straight. Every time you catch yourself slouching, reset. Notice how people respond to you differently. Notice how you feel differently. The feedback loop between posture and confidence is real.

## The "Better Than Yesterday" Journal (Rule 4)

Each evening, write down:
1. One thing I did better today than yesterday
2. One thing I will do tomorrow that I didn't do today
3. One thing I'm grateful for

**The rule**: You cannot compare yourself to others in this journal. The only comparison allowed is the one between today's you and yesterday's you.

## The "Set Your House in Order" Protocol (Rule 6)

A step-by-step process for regaining control when life feels chaotic:

1. **Week 1**: Clean your room. Make your bed. Organize your desk. Do this every day.
2. **Week 2**: Organize your finances. Create a budget. Pay one bill ahead of schedule.
3. **Week 3**: Repair one broken relationship. Apologize for one thing. Make one phone call you've been avoiding.
4. **Week 4**: Address one health issue. Schedule that doctor's appointment. Start exercising.
5. **Week 5**: Address one career issue. Update your resume. Have that conversation with your boss.

**The principle**: Do not move to the next step until the previous one is complete. Fix what you can fix first.

## The Truth-Telling Audit (Rule 8)

For one week, keep a "truth log":
- Where did I tell a small lie today? (commission)
- Where did I avoid saying something true? (omission)
- Where did I exaggerate or minimize?

**Exercise**: Pick one small lie you tell regularly (e.g., "I'm fine" when you're not) and replace it with the truth this week. Notice how it feels to be more aligned with reality. The discomfort of telling the truth is temporary. The discomfort of living a lie is permanent.

## The Precision Practice (Rule 10)

When you face a problem you can't solve, name it precisely:

1. **Write it down in one sentence**: What exactly is the problem who, what, when, where?
2. **Identify the vagueness**: Am I using vague words like "always," "never," "they," "them"?
3. **Make it concrete**: Replace "My job is terrible" with "I feel undervalued because my manager hasn't given me feedback in 6 months."
4. **Identify one action**: Based on the precise description, what is the smallest next step?

> **Case: The vague complaint** (Rule 10, Chapter 10): "I'm unhappy in my relationship" is too vague to address. "I feel lonely because we haven't had a meaningful conversation in two weeks" is precise. Once named, the couple can schedule a weekly check-in. Precision turns an unsolvable problem into a solvable one.

## The Responsibility Inventory

Ask yourself weekly:
- What responsibility am I avoiding?
- What small thing can I fix this week that I've been putting off?
- Who needs me to show up for them?
- What would I regret not doing a year from now?

**The key insight**: When you feel lost, take on more responsibility, not less. Responsibility organizes your life. It gives you structure, purpose, and meaning.
