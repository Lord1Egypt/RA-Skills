## The Anti-Patterns of Consciousness Research

### Anti-Pattern 1: "Neuroscience Will Solve It Eventually"
This assumes the Hard Problem is just a harder Easy Problem. But the Hard Problem is categorically different — it asks why experience exists at all, not how the brain processes information. Better brain scanners won't answer why redness looks red.

### Anti-Pattern 2: "Consciousness Is Just an Illusion" (Dennett's Error)
Dennett argues the Hard Problem is a philosopher's trick — there's no special "experience" beyond behavior and function. But this confuses the map with the territory. The experience of having an illusion is itself a conscious experience that needs explaining.

### Anti-Pattern 3: "A Computer Can Be Conscious If It's Complex Enough"
Functionalism — the idea that consciousness is about what the brain DOES, not what it IS — is intuitive but faces the Chinese Room problem (Searle): running the right program doesn't guarantee understanding. Complexity alone may not suffice.

### Anti-Pattern 4: Confusing NCCs with Explanations
"These neurons fire when you see red" does not explain WHY red looks red. NCCs are the effects of consciousness, not its cause (or at best, correlations). This is the most common error in popular consciousness science.

### Anti-Pattern 5: The "Only Humans" Assumption
Assuming consciousness is uniquely human is both scientifically unsupported and philosophically problematic. Evidence for consciousness in animals (mammals, birds, octopuses) is strong. The Cambridge Declaration on Consciousness (2012) affirms this.
