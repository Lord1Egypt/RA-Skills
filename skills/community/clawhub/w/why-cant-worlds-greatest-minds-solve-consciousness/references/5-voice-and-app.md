## Key Figures in Consciousness Studies

### David Chalmers
- Coined the term "The Hard Problem" (1994)
- Argues consciousness may be fundamental
- The philosophical zombie argument
- Supports panpsychism as a plausible view
- Famous quote (implied): "The Hard Problem persists even when we've solved all the easy problems."

### Thomas Nagel
- "What Is It Like to Be a Bat?" (1974) — the paper that started modern consciousness philosophy
- Argued subjective experience is irreducibly first-person
- No objective third-person account can capture subjective character

### Giulio Tononi
- Developed Integrated Information Theory (IIT)
- Conscious level = Φ (phi), a measure of integrated information
- IIT predicts which brain states are conscious and to what degree
- Implications for AI consciousness: could a sufficiently integrated system be conscious?

### Daniel Dennett
- Consciousness is an illusion of a "self" that doesn't exist
- Denies the Hard Problem exists — calls it a philosopher's trick
- Multiple Drafts model vs Cartesian Theater

### John Searle
- Chinese Room argument against strong AI: syntax ≠ semantics
- Biological naturalism: consciousness is a biological phenomenon, not computational

## The Big Question for the Future
If consciousness is not computable (not reducible to information processing), then AGI may need something fundamentally different from current architectures. The article's central tension: the world's greatest minds haven't solved consciousness because it may require a paradigm shift — not more data.
