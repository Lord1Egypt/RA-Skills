> Source: The Subtle Art of Not Giving a Fck by Mark Manson, Chapters 5-7

# Techniques: The Practical Tools

## The Fck Audit

A quick self-assessment to determine whether you are spending your fcks wisely:

1. List everything you have worried about, been frustrated by, or felt anxious about in the past week
2. Next to each item, mark: "In My Control" or "Out of My Control"
3. For items out of your control: stop giving a fck about them
4. For items in your control: ask "Is this aligned with my values?"
5. If not aligned — stop giving a fck. If aligned — give exactly one fck and take action.

## The Good Values Worksheet

Replace your bad values with good ones:

| Bad Value | Good Alternative |
|---|---|
| "I need to be rich and famous" | "I will create value for others through my work" |
| "Everyone must like me" | "I will be honest and kind, regardless of how others respond" |
| "I should never fail" | "I will learn from every failure" |
| "I deserve to be happy without effort" | "I will solve problems I care about" |

**Exercise**: Write down your top 5 values. Are they good or bad? Replace any bad values with good ones. Live by the new values for one week.

## The Do Something Principle

When you feel stuck or unmotivated:

1. **Identify the smallest possible action** — not "write a chapter" but "open the document"
2. **Set a timer for 5 minutes** — do the action for exactly 5 minutes, no more
3. **After 5 minutes, check in** — do you want to keep going? If yes, continue. If no, stop and try again later.
4. **Repeat daily** — the 5-minute habit builds momentum over time

> **Case: The overwhelmed student** (Chapter 7): A student with a massive research paper was so overwhelmed she couldn't start. Manson told her to write one sentence. "Not a good sentence. Not a complete paragraph. Just one sentence that has something to do with your topic." She wrote one sentence. Then another. Then she realized she was writing the paper. "Action is not the result of motivation. Action is the cause of motivation."

## The Shitty Choices Framework

When facing a difficult decision, reframe it as a choice between two types of suffering:

- "Which pain am I willing to sustain?"
- "Which struggle am I willing to commit to?"

Every positive choice involves some negative experience. The question is not "Which option feels better?" — it is "Which suffering am I willing to accept?"

> **Case: The two job offers** (Chapter 5): A client had two job offers: one high-paying but boring, one low-paying but interesting. Manson didn't ask "Which will make you happier?" He asked: "Which set of problems would you rather have?" The high-paying job meant boredom. The low-paying job meant financial stress. Both had problems. The client chose the interesting job with financial stress — because those were problems he was willing to solve.

## The Responsibility Map

Draw a circle. Inside, write everything you are currently giving a fck about. Now, divide the circle into two halves:

| In My Control | Not in My Control |
|---|---|
| Your response, your effort, your values | Other people's opinions, the economy, the past |

**The practice**: Spend 90% of your energy on the left half. Spend 10% on understanding the right half so you can let it go. Zero percent on worrying about the right half.
