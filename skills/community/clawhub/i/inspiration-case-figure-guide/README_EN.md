# Inspiration Case Figure Guide

This skill turns the core "why this work matters" of a research paper into an inspiration figure. It helps authors extract motivation from cases, failures, observations, or contrasts, and shape them into a visual narrative for paper openings, talks, or rebuttals so readers can quickly understand the value and necessity of the work.

## Design Motivation

Many research figures explain how a method works, but readers first need to understand why the work is worth doing. When the motivation stays only in prose, the problem scenario, failure pattern, or key observation can feel abstract. This skill is designed to compress a paper's motivation into one credible, clear, and memorable figure, so readers can see the research problem before entering the method details.

It is especially useful for showing:

- A concrete case that exposes a research gap
- A failure or limitation of existing methods
- A contrast between current behavior and the desired state
- An observation that leads to a research hypothesis
- A boundary case that reviewers may care about

## Main Steps

1. Collect paper material  
   Confirm the paper topic, target figure slot, and intended motivation source.

2. Choose the figure type  
   Decide whether the material is best represented as a case walkthrough, failure explanation, before/after contrast, observation-driven figure, scenario storyboard, or design-space gap.

3. Clarify the reader takeaway  
   Define the core question the figure should answer: why is this paper necessary?

4. Compare candidate directions  
   Explore several narrative directions before locking a single visual approach.

5. Generate and compare candidate figures  
   Use multiple candidate figures to judge which visual direction is clearest, most credible, and best suited to the paper slot.

6. Prepare the final handoff  
   After choosing a direction, refine the title, caption, legend, and body text that introduces the figure.

## Usage Advice

For best results, provide the paper abstract, introduction excerpt, key motivating case, or failure phenomenon. If you already have reference figures, specify which aspects to borrow, such as style, layout, density, or labeling. The final figure should always use cases, data, and conclusions from the paper itself, rather than adding unsupported content for visual effect.
