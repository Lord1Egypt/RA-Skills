> Source: Rich Dad Poor Dad by Robert Kiyosaki, Chapters 1-3, 5-6

# Key Principles

## Principle 1: Don't work for money — have money work for you

Every hour you trade for a paycheck is an hour you don't have back. Build systems and assets that generate income without your active labor.

> **Case: The boy who didn't want a raise** (Chapter 1): When 9-year-old Kiyosaki asked for a raise at his minimum-wage job for rich dad, rich dad refused. Instead, he taught: "If you work for money, you give your power to your employer. If money works for you, you keep the power."

## Principle 2: An asset puts money in your pocket. Period.

The definition is not about appreciation or tax benefits. If it doesn't generate cash, it's not an asset.

> **Case: The house debate** (Chapter 2): Kiyosaki argues that most people think their house is an asset. But if your house requires you to make mortgage payments, property taxes, and maintenance — it's a liability, not an asset. It takes money out of your pocket, not the other way around.

## Principle 3: Mind your own business

Keep your day job for cash flow, but invest your free time and surplus income in building your own asset column. Your job is someone else's business.

> **Case: The McDonald's franchisee** (Chapter 3): "Ray Kroc, the founder of McDonald's, asked a class of MBA students: 'What business am I in?' They all said 'The hamburger business.' Kroc said: 'I'm in the real estate business.'" McDonald's makes more money from owning prime real estate locations than from selling hamburgers.
> **Key takeaway**: The most successful people understand what business they're really in. You should too.

## Principle 4: Financial education is your biggest asset

Your most valuable asset is not the money you have — it's your ability to make more money. Invest in your financial education before investing in anything else.

> **Case: The doctor and the plumber** (Chapter 2): A highly educated doctor earned $200k/year but had no financial literacy. He was deeper in rat race than a plumber who owned a small real estate portfolio. The doctor's education didn't translate to financial education.

## Principle 5: Pay yourself first

Before paying bills, before buying luxuries, before anything else — invest in your financial growth. This is non-negotiable discipline.

> **Case: The power of discipline** (Chapter 5): "Most people say, 'I can't afford to invest.' What they really mean is, 'I haven't made investing a priority.' If you wait until you 'have enough money' to invest, you'll never start. Pay yourself first — set aside money for your asset column before you pay anyone else."

## Principle 6: Work to learn, not to earn

The most important question to ask about any job is: "What skills will I learn here?" Sales skills, leadership skills, investment skills — these compound more than any salary.

> **Case: The best job** (Chapter 6): "The most important skill you can learn is sales and marketing. If you can sell, you can survive any economic downturn. If you can't sell, you're dependent on others for your income."
