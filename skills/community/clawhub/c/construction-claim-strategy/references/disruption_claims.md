# Disruption Claims Strategy

Disruption claims seek compensation for **loss of productivity or inefficiency** caused by events that do not necessarily delay the overall completion date. They are often more difficult to prove than pure delay claims but can represent significant value.

---

## Key Characteristics

- Focus on **loss of productivity** rather than delay to completion
- Often arise from **cumulative effects** of multiple instructions, changes, or interference
- Require strong evidence linking disrupting events to reduced efficiency
- Usually quantified using **Measured Mile**, productivity studies, or actual cost comparison

---

## Strategic Approach — 7-Dimension Framework

### 1. Situation Assessment & Scope Control

Clearly define the claim as a **disruption/productivity claim**, not an EOT claim. Avoid mixing the two unless both are genuinely present and can be separated.

### 2. Argument Strategy

- In **common law** contexts: strong factual evidence and a credible Measured Mile are usually important
- In **civil law** or mixed settings: good faith and fairness may also support the overall impact argument, but factual evidence remains essential

### 3. Disclosure Control

Be cautious with early disclosure of internal productivity data or tender assumptions. Consider phased disclosure where appropriate.

### 4. Risk Assessment

A major risk is the argument that some disruption was **normal and priced in the tender**. Mitigate by demonstrating that the volume, nature, timing, or cumulative effect exceeded normal expectations.

---

## Recommended Primary Argument

**Factual Distinction** is usually the strongest approach:

1. Acknowledge that some level of instruction or change is normal under the contract
2. Distinguish **why** the actual events created materially different and more inefficient working conditions than could reasonably have been anticipated

---

## Common Law vs Civil Law Comparison

| Aspect | Common Law | Civil Law |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| **Evidential standard** | Strong emphasis on proof of productivity loss | More reliance on good faith and fairness |
| **Measured Mile** | Highly regarded when properly applied | Less rigid requirement for a perfect comparison |
| **Baseline assumptions** | Greater scrutiny on productivity assumptions | Focus on whether changes exceeded reasonable expectations |
| **Cumulative impact** | Needs clear causal chain | May consider cumulative impact and equity |

**Strategic implication:** In common law jurisdictions, invest heavily in strong factual evidence and credible Measured Mile analysis. In civil law or international arbitration, balance factual evidence with fairness and overall impact arguments.

---

## Illustrative Scenarios

### Scenario A — Cumulative Instructions

A contractor experiences 47 instructions over 8 months. Productivity on finishing trades drops significantly. Using a Measured Mile from an unaffected period, the contractor demonstrates a 28% productivity loss. A tribunal would likely scrutinise the quality of the Measured Mile and the link between instructions and loss.

### Scenario B — Repeated Interference

The Employer repeatedly changes access arrangements and work sequences at short notice. The contractor argues that the cumulative effect made efficient working impossible. In a civil law or good faith context, the argument may focus on the Employer not creating conditions that undermine efficient performance.

---

## Arbitration Considerations for Disruption Claims

- Clearly separate disruption from any concurrent EOT claim
- Use clear visuals — productivity charts and comparison tables
- Justify why the level of disruption exceeded what was reasonably foreseeable at tender stage
- In international arbitration, explain which legal tradition or hybrid approach should apply based on the governing law clause
