# Promo Mechanics Guide

Each mechanic has a different margin signature and fits a different goal. Pick by objective, not habit. Margin impact below is relative to contribution margin (CM).

## % off

- **Margin impact:** comes straight out of CM; on a 50% CM item, a 20% cut removes ~40% of per-unit contribution. Needs `d/(m−d)` lift to break even.
- **Best use:** broad demand pull, acquisition, sitewide events, items with healthy CM (≥45%).
- **Risks:** trains shoppers to wait; erodes anchor if frequent; deepest discounts scale poorly (lift required explodes as `d` nears `m`).

## $ off

- **Margin impact:** fixed dollar hit, identical in dollars regardless of how many units; on high-ticket items a flat $X reads as a smaller % and protects margin better.
- **Best use:** higher-priced items where "$10 off" is clearer and feels generous, or to hit a psychological price point ($49 → $39).
- **Risks:** on low-priced items a small $ amount is a big % (e.g. $5 off a $20 item = 25%); always convert to a % and run the break-even check.

## BOGO / Buy-2-Get-1 (B2G1)

- **Margin impact:** BOGO (buy 1 get 1 free) is effectively **50% off** across two units — only viable at very high CM (you give away a full unit's COGS + fees). B2G1 is ~33% off across three units, gentler. Moves volume while the *displayed* unit price stays at full list (protects anchor).
- **Best use:** clearing volume, multi-pack-friendly consumables, gifting moments, when you'd rather protect headline price than post a markdown.
- **Risks:** you eat full COGS + fulfillment on the "free" unit; only works if CM comfortably exceeds the give-away. Can pull forward demand and inflate units-per-order without new customers.

## Tiered / spend thresholds

- **Margin impact:** discount applies only above a spend bar (e.g. "$10 off $60"), so the *effective* discount % shrinks as basket grows — margin-efficient. Set the threshold above current AOV to genuinely lift baskets.
- **Best use:** raising AOV, encouraging add-on purchases, rewarding bigger orders without blanket markdown.
- **Risks:** threshold set too low just discounts orders that would have happened anyway; too high and few qualify. Model the discount at the *threshold* price, not the average.

## Bundles

- **Margin impact:** blended across items (see margin-math-guide §5). A modest bundle discount can raise AOV while keeping blended CM above floor — but a low-margin filler can drag the basket under.
- **Best use:** raising AOV, moving a slow SKU attached to a hero SKU, simplifying choice, increasing perceived value without a headline markdown.
- **Risks:** cannibalizes standalone sales of the hero item; always compute blended bundle CM after the bundle discount.

## Free-shipping threshold

- **Margin impact:** you eat the shipping cost (a real, often $4–8 discount) only on qualifying orders; framed as "free shipping over $X" it lifts AOV cheaply because shipping cost is usually less than the % discount needed to move the same basket.
- **Best use:** AOV lift, cart-abandonment reduction, channels where shipping is the top friction.
- **Risks:** under-set threshold = subsidizing tiny orders; un-modeled shipping cost silently sinks margin. Treat the subsidy as a variable cost.

## Flash sale / limited-time

- **Margin impact:** same per-unit hit as % or $ off, but compressed into a short window — concentrates lift to clear the break-even faster and limits total margin given away.
- **Best use:** urgency, launches, inventory pushes, live-shopping moments, defending against a competitor's promo without a permanent price change.
- **Risks:** if it recurs predictably it becomes a scheduled sale and trains waiting; over-discounting under time pressure; stockouts mid-flash hurt the listing.

## Coupon vs automatic discount

- **Margin impact:** identical depth, different behavior. **Automatic** discounts apply to everyone (max reach, max margin given away). **Coupon/code** discounts are opt-in — capture rate is lower, so realized margin loss is smaller, and they enable targeting (first-order, win-back, creator-specific).
- **Best use:** automatic for broad events and clearance; coupons for targeted acquisition, retention, influencer/affiliate attribution, and price discrimination.
- **Risks:** codes leak to coupon-aggregator sites (everyone gets the "secret" deal); uncapped stacking of code + automatic + voucher can breach the floor. Cap combined depth.

## Loyalty / first-order

- **Margin impact:** a controlled, one-time hit aimed at a specific customer state (new buyer or repeat member). The discount is justified by lifetime value, not the single order's break-even.
- **Best use:** acquisition (first-order code) and retention (member pricing/points); the right lens is CAC and LTV, not single-promo `d/(m−d)`.
- **Risks:** first-order abuse (new accounts to re-claim); margin given to customers who'd have bought anyway; loyalty discounts that quietly become permanent everyday pricing.

## TikTok Shop platform notes

- **Base commission:** a per-category platform commission is deducted on the **discounted** price of each order — net it into contribution before sizing any discount.
- **Vouchers (shop vs platform-funded):** shop vouchers you create are funded by **you** and hit your margin; platform/campaign vouchers may be subsidized by TikTok — confirm the funding split, because only the portion you fund reduces your contribution. Vouchers can **stack** with product discounts; cap total combined depth so a shopper can't drive the unit below your floor.
- **Affiliate / creator commission:** when a creator drives the sale, their commission (often 10–20%, set by you) is **on top of** the base platform commission and the discount. A "20% off + 15% creator commission" order can strip 35%+ before COGS — model all three together.
- **Flash/campaign events:** big platform events pressure sellers into deep cuts and may require minimum discounts to join; only opt in where the concentrated volume clears the higher break-even lift, and watch for forced voucher stacking.
- **Free/subsidized shipping:** shipping incentives are common and material; treat the subsidy as a variable cost in every margin calc, not a marketing freebie.
