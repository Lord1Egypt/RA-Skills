# Core Framework: The Four Laws of Behavior Change

## The Habit Loop

All habits follow the same four-stage neurological pattern:

**Cue → Craving → Response → Reward**

The cue triggers a craving, the craving motivates a response, and the response delivers a reward. The reward then becomes associated with the cue, closing the loop.

> **Case: The British Cycling Team** (Chapter 1): Before 2003, British cyclists had won just one gold medal in 100 years. Performance director Dave Brailsford implemented the "aggregation of marginal gains" — improving everything by 1%: seat adjustments, nutrition, sleep patterns, even painting the inside of the truck white to spot dust. By 2008, British cyclists dominated the Olympics. By 2012, they had won 70% of available gold medals.
> **Key takeaway**: Small improvements compound into remarkable results. But they take time — most people quit before crossing the "Plateau of Latent Potential."

## The Plateau of Latent Potential

> **Case: Ice Cube Analogy** (Chapter 1): If you raise the temperature of ice from 25°F to 31°F, nothing visible changes. At 32°F, the ice melts. The preceding 6 degrees were not wasted — they were necessary. Habits work the same way: you put in work for weeks or months with no visible result, then one day the breakthrough happens.
> **Key takeaway**: The most powerful outcomes of any compounding process are delayed. Patience is not just a virtue — it is a mechanism.

## The Four Laws of Behavior Change

| Law | Purpose | Habit Building | Habit Breaking |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1st Law | Make It Obvious | Implementation intention: "I will [BEHAVIOR] at [TIME] in [LOCATION]" | Make It Invisible: remove the cue |
| 2nd Law | Make It Attractive | Temptation bundling: pair action you need with one you want | Make It Unattractive: highlight the downside |
| 3rd Law | Make It Easy | Reduce friction: design environment for ease | Make It Difficult: increase friction |
| 4th Law | Make It Satisfying | Immediate reward: give yourself a small celebration | Make It Unsatisfying: create an accountability partner |

## Identity-Based Habits

The most powerful shift: from outcome-based goals ("I want to run a marathon") to identity-based goals ("I am a runner"). Every habit is a vote for the person you want to become.

> **Case: The "I'm not a smoker" shift** (Chapter 3): When offered a cigarette, a person trying to quit says "No thanks, I'm trying to quit." An identity-based quitter says "No thanks, I'm not a smoker." The first suggests they still want to smoke. The second declares a new identity.
> **Key takeaway**: Behavior change starts with belief change. The habit becomes part of who you are, not just what you do.
