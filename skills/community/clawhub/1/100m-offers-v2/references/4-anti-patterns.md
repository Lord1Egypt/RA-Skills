## The Anti-Patterns of Offer Creation

### Anti-Pattern 1: Commodity Thinking ("I can't charge more because...")

**The mistake:** Believing your price is determined by market rates, competitors, or what's "fair." Setting price as cost + margin.

**$100M Offers reality:** Price is determined by VALUE. If you can create $10,000 of value, you can charge $2,000. The cost of fulfillment is irrelevant to pricing. The only question is: "How much is the outcome worth to the customer?"

**Symptoms:** "My competitors charge $50 so I charge $50." "I can't raise prices without adding more features." "I'd feel guilty charging that much."

**The fix:** Redesign the offer around the Value Equation. Don't raise your prices on the same old offer—create a new offer that justifies premium pricing. Put the focus on dream outcome, guarantee, and bonuses—not features.

> **Case: From $1,036 to $100M** (Chapter 1): In December 2016, Hormozi had $1,036. His gym launch business was dead. The old model (flying to gyms, running ads for them, getting paid by keeping memberships) was broken. The new model? License the system for $6K-$10K per gym—once. No flying. No ad spend risk. Same knowledge, different offer. From $1,036 to $6.8M in 12 months. **Key takeaway:** You don't need a new business. You need a new OFFER. The same expertise delivered differently can 100x your income.

### Anti-Pattern 2: Spreading Value Thin (The Tier Trap)

**The mistake:** Offering multiple price tiers (Gold/Silver/Bronze) that split your value across packages, each one less compelling than the last. The Bronze tier is weak, the Gold tier is nice but still not irresistible.

**$100M Offers reality:** The Hormozi method is to STACK all the value into ONE offer. No tiers. One Grand Slam Offer. If you must have tiers, make the middle tier the primary and the low tier a loss leader that's designed to upgrade.

**Symptoms:** "My basic package is cheap but nobody buys it." "People always pick the middle option." "My premium package never sells."

**The fix:** Kill the low tier. Move all the high-value components into your main offer. Price it as a premium offer. Add enhancements (guarantee + bonuses) that make it irresistible. One offer. One price. Done.

### Anti-Pattern 3: Fear of the Guarantee

**The mistake:** Avoiding strong guarantees because you're afraid customers will exploit them. "If I guarantee results, people will cheat the system."

**$100M Offers reality:** Most people NEVER claim guarantees. The guarantee is a sales tool, not a cost center. Hormozi's strongest guarantees (work with you until you get results) had actual claim rates below 5%. Every dollar of guarantee-stated risk removed 10+ dollars of buying hesitation.

**Symptoms:** Weak "satisfaction guarantee." "30-day money-back with conditions." No guarantee at all.

**The fix:** Make your guarantee as strong as you can honestly deliver. "I'll work with you until you get results" is always better than "money back within 30 days." The guarantee signals confidence, and confidence sells.

### Anti-Pattern 4: Selling Features Instead of Outcomes

**The mistake:** Listing what your product DOES instead of what the customer GETS.

**$100M Offers reality:** Nobody buys a drill because they want a drill. They buy a hole in the wall. Nobody buys personal training because they want exercise. They want to lose 20 pounds and feel confident at the beach. Sell the OUTCOME. The features are just the mechanism.

**Symptoms:** "We include 12 sessions, a meal plan, and weekly check-ins." Instead of: "You'll lose 20 pounds in 90 days and keep it off."

**The fix:** For every feature, ask: "What OUTCOME does this produce?" That's your sales copy. The feature justifies the price. The outcome generates the desire.

### Anti-Pattern 5: The "Fair Price" Fallacy

**The mistake:** Pricing based on what you think is "fair" rather than what the market will bear.

**$100M Offers reality:** "Fair" is a social construct, not a market mechanism. The entrepreneur who prices at what they think is fair leaves enormous value on the table—and often can't afford to advertise or grow. The "unfair" price that makes you uncomfortable is usually the correct one.

**Symptoms:** Feeling guilty about charging. Wanting to "help people afford" your service. Discounting before being asked.

**The fix:** Price based on value delivered. If your customer gets $10K of value, charge $2-3K. That's still a 3-5x value multiple for them. You're not being unfair—you're being fairly compensated for value you created.

### Anti-Pattern 6: The "Everyone's a Customer" Trap

**The mistake:** Trying to appeal to everyone, resulting in an offer that appeals to no one.

**$100M Offers reality:** The narrower the target market, the easier it is to create a Grand Slam Offer. A specific offer for "gym owners in Texas who are struggling with membership retention" will outsell a generic "business consulting" offer every time.

**Symptoms:** "My product can help anyone." "I don't want to limit my market." "What about [edge case]?"

**The fix:** Define your ideal customer as narrowly as possible. Then create an offer that feels like it was custom-made for them. You can expand later. Start narrow.

> **Case: The gym owner who paid $6K for a system he could have gotten for free** (Chapter 11): A desperate gym owner called Hormozi wanting help launching. Hormozi had zero desire to fly out — he was broke and exhausted. He said "$6K" as the biggest number he could think of to scare the guy off. The owner said "Done." Next call: $8K. "Done." Next: $10K. "Done." In one day: $60K collected for something that cost zero to fulfill. The gym owner could have researched the same information for free on Google. But the packaging — a "system" with a proven track record and a guarantee — made it worth $6K. **Key takeaway:** Don't undervalue your packaging. The same information packaged as a 'system' with a track record is worth infinitely more than raw information.
