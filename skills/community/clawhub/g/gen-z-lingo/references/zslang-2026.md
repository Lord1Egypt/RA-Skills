## Parent guidance distilled

Generally harmless: bet, slay, fire, lit, drip, fam, mood, vibe.

Monitor/contextual: ghost (communication boundaries), stan (fandom boundaries), mewing (body image), touch grass (digital wellness but can insult).

Inappropriate or concerning: simp, gyatt/gyat, catch these hands, IDGAF, FAFO.

Parent strategy:
- Approach with curiosity rather than judgment.
- Ask for context before reacting.
- Set boundaries for profanity, objectification, bullying, threats, and exclusion.
- Watch for excessive use of harmful language, violent terms, bullying, secrecy, or sudden behavior shifts.

## Teacher/classroom guidance distilled

Classroom-appropriate if used sparingly: bet, slay, fire, lit, mood, vibe, fam, GOAT.

Use caution: sus, mid, salty, extra, no chill. These can be casual but may discourage or shame students if aimed at them or their work.

Inappropriate for school: simp, gyatt/gyat, catch these hands, IDGAF, FAFO.

Teacher strategy:
- Use slang occasionally for rapport, not as a persona.
- Turn slang into language-evolution/context lessons.
- Address inappropriate language privately when possible.
- Escalate/document when there is a credible threat, immediate safety concern, targeted harassment, repeated behavior, or policy requirement.
- Provide alternative expressions and enforce policy consistently.

## Workplace guidance distilled

Professional/casual-internal appropriate if used sparingly: bet, slay, fire, lit, GOAT, vibe, flex, glow up.

Casual settings only: sus, mid, salty, extra, no chill, mood, fam, stan.

Never appropriate in workplace: simp, gyatt/gyat, catch these hands, IDGAF, FAFO.

Workplace strategy:
- Slang can build rapport in internal chat, but formal writing should stay professional.
- Avoid slang in client communication, performance reviews, HR-sensitive contexts, regulated industries, legal/healthcare settings, or when audience/culture is unknown.
- Address inappropriate language privately, explain why, give alternatives, and follow policy.

## Writing patterns that sound natural

- “Bet, I’ll handle it.”
- “No cap, that was impressive.”
- “This design is giving clean/futuristic/chaos.”
- “That launch was a W.”
- “The new UI is fire.”
- “This take is kind of mid.”
- “Say less — I’m on it.”
- “ICL, that plan needs work.”

Use grammar that fits the term. “It’s giving [vibe/noun/adjective phrase]” works. “It’s giving very good” is clunky. “That proposal was fire” works casually; “The Q3 compliance memo is bussin frfr” is parody-level cringe unless requested.
