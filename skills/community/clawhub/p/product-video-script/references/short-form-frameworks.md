# Short-Form Script Frameworks

Pick the framework from the product type and the audience's awareness stage, not from habit. Each framework below lists when it wins, the beat structure, and the trap to avoid.

## Awareness stages (decide this first)

- **Problem-unaware** — doesn't know they have the problem. The hook must dramatize the problem before any product appears. Lean toward problem-agitate-solve or before-after-bridge.
- **Problem-aware** — feels the pain, doesn't know solutions. Hook with the pain, then position the product as the fix.
- **Solution-aware** — knows solutions exist, comparing options. Lead with differentiation and proof; testimonial-story works well.
- **Product-aware** — knows your product, needs a nudge. Hook with a new angle, an offer, or social proof; keep it short.

The colder the audience, the more the script must educate before it sells. Warm audiences tolerate a faster, harder CTA.

## 1. Hook–Demo–Offer

**Use when:** visually demonstrable impulse products (gadgets, beauty, kitchen, home) for warm-to-mid audiences. The default for TikTok Shop.
**Beats:** Hook (0–2s tension) → Demo (the product doing its one impressive thing) → Offer (single CTA + light, honest urgency).
**Trap:** demoing five features. Show the single most surprising capability, then sell.

## 2. Problem–Agitate–Solve (PAS)

**Use when:** the product fixes a specific, nameable frustration; problem-aware or problem-unaware audiences.
**Beats:** Problem (name it in the hook) → Agitate (show its real cost — time, money, embarrassment) → Solve (product resolves it on screen).
**Trap:** over-agitating into negativity. Two beats of agitation max, then relief.

## 3. Before–After–Bridge (BAB)

**Use when:** transformations you can show — skincare, organization, cleaning, fitness gear.
**Beats:** Before (relatable bad state) → After (desirable result up front or as payoff) → Bridge (the product is how you get from one to the other).
**Trap:** an unbelievable after. Keep the result honest; exaggerated transformations kill trust and invite policy flags.

## 4. Unboxing–Reaction

**Use when:** giftable, aesthetic, or hyped products where packaging and first impression carry emotion.
**Beats:** Anticipation (what's inside / why it matters) → Reveal (genuine reaction beat) → Detail (one or two standout features) → CTA.
**Trap:** a flat, scripted-sounding reaction. Authenticity is the entire value of this format.

## 5. Testimonial–Story

**Use when:** considered or higher-ticket purchases; solution-aware audiences who need trust before they buy.
**Beats:** Relatable situation → Skepticism or struggle → Turning point with the product → Specific result → CTA inviting research.
**Trap:** vague praise. One concrete, specific result ("cut my morning routine to 6 minutes") beats ten adjectives.

## Choosing fast

| Product signal | Start with |
|---|---|
| Impulse, visually striking | Hook–Demo–Offer |
| Fixes a daily frustration | Problem–Agitate–Solve |
| Visible transformation | Before–After–Bridge |
| Giftable / hyped / aesthetic | Unboxing–Reaction |
| High-ticket / considered | Testimonial–Story |

## Retention devices (layer onto any framework)

- **Open loop:** promise a payoff at the end ("the third one surprised me") and deliver it.
- **Pattern interrupt:** an unexpected visual or sound at ~3s and ~10s to reset attention.
- **Curiosity gap:** state the what, withhold the how until the demo.
- **Specificity:** real numbers and named scenarios out-convert generic adjectives.
