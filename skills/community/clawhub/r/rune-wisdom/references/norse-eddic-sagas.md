## 1. Baldrs draumar / Сны Бальдра (Baldr's Dreams)

### Full Text (Russian translation by А. Корсун)

1 Тотчас собрались все асы на тинг, и асиньи все сошлись на совет: о том совещались сильные боги, отчего сны у Бальдра такие зловещие.

2 Один поднялся, древний Гаут, седло возложил на спину Слейпнира; оттуда он вниз в Нифльхель поехал; встретил он пса, из Хель прибежавшего.

3 У пса была грудь кровью покрыта, на отца колдовства долго он лаял; дальше помчался — гудела земля — Один к высокому Хель жилищу.

4 На восток от ворот выехал Один, где, как он ведал, вёльвы могила; заклинанье он начал и вещую поднял, ответила вёльва мертвою речью:

5 "Что там за воин, неведомый мне, что в путь повелел мне нелегкий отправиться? Снег заносил меня, дождь заливал и роса покрывала, давно я мертва".

6 [Один сказал:] "Имя мне Вегтам, я Вальтама сын; про Хель мне поведай, про мир я поведаю; скамьи для кого кольчугами устланы, золотом пол усыпан красиво?"

7 [Вёльва сказала:] "Мед здесь стоит, он сварен для Бальдра, светлый напиток, накрыт он щитом; отчаяньем сыны асов охвачены. Больше ни слова ты не услышишь".

8 [Один сказал:] "Вёльва, ответь! Я спрашивать буду, чтоб все мне открылось: еще хочу знать, кому доведется стать Бальдра убийцей, кто сына Одина смерти предаст".

9 [Вёльва сказала:] "Хёд ввергнет сюда дерево славы; ему доведется стать Бальдра убийцей, он сына Одина смерти предаст. Больше ни слова ты не услышишь".

10 [Один сказал:] "Вёльва, ответь! Я спрашивать буду, чтоб все мне открылось: еще хочу знать, кто за убийство Хёду отплатит, кем на костер он будет отправлен".

11 [Вёльва сказала:] "Ринд в западном доме Вали родит, и Одина сын начнет поединок, рук не омоет, волос не причешет, пока не убьет Бальдра убийцу. Больше ни слова ты не услышишь".

12 [Один сказал:] "Вёльва, ответь! Я спрашивать буду, чтоб все мне открылось: еще знать хочу, кто эти девы, что будут рыдать, края покрывал в небо бросая".

13 [Вёльва сказала:] "Нет, ты не Вегтам, как я считала, ты, верно, Один, ты древний Гаут!" [Один сказал:] "Ты же не вёльва, провидица вещая, ты, верно, мать трех великанов!"

14 [Вёльва сказала:] "Домой поезжай! Гордись своей славой! Отныне сюда никто не придет, пока свои узы Локи не сбросит и не настанет гибель богов!"

### Scholarly Notes

- **Form:** Closest to the question-and-answer mythological poems, but in content closest to Völuspá. Essentially a prophecy of a völva raised from the dead by Odin.
- **Строфа 2:** Гаут — one of Odin's names. Слейпнир — "the gliding one," Odin's eight-legged horse. Нифльхель — Hel, the realm of death.
- **Строфа 3:** The dog — Garmr, guarding the entrance to Hel. "Отец колдовства" (Father of sorcery) — Odin.
- **Строфа 6:** Вегтам — "accustomed to the road." Вальтам — "accustomed to battle."
- **Строфа 7:** "Накрыт он щитом" — it was customary to cover a brewed drink to prevent it from being "evil-eyed" (сглаз). This is a direct reference to the Norse practice of shielding magical substances from malevolent influence.
- **Строфа 9:** Хёд — the blind god who kills Baldr. "Дерево славы" (tree of glory) — a kenning for Baldr.
- **Строфа 11:** Ринд bore Vali from Odin; Vali avenged Baldr at one day old. Both Rind and Vali appear ONLY in connection with Baldr's vengeance.
- **Строфа 12:** The riddle about "maids who will weep, throwing the edges of their veils into the sky" — Odin's question is in the form of a riddle, and by this riddle the völva recognizes him. The solution is apparently "waves" — the daughters of Ægir mourning Baldr. This is the same recognition-through-esoteric-knowledge pattern found in Vafþrúðnismál.
- **Строфа 14:** Ragnarök begins with Loki breaking free of his bonds.

### Relevance to Rune Practice

**Primary source for the necromantic divination model.** Odin's journey to consult a dead völva is the mythological prototype for all Norse divinatory practice — the practitioner descends to the realm of the dead to extract hidden knowledge from a seeress. Key elements:

1. **The völva controls the flow of revelation.** Her repeated phrase "Больше ни слова ты не услышишь" (No more words will you hear) after each answer establishes that the seeress, not the questioner, determines when the prophecy ends. This directly parallels the Perthro Principle in the runic divination protocol — "this cannot be known" — and the ethical framework's insistence on honoring the boundaries of what the reading reveals.

2. **Disguise and recognition.** Odin conceals his identity (as Вегтам, son of Вальтам), but the völva sees through the disguise when he asks a question only Odin could ask. This pattern — the querent's identity shaping the answer — is foundational: the same runes drawn for different querents may yield different meanings because the question itself is the Urðr seed.

3. **The journey structure: question → descent → encounter → revelation → boundary.** This maps directly onto the divination protocol's seven-phase reading process: Preparation → Draw → Initial Scan → Position-by-Position Interpretation → Numerological Analysis → Narrative Synthesis → Presentation.

4. **The shielding of the drink** (stanza 7) — covering the mead with a shield to prevent сглаз (evil eye) — is a practical magical precaution with direct application: runic talismans and readings should be shielded from malicious interference.

### Scholarly Notes

- **Rig = Heimdallr.** The prose introduction identifies Rig with Heimdallr. In Völuspá, Heimdallr is called "father of mankind," and Rig is also "father of mankind" per this poem. Some scholars argued Rig is Odin, but the Heimdallr identification is supported by the broader mythological framework. **The poem's rune-teaching scene (stanza 36) thus attributes the transmission of runic knowledge to humans specifically to Heimdallr**, not Odin — a second mythological line of rune transmission complementing Odin's self-sacrifice on Yggdrasil.

- **Строфа 2:** "Прабабка" (edda) — this word occurs in the meaning "great-grandmother" ONLY here. It has been speculated that this is the origin of the word "Edda" as the title of the collection.

- **Строфа 7:** "Водой окропили" — Sprinkling with water at naming is a pagan rite. Трэль — "slave."

- **Строфа 10:** Тир — "slave-woman."

- **Строфы 12-13:** The names of Thrall's and Tir's sons mean roughly "herdsman," "lout," "stump," "lazybones," "foul," "stooped," etc. The daughters' names mean "pot-bellied," "thick-legged," "babbler," "ragamuffin," etc.

- **Строфа 21:** Карл — "man," "old man," also "free man without hereditary land," i.e., "farmhand."

- **Строфа 34:** Ярл — roughly equivalent to a duke in Scandinavian kingdoms. Originally the highest representative of hereditary nobility.

- **Строфа 41:** Кон (Konr) means "offspring," and "Konr ungr" (Kon the young) sounds like "konungr" (king). The wordplay is deliberate and central to the poem's political purpose.

- **Строфа 48:** Дан and Данп — legendary ancestors of Danish kings. "Дан" is derived from "Denmark," and "Данп" is a Gothic name for the Dnieper (Днепр). The poem thus connects the Danish royal line to Gothic traditions of Eastern Europe.

### Relevance to Rune Practice

**THE primary mythological source for Heimdallr as the transmitter of runes to humanity.** This is critical because:

1. **Heimdallr teaches runes to Jarl (stanza 36).** "Тут из лесов Риг появился, Риг появился, стал рунам учить" — Heimdallr/Rig appears from the forests and begins to teach runes. This is the ONLY Eddic passage where a god explicitly teaches runes to a human. Odin wins runes through self-sacrifice; Heimdallr transmits them. This dual-transmission model — Odin acquires, Heimdallr distributes — has direct implications for the second aett (Heimdall's aett) as the "bridge" between primal forces (first aett) and human social order (third aett).

2. **Kon's rune mastery surpasses his father's (stanzas 43-45).** "Кон юный ведал волшебные руны, целебные руны, могучие руны" — the three categories of runes (magical, healing, mighty) echo the Sigrdrífumál rune-type prescriptions. Kon can "help women in childbirth, blunt swords, calm the sea" — these are exactly the practical runic abilities listed in Sigrdrífumál. Kon then disputes rune-knowledge with his father Rig and wins, acquiring "the right to be called Rig and to wield mighty runes." This establishes the principle that runic knowledge can be advanced beyond what was taught — the student can surpass the master.

3. **The social hierarchy and rune access.** Runes are taught ONLY to Jarl (the noble class), not to Thrall or Karl. This reflects the historical reality that runic literacy was primarily an elite skill. However, Kon's ultimate acquisition of rune-mastery through intellectual competition (not birth) suggests that true rune-wisdom transcends social station — a principle consistent with Odin's own pursuit of wisdom across all realms.

4. **The three-night, nine-month pattern.** Rig stays three nights, and nine months later a child is born — the number three (Odin's number) and nine (the sacred number) structuring the creation of each social class. This mirrors the numerological framework in runic practice.

5. **The raven's challenge (stanzas 47-48).** The raven — Odin's bird — interrupts Kon's rune-practice with bird-charming and redirects him toward kingship and warfare. This is the voice of Odin asserting that rune-knowledge must serve worldly action, not remain in contemplative isolation. For rune practitioners, this affirms that runic wisdom must be applied, not merely studied.

## 3. Hyndluljóð / Песнь о Хюндле (Song of Hyndla)

### Full Text (Russian translation by А. Корсун)

[Фрейя сказала:] 1 Проснись, дева дев! Пробудись, подруга, Хюндла, сестра, в пещере живущая! Ночь непроглядна, настало нам время в Вальгаллу ехать, в чертоги священные!

2 Ратей Отца попросим о милости! Золото воинам он раздает; шлем дал он Хермоду, дал и кольчугу, Сигмунду меч разящий вручил он.

3 Победу одним, другим же богатство, иным красноречье и разум дает он; ветер пловцам, песнопения скальдам и мужество в битвах воинам многим.

4 Жертвы для Тора я приготовлю, чтоб милость свою он тебе даровал, хоть он не жалует ётунов жен.

5 Волка скорее из стойла выведи, пусть он поскачет с вепрем моим!

[Хюндля сказала:] 6 Плох твой кабан для дороги богов; и коня моего неохота мне мучить. Ты, хитрая Фрейя, меня испытуешь: взоры твои о помощи просят, ведь милый твой здесь, на дороге мертвых, юноша Оттар, Иннстейна сын.

7 [Фрейя сказала:] Ошиблась ты, Хюндля, грезишь во сне ты, милого нет на дороге мертвых, то вепрь сияет щетиной из золота, вепрь Хильдисвини, его мне когда-то карлики сделали Дайн и Набби.

### Scholarly Notes

- **Stanzas 29-44** constitute an independent composition known as the **"Short Völuspá" (Völuspá in skamma)**, an apparent imitation of Völuspá usually dated to the 12th century. Its content is genealogical information about gods and giants.

- **Строфа 7:** Хильдисвини — "battle-boar." Made by dwarves Дайн (Dáinn) and Набби (Nabbi). Dáinn is also the name of the dwarf who brought runes to the light elves (Ljósálfheimr) per the Hávamál transmission chain.

- **Строфа 33:** **The genealogy of magical practitioners.** Видольв (Vidólfr) is the progenitor of all völur; Вильмейд (Vilméðr) is the progenitor of all prophets (провидцы); Черная Глава (Svarta höfuð — "Black Head") is the progenitor of all sorcerers (чародеи); and Имир (Ymir) is the root of all giants. **This is the ONLY Eddic passage that provides an origin-taxonomy for different types of magical practitioners**, establishing distinct lineages for völur, prophets, sorcerers, and giants. For rune practice, this affirms that different magical traditions have different roots — the völva's art, the prophet's sight, and the sorcerer's craft are not interchangeable, even though they may overlap.

- **Строфы 35-37:** The nine mothers of Heimdallr. The names (Gjálp, Grein, Eistla, Eyrgjafa, Angeyja, Atl, Ulfrún, Imd, Jarnsaxa) are names of waves — Heimdallr's mothers were the daughters of the sea giant Ægir and his wife Rán. This connects Heimdallr (the rune-teacher of Rígsþula) to the primordial forces of the ocean, paralleling how Odin's runic wisdom comes from the world-tree.

- **Строфа 38:** "Взял силу земли он, студеного моря и силу кабаньей жертвенной крови" — Heimdallr was protected from evil forces through earth, salt, and boar's sacrificial blood, substances still used in folk magic. The boar's blood connects to Freyja's golden boar Hildisvíni and to the sacrificial practices surrounding Freyr.

- **Строфа 40:** Ангрбода — "she who offers grief." Слейпнир — Odin's eight-legged horse, born from Loki's union with the stallion Svadilfari while Loki was in mare form. "Брат Бюлейста" (Býleistr's brother) — a kenning for Loki.

- **Строфа 41:** Лофт — another name for Loki. The half-burned woman's heart that Loki ate to beget monsters is one of the most vivid images of magical contamination in the Edda — consuming death to produce further death.

- **Строфа 44:** Possibly refers to the Christian God, suggesting Christian interpolation. "После битвы Одина с Волком" — after Ragnarök.

- **Строфы 45-50:** The "memory beer" (minnisö) that Freyja requests for Ottar (in boar form) so that he will remember his genealogy and win his inheritance dispute. Hyndla agrees to give the beer but poisons it. Freyja neutralizes the curse. **The memory beer is a magical substance that enhances recall of ancestral knowledge** — directly parallel to the Mead of Poetry (Óðrœrir) and to the runic practice of using galdr and ritual to access deep memory during readings.

### Relevance to Rune Practice

1. **The genealogy of magical practitioners (stanza 33)** provides the mythological basis for distinguishing between different types of magical work in Norse tradition — völur, prophets, sorcerers, and giant-kin each have their own lineage. In rune practice, this maps onto the distinction between divination (völva-line), prophecy (prophet-line), active magic/crafting (sorcerer-line), and raw cosmological force (giant-line). A practitioner may draw from multiple lineages, but understanding which lineage they are accessing in any given working affects the method and the outcome.

2. **Heimdallr's nine wave-mothers (stanzas 35-37)** connect the rune-teacher to the ocean's chaotic power — the same sea from which Rán collects the drowned and Ægir brews the ale of the gods. This is the complement to Odin's tree-ascent: Odin goes UP for runes, Heimdallr comes FROM the depths. The second aett (Hagalaz through Sowilo), attributed to Heimdallr, accordingly deals with forces that emerge from chaos into order — hail becoming water, need becoming resilience, ice becoming stillness, etc.

3. **The memory beer** is the practical magical parallel to the Mead of Poetry. Where Óðrœrir grants poetic inspiration (the ability to compose and speak beautifully), the minnisö grants genealogical memory (the ability to recall ancestral knowledge). For rune readings, this distinction matters: the reading itself is a form of minnisö — helping the querent access and remember what the runes are revealing about their situation and lineage.

4. **Freyja's magical conflict with Hyndla** (fire-circling, poisoned beer, counter-spells) is a model for the magical adversarial dynamic: the practitioner may need to counteract hostile magical forces that try to corrupt the reading or the knowledge being transmitted.

## 4. Gróttasöngr / Песнь о Гротти (Song of Grotti)

### Prose Introduction (Summary)

Скьёльд был сын Одина. Его сын Фридлейв, а его сын Фроди. Фроди правил в то время, когда Август Цезарь установил мир во всем мире (начало нашей эры). "Мир Фроди" — никто не причинял вреда другому. Фроди купил двух рабынь, Фенью и Менью, больших и сильных. В Дании были жернова Гротти, которые намалывали всё, что пожелаешь. Их дал Фроди Хенгикьёфт (одно из имён Одина — "с отвисшей челюстью"). Фроди заставил рабынь молоть без отдыха, пока они не спели песнь...

### Full Text (Russian translation by А. Корсун)

1 Вот появились в палатах конунга вещие девы Фенья и Менья; Фроди они, Фридлейва сыну, сильные девы, отданы в рабство.

2 К мельнице их подвели обеих, жернов вращать велели тяжелый; не дал им Фроди ни на миг передышки, пока они песню ему не запели.

3 Начали песню, прервали молчанье: "Поставим-ка жернов, камни поднимем!" Дальше молоть повелел он девам.

4 Пели, швыряя вертящийся камень, спали в тот час челядинцы Фроди; молвила Менья, молоть продолжая:

5 "Намелем для Фроди богатства немало, сокровищ намелем на жернове счастья; в довольстве сидеть ему, спать на пуху, просыпаться счастливым; славно мы мелем!

6 Никто здесь не должен зло замышлять, вред учинять иль убийство готовить; рубить не пристало острым мечом даже брата убийцу, в узах лежащего!"

7 Он им сказал: "Срок вам для сна — пока куковать не кончит кукушка иль, замолчав, опять не начнет!"

8 "Фроди, ты не был достаточно мудр, приязненный к людям, рабынь покупая: ты выбрал по силе, судил по обличью, да не проведал, кто они родом.

9 Хрунгнир с отцом храбрейшими были, но Тьяци мог с ними в силе тягаться; Иди и Аурнир — родичи наши, ётунов братья, мы их порожденье.

10 Гротти не вышла б из серого камня, камень бы твердый земли не покинул, так не мололи бы ётунов девы, когда б волшебства не было в мельнице.

11 Мы девять зим, подруги могучие, в царстве подземном росли и трудились; нелегкое дело девам досталось — утесы и скалы мы с места сдвигали.

12 Камни вздымали на стену турсов так, что вокруг содрогалась земля, так мы швырнули вертящийся камень, что впору мужам лить было схватить его.

13 За этим вослед мы, вещие девы, в Свитьоде вместе вступали в сраженья; рубили кольчуги, щиты рассекали, путь через войско себе пробивая.

14 Конунга свергли, сражаясь за Готторма, смелому князю помочь мы смогли; не было мира до гибели Кнуи.

15 Так, дни за днями, мы доблестно бились, что слава пошла о сраженьях наших; мы копьями там кровь проливали, мечи обагряли вражеской кровью.

16 Вот мы пришли к палатам конунга, мы обездолены, отданы в рабство; холод нас мучит, и ноги ест грязь, мы жернов вращаем, нам плохо у Фроди!

17 Рукам дать покой бы, жернов не двигать, — смолола я больше, чем было мне сказано! Нельзя дать покой рукам, пока вдоволь помола для Фроди мы не намелем!

18 В руки бы дали крепкие древки, мечи обагренные! Фроди, проснись! Фроди, проснись, если хочешь ты слышать старые песни и древние саги!

19 От палат на восток вижу я пламя — то весть о войне, знак это вещий; войско сюда скоро приблизится, князя палаты пламя охватит.

20 Ты потеряешь Хлейдра престол, червонные кольца и камень волшебный. Девы, беритесь за рычаги! Нам здесь не согреться кровью сраженных.

21 Сильно молоть я постаралась, видя грядущую гибель для многих; прочь с основанья, в ободьях железных, сброшена мельница, — мелем мы снова!

22 Мелем мы снова: сын Ирсы местью Фроди ответит за гибель Хальвдана; братом ее назван он будет и сыном ее; мы это знаем.

23 Девы мололи, меряясь силами, юные были как ётуны в гневе; балки тряслись, основа слетела, надвое жернов тяжелый расколот.

24 Слово сказала тогда исполинша: "Мы Фроди смололи славный помол; вдосталь над жерновом девы стояли".

### Scholarly Notes

- Based on a tale from the history of the Danish royal Skjöldung dynasty. The magic mill that grinds whatever you wish is also known from Scandinavian folktales and the Finnish Kalevala (the Sampo). The prototype may have been a working song performed by two women at a grinding-stone.

- **Строфа 9:** The giantesses name their lineage: Hrungnir, Thjazi, Iði, and Aurnir — all giants.

- **Строфы 13-15:** The giantesses reveal they were formerly valkyries who participated in battles, helping King Gottorm against King Knúi. This is a key text for the valkyrie-giantess overlap: the same beings can be valkyries in war and giantesses in peacetime, depending on context.

- **Строфа 22:** Prophecy that Hrólf Kraki (the famous Danish hero, son of Yrsa) will avenge Halfdan's death upon Frodi. The genealogical complexity — Frodi is both brother and son of Yrsa — mirrors the mythological pattern of fated, inescapable violence within families.

- **The giver of the mill:** Хенгикьёфт (Hengikjóft) — one of Odin's names, literally "with hanging jaw." Odin gives the magic mill to Frodi, establishing that Odin is the source of magical technology in the Norse cosmos — just as he is the source of runes, poetry, and mead.

### Relevance to Rune Practice

1. **Work-song as magical act.** The giantesses' grinding-song doesn't accompany the work — it IS the work. As they change the content of their song from "wealth and peace" (stanzas 5-6) to "war and destruction" (stanzas 19-22), the mill's output changes accordingly. This is a direct parallel to galdr: the sound and words spoken during magical work determine the outcome. The galdr tradition of chanting rune-names while carving follows the same principle.

2. **Valkyrie-giantess duality.** Fenja and Menja are simultaneously giantesses (jötnar by birth) and valkyries (by function in war). This is the same dual nature found in the Norns (who are sometimes counted among the gods, sometimes among the giants) and in the völva tradition (human practitioners who channel non-human forces). For rune practice, this affirms that the practitioner does not need to be "pure" in lineage — what matters is the function they serve in the moment of the working.

3. **The mill as paradigm for magical objects.** Grotti grinds what the operator intends — but the operator's INTENT, not the object's nature, determines the outcome. Frodi's greed (refusing rest, working the giantesses beyond all limit) turns a mill of plenty into a mill of destruction. The same principle applies to rune magic: the runes are neutral forces; the practitioner's intent and ethics determine whether they produce benefit or harm.

4. **Odin as source of magical technology.** The mill was given by Hengikjóft (Odin), just as runes were won by Odin on Yggdrasil. Odin distributes magical tools, but he does not control their use — that depends on the wisdom and restraint of the recipient.

## 5. Darraðarljóð / Песнь валькирий (Song of the Valkyries)

### Full Text (Russian translation by А. Корсун)

1 Соткана ткань большая, как туча, чтоб возвестить воинам гибель. Окропим ее кровью, накрепко ткань стальную от копий кровавым утком битвы свирепой ткать мы должны.

2 Сделаем ткань из кишок человечьих; вместо грузил на станке — черепа, а перекладины — копья в крови, гребень — железный, стрелы — колки; будем мечами ткань подбивать!

3 Хьёртримуль, Хильд, Саннгрид и Свипуль, мечи обнажив, начали ткать; сломятся копья, треснут щиты, если псы шлема вцепятся в них.

4 Мы ткем, мы ткем стяг боевой; был он в руках у конунга юного: выйдем вперед, ринемся в бой, где наши друзья удары наносят!

5 Мы ткем, мы ткем стяг боевой; конунгу вслед пора нам скакать! Гендуль и Гунн за ним помчались, кровь на щитах увидят они.

6 Мы ткем, мы ткем стяг боевой; рвутся вперед смелые воины. Конунга жизнь мы защитим — нам выбирать, кто в сече погибнет.

7 Будут землей люди владеть, что жили досель на дальних мысах; Бриану конунгу смерть суждена; Сигурда ярла копья пронзят.

8 Ирам готов горький удел, память о нем вечною будет; соткана ткань, поле боя в крови; о мертвых по свету молва прошумит.

9 Страшно теперь оглянуться: смотри! По небу мчатся багровые тучи; воинов кровь окрасила воздух — только валькириям это воспеть!

10 Спели мы славно о конунге юном; слава поющим! Слышавший нас песню запомнит, людям расскажет о том, что слышал от жен копьеносных!

11 Мечи обнажив, на диких конях, не знающих седел, прочь мы умчимся.

### Scholarly Notes

- Preserved in **Njáls saga** (Сага о Ньяле). The historical basis is the **Battle of Clontarf** (Good Friday, 1014, near Dublin), where Irish king Brian Bóruma defeated Scandinavian Vikings but died in the battle. The jarl Sigurd of Orkney also fell.

- **The weaving = battle magic paradigm.** Twelve valkyries arrive at a house with a loom and weave cloth from human intestines, using weapons as loom-parts. Their weaving magically influences the battle's outcome. The cloth they weave IS the battle-standard (боевой стяг). When they finish, they tear the cloth apart and ride off in different directions — the tearing symbolizes the shattering of armies.

- **Valkyrie names:** Хьёртримуль (Hjörþrimul), Хильд (Hild), Саннгрид (Sanngríð), Свипуль (Svipul), Гендуль (Göndul), Гунн (Gunn). Hild means "battle," Gunn means "war," the others carry meanings of "sword-crusher," "very violent," "changeable."

- **The raven banner:** In the Orkneyinga saga, Sigurd's mother wove him a battle-standard with a raven. The banner brought victory to whoever carried it — but also death. At Clontarf, two standard-bearers were killed before Sigurd took it himself and died.

- **Irish influence:** The poem is generally considered to show Irish influence and was likely composed in the early 11th century in the Orkney Islands, a Norse-Gaelic cultural zone.

- **The witness Dörruð:** In Njáls saga, a man named Dörruð in Scotland saw the twelve valkyries ride to the house and observed their weaving and singing. This is the Norse equivalent of the "second sight" — a mortal witnessing the supernatural forces shaping events.

### Relevance to Rune Practice

1. **Weaving as the master metaphor for fate-shaping.** The valkyries at the loom are performing the same function as the Norns at their well: weaving the web of wyrd. The Norns weave destiny for all beings; the valkyries weave the fate of a specific battle. The loom-parts made of weapons (sword-shuttles, arrow-pins, skull-weights) show that the tools of destruction are also the instruments of fate — the battle IS the weaving. For rune practice, this means that the act of casting runes is itself a form of weaving: each rune drawn is a thread placed in the querent's pattern, and the practitioner is participating in the Norns' work.

2. **The tearing of the cloth.** When the valkyries finish weaving, they tear the cloth apart and ride off. The completed pattern must be destroyed for its power to be released. This has a direct parallel in rune magic: the runic inscription (a bindrune, a runescript, a talisman) gains its full power not when it is complete, but when it is activated — which may involve destruction, concealment, or ritual "release." The divination reading, too, is "torn apart" when the querent leaves and the runes are gathered back — the pattern existed for a moment, changed the querent's understanding, and then dissolved.

3. **Named valkyries as functional forces.** The six named valkyries (Hjörþrimul, Hild, Sanngríð, Svipul, Göndul, Gunn) represent specific aspects of battle-force. In rune readings, these names and functions can be invoked or referenced when relevant runes appear — particularly Tiwaz (warrior justice), Algiz (shield-protection), and Sowilo (victory).

4. **The witness and the second sight.** Dörruð sees what others cannot — the supernatural dimension of a mundane event. The rune practitioner serves the same function: revealing the hidden pattern behind visible events. The valkyries' song is the reading; Dörruð's observation is the querent's recognition.

5. **The raven banner as a talismanic object.** The raven banner brings victory AND death — a magical object with dual consequences. This is a model for understanding runic talismans: they are not "good" or "bad" but carry consequences that must be fully understood before use.

## 6. Hlöðskviða / Песнь о Хлёде (Song of Hlöd / The Battle of the Goths and Huns)

### Full Text (Russian translation by А. Корсун, from the Saga of Heidrek/Hervör)

### Scholarly Notes

- Also called "The Battle of the Goths and Huns." Preserved in fragments in the 13th-century Saga of Heidrek (Hervarar saga). Possibly traces back directly to a Gothic heroic poem from the era of the great migrations (4th-5th centuries). The poem's great antiquity is suggested by its focus on mass armies and tribal destinies, not just individual heroes.

- **Строфа 1:** Хумли — at Saxo Grammaticus, corresponds to Humblius, son of Dan. Кьяр — "Caesar" (Roman emperor?). Валы — Celts (and Romans).

- **Строфа 2:** "С ножом и мечом... и с резвым конем" — the saga explains: "In those days there was an ancient saying that a person is born with weapons and horses. This meant the weapons forged when a person was born, and the livestock and horses born at the same time. Nobles were endowed with all of these."

- **Строфа 8:** Мюрквид (Myrkwood) — "the dark forest" separating the land of the Huns from the land of the Goths. Данп — the Dnieper (Днепр). Some scholars speculate the "graves" and "stone" mentioned refer to Kiev, Askold's Grave, and the Pechersk Monastery — but Gothic presence on the Dnieper dates to the 4th century, when Kiev likely did not yet exist.

- **Строфа 9:** **Тюрвинг (Tyrfing)** — the famous magic sword forged by dwarves for King Svafrlami, passed down to Angantyr through his father Heidrek. The Tyrfing saga forms the connecting thread of the Hervarar saga. Tyrfing can never be drawn without killing a person, and it never misses a strike.

- **Строфа 26:** **Gizur's spear-throwing invocation to Odin.** "Пусть Один направит копье, как сказал я!" — Gizur throws a spear over the enemy army, dedicating it to Odin and invoking the god to direct the spear as he has spoken. This is the EXACT ritual described in the Ynglinga saga as Odin's practice: "Odin would cast his spear over the enemy host and dedicate them to himself." The spear-throw is simultaneously a magical act (invoking divine power), a ritual act (offering the enemy to Odin), and a psychological act (intimidation through supernatural declaration).

- **Строфа 31:** "Зол норн приговор" — "harsh is the norn's verdict." This is one of the few direct references in the Eddic corpus to a norn delivering a specific judgment — in this case, the judgment that Angantyr must kill his own brother. The norn's verdict is inexorable and transcends personal will.

- **Geography:** The place names point to Eastern Europe. Дюльгья may be the Dunajec (Vistula tributary) or the Morava (Danube tributary). Дунхейд has been connected to the Danube, the Upper Vistula, and the Don. Ёссурские горы ("Ash Mountains") are usually identified as the Little Carpathians. Архейм ("River Settlement") has been placed at the Vistula mouth, the Dnieper basin, and the Danube.

### Relevance to Rune Practice

1. **The spear-throwing dedication ritual (stanza 26).** Gizur's act of throwing a spear over the enemy army while invoking Odin is the primary mythological model for dedicating a working to a deity. In rune magic, this translates to: before beginning a runescript or talisman, the practitioner may ritually dedicate the working to the appropriate deity by speaking the invocation aloud and performing a symbolic act (not necessarily a weapon-throw — the principle is the act of offering the working to the divine force). The spear is Odin's weapon; the rune-reading is the practitioner's weapon — and both are offered upward for divine direction.

2. **The Tyrfing sword as model for runic talismans.** Tyrfing has three properties: it never misses, it must kill whenever drawn, and it was forged by dwarves. These are the properties of an ideal runic talisman: it is always effective, it has unavoidable consequences (not necessarily destructive, but always impactful), and it draws on non-human craft (the dwarves being the master-craftsmen of the Norse cosmos). The warning implicit in Tyrfing — that its power cannot be sheathed without consequence — applies to all potent rune-magic: once activated, it cannot be "un-activated."

3. **The norn's verdict (stanza 31).** Angantyr kills his brother and immediately recognizes this as the norn's judgment — not his personal choice, but fate working through him. "Зол норн приговор" is the Norse acknowledgment that some outcomes are woven by the Norns and cannot be averted, only accepted. In rune readings, this corresponds to the most difficult readings: when the runes show an outcome that cannot be changed, the practitioner must deliver the norn's verdict with honesty and compassion, just as Angantyr names it even as he grieves.

4. **Myrkwood as boundary.** The dark forest separating Hunland from Gotland is a cosmological border — like the barrier between Midgard and Jotunheim, or between the known and the unknown. In readings, when boundary-runes appear (Eihwaz, Thurisaz, Dagaz), the Myrkwood archetype may be invoked: the querent is at a border between worlds, and the crossing will be perilous regardless of which direction they choose.

## 7. Skírnismál — For Scírnis (The Lay of Skírnir)

*Source: vikingr.org edition, English translation by Marius Christensen. The Skírnismál is found in the Codex Regius (GKS 2365 4to) and AM 748 I 4to. It is included here because it contains the single most explicit description of runic magic in the entire Poetic Edda: Skírnir's carving of three runes to compel the giantess Gerðr.*

### The Rune-Carving Scene (verses 26–39)

The plot: Freyr has seen the giantess Gerðr from Óðinn's high seat and fallen desperately in love. He sends his servant Skírnir to woo her. Skírnir offers golden apples (rejected), the ring Draupnir (rejected), and finally turns to threats and magical coercion. The climax is verse 37, where Skírnir carves runes as an act of operative magic.

**26.** "I strike you, maid, with my magic staff
To make you obey my will;
You will go where no human will ever see you again.

**27.** "On the eagle's hill you will always sit
And look at the gates of Hel;
Your food will become more loathsome to you
Than a pale snake is to men.

**28.** "If you come out, Hrimnir will stand and stare at you in fear
(People will marvel at you);
You will become more famous than the watchman of the gods!
Come out of your prison, then,

**29.** "Rage and longing, chains and wrath,
Tears and torment will be yours;
Wherever you sit, my doom will be upon you
With heavy heart
And double pain.

**30.** "In the giants' home, vile things will harm you
Every day with evil deeds;
You will get grief instead of happiness,
And sorrow to suffer with tears.

**31.** "You will live forever with three-headed giants
Or never know a husband;
(Let longing grip you, let wasting waste you —)
You will be like the thistle
That was thrown into the loft and crushed there.

**32.** "I am going to the wood,
And to the wet forest,
To find a magic wand;
I have found a magic wand.

**33.** "Odin is angry, the best of the gods is angry,
Freyr will be your enemy,
Worst of maidens, who has earned
The magic wrath of the gods.

**34.** "Listen, rulers of frost, hear, giants,
Sons of Suttung,
And gods, too,
How I forbid and how I ban
The meeting of men with the maiden,
(The joy of men with the maiden.)

**35.** "Hrimgrimnir is he, the giant who will have you
In the depth by the doors of Hel;

Crawling and craving in vain,
(Crawling and having no hope.)

**36.** "There, base creatures by the root of the tree
Will give you horns of filth to drink;
You will never find a fairer drink,
Maiden, to meet your desire,
(Maiden, to meet my desire.)

**37.** "I am writing you a charm and three runes with it,

And take the frost-cup filled with mead;
Though I never believed that I would love
Anyone of the Wanes."

Skírnir spoke:

**39.** "I must learn all my news…"

### Scholarly Notes

- **Verse 37 is the most important verse in the Eddic corpus for understanding runic magic as a technology.** The key phrase is "rita ek þér á rúnar" — "I write/carve for you runes." The verb *rita* specifically means "to carve" or "to inscribe" (cognate with English "write," but with the physical act of cutting into a surface). This is not metaphorical — Skírnir is performing a physical act of rune-carving as operative magic.

- **"Three runes" (þrúrúnar).** The number three is structurally significant: three Norns, three aetts, three roots of Yggdrasil, three positions (Direct/Mirrored/Inverted). Three runes carved together form a **bindrune** or **runescript** — the minimal operative unit in runic magic. The three specific effects named (longing/Óðinn's desire, madness/geð, lust/herða) are the three aspects of the compulsion being laid.

- **"But what I have written I may yet erase."** This is extraordinary: it establishes that runes can be both inscribed AND erased — that runic magic is not irreversible. The condition for erasure is "if I find a need for it" — implying that the practitioner retains the power to undo their own working. This has direct implications for rune practice: a talisman or bindrune can be deactivated by erasing the runes (physically or ritually), returning the energy to its unformed state.

- **The magic staff (gambanteinn, verse 26/32).** Skírnir goes to the forest to find a magic wand before threatening Gerðr. The staff is the ritual tool that channels the rune-carving. In practice, this corresponds to the practitioner's ritual tool — the carving instrument itself becomes a magical object when used with intent.

- **The escalation pattern.** Skírnir begins with gifts (apples, Draupnir), escalates to threats (curse descriptions), then finally performs operative magic (rune-carving). This three-stage escalation — offering → declaration → inscription — is a structural model for runic magical operations: first you appeal (the Gebo approach), then you declare intent (the Ansuz/Tiwaz approach), then you inscribe (the operative act).

- **Gerðr's capitulation (verse 38).** Gerðr accepts the mead-cup AFTER the runes are carved but BEFORE they take effect. The runes create the possibility; the target's acceptance seals it. This has implications for rune readings: the runes show the trajectory, but the querent's acceptance or resistance shapes the outcome.

### Relevance to Rune Practice

1. **Rune-carving as operative magic.** The Skírnismál provides the primary Eddic evidence that runes were used as an active magical technology, not merely as a writing system or divinatory tool. When Skírnir carves three runes with a specific intent (longing, madness, lust), he is performing the same operation that a rune practitioner performs when creating a talisman or bindrune: selecting specific rune-forms to channel specific energies toward a specific target.

2. **The erase-clause.** The statement "what I have written I may yet erase" is the mythological precedent for the principle that rune-magic can be undone. A carved rune can be physically scraped away; a charged talisman can be ritually deconsecrated. The practitioner who inscribed the working retains the authority to dissolve it. This is especially relevant for readings where the querent asks about reversing a previous magical working.

3. **Three-runecraft.** Skírnir's three runes correspond to the three-layer structure found throughout runic practice: three Norns, three aetts, three positions. In practical terms, a three-rune spread (past/present/future, or situation/action/outcome) is the minimal complete divinatory structure — just as three runes are the minimal complete magical structure.

4. **The staff and the carving.** The relationship between Skírnir's gambanteinn and the rune-carving suggests that the physical instrument of inscription (the carving tool) is itself a ritual object, not merely a convenience. In contemporary practice, this translates to the principle that the tools of rune-work (the tile set, the cloth, the carving knife) should be treated as extensions of the practitioner's intent, not as neutral implements.

# §2 Witchcraft & Craft in the Sagas

*Source: Ведьма Хельга / Velya forum (t5063) — "Колдовство и ремесло в исландских сагах"*

This section documents the practical magical traditions recorded in the Icelandic sagas, providing historical grounding for runic and magical knowledge. The sagas, though written during the Christian period, preserve invaluable accounts of how Norse people actually practiced magic — including the use of runes, galdr, seiðr, weather-working, and the social context of practitioners.

---

## 1. Witchcraft as Skill and Craft

The attitude of ancient Scandinavians toward the ability to practice magic was remarkably pragmatic. Pagans often treated it as an additional skill a person possessed. This applied equally to men and women. Examples from the sagas:

- **Torgunna** in the *Saga of Eirik the Red*: "Leiv took a liking to a woman named Torgunna there. She was of noble birth and, as Leiv understood, skilled in witchcraft" — note the casual, descriptive tone.
- **Svan** in the *Saga of the People of Laxárdalr*: "Svan was very skilled in witchcraft... He was of heavy temper and hard to deal with" — skill noted alongside character, not as a supernatural aberration.

Despite this pragmatism, witchcraft was not an especially honorable occupation. Sorcerers and witches were sensibly feared and not trusted. This negative attitude intensified with the arrival of Christianity.

**The "Good Dis" advice to Gisli** (*Saga of Gisli*): A dream-spirit advises Gisli to abandon the old faith, cease sorcery, and be kind to the lame, the blind, and the weak:

> *Nourisher of eagles, listen, — spoke Lonn of gold, — forever abandon witchcraft, sorcery ill suits a skald. It is known to all that it is unfitting for the shaker of spears to trust in the worst evil, the secret knowledge of witches.*

This passage reveals a key tension: the Christian-era recording of the sagas imposed moral judgment on magical practice, but the underlying narrative shows magic as an embedded, functional part of pre-Christian society.

---

## 2. Witchcraft and Religion

Witchcraft in pagan times was tightly intertwined with religion — just as Christianity itself mixed with witchcraft, folk healing, and charmed practice when religion ceased to be abstract and became part of daily ritual. Key observations:

### Egil Skallagrimsson's Religious-Magical Actions

**The Legal Dispute Curse** (*Saga of Egil*): When Egil perceived injustice at an assembly court, he pronounced a warning, calling the people and gods as witnesses and placing a prohibition on the disputed land. He considered himself in the right and therefore called down the wrath of the gods upon the wrongdoer. The question had not yet been decided, but through this action Egil unilaterally concluded it in his own favor. This is described as a "typical witchcraft action": the desired is declared as actual, accompanied by threat, invocation of gods, and public proclamation. Later, in his verses and spells about this dispute, Egil always called upon Freyr and Njörðr for aid.

**The Níðstöng (Scorn-Pole) Ritual** (*Saga of Egil*): Egil's most famous curse — performed with a hazel pole and a horse's skull set into a cleft in the rock — was fundamentally a religious-magical operation. The spirits of the land (landvættir) were to ensure that King Eirik and Gunnhildr would find no peace in Norway. The ritual was aimed at the local spirits, making it a religious act (invoking the spiritual inhabitants of the land) combined with a magical technique (the níðstöng). Notably, Egil was not the "wronged party" at this point — he had just killed dozens of the king's men, including Eirik and Gunnhildr's son Ragnvaldr, and captured their property. His thirst for vengeance was genuinely inexhaustible.

### The Gisli Rune-Stick Episode

A crucial and often misunderstood passage: Gisli, needing his brother Thorkell to open the door, takes a stick, carves runes on it, and throws it into the house. Thorkell sees the stick, reads the runes, and opens the door.

**The author's interpretation** (disagreeing with scholars who see this as "one of the rare mentions of runes used for simple information transfer"): Gisli, who was known to practice witchcraft, carved an angry spell-warning on the stick — not an informational message. Thorkell saw it, was frightened, and opened the door. There was no point writing something "informational" — Gisli could have spoken through the door. But frightening someone with a spell was the best way to get results.

This interpretation is significant for rune practitioners: runes carved in anger or urgency carry the force of a magical command, not mere communication. The very act of carving runes was an act of power.

---

## 3. Weather Magic: Summoning Wind, Fog, or Storm

The ability to control weather was one of the most valued magical skills, especially for seafaring people — fishermen and Vikings alike.

### Raud the Powerful (*Saga of Olaf Tryggvason*)

Raud was a staunch pagan and great sorcerer. Wind always favored him wherever he sailed. When King Olaf tried to reach Raud to convert or destroy him, a fearsome wind prevented entry into the fjord. Olaf turned to other places and baptized people there. When he returned, a terrible blizzard and storm arose again. The king then turned to his bishop, recognizing this as witchcraft and "devilish delusion." The bishop sprinkled the entire ship with holy water, and the wind ceased. Through this Christian "counter-magic," they captured and killed Raud.

**Key observation**: The saga presents this as a contest between pagan and Christian magic — both are effective, both are real. The bishop's holy water is functionally equivalent to a countering spell.

### Audborg's Weather Curse (*Saga of Gisli*)

After her son Thorstein was struck by Berg, the old woman Audborg could not sleep. She went outside in cold, windless, cloudless weather. She walked around the house *against the sun* (widdershins — note the direction!) and, tilting her head back, drew in air from all sides. The weather changed: a great blizzard rose, then a thaw, and a flood of meltwater triggered an avalanche on Berg's farm, killing twelve people. The traces of this avalanche are still visible today.

**Magical technique recorded**: Walking widdershins (against the sun) + drawing in air from all directions = weather change. This is a rare and detailed description of the physical technique of weather magic in the sagas.

### The Sorceresses Heiðr and Hamglama (*Saga of Frithjof the Bold*)

King Helgi and his brother Halfdan sent for two sorceresses — Heiðr and Hamglama — paying them to call up a storm that would drown Frithjof and his men at sea. They prepared charms and mounted a raised platform (the *seidhjallr*, directly paralleling the völva's platform in the *Saga of Erik the Red*) with witchcraft and incantations.

When Frithjof was far from land, a great storm with snow arose. Frithjof immediately recognized this as a magical, not natural, storm. He climbed the mast and described what he saw: an enormous whale lying in a ring around the ship, with two women on its back — these were the sorceresses in animal form, causing the storm.

Frithjof then attacked them directly: he ran to the prow with a spear and sang:

> *Good, Ellida! Run over the waves! Break the witches' teeth and brow, cheekbones and jaws of the evil women; break a leg or both of these monsters.*

He struck one sorceress with a spear, and the ship's prow hit the other in the back. Both had their spines broken, and the whale sank. Meanwhile, back on land, both sorceresses fell from their magical platform and broke their backs — what happened to the magical animals happened to the practitioners.

**Names decoded**: Heiðr — a common name for seeresses and witches (the same Heiðr who is the völva in *Völuspá*). Hamglama — likely "she who takes deceptive shapes" (a shape-shifter, equivalent to a hypnotist or illusion-worker). These could have been real names, adopted magical names, or generic terms for a type of practitioner.

### The Laxárdalr Family Storm (*Saga of the People of Laxárdalr*)

The sorcerous family (see Section 8 below) also summoned a storm to drown their accusers. The head of the family built a large platform for witchcraft, and all of them began singing incantations. A storm arose and the ship with their enemies sank.

### Svein's Fog (*Saga of Njal*)

Svein, another practitioner with a heavy and morose character, could summon fog. When men pursued the killer Thjóstólf whom Svein was sheltering, Svein first began to yawn heavily — perceiving the approach of an enemy's spirit-double. He then went outside, pulled a goatskin over his head, and called fog, blindness, and bewilderment upon the pursuers. A thick fog and darkness covered the men's eyes, and they scattered — some into bogs, some into forests. Their leader Ósvífr said: "If I could find my horse and weapons, I would turn back." Immediately everyone could see again and found their horses and weapons. But as soon as they resumed the pursuit, the same bewilderment struck again, and they had to turn back.

**Magical technique recorded**: (1) Yawning as a sign of approaching spiritual perception — the practitioner senses a pursuer's spirit-double. (2) Goatskin over the head — a ritual garment or masking technique. (3) Calling specific effects: fog, blindness, bewilderment (morok). The fog is targeted and specific — it affects only the pursuers and lifts when they abandon the chase.

## 4. Magical Drink (Poisoned and Enchanted Beverages)

### Grímhildr's Potion of Forgetfulness (*Völsunga Saga*)

Grímhildr, the sorceress, twice administers a magical drink of forgetting: first to Sigurd (to forget Brynhildr), then to Gudrún (to forget Sigurd's death and all her sorrows). The drink given to Gudrún is described in detail:

> *Many runes of various kinds were on the horn, red-stained, carved, hard to read: the long fish of the steppe (= serpent) from the land of the Haddings, uncut grain, deer sinews. In that brew were troubles and pains, burnt acorns, entrails of sacrifices, various roots, ashes of the hearth, boar's liver — so that evil might be forgotten.*

**Key observations for rune practitioners**:
- The vessel itself bore **runes carved and stained red** — a complete magical inscription on the drinking horn.
- The ingredients include both physical components (herbs, animal parts) and metaphorical/poetic elements (the "long fish of the steppe" = serpent).
- "Entrails of sacrifices" indicates the religious dimension — the drink was not merely herbal but ritually prepared.
- The runic inscription on the vessel is integral to the enchantment — the drink and the runes work together.

### Egil's Counter-Spell (*Saga of Egil*)

When King Eirik's wife Gunnhildr and the host Bard attempted to poison Egil's drink, Egil carved runes on the horn, colored them with his own blood, spoke a verse, and the horn shattered to pieces.

**Analysis**: The author notes this could be read multiple ways — Egil was enormously strong and could have crushed the horn physically, or the runic inscription could have caused it to shatter. The author personally has observed "enchanted" objects suddenly shattering. The episode also reveals the principle: **runes carved with blood on a vessel can counteract enchantment or poison within that vessel**.

## 5. Witchcraft "For Restlessness" (Magical Banishment)

### Egil's Níðstöng Against Eirik and Gunnhildr

Egil's scorn-pole curse demanded that the land-spirits grant no peace to Eirik Bloodaxe and Gunnhildr in Norway. This is the classic example of "magical banishment" — making it impossible for someone to remain in a place.

### Gunnhildr's Counter-Curse

In turn, after being forced to leave Norway, Gunnhildr allegedly worked magic so that Egil would find no peace in Iceland until they met again. Remarkably, she accomplished this from England — a different island, not even on the same continent. The effect: Egil began to feel severe anxiety and restlessness and decided to sail to England to King Aethelstan. But a great storm drove his ship ashore near where King Eirik was — delivering Egil directly to his enemies.

### Thorgimr Nose's Curse (*Saga of Gisli*)

Thorgimr Nose pronounced that the killer (Gisli) would find no peace in the land, and no one could help him even if they wanted to. However, Thorgimr failed to account for islands — and Gisli found refuge on a small island.

**Observation**: The curse's boundary matters. A curse on "the land" does not extend to islands. This reflects the Norse understanding of magical jurisdiction — spells have specific territorial limits.

### The Runic Inscription of the 6th Century

A runic inscription from the 6th century concludes: *"And whoever destroys this, may he be in the power of restlessness and depravity, may he die a treacherous death."*

This is a direct historical example of the "restlessness curse" inscribed in runes — the magical technique of banishment or harassment through runic inscription.

## 6. Sexual Binding (Egilett)

### Queen Gunnhildr's Binding of Hrút (*Saga of Njal*)

Gunnhildr, the widowed queen and mother of the Norwegian king Harald Greyhide, took the Icelander Hrút as her lover — he did not dare refuse an older woman who wielded both secular power and sorcerous reputation. When Hrút prepared to return to Iceland and his betrothed Unn, Gunnhildr gave him a golden arm-ring (note: arm-rings symbolize binding obligations in Norse culture), embraced him around the neck (another binding gesture), and pronounced:

> *"If my power over you is as great as I think, then you shall have no pleasure in Iceland with the woman you have in mind. But with other women you will achieve what you want."*

Hrút returned, married Unn, but could not consummate the marriage. After two years, Unn divorced him publicly. This is described as a classic case of "sexual binding" (эгильет) — a curse that prevents sexual function with a specific person.

**The author's nuance**: The situation may not be purely magical. Hrút, feeling guilty about his liaison with an older woman of authority, could have psychosomatically "punished himself" after marrying Unn. If Hrút could not be a man for Unn specifically (but could for others), that aligns with the curse. If he could not for any woman, that confirms Gunnhildr's sorcery.

## 7. The Creation of an Artificial Human (Norse Golem Analog)

### Thorgardr the Wooden Man (*Þáttr of Thorleif Jarl's Skald*)

Jarla Hákon, offended by a sorcerous prank of Thorleif's that caused him illness, decided to take revenge. He summoned two women — Þorgerðr Hölgabrúðr (Temple Bride) and her sister Irpa — who are described as either witches or pagan priestesses. (These same figures appear in the *Saga of Njal* where they are robbed in Hákon's shrine, and Þorgerðr is described as being the size of a grown man. They were mortal women, not goddesses.)

Hákon performed rich sacrifices and received a favorable prophecy. He then ordered a log washed ashore by the surf to be made into a wooden man. Through his own sorcery and the witchcraft of the sisters, they killed a man, extracted his heart, and placed it in the wooden figure. They gave it clothes and named it **Thorgardr**. They endowed it with such enormous "satanic power" that it could walk and talk with people. They put it on a ship and sent it to Iceland to kill Thorleif Jarl's Skald. Hákon gave it an axe taken from the sisters' shrine. Thorgardr killed Thorleif and then sank into the earth.

**Key observations**:
- The artificial person is named **Thorgardr** — almost the same as the priestess **Þorgerðr**, suggesting a magical correspondence between the maker and the made.
- Made from driftwood — the same material from which the gods (Æsir) created the first humans (Ask and Embla were made from trees). The creation mirrors the divine act.
- Unlike the Jewish Golem (made of clay), the Norse artificial human could **speak**.
- The heart of a living man was required — this is not creation from nothing but transference of life force.
- After completing its mission, Thorgardr sank into the earth — it did not remain in the world of the living.

### The Clay Giant from the Prose Edda

In Snorri's *Prose Edda*, the giants molded a clay man at the Stone Courts who was nine leagues tall and three leagues around. They could find no heart large enough until they took a mare's heart, "and it was far from fearless when Thor appeared." This clay giant is explicitly made from clay — like the Jewish Golem. Since giants in Norse mythology were primarily bearers of hostile magic and cunning sorcerers from whom even the gods borrowed, the author suggests that the hostile magic practiced among humans was attributed to giants in the mythological narrative.

## 8. Witchcraft Families

### The Family of Kotkel (*Saga of the People of Laxárdalr*)

The most dangerous practitioners were entire families of sorcerers. The head of the family was **Kotkel**, his wife **Grima** (a common name for a witch), and their two sons **Hallbjörn Grinder-in-the-Whetstone** and **Stingandi**. They came from the Hebrides and were outsiders in Iceland. All were "very knowledgeable in witchcraft and skilled in sorcery."

**Key dynamics of witchcraft families**:
- **Nomadic existence**: Sorcerers of different countries and peoples often had to move from place to place due to their character and occupation. Conversely, closed communities often accused strangers of witchcraft.
- **Patronage by the wealthy**: Rich and noble people frequently took foreign sorcerers under their protection. The sorcerers, being difficult in temperament, quarreled with locals, or locals envied the outsiders.
- **Self-sufficiency through magic**: "Kotkel and his people did not work much for their sustenance, but by winter they did not need to buy hay or food. Everyone was displeased that they had settled here. But no one dared to offend them out of fear of Thórleik." — Sorcerers obtained provisions through payment for magical work, threats, or cunning.
- **The cost of patronage**: Their patron Thórleik eventually asked them to do something bad to his enemy Hrút. The family agreed. All four of them came to Hrút's farm and "began great witchcraft there. And when the witchcraft sounds flew forth, the people who were in the house could not understand what it meant. But their singing was wondrous to hear." Hrút guessed what was happening and told his household to stay awake as long as possible — revealing the belief that **magic is more effective against sleeping victims**. Eventually everyone fell asleep, and Hrút's beloved son Kári walked to the place of the witchcraft and immediately died.
- **Destruction of sorcerers**: The community killed the family members by stoning (Kotkel and Grima — the mound of stones over them is still called Skrattavardi, "Witch Stones") and drowning (Hallbjörn). When captured, a **bag was placed over their head** to prevent the evil eye — indicating the power of a sorcerer's gaze even when captured.
- **The last curse**: Hallbjörn, before being drowned, cursed Thórleik and his dwelling. "And this curse, the saga says, must have taken effect."
- **Stingandi's final act**: The escaped son became a bandit. When captured, a bag was placed over his head, but it had a hole. Through the hole, Stingandi looked at a fertile mountainside. Immediately it was as if a whirlwind struck — the earth was torn up, and grass never grew there again. He was then stoned to death.
- **The undead return**: Hallbjörn, who had been drowned rather than stoned and did not have stones piled over him, later returned as an undead being (draugr). He fought with a strong and fearless man named Thorkell the Bald, could not defeat him, and went back underground.

**Critical observations for rune/magic practice**:
1. **Method of killing matters**: Stoning + stone mound = the dead stay dead. Drowning without a stone mound = the dead may return. The physical treatment of the sorcerer's body determines their post-mortem power.
2. **The evil eye**: A sorcerer's gaze retains power even when they are captured and helpless. A bag over the head neutralizes this.
3. **Last glance curse**: What a dying sorcerer sees in their final moments can be permanently affected — Stingandi's glance made fertile land barren forever.
4. **Sleep vulnerability**: Magic works best on sleeping targets. Staying awake is a defense.
5. **Witchcraft sounds**: The singing/incantation is described as "wondrous to hear" — even the victims acknowledge the beauty of the magical chant, paralleling the galdr tradition where the aesthetic quality of the chant is part of its power.
