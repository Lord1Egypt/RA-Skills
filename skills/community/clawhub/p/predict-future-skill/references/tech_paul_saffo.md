# Paul Saffo — Tech Forecasting

Silicon Valley forecaster; Stanford, Long Now Foundation. Primary source:
*Six Rules for Effective Forecasting*, HBR July 2007 (R0707K). Amara's Law
(attributed to Roy Amara, Institute for the Future) appears throughout Rule 2.

**Role in the tech stack:** *How* to map uncertainty — cones, S-curves, weak
signals, forecast discipline.

---

## Forecasting vs. prediction

Saffo's distinction (HBR 2007):

- **Prediction** assumes preordained outcomes — myth territory.
- **Forecasting** reads hidden currents in the present to signal **possible
  changes in direction** for companies, societies, or industries.

Primary goal: identify the **full range of possibilities**, not a single
illusory certainty. The forecaster maps uncertainty; the consumer must
understand the logic well enough to **critique** it.

For **30-day forecasts**: deliver actionable context (what to watch, what
could shift) — not prophecy.

---

## Rule 1 — Define a cone of uncertainty

**Process (iterative):**

1. Anchor on a moment or event (e.g. "AI coding assistants, next 30 days").
2. List **inside the cone**: core drivers, competing technologies, plausible
   product/market paths.
3. List **at the edge**: wild cards — low probability (<10%) or unquantifiable,
   but **disproportionate impact** if they occur.
4. Set **breadth first** — a narrow cone causes avoidable surprises; refine
   later.
5. Distinguish **highly improbable** from **wildly impossible**; edge elements
   should sit on the "ragged edge of plausibility."

**Common errors:**

- Treating wild cards as certainties (Y2K) or dismissing them after a nonevent
  (9/11).
- Cluttering the edge while neglecting **center-of-cone** probabilities.

## Rule 2 — Look for the S curve

Change follows **power-law / S-curve** dynamics: slow start → inflection →
explosive growth → taper.

**Forecaster moves:**

1. Watch the **left side** of the curve (quiet precursors), not only the
   inflection — "standing on a whale, fishing for minnows" if you miss the
   larger curve behind a small one.
2. Apply **Amara's Law**: we **overestimate** short-term impact and
   **underestimate** long-term impact.
3. Default to **slow** until evidence of inflection — Silicon Valley ideas often
   take **~20 years** to become overnight successes (TV, Internet, PCs).
4. Folk wisdom: **"Never mistake a clear view for a short distance."**
5. After inflection, expect **faster** change than linear intuition suggests;
   opportunities often arrive in **unexpected forms** (PCs sold for work; home
   adoption was entertainment).

**Fractal S curves:** large curves compose smaller linked S curves — identify
which level you are forecasting.

---

## Rule 3 — Embrace things that don't fit

Gibson: *"The future's already here. It's just not evenly distributed yet."*

**Hunt for:**

- Events you **cannot classify** with existing categories.
- **Interesting failures** — smart ideas that went nowhere (Habitat → MUDs →
  EverQuest → Second Life).
- **Anomalies** that incumbents dismiss (Roomba enthusiasm + DARPA Grand
  Challenge progress → robotics inflection cluster).

Indicators arrive in **clusters** — aggregate weak signals before declaring
"nothing happening."

---

## Rule 4 — Strong opinions, weakly held

Form a forecast **fast**, then **actively seek disconfirmation** (Kennedy vs.
Delphy, 1923 — multiple weak conflicting signals beat one strong wrong signal).

**Practice:**

1. State the strongest version of your view.
2. List what would prove you wrong.
3. Update or discard on new data — "forecast often; be first to prove yourself
   wrong."
4. Prefer **interlocking weak information** over one compelling data point.

Pairs naturally with Bayesian updating in `general_Philip_Tetlock.md`.

## Rule 5 — Look back twice as far as you look forward

Use history for **rhymes**, not repetition. McLuhan: rearview mirror comfort —
use the mirror for **illumination**, not support.

**Process:**

1. If forecasting 30 days ahead, scan **~60 days to several years** of analogs.
2. Find **pattern similarity** (e.g. mass media order of the 1950s vs. personal
   media / Web platform wars).
3. Reject **straight-line extrapolation** of the recent past.
4. If the double interval is insufficient, look **further back**.

**Error to avoid:** using history like a drunk uses a lamppost — for support, not
light (Levin / AOL–Time Warner timing).

---

## Rule 6 — Know when not to forecast

When the cone **suddenly widens** (Berlin Wall 1989), **pause point forecasts**
and switch to **indicator collection**. Even in rapid change, **more elements
stay constant than change** — dot-com era still had old economics and human
desires.

**Signs to defer:** unprecedented structural break, contradictory macro forces,
no stable reference class. Wait for indicators to **narrow the cone** again.

## How to apply in this skill

Layer on `general_Philip_Tetlock.md` for tech queries. Use with
`tech_geoffrey_moore.md` (adoption stage) and `tech_clayton_christensen.md`
(industry structure).

1. **Cone** — Draft wide; list drivers, paths, wild cards; label center vs. edge.
2. **S-curve position** — Precursor / pre-inflection / inflection / maturity;
   apply Amara calibration to hype vs. substance.
3. **Anomaly scan** — What doesn't fit? Any failure clusters?
4. **Strong/weak** — State view + explicit disconfirmers; update on new evidence.
5. **Historical rhyme** — Name 1–2 analogs at ≥2× your forward horizon.
6. **Forecast gate** — If cone too wide, output scenarios + watchlist instead of
   a single point estimate.

**Search targets:** patent filings, GitHub/arxiv velocity, pilot deployments,
DARPA/industry challenge results, fringe user behavior, failed predecessor
products, analyst hype vs. revenue cohort data.
