# Marketing Strategy — Accounting, CPA & Tax Firm Marketing Kit

## Market Size

- **665,000+** licensed CPAs in the United States (AICPA 2025)
- **130,000+** CPA firms (most solo or 2-5 person shops)
- **350,000+** enrolled agents (EA credential — IRS representation rights)
- **1.7 million+** paid tax preparers (PTIN holders who file returns for compensation)
- **$130 billion** US tax preparation and accounting services industry (IBISWorld)
- **98%** of tax/accounting firms are small businesses with no in-house marketing team

**High-value sub-segment:** Accounting firms earning $200K-$2M/year. They can afford marketing but typically don't have a dedicated marketer — every campaign is done by the CPA principal or a part-time admin. These firms are the ideal DFY customer.

### Tax Season Multiplier
Tax season (Jan–Apr) drives 3-4× normal demand. A firm CPA panic-buying marketing help in February will pay $247 DFY without blinking. Retainer revenue is concentrated in the October–April window.

### Referral Network Effect
CPA firm principals talk to each other (state CPA society meetings, AICPA events, referral networks). One DFY client who shows peers the output can generate 3-5 referrals. Unlike consumer tools, this is a professional word-of-mouth market.

## Target Buyer Personas

### 1. Solo CPA Principal ("The Time-Starved Practitioner")
- 1-3 person firm, $300K-$800K revenue
- Does client work + all admin + all marketing
- Pain: spends February/March writing the same newsletter every year
- Trigger: "I need something that doesn't sound like a robot wrote it AND won't get me a board complaint"
- Conversion: $29 Pro → $97 DFY upgrade in year 2

### 2. Mid-Size Firm Marketing Admin ("The Part-Time Content Creator")
- 5-20 person firm, $1M-$5M revenue
- Admin or junior staff handles LinkedIn, newsletters, Google Ads
- Pain: doesn't know accounting compliance rules; writes posts that make the managing partner nervous
- Trigger: "The managing partner keeps editing everything I write because she's worried about compliance"
- Conversion: $29 Pro (purchased by admin, expensed as software)

### 3. Tax Franchise Owner ("The Season Sprinter")
- H&R Block franchisee, Liberty Tax, Jackson Hewitt operator, or independent chain
- Runs paid ads heavy January–April; needs fresh creative every year
- Pain: franchise corporate provides cookie-cutter content; local differentiation requires custom copy
- Trigger: January — "I need my Google Ads updated before the season starts"
- Conversion: $247 DFY full kit (high urgency, cost is trivial vs. ad spend)

### 4. Enrolled Agent ("The IRS Specialist")
- EA credential = right to represent taxpayers before IRS
- Specializes in back taxes, IRS audits, offers in compromise
- Pain: "audit defense" marketing is legally sensitive — easy to make misleading claims
- Trigger: IRS compliance enforcement uptick (any news cycle about IRS audits drives their business)
- Conversion: $97 DFY (EA firms are smaller, more price-sensitive than CPA firms — $97 is the sweet spot)

## Competitive Moat Durability

Circular 230 and AICPA advertising rules have been stable for decades. State board disclosure requirements are enforced more, not less, as AI-generated content proliferates. The compliance moat strengthens over time as generic AI tools generate more board complaints — which will drive CPA principals to seek compliant alternatives.
