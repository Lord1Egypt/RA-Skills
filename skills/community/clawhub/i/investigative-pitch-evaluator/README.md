# investigative-pitch-evaluator

Turn a tip, lead, or rough story idea into an **editor-ready investigative pitch packet** and a scored **editorial-meeting evaluation rubric** — produced from the reporter's raw notes and source-tier inventory.

## When to use

A reporter, freelancer, investigative editor, or newsroom intake desk has:

- a lead (tip, document drop, FOIA return, data set, source allegation, or anomaly noticed in reporting), and
- needs to (a) defend the story in an editorial meeting, or (b) decide whether to commit reporting hours, legal review, and travel budget.

The skill produces both the pitch and the editor's evaluation — so the reporter can sharpen the pitch *before* the meeting, and an editor can use it for triage.

## What it produces

- One-paragraph **lede statement** (the "if true, then what" of the story)
- Story type tag (accountability / explanatory / data-driven / narrative / breaking-news follow-up / watchdog)
- **Public-interest justification** (specific harm, decision-maker accountability, policy stakes)
- **Source inventory** with tier rating (Documentary primary, Documentary secondary, On-record human, On-background human, Anonymous human, Aggregated/social)
- **Evidence-to-claim map** — every load-bearing claim is tied to a tier-rated source
- **Reporting plan** (next 5–15 steps: FOIA targets, data pulls, interview list, on-the-ground visits)
- **Legal & ethics checklist** (defamation exposure, privacy, minors, victims of crime, anonymous source policy, conflict of interest, AI-disclosure)
- **Originality check** (what has already been published; what is genuinely new)
- **Editorial rubric scoring** (Originality, Evidence base, Public-interest impact, Feasibility, Legal/ethics risk, Reporter fit)
- **Verdict:** Greenlight / Develop / Park / Decline — with the specific condition that would flip it
- **Time + cost estimate band** (reporter weeks, expected legal-review hours, travel)

## Inputs the agent will ask for

One question at a time:

1. Lead / tip in plain language
2. Target outlet and section (and whether it is a pitch to your own desk or external)
3. What is genuinely new vs. previously reported
4. List of sources and document/data already in hand
5. Public-interest justification
6. Known legal or ethical exposure
7. Reporter availability and time budget
8. Hard deadline or news peg, if any
