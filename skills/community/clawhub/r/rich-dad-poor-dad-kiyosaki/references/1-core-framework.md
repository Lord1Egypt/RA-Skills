> Source: Rich Dad Poor Dad by Robert Kiyosaki, Introduction, Chapters 1-3

# Core Framework: The 6 Lessons + The Rat Race

## The Rat Race

The central metaphor of the book: most people are trapped in a pattern of working for money, spending that money, and needing to work for more. The only way out is to buy assets that generate enough income to cover expenses.

> **Case: Kiyosaki's two dads** (Introduction): Kiyosaki had two father figures. His "poor dad" was his biological father — highly educated, worked for the government, believed in job security, and struggled financially. His "rich dad" was his best friend's father — dropped out of school in 8th grade, built businesses, and became one of the richest men in Hawaii.
> **Key takeaway**: The same financial facts can be interpreted completely differently depending on your mindset. Rich dad taught: "The rich don't work for money." Poor dad taught: "Study hard so you can find a good company to work for."

## The 6 Lessons

| Lesson | Core Idea | Application |
|---|---|---|
| 1: The Rich Don't Work for Money | Money works for the rich. The middle class works for money. | Find ways to make money without trading your time |
| 2: Why Teach Financial Literacy? | Know the difference between assets and liabilities. Buy assets. | Financial literacy is reading a balance sheet |
| 3: Mind Your Own Business | Build your own asset column while working your day job | Start a side business or investment portfolio |
| 4: History of Taxes & Power of Corporations | The rich use corporations to legally pay less tax | Understand the tax advantages of business ownership |
| 5: The Rich Invent Money | Financial intelligence creates opportunities | Learn to see what others miss |
| 6: Work to Learn — Don't Work for Money | Choose jobs for the skills they teach, not the salary | Sales skills > salary |

> **Case: The comic book store (Lesson 1)** (Chapter 1): As a 9-year-old, Kiyosaki and his friend Mike (rich dad's son) wanted to make money. They started melting lead from toothpaste tubes to make coins — a terrible idea that backfired. Rich dad didn't punish them. He taught them: "The rich don't work for money. They work to build assets that make money for them." Instead of paying them a wage, rich dad gave them opportunities to learn.
> **Key takeaway**: Making mistakes is how you learn about money. School punishes mistakes. Rich dad saw mistakes as learning opportunities.

## Assets vs Liabilities — The Cashflow Patterns

The single most important distinction in the book:

**The Poor Cashflow Pattern**: Income (job) → Expenses (bills) → no assets left
**The Middle Class Cashflow Pattern**: Income (job) → Expenses + Liabilities (mortgage, car payments) → no assets left
**The Rich Cashflow Pattern**: Income (assets) → Expenses covered by asset income → surplus buys more assets

> **Case: The lottery winner (Chapter 2)**: Most lottery winners are broke within 5 years. Why? Because they didn't change their cashflow pattern. They suddenly had money, but they spent it on liabilities (houses, cars, gifts) instead of assets. Their financial literacy didn't improve with their bank balance.
> **Key takeaway**: Your cashflow pattern determines your financial future, not your income level. A $500k income with a $500k spending pattern is the same trap as a $50k income with a $50k spending pattern.

## The CASHFLOW Quadrant

A model for understanding how people generate income:
- **E (Employee)**: Works for someone else
- **S (Self-Employed)**: Owns a job
- **B (Business Owner)**: Owns a system that works without them
- **I (Investor)**: Money works for them

The goal: move from E and S to B and I.
