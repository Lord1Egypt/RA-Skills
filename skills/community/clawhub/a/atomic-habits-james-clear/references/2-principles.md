> Source: Atomic Habits by James Clear, Chapters 1-3, 16, 20

# Key Principles

## Principle 1: Focus on systems, not goals

Goals are about the results you want. Systems are about the processes that lead to those results. Winners and losers can have the same goals. What separates them is the system.

> **Case: Two students with the same goal** (Chapter 1): Two people both want to get an A in a class. One builds a system of daily study, review, and practice. The other declares "I want an A" but has no system. The goal is identical. The outcome diverges because of the system, not the goal.

## Principle 2: Habits are identity votes

Every action you take is a vote for the type of person you wish to become. The goal is not to read one book — it's to become a reader. The goal is not to run one mile — it's to become a runner.

> **Case: The "reader" vs the "person who reads"** (Chapter 3): A person who identifies as a reader makes time for books naturally. A person who says "I need to read more" fights against their own identity. The identity shift is the bridge from effortful to effortless behavior.

## Principle 3: Environment is the invisible hand

Motivation is overrated. Environment is underrated. You don't need to be more motivated — you need to design a space where good habits are easy and bad habits are hard.

> **Case: The hospital cafeteria** (Chapter 6): When a hospital rearranged the cafeteria to make bottled water more accessible than soda, water sales went up 25% and soda sales dropped. Nobody made a conscious decision to drink more water — the environment did the work.

## Principle 4: Friction is the enemy of consistency

Make good habits easy. Make bad habits hard. The most reliable way to build a habit is to reduce the friction between you and doing it.

> **Case: The 2-Minute Rule** (Chapter 13): "Read before bed" becomes "Read one page." "Run three miles" becomes "Tie your running shoes." The goal is to master the habit of showing up, then improve the performance.

## Principle 5: Never miss twice

The first mistake is never the one that ruins you. It's the spiral of repeated mistakes. Missing one workout is fine. Missing two starts a new habit.

> **Case: The gym experiment** (Chapter 4): "The cardinal rule of behavior change is this: what is rewarded is repeated. What is punished is avoided." The key is to never let a slip turn into a slide.

## Principle 6: Success is design, not willpower

The most disciplined people spend less time in situations that test their discipline. They design their lives so that the right thing is easy to do.

> **Case: The disciplined vs the designed** (Chapter 13): A study of people who exercised regularly found they weren't more motivated — they simply made exercise more convenient. They lived closer to the gym, laid out their clothes the night before, and went before their energy was depleted by other decisions.
