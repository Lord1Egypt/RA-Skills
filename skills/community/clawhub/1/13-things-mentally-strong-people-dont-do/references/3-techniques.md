> Source: 13 Things Mentally Strong People Don't Do by Amy Morin, Chapters 5-6, 8, 12-13

# Techniques: Building Mental Strength Day by Day

## The Mental Strength Audit

A self-assessment to identify which of the 13 things you are currently doing:

1. List all 13 things on a piece of paper
2. Next to each, rate yourself 1-10 (1 = never do this, 10 = do this constantly)
3. Pick your top 3 highest scores — these are your priority areas
4. For each, identify one exchange behavior you can practice today

## The Gratitude Exchange (For Self-Pity)

When you feel self-pity creeping in:

1. Stop the thought: "I am feeling sorry for myself right now. That is a choice."
2. Name three things you are grateful for, no matter how small
3. Take one action that moves you forward, even if it's tiny

> **Case: The gratitude journal** (Chapter 1): Morin recommends writing down three things you are grateful for every day. Not because life is perfect, but because gratitude is the direct antidote to self-pity. "On days when I felt like the world was against me, writing down three small graces shifted my entire perspective."

## The Power Audit (For Giving Away Power)

When you catch yourself saying "He made me feel..." or "She made me angry...":

1. Recognize: you have given away your power
2. Reframe: "I chose to feel angry when he said that. I could choose differently."
3. Practice a 30-second pause between stimulus and response — choose your response

> **Case: The reactive employee** (Chapter 2): A client who constantly felt attacked by his boss learned to pause before reacting. Instead of immediately responding defensively, he would say: "Let me think about that for a moment." The 10-second pause gave him time to choose a productive response instead of a reactive one.

## The "Can I Control This?" Filter (For Wasting Energy)

Before spending emotional energy on anything, ask three questions:

1. Can I control this? → If yes, act. If no, stop investing energy.
2. Can I influence this? → If yes, act within your influence. If no, let it go.
3. Is this worth my emotional energy? → If it won't matter in a year, it's not worth stressing over now.

## The Risk Calculator (For Fear of Taking Risks)

Before deciding to take (or avoid) a risk:

1. What is the worst that could happen? Be specific.
2. What is the best that could happen? Be specific.
3. What is the most likely outcome?
4. Can I survive the worst case? If yes, take the risk.
5. What would I tell a friend to do in this situation?

> **Case: The reluctant job seeker** (Chapter 6): A client was afraid to apply for a new job because she feared rejection. She ran the risk calculator: worst case = she doesn't get an interview (no change). Best case = she gets a better job. Most likely = she gets an interview, learns something, and grows. She applied. She got the job.

## The Exchange Strategy

For every bad habit, identify a healthy exchange:

| Bad Habit (Don't) | Exchange (Do) |
|---|---|
| Feel sorry for yourself | Practice gratitude |
| Give away your power | Take a 30-second pause |
| Dwell on the past | Schedule 10 min of reflection, then take action |
| Fear taking risks | Run the risk calculator |
| Resent others' success | Write down what you can learn from them |

## The One-Week Challenge

For any of the 13 things you want to eliminate:

1. Pick one. Just one. Don't try to fix all 13 at once.
2. Practice the exchange behavior every day for one week.
3. Track your progress with a simple checklist.
4. At the end of the week, reflect: How much emotional energy did I save?
5. Add the next thing in week two.
