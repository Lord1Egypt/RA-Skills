# Pricing Test Patterns

A catalog of common pricing and offer-structure tests. For each: what it tests, when to use it, and what to watch for. These are tools, not guarantees — every one should be validated on **profit per visitor**, not assumed to work because it's well known.

## 1. Charm / odd pricing (.99 / .95)

- **What it tests:** Whether a price ending in .99 or .95 (e.g., $39.99 vs $40.00) converts or earns better than a round number.
- **When to use:** Value-positioned or impulse products; low-to-mid price points where the left-most digit changes ($40 → $39.99 reads as "thirties").
- **Watch for:** Premium/luxury positioning often does *better* with round prices ($40, $200) because charm pricing can signal "cheap/discount." The effect is usually small — make sure your test is powered to detect it, and judge on profit/visitor, not vibes.

## 2. Price thresholds & psychological barriers

- **What it tests:** Whether crossing a "round" barrier ($49 → $50, $99 → $100) causes a disproportionate conversion drop, and where the cliff actually is.
- **When to use:** When considering a price increase that would cross a salient number, or to find headroom just under one.
- **Watch for:** Don't assume the barrier exists — test it. Sometimes demand is smooth and you're leaving margin on the table by hugging $99. Test a few points around the suspected barrier ($94.99, $99.99, $104.99) rather than just two.

## 3. Anchor pricing

- **What it tests:** Whether showing a higher reference price (original/list, or a premium variant) next to the target price lifts perceived value and conversion.
- **When to use:** Genuine sale prices, or catalogs where a higher tier can legitimately anchor a mid tier.
- **Watch for:** Anchors must be truthful — fake "was" prices are deceptive and illegal in many jurisdictions. Over-anchoring can also cheapen the brand. Measure whether the anchor lifts *profit*, not just clicks.

## 4. Decoy pricing

- **What it tests:** Whether adding a deliberately less-attractive option makes a target option look like the obvious choice (classic: a near-useless middle tier that pushes buyers to the top tier).
- **When to use:** Three-tier menus or bundle ladders where you want to steer choice toward a specific, more profitable option.
- **Watch for:** Decoys can backfire by adding choice overload or looking manipulative. Keep the lineup honest and simple; verify the decoy actually shifts mix toward the high-profit option and lifts profit/visitor overall.

## 5. Bundle / quantity discounts

- **What it tests:** Whether 2-packs, 3-packs, or "buy 2 get 10% off" raise AOV and profit/visitor versus selling singles.
- **When to use:** Consumables and repeat-use products where buying ahead is natural; products with healthy unit margin and shippable in one parcel.
- **Watch for:** Bigger bundles usually lower CR while raising AOV — net effect on profit/visitor is the question, so compute it explicitly. Watch refund rate (remorse on larger purchases), inventory tie-up, and whether bundles cannibalize repeat orders you'd have gotten anyway.

## 6. Free-shipping thresholds

- **What it tests:** Whether a "free shipping over $X" threshold lifts AOV (and profit/visitor) versus charging shipping or offering free shipping unconditionally.
- **When to use:** When average order is below a level where free shipping is affordable and a nudge to add one more item is plausible.
- **Watch for:** Set the threshold so the extra item's margin covers the shipping you give away — a too-low threshold just gives away shipping; too-high suppresses orders. Measure profit/visitor including shipping cost, and watch for higher returns on "filler" items added to hit the threshold.

## 7. Tiered good / better / best

- **What it tests:** Whether a three-tier lineup increases overall profit/visitor versus a single offer, and which tier should be the visual default.
- **When to use:** Products with natural variants (size, quantity, feature level) or services with plan levels.
- **Watch for:** More tiers = more choice friction; three is usually the sweet spot. The default/highlighted tier strongly steers choice, so test which one to emphasize. Judge on blended profit/visitor across the whole lineup, not the conversion of any single tier.

## 8. Intro vs full price

- **What it tests:** Whether a lower introductory price (first order or first subscription period) wins on lifetime value, or just buys low-quality, churn-prone first orders.
- **When to use:** Subscriptions and repeat-purchase models where the first order is the hard part.
- **Watch for:** First-order CR will look great; the real metric is **profit over the customer's lifetime / second-order or renewal rate**, which needs a longer measurement window. Discounting can attract deal-seekers who churn or refund. Don't call the winner on day-one numbers.

## 9. Shipping-included vs added at checkout

- **What it tests:** Whether folding shipping into the price ($44.99 shipped) beats a lower price plus shipping ($39.99 + $5) at the same total.
- **When to use:** Wherever shipping is currently revealed late and may cause cart abandonment.
- **Watch for:** "Free shipping" framing often reduces checkout-stage abandonment even at an identical total, but the higher sticker can dent PDP-stage CR — so measure end-to-end profit/visitor, not just one stage. Keep the total honest; surprise fees at the last step are the worst of both worlds.

---

**General cautions for all patterns**

- Test one pattern at a time so the result is interpretable.
- Effects are often small and context-dependent; what worked for another brand may not work for yours — that's why you test.
- Always evaluate on profit per visitor with guardrails (refund/chargeback rate), never on conversion rate alone.
- Keep every offer and every claimed discount truthful and compliant.
