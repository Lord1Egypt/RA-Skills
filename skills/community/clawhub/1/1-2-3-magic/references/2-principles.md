## Key Principles

### 1. No Talking, No Emotion
The two biggest discipline mistakes are talking too much and getting emotional. During a count, you are a machine. "That's 1." Pause. "That's 2." Pause. "That's 3. Take a break." No more words. No anger. Boring is powerful.

### 2. The Count Is a Teaching Tool, Not a Punishment
Counting gives the child information: "This behavior is not acceptable. You have a chance to stop." It teaches self-regulation. Children learn to catch themselves before reaching 3. That's the goal — not the timeout, but the self-correction.

### 3. The Little Adult Assumption Is Dangerous
Parents often assume their children think like small adults — that explaining, reasoning, and logic should work. But children's brains process differently. A 5-year-old cannot "understand why whining doesn't work" through explanation. They learn through consistent consequences.

### 4. Stop ≠ Start
Stop behaviors (whining, fighting, arguing) = counting. Start behaviors (homework, chores, bed) = motivation strategies. Trying to count a child into starting homework is like trying to honk a car into moving faster — wrong tool for the job.

### 5. One Count per Behavior
If a child fights AND whines, that's two counts. Not one combined supercount. Each incident gets its own count. This prevents "I only had 2, why am I getting punished?"

### 6. Consistency Over Perfection
You don't have to be perfect. You just have to be more consistent than you were before. Even 60% consistency with 1-2-3 Magic will show noticeable improvement within 2 weeks.

### 7. Children Get Worse Before They Get Better
About half of children become "immediate testers" when you start 1-2-3 Magic. They escalate to test if you really mean it. This lasts about 7-10 days. If you hold the line, they settle down. The testing phase is a sign the program is working.
