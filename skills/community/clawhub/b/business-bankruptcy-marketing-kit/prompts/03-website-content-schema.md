**H1:** Chapter 11 Business Reorganization in Nevada  
**H2:** Is Your Business a Subchapter V Candidate?

Chapter 11 bankruptcy allows businesses to restructure debt while continuing operations. Nevada businesses filing Chapter 11 should begin with a critical threshold question: does the company qualify for Subchapter V — the streamlined reorganization path created by the Small Business Reorganization Act of 2019 (11 USC §§ 1181–1195)?

**Subchapter V eligibility:** Non-contingent, liquidated debts of $7.5 million or less at the time of filing. [Note: This threshold is subject to change — verify current figure with U.S. Trustee Program before publishing.]

**If you qualify for Subchapter V:**
- No disclosure statement required — eliminates $20,000–$80,000 in preparation and approval costs
- No creditors' committee — eliminates committee counsel fees paid by the estate
- Plan filed only by debtor — no competing plans from creditors
- 90-day deadline to file plan (§ 1189(b))
- Modified absolute priority rule (§ 1191): equity can retain interests if plan funded from projected disposable income
- Standing trustee oversees the case but does not operate the business

**If Subchapter V doesn't apply (standard Chapter 11):**
- Disclosure statement required — court approval process, significant legal cost
- Creditors' committee may be appointed (§ 1102)
- Plan exclusivity period: 120 days (§ 1121(b)), extendable
- Absolute priority rule in full force: equity retains nothing if any dissenting unsecured class remains unpaid
- New value exception: equity may contribute capital to retain interest, but capital contribution must equal or exceed retained value

**H2:** Day 1 of Your Chapter 11: Cash Collateral

When you file Chapter 11, you cannot use cash, bank accounts, or accounts receivable that are subject to a security interest without court authorization or secured creditor consent (§ 363(c)(2)). This means you cannot make payroll, pay rent, or fund operations from secured-collateral revenue without a court order.

We file a cash collateral motion before your filing date. The order is in place before you open for business on Day 1.

**H2:** Preference Analysis Before You File

Payments made to creditors within 90 days of filing (1 year for insiders) are subject to clawback by the bankruptcy trustee (§ 547). We perform a preference analysis as the first step in every engagement — identifying exposure and, where possible, structuring pre-filing payments to support the ordinary course defense.

**H2:** Lease and Contract Strategy Under § 365

Chapter 11 gives you the right to assume (keep) or reject (walk away from) leases and executory contracts. For commercial leases: 120-day deadline to assume or reject (§ 365(d)(4)), extendable once to 210 days. Missing the deadline = deemed rejected by operation of law. Assumption requires cure of all monetary defaults and adequate assurance of future performance.

## Practice Area Page 2 — Subchapter V Small Business Reorganization

**Title tag:** Subchapter V Small Business Bankruptcy Attorney Nevada | [FIRM]  
**Meta description:** Subchapter V: Nevada small business reorganization without disclosure statements or creditors' committees. Qualify under $7.5M debt. Free consultation. This is an advertisement.

**H1:** Subchapter V — Nevada's Small Business Reorganization Path  
**H2:** Why Subchapter V Exists

In 2019, Congress recognized that standard Chapter 11 was too expensive and complex for small businesses. The Small Business Reorganization Act created Subchapter V (11 USC §§ 1181–1195) — a dedicated, streamlined Chapter 11 path designed to make reorganization accessible for businesses with manageable debt loads.

**H2:** Subchapter V vs. Standard Chapter 11

| Feature | Subchapter V | Standard Chapter 11 |
|---|---|---|
| Eligibility | ≤ $7.5M non-contingent liquidated debt | Any amount |
| Disclosure statement | NOT required | Required (expensive) |
| Creditors' committee | NOT appointed | May be appointed (fees paid by estate) |
| Who can file a plan | Debtor only | Any party after exclusivity expires |
| Plan deadline | 90 days from petition | None (exclusivity period) |
| Absolute priority rule | Modified (§ 1191) | Full force |
| Trustee | Standing trustee (oversees) | No trustee if DIP |
| Typical cost | 40–60% less than standard Ch. 11 | Full cost |

**H2:** The Modified Absolute Priority Rule

In standard Chapter 11, the absolute priority rule requires that equity retain nothing if any class of unsecured creditors is impaired and doesn't consent — meaning the business owner keeps ownership only if all unsecured creditors are paid in full.

Subchapter V changes this: under § 1191, a nonconsensual plan can be confirmed if it is "fair and equitable" and provides that "all of the debtor's projected disposable income" over the plan period is committed to plan payments. This means the business owner can retain equity even if unsecured creditors receive less than full payment — as long as all projected disposable income is contributed.

**H2:** Nevada Entity Prerequisites for Subchapter V

Before filing, your business entity must be in good standing with the Nevada Secretary of State. Revoked articles under NRS 78.175 (corporations) or NRS 86.263 (LLCs) can result in a motion to dismiss from the U.S. Trustee. We verify and restore entity good standing before filing.

**H1:** Chapter 7 Business Liquidation in Nevada

**H2:** What Chapter 7 Does for a Business

Unlike personal Chapter 7, business Chapter 7 does not result in a "discharge" — the business entity ceases to exist. The bankruptcy trustee liquidates all business assets, pays creditors in the statutory priority order, and closes the case. The business entity is administratively dissolved.

**Chapter 7 vs. alternatives:**
- **Assignment for Benefit of Creditors (ABC):** Nevada out-of-court alternative — assignee liquidates assets for creditor benefit. Faster and cheaper than Chapter 7 but limited tools
- **State court receivership:** NRS 32.010 — court-supervised liquidation, useful for real property-heavy estates
- **Chapter 7:** Federal bankruptcy, broader avoidance powers, more creditor protections, § 341 meeting requirement

**H2:** The Personal Liability Problem

Business Chapter 7 protects the entity — not the individual. Personal guarantees, trust fund recovery penalties (§ 6672 — unpaid payroll taxes), and alter ego claims follow the officers and owners. We analyze personal exposure in every business Chapter 7 engagement — so you enter with a complete picture.

**H2:** Pre-Filing Preference and Fraudulent Transfer Audit

The Chapter 7 trustee will investigate the last 12 months of transactions for preference exposure (§ 547) and the last 4 years for fraudulent transfers under Nevada UFTA (NRS 112.180, extended via § 544(b)). Officers who received payments or asset transfers may be personally named in avoidance actions. We audit pre-filing transactions before the trustee does.

**Meta description:** Nevada bankruptcy creditor representation: proof of claim, plan objection, preference defense, automatic stay relief, adequate protection. This is an advertisement.

**H1:** Creditor Representation in Nevada Bankruptcy Cases

**H2:** Secured Creditor Rights

Secured creditors retain their liens through bankruptcy. The automatic stay prohibits enforcement without court relief (§ 362(d) motion for relief from stay). Grounds for relief: lack of equity in collateral, debtor cannot provide adequate protection. Adequate protection options: replacement liens, cash payments, equity cushion.

In Chapter 11 cram-down: secured creditor can be crammed down to collateral value if collateral is less than debt, with interest rate set at Till formula rate. Secured creditor can credit bid (§ 363(k)) in a § 363 sale to protect collateral value.

**H2:** Unsecured Creditor Committee Participation

In standard Chapter 11, the U.S. Trustee appoints an Official Committee of Unsecured Creditors (§ 1102). Committee has rights to: investigate debtor's affairs, retain professionals (paid by estate), object to plan, and negotiate plan terms. We represent creditors seeking appointment to the committee and advise on committee strategy.

**H2:** Preference Defense

If you've been sued by a trustee or debtor in possession for a preference (§ 547), defenses are available: ordinary course of business, contemporaneous exchange for new value, new value given after the payment, enabling loan. We assess your defense posture and respond fast.

## Practice Area Page 5 — Preference Defense

**Title tag:** Bankruptcy Preference Defense Attorney Nevada | [FIRM]  
**Meta description:** Trustee clawback lawsuit? § 547 preference defense Nevada: ordinary course, new value, contemporaneous exchange. Free initial assessment. This is an advertisement.

**H1:** Preference Defense in Nevada Bankruptcy Cases

**H2:** What a Preference Lawsuit Is

When a debtor files bankruptcy, the trustee or debtor in possession has the power to recover payments made to creditors in the 90 days before filing — or 1 year for insiders. This is called a preference action (§ 547). If you received a demand letter or adversary proceeding complaint from a bankruptcy trustee, you are facing a preference action.

**H2:** Preference Thresholds

For business debts: aggregate payments of $6,825 or more within the 90-day lookback period. For consumer debts: $600. [Current figures — verify with U.S. Trustee for current inflation adjustment.] Payments below the threshold cannot be avoided as preferences.

**H2:** Defenses (§ 547(c))**

1. **Ordinary course of business** — payment was made according to ordinary business terms or in the ordinary course of dealing between the parties
2. **Contemporaneous exchange for new value** — you provided goods or services at the same time as the payment
3. **New value** — after receiving the payment, you provided additional goods, services, or credit that reduced the estate's net preference exposure
4. **Enabling loan** — payment secured by the specific collateral being financed by the loan
5. **Domestic support obligation** — payment was a domestic support obligation (§ 101(14A))
6. **Below threshold** — aggregate payments below $6,825 (business) cannot be avoided

**H2:** Strategic Response

We request the full preference analysis from the trustee, evaluate each payment against available defenses, and typically negotiate a settlement below the gross preference amount. If the case proceeds, we litigate in the adversary proceeding in the bankruptcy court.

## 15-Question FAQ

**Q1: What's the difference between Subchapter V and standard Chapter 11?**
Subchapter V (11 USC §§ 1181–1195, Small Business Reorganization Act 2019) is available to businesses with ≤ $7.5M in non-contingent, liquidated debt. Key differences: no disclosure statement required, no creditors' committee, debtor-only plan, 90-day plan filing deadline, and a modified absolute priority rule that allows equity retention funded by projected disposable income. Standard Chapter 11 requires a disclosure statement, allows creditor committees and competing plans, and applies the full absolute priority rule.

**Q2: Can my business stay open during Chapter 11?**
Yes, but Day 1 requires a cash collateral order. Under § 363(c)(2), you cannot use cash or receivables subject to a security interest without court authorization or secured creditor consent. We file a cash collateral motion before your filing date so authorization is in place before you reopen post-filing.

**Q3: What is a preference and how could it affect our filing?**
A preference (§ 547) is a payment made to a creditor within 90 days before filing (1 year for insiders) that can be clawed back by the trustee. Defenses exist: ordinary course of business, new value, contemporaneous exchange. We perform a preference analysis before you file to identify and, where possible, mitigate exposure.

**Q4: What happens to our office lease if we file bankruptcy?**
Under § 365, you have the right to assume (keep) or reject (walk away from) the lease. For commercial leases, you must make this decision within 120 days of filing (extendable once to 210 days by court order). Assumption requires curing all past-due rent. Rejection terminates the lease, and the landlord's damages claim is capped under § 502(b)(6).

**Q5: Can we keep our equity in the business after Chapter 11?**
In Subchapter V: yes, if the plan is funded from projected disposable income (§ 1191 modified absolute priority rule). In standard Chapter 11: only if all dissenting unsecured creditors are paid in full, or equity contributes new capital equal to the retained value (new value exception to absolute priority rule).

**Q6: What is cram-down and how does it work?**
Cram-down (§ 1129(b)) allows Chapter 11 plan confirmation over a rejecting class of creditors if the plan is "fair and equitable" and doesn't unfairly discriminate. For secured creditors: retain lien plus deferred payments at market interest rate (Till formula). For unsecured creditors in standard Chapter 11: absolute priority rule applies.

**Q7: What are first-day motions and why do they matter?**
First-day motions are emergency requests filed simultaneously with or immediately after the bankruptcy petition. Critical first-day motions include: cash collateral authorization (§ 363), DIP financing approval (§ 364), wage and benefit continuation, critical vendor payments, and customer program preservation. Without a cash collateral order, you cannot fund operations from secured-collateral revenue.

**Q8: Can we get new financing after filing bankruptcy?**
Yes, through § 364. Unsecured credit in the ordinary course requires no court order (§ 364(a)). New secured or superpriority financing requires court approval (§§ 364(c)-(d)). Priming liens — new financing that jumps ahead of existing secured creditors — require court approval plus adequate protection for existing secured creditors.

**Q9: Is our Nevada LLC or corporation in good standing?**
Check nvsilverflume.gov before filing. Revoked articles (NRS 78.175 for corporations, NRS 86.263 for LLCs) can result in a U.S. Trustee motion to dismiss the bankruptcy case. We verify entity standing and restore it before filing if necessary.

**Q10: Does filing protect us from personal liability?**
Business bankruptcy protects the entity, not individuals. Personal guarantees survive the business filing. Trust fund recovery penalties (§ 6672 — unpaid payroll taxes) follow responsible officers personally. Alter ego claims under NRS 78.747 / NRS 86.401 survive if corporate formalities were not observed. We map personal exposure in every business engagement.

**Q11: Can the trustee challenge transfers my business made before filing?**
Yes. Preference actions (§ 547) cover 90 days before filing (1 year for insiders). Fraudulent transfer actions under § 548 cover 2 years. Nevada UFTA (NRS 112.180) via § 544(b) extends to 4 years. Pre-filing asset transfers to related parties, family members, or friendly creditors are high-risk. We audit before filing.

**Q12: What is an automatic stay and what are its exceptions?**
The automatic stay (§ 362(a)) halts most collection actions immediately upon filing. Exceptions under § 362(b) include: governmental police/regulatory power, criminal proceedings, domestic support collection, IRS tax audits and assessments, and certain securities proceedings. The stay does not stop every creditor action — we assess your specific creditor mix.

**Q13: What does the Subchapter V trustee do?**
The Subchapter V trustee (§ 1183) is a standing trustee appointed by the U.S. Trustee Program. The trustee reviews the debtor's financial affairs, facilitates plan negotiations, and may object to plan confirmation. Unlike a Chapter 7 trustee, the Subchapter V trustee does not take control of the business — the debtor remains in possession.

**Q14: Can we reject a software license, vendor contract, or franchise agreement?**
Generally yes, under § 365. However: software licensors have specific protections under § 365(n) — the debtor can reject a software license, but the licensee may elect to retain its rights. Franchise agreements with anti-assignment clauses may restrict assignment under § 365(c)(1). Rejection terminates the contract as of the petition date; damages are treated as pre-petition unsecured claims.

**Q15: What is the absolute priority rule and how does it affect my reorganization?**
In standard Chapter 11, the absolute priority rule requires that equity receive or retain nothing unless all senior classes (including unsecured creditors) are paid in full or consent. Subchapter V modifies this: equity can retain interests if the plan is funded by all projected disposable income, regardless of unsecured creditor impairment. This is one of the most important reasons to determine Subchapter V eligibility at the outset.
