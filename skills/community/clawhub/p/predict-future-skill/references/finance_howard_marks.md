# Howard Marks — Finance Forecasting

Oaktree Capital co-founder; credit investor. Primary sources: *The Most Important
Thing* (2011); memos *It's Not Easy* (2015), *You Can't Predict. You Can
Prepare.* (2001), *The Realist's Creed* (2002), *The Illusion of Knowledge*
(2020).

**Role in the finance stack:** *How* to think — consensus, cycles, forecast
skepticism.

---

## Second-level thinking

**First-level:** react to surface facts ("good product → buy the stock";
"rates up → sell bonds"). Seductively easy, often wrong because price already
reflects the obvious.

**Second-level:** map outcome ranges, your probability, **consensus beliefs**,
and **what is priced in**. Being right is necessary but not sufficient — you
must be **more right than others**, which requires thinking differently
(*It's Not Easy*, 2015).

Marks's buying checklist:
- Range of likely future outcomes — which do I expect?
- Probability I'm right?
- What does consensus think? How does my view differ?
- How is the asset priced given consensus vs. my view?

**Keynes's beauty contest** (via Marks): naive players pick the prettiest face;
winners pick whom *others* will vote for. Markets reward anticipating the
anticipation of others — not your isolated view of fundamentals.

Classic contrast (*The Most Important Thing*):
- First-level: "It's a good company; buy."
- Second-level: "It's good, but everyone thinks it's great — overpriced; sell."

## The Realist's Creed on macro forecasts

Three beliefs (synthesized across *Realist's Creed*, *Illusion of Knowledge*):

1. **Prices embed consensus** — even a correct consensus view rarely beats the
   market, because it's already reflected.
2. **Most macro forecasts extrapolate** the recent past — correct when nothing
   breaks, unprofitable because markets anticipate it.
3. **Big moves come from surprises** — "everything important in financial
   history has taken place outside two standard deviations" (Ric Kayne, via
   Marks). Surprises are hard to see by definition.

Implication for this skill: your forecast must state **what differs from
consensus/extrapolation** and **why that difference might be right** — with
granular probability, not pundit adjectives.

Marks dismisses most economic forecasts as low value; he still uses **scenario
thinking** and cycle awareness.

## Cycles, pendulum & credit

**Can't predict — can prepare:** knowing **where you are in a cycle** is not the
same as predicting the timing, size, or shape of the next turn (*You Can't
Predict*, 2001).

**Pendulum** (1991 memo, reprised often): markets swing between extremes —
greed/fear, optimism/pessimism, risk tolerance/aversion, credulity/skepticism.
The pendulum spends little time at the midpoint; near an extreme, reversal
energy builds. Near **euphoria**: trim risk (high prices, low prospective
return). Near **panic**: add risk.

**Credit cycle** (Marks's emphasis): small economic fluctuations produce **large**
swings in credit availability → amplified asset prices → feedback to the
economy. Prosperity → easy credit → risk-taking → distress → repeat.

**Cycle rules:**
- Cycles are **inevitable** — "this time is different" usually isn't.
- Cycles are **self-correcting** — success seeds failure, failure seeds success.
- **Risk** (Marks): not volatility but probability of **permanent loss**; greatest
  risk when investors believe there is none.

## How to apply in this skill

Layer on `general_Philip_Tetlock.md` for finance queries.

1. **Consensus & pricing** — What is the extrapolation baseline? What must be
   true for your view to beat it?
2. **Pendulum / cycle** — Euphoria or fear dominant? Credit easy or tight?
3. **Second-level checklist** — Run all four questions before final probabilities.
4. **Forecast humility** — Prefer scenario bands; label surprises explicitly.
5. **No unscored gurus** — Sell-side targets are not base rates without
   track-record evidence.

**Search targets:** consensus EPS/revenue, Fed funds futures, credit spreads,
high-yield OAS, AAII sentiment, fund flows, VIX, loan-officer surveys.
