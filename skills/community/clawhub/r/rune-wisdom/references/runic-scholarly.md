## 1. Speech Act Theory: How Runes *Did Things*

*Source: Christiane Zimmermann, "'How to Do Things with Runes': Illocutionary Forces and Communicative Purposes behind the Runic Inscriptions in the Older Fuþark," Futhark 1 (2010), pp. 85–107.*

Zimmermann applies Austin and Searle's speech act theory to older fuþark inscriptions, demonstrating that runic texts should be classified by their **illocutionary force** — what the writer *does* by writing, not merely what the text *says*. This framework reveals that runes were not primarily informational but **performative**: the inscription enacted something in the world.

### 1.1 Five Categories of Runic Speech Acts

Searle's classification, applied to the runic corpus:

| Category | Function | Runic Examples | Mechanism |
|----------|----------|---------------|-----------|
| **Representatives** | Assert, report, state | *niþijo tawide* "Niþijo made [this]" | Carver commits to truth of a claim |
| **Directives** | Request, command, forbid | Tørvika stone B: "Clear off from here!" | Carver attempts to make reader act |
| **Commissives** | Promise, threaten, prophesy | Björketorp: *uþArAbAsbA* "harm-prophecy" | Carver commits to a future course |
| **Expressives** | Wish, greet, name, lament | Women's fibulae: naming a beloved | Carver expresses a psychological state |
| **Declarations** | Appoint, name, dedicate | Lindholmen: *ek erilaz... haitēka* "I the eril am called..." | Carver creates a new state of affairs by naming |

### 1.2 The Commissive: Curses as Performative Prophecy

The Björketorp stone's *uþArAbAsbA* — interpreted as "harm-prophecy" — is a **commissive speech act**: it does not merely predict harm but *creates the threat by being written*. The curse is not a warning about what will happen; it is the mechanism by which harm is set in motion. This distinction is crucial for understanding how curse-inscriptions worked: the writing itself was the magical act, not a description of a magical effect.

### 1.3 The Declaration: Naming as World-Making

The *erilaz*-formula inscriptions (5th–6th centuries) use *haitan* "to be called/named" as a **declarative speech act**: by inscribing "I the eril am called X," the carver does not report a pre-existing name but *performs* an identity into being. The name comes into force through the act of carving. The *erilaz* inscriptions are confined to the 5th–6th centuries — they represent a specific, time-limited tradition of runic self-declaration, not the universal model of "rune-master" identity.

### 1.4 Writing as Preservation of Utterance

"Writing down an utterance might also suggest itself when the utterance comprises a communicative act which is somehow connected with a future situation, e.g., commissive acts such as promises and threats." (Zimmermann, p. 91)

This explains why curse stones and amulets were *written*: the writing extends the performative force of the utterance into the future. A spoken curse expires with the breath; an inscribed curse persists. The medium is part of the message — **the choice to write something in runes is itself a communicative act**.

### 1.5 The Object Is Part of the Meaning

"Utterances are often written down on meaningful objects, objects which have a specific place and function in their respective material culture." (Zimmermann, p. 92)

A rune inscribed on a weapon means differently than the same rune inscribed on a woman's fibula. Women's fibulae inscriptions were **hidden, personal, expressive** — naming a man's name on a woman's personal object, with no public recipient. Weapon inscriptions were **visible, representative** — declaring maker or owner to anyone who saw them. The distinction between **concealed/expressive** and **visible/representative** rune-use maps onto the distinction between private magic and public statement.

### 1.6 Multiple Runic Cultures, Not One

"It would seem much more reasonable to reckon with a set of differing 'cultures' which might be characterised by different text-types showing a particular distribution in time, space and context." (Zimmermann, p. 87)

There was no single "runic magic." Different regions, periods, and social contexts produced different communicative traditions. The older fuþark inscriptions of 200–600 AD, the Viking Age runestones, and the medieval amulets represent three distinct cultures of runic use, each with its own text-types and illocutionary norms.

## 2. Named Categories of Functional Runes

*Source: Rikke Steenholt Olesen, "Runic Amulets from Medieval Denmark," Futhark 1 (2010), pp. 161–176.*

Olesen's survey of 48 metal amulets with runic inscriptions from medieval Denmark (11th–15th c.) attests **named categories of functional runes** — specific types of runes classified by their purpose. These categories are not modern scholarly inventions but **indigenous classifications** preserved in the inscriptional record and in Eddic poetry.

### 2.1 Bjargrúnar — Help-Runes

**bjargrúnar** 'help-runes' — runes of rescue, deliverance, and aid. Attested on the Østermarie silver amulet and in Sigrdrífumál (the lay where Sigrdrífa instructs Sigurðr in runic knowledge). The Bergen inscription B 257 pairs them: *Ríst ek bótrúnar, ríst ek bjargrúnar* — "I cut runes of help and recovery, I cut help-runes."

The term *bjarg* carries the sense of a rock, a cliff — something solid and immovable that provides refuge. Bjargrúnar are not gentle healing but **structural rescue**: they are called upon when the situation has become untenable and requires a firm hand to pull one from danger.

### 2.2 Bótrúnar — Runes of Recovery

**bótrúnar** 'runes of help and recovery, remediation-runes.' Paired with bjargrúnar in the Bergen formula. The term *bót* means both 'remedy, cure' and 'compensation, atonement, redress' — the legal and the medical are one. Bótrúnar restore balance, whether that balance is health, justice, or cosmic order.

### 2.3 Lyfrúnar — Healing Runes

**lyfrúnar** 'healing runes' — from ON *lyf* 'charm, magic remedy' and *lyfja* 'to heal, cure.' Attested on the Skänninge copper amulet and the Sigtuna amulet (*Njót lyfja* "Make good use of the healing charms"). The Ribe healing-stick contains *ok lyf-tungu at lyfja* — "and a healing tongue to cure" — combining the written rune-formula with the spoken incantation, the two media working together.

### 2.4 The Performative Act of Writing Was the Magic

"The process of writing itself, perhaps together with an oral realisation, was presumably what made the magic effective." (Olesen, p. 174)

Amulet users often could not read the inscriptions — the **act of inscription** and the **material presence** of runes was what mattered. The writing *was* the spell, not a record of one. This has profound implications for runic practice: the physical act of creating rune-marks is itself a magical operation, regardless of whether the result is legible.

### 2.5 Concealment Amplifies Runic Power

Amulets were **folded** so the text was hidden. Some contained "nonsensical" runic sequences — the user couldn't verify the content. "The healing procedure was meant to work in an atmosphere of secrecy." (Olesen, p. 171)

This supports the principle that **concealment amplifies runic power**: runes hidden from view do not lose their efficacy — they may gain it. The secret rune is more potent than the visible one. This principle underlies the entire cryptographic tradition (Section 8 below) and the practice of inscribing runes on the *inside* of objects.

### 2.6 Runes Preferred Over Roman Letters for Amulets

"If the majority of metal amulet finds in Denmark turn out to be runic, we may perhaps conclude that runes were considered more effectual than roman letters." (Olesen, p. 172)

In medieval Denmark, where both runes and Roman script were available, **runes were chosen for magical purposes**. This is not because runes were more familiar — in many cases, the carvers knew Roman script as well — but because **runes carried inherent apotropaic power that Roman letters lacked**. The choice of runic script for an amulet was itself a magical act, selecting the medium with greater supernatural force.

### 2.7 Christian-Pagan Hybrid Practices

The Hebrew acronym **AGLA** (*Attā Gībōr Ləʿōlām ʾĂḏōnāy* "Thou art mighty forever, O Lord") appears in 7 of 10 Latin amulet inscriptions, often transposed as **agla gala laga** — a palindrome-like rearrangement that transforms the sacred abbreviation into a sound-pattern. Runic amulets are **Christian-pagan hybrids**: runes carrying Latin/Hebrew magical formulas, the old script serving as a vehicle for the new religion's sacred words, while retaining its own inherent power.

### 2.8 Physical Interaction with Amulets

Amulets were worn close to the body: pierced for suspension on a string, kept in leather purses, found in graves positioned near the body. Some had **tooth marks** from being bitten — evidence of tactile/physical interaction with the runic object. The amulet was not merely carried but *handled, touched, bitten* — the body participated in the magic through direct physical contact with the inscribed object.

## 3. Bracteate Magic: The Formulaic Vocabulary of Runic Amulets

*Source: Nancy L. Wicker and Henrik Williams, "Bracteates and Runes," Futhark 3 (2012), pp. 151–213. Review of the final volume of *Die Goldbrakteaten der Völkerwanderungszeit* (IK 3).*

Gold bracteates (5th–6th c.) are the most important class of runic amulets, with **143 distinct runic inscriptions** — nearly one-third of all older-futhark inscriptions. Their formulaic words constitute the core vocabulary of runic magical utterance.

### 3.1 The Five Key Formulaic Words

| Word | Meaning | Magical Function | Attested Context |
|------|---------|------------------|-----------------|
| **alu** | 'beer' (also interpreted as 'protection') | Ritual drinking / Apotropaic | Most common formulaic word; found on many bracteates and stones. **See §13 for the 'beer' reinterpretation.** |
| **laþu** | 'summoning, invitation' | Calls helpers in animal form | Invocatory — the depicted god summons allies |
| **laukaR** | 'leek' (healing plant) | Healing and regeneration | Óðinn's power of restoration; inscribed along animal's foreleg |
| **ōtta** (*ota*) | 'fear, horror' | Inspires terror in enemies | Possibly an Odinic name, parallel to *Yggr* 'the terrible'; Saxo describes Othinus as "the horrendous husband of Frigga" |
| **auja** | 'luck' | Confers fortune and favor | Triple invocation on IK 161 Skodborg: *auja alawinauj alawinauj alawinjalawid* |

### 3.2 The Schwindeschema — Gradual Disappearance Formula

The **Schwindeschema** (diminishing formula) is attested in abbreviations of *laukaR*:

**laukaR → lakR → lkaR → lauR → luR → lR → l**

This progressive abbreviation is not laziness but a **formal method of making words progressively more arcane** — each step removes a sound, taking the word further from legibility and closer to pure magical power. The fully abbreviated *l* is no longer a word but a **trace** — the ghost of the original formula, still potent because the rune-carver and the supernatural recipient both know what it stands for. This principle — that reducing a formula's visibility increases its magical density — parallels the concealment principle from Section 2.5.

### 3.3 Glossolalia — Ecstatic Utterances on Bracteates

Meaningless vowel-consonant sequences appear on bracteates: *iiaeiau* (IK 70 Halsskov Overdrev), *Rm!lhþ* (others). These may represent **ecstatic utterances** — sounds produced in a trance-like state, beyond normal language but carrying magical force. Düwel identifies 14 methods of making arcane inscriptions on bracteates, including **acrostics, alphabets, anagrams, palindromes**, and **glossolalia**.

The rune row itself was interpreted as containing "every sound and character of all imaginable lexical items, including the healing words" — the complete futhark is a magical totality, every possible utterance latent within it.

### 3.4 The God Speaks: Present-Tense as Divine Utterance

Düwel and Nowak's framework distinguishes:
- **Present-tense verbs** on bracteates suggest the **depicted god speaks**: *Glīaugiʀ wīu r[ū]n[ō]ʀ* — "Gleaming-eye consecrates runes" — the god shown on the bracteate is the one performing the speech act.
- **Preterite verbs** indicate a **human runemaster**: *Wurtē rūnōʀ* — "wrought runes" — the carver declares his completed act.

Key example of divine speech: IK 189 Trollhättan — *Tawō laþōdu* — "I prepare an invitation" — interpreted as the depicted god making an invocation, "bracteate magic instituted by the god himself."

### 3.5 No Divine Names — The Taboo on Naming

Not a single name of any Scandinavian god appears on bracteates. All proposed Odinic names (*Fākaʀ, Glīaugiʀ, Hariūha, Alawiniʀ*) are **bynames derived from qualities associated with Óðinn**, requiring the assumption that divine names were **taboo** — too powerful to inscribe, substituted with descriptive epithets. This taboo parallels the Indo-European practice of avoiding the "true name" of a deity (comparable to the Hebrew prohibition on pronouncing the Tetragrammaton).

### 3.6 Bracteate Themes: Healing, Regeneration, Protection

Karl Hauck's thesis (critically evaluated by Wicker) holds that bracteates depict **Óðinn's healing of Baldr's foal** — the ritual preserved in the **Second Merseburg Charm**: *Phol ende Uuodan uuorum zi holza* "Phol and Wodan rode to the wood" — where Wodan blows healing breath into the horse's ear and recites words of healing. Whether or not Hauck is right about every bracteate, the core themes are consistent: **healing, regeneration, and protection from evil demonic forces**.

### 3.7 The Undley Bracteate and Warrior Bands

An alternative theory (Williams) proposes that bracteate inscriptions come from **sodalities** — warrior bands and oath-bound fellowships. The word *gaganga* 'follower, companion' on the Undley bracteate supports this: bracteates may have been **badges of allegiance**, with the runic inscriptions encoding the bond between chieftain and follower, the magical vocabulary reinforcing the social relationship.

## 4. Málrúnir — Speech-Runes as the Foundation of Runic Knowledge

*Source: Alessia Bauer, "Die späten Runica Manuscripta aus Island. Was versteht man unter málrúnir?," Futhark 1 (2010), pp. 197–223.*

### 4.1 Definition and Snorri's Authority

**Málrúnir** = 'speech-runes' or 'language-runes.' In Snorri's Háttatal:

*"Þessi [dróttkvæðr háttr] er upphaf allra hátta, sem málrúnar eru fyrir ǫðrum rúnum"* — "The dróttkvætt meter is the origin of all meters, just as the speech-runes are the origin of the other runes."

Málrúnir are the **foundational runes from which all others derive** — the "alphabet" of poetic and runic meaning. They are not one type among many but the **ground** on which all runic knowledge stands.

### 4.2 Rune-Names as Málrúnir

"Es wird zudem erklärt, dass die Runennamen málrúnir seien, die in der Kunst des Dichtens reichlich verwendet werden" — "It is also explained that the rune-names are málrúnir, which are abundantly used in the art of poetry." (Bauer, p. 207)

**Rune-names are the primary form of málrúnir.** They are the keys that unlock the poetic/kenning system. To know the rune-name is to possess the málrún — the speech-rune — that generates meaning in both divination and poetry.

### 4.3 Málrúnir vs. Galdrarúnir — Two Categories of Runic Knowledge

| Category | Meaning | Function | Form |
|----------|---------|----------|------|
| **Málrúnir** | Speech-runes, language-runes | Poetic, naming, kennings, meaning-generation | Rune-names and their periphrases |
| **Galdrarúnir** | Magic-runes, spell-runes | Incantation, sorcery, magical effect | Bind-runes evolving into abstract symbols |

Galdrarúnir evolved from bind-runes into increasingly abstract symbols that "differentiated themselves ever more strongly from the runic script" (Bauer, p. 205). They accompany *galdrar* (magic spells) and belong to the performative/magical dimension. Málrúnir, by contrast, are the **linguistic/poetic** dimension — the system of names, meanings, and semantic connections that makes runes intelligible.

For the divinatory skill: **every rune reading operates simultaneously in both registers.** The málrúnir dimension provides meaning (what the rune signifies), while the galdrarúnir dimension provides power (what the rune does). A reading that attends only to meaning neglects the performative force; a reading that attends only to power neglects the semantic depth.

### 4.4 Runes as Dangerous Knowledge

"In the 17th century on Iceland, runes were suspected of being sorcery, and engaging with this script could become dangerous. Consider e.g. Jón Rögnvaldsson, who in 1625 was sentenced to burn at the stake for sorcery, among other things involving runes." (Bauer, p. 201)

The same Björn Jónsson treatise that discusses rune theory also contains chapters on "the power and force of runes and gives instructions regarding what is permitted and what is forbidden with runes." The tradition itself recognized that runic knowledge was **morally ambivalent** — it could heal or harm, protect or endanger, and the boundary between permitted and forbidden use was a matter of genuine concern.

### 4.5 Hávamál Odin-Strophes as Runic Theory

The Odin-strophes from Hávamál (Stanza 138 ff.) — Odin's self-sacrifice on Yggdrasil to win the runes — appear in multiple Icelandic manuscripts as part of runic theoretical material. The **mythological origin of runes** was not a separate story but integral to how Icelanders understood the nature and authority of runic knowledge: the runes are not human inventions but divine revelations, won through sacrifice.

## 5. Metrical Inscriptions: Magical Content = Verse Form

*Source: Edith Marold, "Vers oder nicht Vers? Zum metrischen Charakter von Runeninschriften im älteren Futhark," Futhark 2 (2011), pp. 63–102.*

Marold demonstrates that **runic inscriptions used formal Germanic verse from as early as c. 200 AD**, and that magical/religious content was consistently composed in metrical form while narrative/prose content was not. This directly connects the runic inscription tradition to the **galdr/incantation tradition**.

### 5.1 The Björketorp Curse Is Verse

The Björketorp curse inscription forms a **stanza of two long-lines with three-stress full-lines**:

*haidʀrūnō ronu / aragiu hearamalausʀ / saʀ þat barȳtʀ / falahak hedera ginnarūnaʀ / ūti eʀ wēladaudē*

"The row of bright runes I hid, here magic runes, by perversion restless, outside may he be by a magic death, who breaks this."

This is direct archaeological evidence of **curses carved in metrical form** — the closest physical parallel to spoken galdr. The curse does not merely state harm; it **sings** harm into being.

### 5.2 The Noleby Inscription as Ritual Utterance

*rūnō fāhi raginakundō* — "A rune I write, a god-descended one"

This is a perfect long-line (Marold, p. 83), and only the "solemn introductory part" is metrical — "der feierliche Einleitungsteil" — suggesting it was a **ritual/priestly formula** that opened a sacred operation. The word *raginakundō* 'god-descended, divinely originated' declares the rune's supernatural provenance before the practical content begins.

### 5.3 Magical Content = Verse; Narrative Content = Prose

"Ist es vielleicht so, dass Teile mit mythischem, religiösem oder magischem Inhalt in Versen abgefasst wurden?" (Marold, p. 93)

On the Eggja stone, the magical/mythical sections are verse while the prose sections are not. This is a crucial observation: **the choice of verse form itself signals magical intent.** When a rune-carver shifted from prose to verse, they were shifting from the communicative register to the performative/magical register. The meter IS the magic.

### 5.4 Hypermetrical Lines as Forerunners of Ljóðaháttr

The combination of two-stress and three-stress lines in the same inscription "könnte man als Vorformen des späteren Ljóðaháttr betrachten" (Marold, p. 95). **Ljóðaháttr** is literally "incantation-meter" — the meter of magical speech in Old Norse poetry. The earliest runic inscriptions contain the metrical ancestors of the form later codified for magical utterance. The metrical DNA of galdr is present from the very beginning of runic writing.

### 5.5 The Strøm Inscription: Agricultural Charm

The Strøm inscription uses a completely different metrical system: regular alternation of stressed/unstressed syllables with equal syllable counts:

*wātē halli / hāha skaþī / hinō horna / hāþu ligī*

"Let the horn wet this stone, harm the mowing, let the mowing lie."

This may be a **magical/agricultural charm** — possibly the oldest attested example of a rune-inscribed charm with a practical (non-combat, non-funerary) purpose. Its unique rhythm distinguishes it from the alliterative tradition of warrior and curse inscriptions.

## 6. Non-Lexical Inscriptions: The Act of Carving as Ritual

*Source: Martin Hannes Graf, "Schrifttheoretische Überlegungen zu nichtlexikalischen Inschriften aus dem südgermanischen Runenkorpus," Futhark 2 (2011), pp. 103–122.*

Graf provides a theory of how runes functioned when the message was not linguistic but **performative/magical** — when what mattered was not what the runes *said* but what the act of carving *did*.

### 6.1 The Act of Carving Was the Ritual

"Epigraphische Schriftverwendung in frühen Schriftkulturen dient also vielfach nicht der einmaligen, codierten Aufzeichnung von Rede, sondern sie war in solchen Fällen Teil einer Handlung und Teil eines semiotischen Kontexts. Als zentral kann daher die Handlung selbst (das 'Schreiben') angesehen werden, nicht das Resultat." (Graf, pp. 112–113)

"In early writing cultures, epigraphic script use often served not the one-time, coded recording of speech, but was in such cases part of an action and part of a semiotic context. The action itself (the 'writing') can therefore be considered central, not the result."

**The act of carving was the ritual; the readable result was secondary.** This explains why so many runic inscriptions are "nonsensical" — they were never meant to be read. The magical efficacy lay in the *process* of inscription, not in the *product*.

### 6.2 Non-Lexical Sequences as Voces Magicae

"Vergleichbar ist ein solches Schriftverständnis mit den sogenannten voces magicae der (früh)mittelalterlichen Zaubersprüche — nur in einem anderen Medium." (Graf, p. 107)

Non-lexical runic sequences function like the **nonsense syllables in magic spells** — they suggest meaning but deliberately withhold it. The *voces magicae* of medieval charms (*abracadabra, sabaoth, etc.*) work not through semantic content but through **sound and pattern**. Non-lexical rune sequences work through **form and presence** — the visual shape of the runes, their arrangement on the object, their material facticity.

### 6.3 Futhark-Row Inscriptions: Completeness and Order

Futhark-row inscriptions "repräsentieren also 'Vollständigkeit und Ordnung'" (Graf, p. 116, citing Düwel/Heizmann 2006). They were "indexikalisches Incipit" — an indexical opening that marked what followed as specifically runic. The complete rune-row inscribed on an object declares: **all possible rune-power is present here.** It is not a learning exercise but a **totalizing magical gesture** — the futhark itself is the most powerful formula, because it contains every rune at once.

### 6.4 Runic Objects Pointed Inward

"Selbstreferenzielle Aspekte wichtig sind, indem Gegenstand, Beschriftung und bildliche Darstellungen immer wieder auf sich selbst, 'nach innen', auf das Gegenständliche weisen, selten hingegen 'nach außen' kommunizieren." (Graf, p. 108)

Runic objects pointed **inward to themselves**, not outward to a reader. The inscription on an amulet or weapon referred back to the object itself — it was a **self-referential system** that gained power from its own closure. This is the opposite of modern writing, which exists to communicate outward; runic inscription existed to **concentrate power inward**.

### 6.5 The "Stimmgabel" Sign: A Mark of Power

The "Stimmgabel" (tuning-fork) sign on the Wurmlingen lance is a parasymbolic sign that recurs across multiple Alemannic elite objects. Graf suggests it may be "als Herrschaftszeichen, Schicksalszeichen, Apotropaion o. ä. zu deuten" — a **sovereignty mark, fate-sign, or apotropaion**. This is a mark that exists in the gray zone between writing and symbol — not a rune, but a sign that carries power through its form and placement rather than through linguistic reference.

## 7. Rune-Name Semantic Evolution

*Sources: Inmaculada Senra Silva, "The Names of the u-Rune," Futhark 1 (2010), pp. 109–122; Alan Griffiths, "The Anglo-Saxon Name for the s-Rune: Sigel, a Precious Jewel," Futhark 2 (2011), pp. 123–143.*

### 7.1 The U-Rune: From Aurochs to Rain to Sparks

The semantic evolution of the u-rune demonstrates how rune-meanings develop through **homonym substitution, cultural forgetting, and metaphorical extension**:

| Stage | Name | Meaning | Mechanism |
|-------|------|---------|-----------|
| **Proto-Germanic** | *ūruz* | 'aurochs' (wild, fierce, horned beast) | Original meaning; animal known on the Continent |
| **Scandinavian shift** | *úrr* → *úr* | 'aurochs' → 'light rain/drizzle' | Homonym confusion — the animal was unknown in Scandinavia, so the identical-sounding word for 'rain' took over |
| **Norwegian Rune Poem** | *úr* | 'slag' (from heated iron) | Metaphor: sparks from the forge are a rainlike spray — *úr* as 'rain' metaphorically applied to the sparks that fall like rain from white-hot iron |
| **Icelandic Rune Poem** | *úr* | 'crying of clouds, destroyer of mown hay, shepherd's hate' | All rain-kennings — the 'rain' meaning fully established |
| **Swedish Rune Poem** | *úr* | 'rain in the west wind / stormy weather' | Confirms the meteorological meaning |

Ole Worm's 17th-century interpretation (1636/1651) is surprisingly sophisticated: *úr* indicates rivers flowing with force, cloudbursts, and **metaphorically** the sparks from white-hot iron when struck. Worm understood that the 'slag' meaning was a metaphor of the 'rain' meaning, not a separate semantic root.

**Key insight:** Rune meanings are not stable — they shift through phonological coincidence and cultural forgetting. The u-rune's journey from 'aurochs' to 'rain' to 'sparks' is a model for understanding how ALL rune-names acquired their meaning-layers. Each layer does not erase the previous one but **accumulates** alongside it, creating the polysemic depth that modern divination relies on.

### 7.2 The S-Rune: Sigel as "Seal," Not "Sun"

Griffiths argues that the OE *sigel* derives from Latin *sigillum* (seal), specifically the **Seal of the Holy Spirit at baptism** — not from a native word for "sun." The maritime imagery in the Rune Poem stanza refers to Noah's Ark and the olive branch of salvation, not to seafaring per se.

The semantic chain: Hebrew **zayin/zaith** (the s-position in the Semitic alphabet) → Jerome glosses as *oliva* (olive) → oil of chrismation → Seal of the Holy Spirit → *sigel*.

Nordic *sól* 'sun' is a **pagan re-interpretation** of the Christian *sigel*, not the original form. The Norwegian Rune Poem's *sól* stanza includes *luti ek helgum dome* "I bow to that which is holy" — acceptance of Christianity, possibly referencing Constantine's vision of the Cross in the sun's halo.

**Key insight:** Rune-names were not purely indigenous Germanic creations but were **inspired by Hebrew and Greek letter-names** introduced by Christian missionaries. The process of pagan re-interpretation — taking a Christian coinage and giving it a native meaning — is the same process that turned *sigel* 'seal' into *sól* 'sun.' This means the "traditional" meanings of some runes may be **secondary formations**, not primary.

### 7.3 The Bø Church Inscription: Proof of the 'Rain' Meaning in Norway

A love poem in the Bø church (Norway) encodes the name *Guðrún* using rune-name kennings. The u-rune is identified by two circumlocutions: *fjón svinkanda* 'workers' hate' and *heys víti* 'hay's destruction' — both rain-kennings. This proves that in medieval **Norway** the u-rune name was understood as 'rain', NOT 'slag'. The Norwegian Rune Poem's 'slag' meaning was likely a **learned metaphor** misunderstood by later readers as the primary meaning.

### 7.4 Rímur Poets and Fólgin Nöfn (Concealed Names)

Icelandic rímur poets (post-1600) used rune-name synonyms to conceal their own names in verses — *fólgin nöfn* 'concealed names.' *Úr* is replaced by synonyms like *regn* 'rain' or *úði* 'drizzle.' Jón Ólafsson's *Runologia* (1732) provides a vast catalogue of rune-name periphrases. For *úr*: 'clouds' tears, damage to dryness, shepherd's hate, thaw's storm, heaven's sweat, cause of swells.' The meaning 'slag' is **nowhere recorded** in the Icelandic tradition.

This is a living tradition of **runic cryptography in poetry** — the rune-names serve as keys to a code that encodes personal identity within verse. The poet hides himself in the runes.

## 8. Runic Cryptography: Codes, Riddles, and Concealment

*Sources: K. Jonas Nordby, "Ráð þat, If You Can!," Futhark 3 (2012), pp. 81–88; Alessia Bauer, Section on Geheimrunen in Futhark 1 (2010), pp. 197–223; James E. Knirk, "A Runic Name-Riddle in Lbs 2565 8vo," Futhark 2 (2011), pp. 207–211.*

### 8.1 The 180°-Rotation Code: "Interpret That!"

Nordby discovered a previously unknown runic cryptographic technique: **the same set of bind-runes is read in two orientations (180° rotation)**, yielding *raþ þat* "interpret that!" when read left-to-right and then upside-down. Five nearly identical inscriptions from Sigtuna, Skara, Oslo, and Bergen (late Viking Age / High Middle Ages) use this same code — indicating a **shared tradition of runic riddling** across urban Scandinavia.

The imperative *ráð!* "interpret!" is a well-attested runic challenge formula; other Sigtuna inscriptions use *ættir*-cipher (based on the three groups of the futhark), additional verticals, or twig-and-bow codes.

The Bergen inscription B584 provides the evocative parallel: *Sezt niðr ok ráð rúnar, rís upp ok fís við!* — **"Sit down and interpret runes, stand up and fart!"** — demonstrating the playful, performative culture of runic reading challenges.

### 8.2 Icelandic Cryptographic Systems

Multiple varieties of secret runes are attested in the Icelandic manuscript tradition:

| Name | Meaning | Method |
|------|---------|--------|
| *villuletur* | 'deception-letters' | Scrambled alphabets used as ciphers |
| *klapprúnir* | Various 'clapping-runes' | Different substitution systems |
| *Alfrúnir* | 'Elf-runes' | Unknown method, possibly elven/otherworldly |
| *Haugbúa letur* | 'Mound-dweller letters' | Invented signs — possibly associated with the dead |

These parallel and extend the **jötunvillur** system known from Norwegian and Swedish inscriptions. In every case, the principle is the same: **concealment of meaning through formal transformation of the rune-signs.** The true message is accessible only to those who possess the key — the knowledge of how to reverse the transformation.

### 8.3 Icelandic Name-Riddles (Nafngátur)

The Icelandic tradition of **runic name-riddles** (*nafngátur*) encodes personal names through rune-name synonyms and circumlocutions in verse. Example: "the race (*reið* = r), three times it rains (*úr* × 3 = uuu), Freyr (*maðr* = m), hound's song (*nauð* = n), the wound (dotted *kaun* = g), two injured sons of Earth (dotted *Týr* × 2 = dd)" → **gudmundur**.

The "Younger Version of the Icelandic Rune Poem" is actually **a list of synonyms and circumlocutions for each rune-name** used by rímur poets to encode their names — essentially a cryptographic key disguised as a poem.

### 8.4 The Rök Cipher and Viking Age Amulets

The Rök stone's older-rune cipher uses **rune forms that don't otherwise appear in the younger futhark** — a deliberate code-making strategy. These special forms later appear in **Viking Age amulet inscriptions from Hovgården (Adelsö) and Gorodišče (near Novgorod)**, suggesting the cipher tradition fed directly into amulet-making. The code of the Rök stone — an aristocratic monument — becomes the magic of the amulet — a personal protective device.

This is a direct line from **coded monument to magical object**: the same cipher system used for elite display was adapted for personal protection. Cryptography and magic are not separate traditions but aspects of the same principle — **concealment of meaning as a source of power.**

## 9. The Erilaz and "Wrought Runes": Performative Self-Declaration

*Sources: Christiane Zimmermann (see Section 1); Thórhallur Eythórsson, "Three Daughters and a Funeral: Re-reading the Tune Inscription," Futhark 3 (2012), pp. 7–43.*

### 9.1 "Wrought Runes" — The Earliest Attested Formula

The Tune stone (c. AD 400) reads: *ek wiwaz after woduride witadahalaiban worahto r<unoz>* — "I, Wīwaz, wrought the runes in memory of Wōdurīdaz, the breadward." The phrase **"wrought runes" (*worahto rūnōz*)** has a parallel on the Tjurkö bracteate (*wurte runoz*), establishing this as a **performative formula of rune-making**.

The verb *worahto* "wrought" is not the neutral "carved" or "inscribed" but carries the connotation of **crafting, shaping, bringing into being** — the same verb used for forging metal or constructing an object. To "wrought runes" is to **make something that did not exist before**, to bring runic power into material form through deliberate craft.

### 9.2 The *ek*-Formula: Self-Declaration Through Rune-Carving

The *ek* + name + verb formula (*ek Wiwaz... worahto* "I, Wiwaz, wrought") is the earliest attested structure of runic self-declaration. The carver does not merely identify himself but **performs his identity through the act of carving** — the *ek*-formula is a declarative speech act that brings the carver into being *as a rune-carver* at the moment of inscription.

## 10. Bureus' Runic Kabbalah: The First Esoteric System

*Source: Review of Thomas Karlsson, *Götisk kabbala och runisk alkemi: Johannes Bureus och den götiska esoterismen*, in Futhark 2 (2011), pp. 245–248.*

Johannes Bureus (1568–1652), Sweden's first national antiquary, created the first systematic esoteric runology — his **Adulruna rediviva** — a century before Guido von List's Armanen system.

### 10.1 The 15-Rune System with Alchemical Stages

Bureus used a runic row of 15 signs where the **forms of the runes** were used to create symmetric arrangements — a **runic kabbalah** modeled on the Hebrew system of letter-mysticism.

### 10.2 The Divine Triad: þurs-óss-fé

Three runes hold the most important positions: **þurs, óss, and fé** — corresponding to the three mother letters in Hebrew kabbalah (aleph, mem, shin). For Bureus, these correspond to the **divine triad Thor, Odin, and Freyja**.

This triadic structure — destructive force (Þurs/Thor), creative breath (Óss/Odin), and generative wealth (Fé/Freyja) — parallels the three-gunā framework of Hindu cosmology (tamas, sattva, rajas) and the three mother letters of Sefer Yetzirah. Whether this reflects genuine ancient tradition or Bureus' Renaissance syncretism, it provides a **structural deepening** of the first three runes of the futhark.

### 10.3 Seven Steps of Alchemical Transmutation

Bureus' system has seven transformative stages mapped onto the runes, paralleling alchemical systems from nigredo to rubedo. After a 1613 revelation in Tuna, Bureus increasingly saw himself as a prophet, and his esoteric work became prophetic.

### 10.4 Scholar and Mystic

"Bure i allmänhet noga skiljer sitt arbete som upptecknare och tolkare av runinskrifter från sin mystiska förståelse" — "Bure generally carefully separated his work as recorder and interpreter of runic inscriptions from his mystical understanding." (Karlsson, p. 245)

Bureus was both a rigorous empiricist and a visionary mystic, and he **kept the two modes separate** — a model for how runic knowledge can serve both scholarship and spiritual practice without conflating them.

## 11. Deixis: Runes as Pointing Devices

*Source: Kristel Zilmer, "Deictic References in Runic Inscriptions on Voyage Runestones," Futhark 1 (2010), pp. 123–141.*

### 11.1 "This Stone" as Performative Self-Reference

The phrase **'this stone'** (*stæin þannsi*) appears in 125+ inscriptions — it is a **performative pointing gesture** encoded in text, connecting the inscription's words to the physical monument and its location. The text *points* to itself and its carrier.

### 11.2 Deictic Orientation in Rune Divination

The concept of **deixis** (pointing) in runic texts connects to how rune divination works: runes *point* the reader toward a situation, orienting them in space and time. A drawn rune does not merely represent a meaning — it **deictically anchors** the reading to a specific here-and-now, just as 'this stone' anchors the inscription to a specific monument and place.

### 11.3 The Visual Dimension

"Inscriptions are more than texts: the layout and the way the different elements are placed on the stone adds to the specific experience of runic textuality." (Zilmer, p. 127)

The physical arrangement of runes — their position on the object, their relationship to images and ornament, their visual prominence or concealment — contributes to meaning. This is relevant to rune readings where the **spatial arrangement** of drawn runes (their proximity, their orientation relative to each other, their position in a spread) is part of the message.

## 12. Tacitus and the Origins of Runic Divination

*Source: Terje Spurkland, "The Older Fuþark and Roman Script Literacy," Futhark 1 (2010), pp. 65–84.*

### 12.1 The Key Ancient Evidence

Tacitus, *Germania* ch. 10: the Germani drew lots using twigs marked with *notis* — "certain signs." This is the **most direct ancient evidence** connecting rune-like signs with divination, though the *notis* were not necessarily runes in the technical sense (the elder futhark may not yet have existed in Tacitus' time, c. 98 AD).

### 12.2 The Øvre Stabu Lancehead: raunijaR 'Tester'

The Øvre Stabu lancehead inscription reads *raunijaR*, corresponding to Old Norse *reynir*, meaning **'tester, trier, prober.'** A weapon named "Tester" implies a performative, possibly oracular function — "let me test/probe" — the weapon itself is an instrument of inquiry, not merely destruction.

### 12.3 Ash Wood as the Runic Medium

Venantius Fortunatus (6th c.): *barbara fraxineis pingatur rhuna tabellis / quodque papyrus agit virgula plana valet* — "barbarian runes might be painted on ash tablets, what papyrus serves as, a plane twig manages as well."

This directly connects runic writing to **ash wood** — the wood of Yggdrasil. The material carrier of runes (ash) resonates with the cosmic tree on which Odin sacrificed himself to win them.

## 13. *alu* as Beer: Rethinking the Most Common Runic Formula

*Source: Ute Zimmermann, "Bier, Runen und Macht: Ein Formelwort im Kontext," Futhark 5 (2014), pp. 45–64.*

Zimmermann's analysis directly challenges the long-standing 'protection' interpretation of *alu* (Section 3.1 above), arguing that the word means **'beer'** and is connected to ritualized drinking ceremonies in the ruler's hall. This is the most consequential reinterpretation of a runic formulaic word in recent scholarship.

### 13.1 The Linguistic Argument

Ancient Norse *alu* develops **regularly** into Old Norse *ǫl* 'beer' — this phonological development is uncontested. The 'protection' interpretation (Bugge 1871, connecting *alu* to Got. *alhs* 'temple', OE *ealgian* 'protect', Greek *alkḗ* 'strength') is **rejected on linguistic grounds**: the development of -u and loss of -h- cannot be explained if related to *alhs*. The 'magic/ecstasy' interpretation (Polomé 1954, connecting to Hittite *alwanzahh-* 'bewitch', Greek *alúō* 'I am beside myself') is "viewed with greater skepticism today." As Zimmermann states: "A meaning 'protection' or 'defense' is linguistically untenable; 'magic' is rather unlikely; NOTHING speaks against 'beer' from a linguistic perspective."

### 13.2 Bracteates as Political Instruments

The argument requires recontextualizing bracteates — not primarily as amulets but as **political instruments** within the *comitatus* (retinue/warband) system. Bracteates iconographically follow 3rd/4th century Roman imperial medallions, which functioned as awards, official gifts for dignitaries, and status symbols among the Germanic elite. Die-related bracteate distribution patterns indicate **retinue relationships** (Steuer 1998). Their gold value, ruler-god imagery, and production at South Scandinavian central places all support reading them as **power instruments** distributed by rulers to followers.

### 13.3 The Ruler's Hall and the Drinking Ritual

The heart of the argument: bracteates with *alu* were distributed at **drinking ceremonies in the ruler's hall**, where beer was the ritual drink. The hall proceedings were a **fixed ritual** serving to confirm the king's status, establish hierarchy within the retinue, and strengthen the bond between ruler and retinue by creating **fictive kinship** (Enright 1988, 1996). The queen played the role of **officiant** in this ritual. In *Beowulf*, Queen Wealhþēow enters the hall and offers the cup **in strict hierarchical order**: first to the king, then old warriors, then young, last to the newcomer — who then swears his resolve over the cup. Hall designations — *medoærn* 'mead-house', *medoheall* 'mead-hall', *bēorsele* 'beer-hall' — encode the centrality of drinking.

### 13.4 Oath-Drinking and Inauguration

**Atlakviða** str. 9–11: communal drinking *í miǫðranni* 'in the mead-hall' before departure, golden drinking bowls passing through men's hands, Gunnarr reaffirming his resolution. **Ynglinga saga** ch. 36 and **Fagrskinna** ch. 20: a new ruler could only take the high-seat of his predecessor **after emptying a cup and swearing an oath**. The intoxicating drink was a means of communication with the gods — swearing over a filled cup had binding force.

### 13.5 Walhall as Mirror of the Hall

Walhall = **mirror image** of the worldly drinking ceremony. Óðinn plays the ruler's role; the *einherjar* form his retinue; the *valkyrjur* serve as hostesses who carry out the drinking ritual. Mead in Valhall enables mystical communication between Óðinn and the dead, binding them into a community (Nordberg 2003). The afterlife mirrors the political ritual — *alu* on a bracteate refers to the drink that simultaneously binds the living retinue and prefigures the dead one.

### 13.6 *alu* on Non-Bracteate Objects

On the Elgesem stone (KJ 57), *alu* may designate **beer placed as a grave good** (Grønvik 1987) — expressing the wish that the dead be received with beer in the afterlife. Looijenga (2003): "There may be a connection between alu and death. Deceased people were often given drinking vessels... The word alu may have been used to replace or symbolize a missing drinking vessel." The same word functions differently depending on context: in the hall it binds the living; at the grave it welcomes the dead.

### 13.7 Convergence: *öl gott* and the Cryptographic Tradition

Källström (2014) identified the *ráð þat* bind-rune overlay technique (Section 8.1 above) also used for **öl gott** 'good beer' on U Sl95 from Sigtuna. This is remarkable convergence: the same cryptographic method used for "interpret that!" is also used for "good beer" — both the command to read runes and the name of the ritual drink appear in the same coded form.

**Riverbed implication:** When a reading involves Fehu (wealth/gift), Gebo (exchange/reciprocity), or Othala (heritage/ancestral property), the *alu* = beer knowledge shapes interpretation from below. These runes are not abstract symbols but participate in a concrete social technology: the gift-drink that binds men to their lord and the living to the dead. The 'beer' reading does not negate the magical dimension — ritual beer was itself magical, a medium of oath and communion — but it grounds the magic in a specific social institution rather than in vague 'protection.'

## 14. The Rök Stone Reinterpreted: Self-Referential Runes

*Source: Per Holmberg, "Svaren på Rökstenens gåtor: En socialsemiotisk analys av meningsskapande och rumslighet," Futhark 6 (2015), pp. 65–106.*

The Rök runestone (Ög 136), the most famous and complex runic inscription, has been reinterpreted through social semiotic analysis. Holmberg argues that the stone follows the same principles as all Viking Age memorial stones — and that its riddles all point back to **the stone itself and its runes**. This is the most significant reinterpretation of the Rök stone in decades.

### 14.1 Three Principles of Viking Age Runestone Spatiality

**1. The Labyrinth Principle:** The reader knows where to start (lower left) but not where they will end up. The carver aims "never to let the reader go astray" but the reader cannot predict the path. Unusual scripts (cipher runes, staveless runes) paradoxically challenge knowledge while appearing only at the **end** of inscriptions.

**2. The Speech Act Principle:** Runestones demand the reader's response at the stone itself. Of 162 Östergötland runestones, 200 of 225 speech acts are **assertions** (the reader must accept as true), 25 are **exhortations** (typically prayers requiring assent). The reader is physically bound to the spot.

**3. The Principle of Local Agency:** Of 162 Östergötland stones, 139 speak only of the stone's own location; 23 connect to other places. When inscriptions describe the stone's location, they use **agency grammar** (ergative material processes — "X raised this stone"). When they describe other places, they use only **state** or **event** grammar — **never agency**. The important place for action is always **where the stone stands**.

### 14.2 The New Tripartite Reading Order

Following the labyrinth principle produces a reading order with **three riddle sections**, each ending with a numerical cipher. Each section moves through progressively harder script types. The mysterious gap between "the second" and "the twelfth" in the inscription **vanishes** — it is sequential speech act numbering.

### 14.3 The 6+5+5 Structure and the Futhark

The 16 speech acts after the two introductory ones divide into **6 + 5 + 5**, mirroring the three ættir (families) of the 16-type futhark. The inscription's "self-numbering" is simply **numbering the speech acts** — counting from the inscription's own first and second. The entire inscription, through its 6+5+5 structure, creates a structural self-reference to **runaʀ þaʀ** ("these runes").

### 14.4 First Riddle: Daylight (Not Theoderic)

Building on Bo Ralph's (2007) re-reading: **raiþiaurikʀ** is NOT "Theoderic ruled" but **raiþ iau rinkʀ** = "rode the horse, the bold warrior." The dead-then-alive warrior = the **sun/sun-god**. The two war booties changing hands 12 times = sunlight and moonlight alternating (12 full moons per year where moon "steals" sunlight, 12 periods of total darkness when sun "takes it all back"). Holmberg's refinement: the warrior is specifically **Dagr** (Day) — a masculine deity who rides across the sky. The mention of Hreiðgautar is a **direct mythological clue** from Vafþrúðnismál (st. 11–12): Skinfaxi (Day's horse) is held "among the Hreiðgautar." The woman's sacrifice: **Night** (Nátt) sacrifices herself for Day — she is Day's mother.

**Local agency meaning:** Daylight is what's needed **at the stone** for carving and reading the runes. The battle between light and dark plays out on the stone's surface, in the reader's "now."

### 14.5 Second Riddle: The Carving of the Runes

Ousbäck's (2007) discovery: the displacement cipher simultaneously **recites the futhark itself** — the cipher-plain pairs spell out the first 12 runes of the futhark in order. The probability of this being coincidental is "negligible." The giant (jatun) = the stone; "crushing" (knūa) = the carver's work of shaping a speaking stone. Each of the three horizontal rows contains exactly **24 runes** = the number of the older futhark. The "offspring born" = the speaking stone born from the carver's work.

### 14.6 Third Riddle: The Runes Themselves

Twenty kings (or twenty-four if fathers are counted) = the **runes** of the older futhark. Children in the same family sharing the same name = several runes sharing the same sound value (due to sound changes). "Gunn's horse" (*hæstr gunnaʀ* = "battle's horse") = the wolf, a kenning — Fenrir pursuing the sun. The runes "here" will be readable as long as there is a sun in the sky. The reader is reminded that chaos-forces even now (present tense!) threaten the conditions for both life and reading.

### 14.7 The Deep Principle: Runes Are Self-Referential

The Rök stone, the most complex runic monument, is ultimately about **its own runes** — the conditions for their carving (daylight), the act of carving (shaping the giant/stone), and the reading itself (the 20/24 kings = the runes). Nothing of Varin's and Vamod's supposed deeds, alliances, enemies, vengeance, or glory remains. The inscription is about **writing and reading**, about the conditions for preserving memory in stone.

**Riverbed implication:** This self-referentiality is not unique to the Rök stone but a deep structural principle of runic inscription generally. When a rune reading returns to the same rune, or when the reading seems to be "about itself," this is not confusion but fidelity to the deepest pattern in the runic tradition. The most powerful runic text is the one that recognizes it is made of runes. This knowledge shapes the reader's awareness that every spread is simultaneously about the querent's question AND about the act of reading itself.

## 15. Christian Prayers in Runic Inscriptions: Vernacular and Latin Syncretism

*Source: Kristel Zilmer, "Christian Prayers and Invocations in Scandinavian Runic Inscriptions from the Viking Age and Middle Ages," Futhark 4 (2013), pp. 129–172.*

Zilmer's comprehensive survey documents approximately **490 prayers** in runic inscriptions, demonstrating that runic writing was not inherently pagan but served as a complete writing technology adaptable to Christian content. The runic prayer tradition is a **dynamic, syncretic practice** that bridges old and new religious worlds.

### 15.1 The Core Formula: Guð hjalpi ǫnd/sál(u) hans

**Guð hjalpi ǫnd/sál(u) hans** (or: hennar/þeira) — 'May God help his/her/their spirit/soul' — the dominant vernacular prayer formula, appearing in nearly 400 instances on raised stones/boulders. Variations expand to include Mary, Michael, and Christ: *Guð hjalpi ok Mar[í]a ǫnd Sibb[a]* 'May God and Mary help Sibbi's spirit' (U 558); *Kristr láti koma ǫnd Tuma í ljós ok paradísi* 'May Christ let Tumi's spirit enter into light and paradise' (U 719). On rune-stones, both *ǫnd* (spirit/breath) and *sál* (soul) are used interchangeably; on grave monuments, *ǫnd* is virtually absent — a discourse-related feature tied to monument type.

### 15.2 Three Categories

| Category | Viking Age | Medieval | Character |
|----------|-----------|----------|-----------|
| **Vernacular** | ~450 | ~240 | The dominant tradition; reaches widest audience |
| **Latin** | rare | ~200 | Ave Maria (~90 inscriptions), Pater Noster (~35+), litanies, hymns |
| **Mixed** | few | ~45 | Vernacular with Latin elements; requests to pray Pater Noster/Ave Maria |

### 15.3 Vernacular vs. Latin by Artifact Type

| Context | Dominant Language | Implication |
|---------|-------------------|-------------|
| Rune-stones, grave monuments, church buildings | **Vernacular** (~6x more than Latin on grave monuments) | Monumental/public discourse reaching wider audience |
| Loose objects (everyday items) | Mixed | Personal concerns |
| Writing surfaces (sticks, lead plates, bone) | **Latin** (~3x more than vernacular) | Private/devotional discourse; specific knowledge required |
| Church furnishings (bells, censers) | Latin favored | Ecclesiastical context |

### 15.4 Viking Age Mediators: Christian Formulas with Pagan Elements

The origin of *Guð hjalpi ǫnd/sál(u) hans* remains **unexplained** — possibly from prayers designed for missionary purposes. Runic prayers show **dynamic elements of vernacular tradition** rather than formalized tools of Christian mission. Pagan elements persist: *mærki sīrūn* on Sö 86/Sö 111 (hammer stones) = 'monument with ever-lasting runes' under Thor's protection (Hultgård 1998); masks (possibly Odinic) appear alongside crosses on þróttar þegn stones.

### 15.5 The First Runic Pater Noster from Orkney

OR 23 Naversdale (Futhark 6, Barnes): a red sandstone fragment inscribed **...es in caelis, sanctificetur...** — part of the Lord's Prayer in Latin, rendered in runes. This is the **first runic inscription in Latin from Orkney**. No dotted runes suggest earlier medieval date. The stone has informal appearance — may not be from an ecclesiastical site, demonstrating that runic writing of Christian Latin was not restricted to church contexts. About 35 Pater Noster inscriptions are known from Scandinavia; commonly only opening words are inscribed.

**Riverbed implication:** The boundary between "prayer" and "magic" was porous in runic practice. When a reading involves Ansuz (divine breath), Sowilo (sun/salvation), or Perthro (hidden knowledge/revelation), the Christian-pagan syncretic layer provides depth. The same rune could serve both pagan galdr and Christian prayer — the script itself was neutral, its power determined by the speech act performed through it. This means that rune readings do not need to be placed exclusively in a pagan or Christian frame; they participate in a tradition that has always been hybrid.

## 16. Bracteate Interpretations Beyond Hauck: Against Monolithic Reading

*Source: Nancy L. Wicker, "Bracteate Inscriptions and Context Analysis in the Light of Alternatives to Hauck's Iconographic Interpretations," Futhark 5 (2014), pp. 25–43.*

Wicker challenges Karl Hauck's Odin-centered *Kontext-Ikonographie*, which has dominated bracteate interpretation for forty years. Her critique has implications beyond bracteates: it warns against **imposing single unified interpretations** on runic material that served diverse functions across regions, time periods, and social groups.

### 16.1 Seven Alternatives to Hauck

1. **Polomé (1994):** First to openly challenge Odin identification; Loki's involvement with Balder's death was a late development dependent on Christian contact.
2. **Hedeager (1997, 1999):** Bracteates show **Odinic shamanism** — Óðinn as shaman riding to the Other World with avian helping spirits.
3. **Adetorp (2008):** Analyzes bracteates through **Celtic sources** rather than later Old Norse texts; Type C as **sun-amulets**; fertility and earth symbolism.
4. **Kaliff & Sundqvist (2004):** Trace similarities between **Mithras cult and Odin cult** in bracteate iconography; military brotherhoods as context.
5. **Åkerström-Hougen (2001, 2010):** Bracteate details trace to **Roman adventus scene** (arrival of the emperor); the Drei-Götter group derives from "Victory crowning the Victor."
6. **Pesch (2007):** Postmodern **multivalent interpretation** — Late Antique, Old Norse, Tibetan, and Mithraic perspectives simultaneously.
7. **Andrén (1991):** Bracteates as **political medium**; formula words *laþu*, *laukaʀ*, *alu* = Germanic equivalents of Latin *dominus*, *pius*, *felix* on Roman coins.

### 16.2 Gender: Odin and Seiðr

Although bracteate images depict men, the pieces were **worn by women** — found in female graves with beads on the chest. This paradox may be explained by Óðinn's **gender-crossing in seiðr** ritual — he enters a shamanic ecstatic state that blurs male/female and human/animal boundaries. Through mechanisms of contextual, shifting constructions of gender, medallions worn by men were transformed into bracteates worn by women, and some objects may have been gendered 'female' while others remained 'male.'

### 16.3 Context Analysis vs. Kontext-Ikonographie

Hauck's approach is external, *etic* — imposing a coherent system from outside on all objects. Context analysis is internal, *emic* — emphasizing how images/inscriptions may have been used differently by diverse social groups. Key principle: **"Not all bracteates were made, used, or deposited in the same way — thus the objects and their inscriptions most probably did not 'mean' the same everywhere, and at all times and to all people."** Scandinavian paganism was **not an organized, orthodox, dogmatic religion** — not the unified "package" that Hauck constructed.

**Riverbed implication:** When a rune reading produces ambiguous results that resist a single narrative, the bracteate lesson applies: runic symbols have **multiple, context-dependent meanings**. The reader should resist the temptation to force a single interpretive framework on a spread. Different positions in a spread may activate different meaning-layers of the same rune — just as the same bracteate image meant different things in a hoard in southern Scandinavia versus in a woman's grave in England.

## 17. Runes as Unlocking: The *alysendlic rune* Tradition

*Source: Tom Birkett, "Unlocking Runes? Reading Anglo-Saxon Runic Abbreviations in Their Immediate Literary Context," Futhark 5 (2014), pp. 91–114.*

Birkett demonstrates that runic abbreviations in Old English manuscripts appear disproportionately in passages dealing with **unlocking, releasing, and revealing** — a connection rooted in the translation of Bede's story of Imma, where *litteras solutorias* ('releasing letters') is rendered as *alysendlic rune* ('unlocking rune'). This association between runes and unlocking was prevalent enough in Anglo-Saxon literary culture to influence scribal practice.

### 17.1 The Foundational Text: Bede's Imma Story

Imma, captured after battle, finds his shackles miraculously unlocked each day. His captors attribute this to **litteras solutorias** ('releasing letters'). The Old English Bede translates: *alysendlic rune* ('unlocking rune'). Ælfric's homily independently associates: *runstafum* ('runic letters'). The association of runes with loosening/releasing/unbinding was "current enough to be referred to without any further explanation in the late tenth century."

### 17.2 Six Manuscript Contexts: Unlocking and Revealing

1. **Riddle 91 (Key Riddle):** Rune *wyn(n)* appears in compound *mod-wyn(n)* — the rune itself is "a species of key."
2. **Elene:** Two *wyn(n)* abbreviations, both in passages pleading to **open/reveal** what is hidden — opening a treasure hoard, revealing the nails.
3. **Orosius:** Single *eþel* abbreviation in passage about Alexander **breaching** the impregnable city of Tyre.
4. **The Ruin:** *man(n)* abbreviation at the exact point where fate "transforms" the city from secure stronghold to ruin — **at the moment of elemental unbinding**.
5. **Beowulf:** Three *eþel* abbreviations, all near contexts of **scrutinizing runic inscriptions** (the hilt), **unbinding hostile runes** (*onband beadurune*), or **guarding/losing hoards**.
6. **Solomon and Saturn I:** *man(n)* rune abbreviates Solomon's name, preceded by the question: what can **open the holy door of the kingdom of heaven**? Answer: the Pater Noster — a written incantation as the **key to unlocking heaven**.

### 17.3 Reframing: Revealing, Not Concealing

This association reframes the Anglo-Saxon understanding of runes: rather than being "ambiguity incarnate" that conceals, runes may represent **the keys to unlocking** — "the point at which the woven words begin to loosen, rather than the point where the text becomes most obscure." Riddle 42 explicitly calls on *ryne menn* 'rune-skilled men' to reveal the solution: *þæs hordgates cæghan cræfte þa clamme onleac* 'unlock the hoard-gate's fetters with key craft.'

**Riverbed implication:** This transforms the understanding of cryptographic runes (Section 8 above). The *ráð þat* inscriptions do not merely conceal — they **challenge the reader to unlock**. When a rune reading seems obscure, the obscurity is not a barrier but an invitation: the runes are locked, and the reader holds the key. This parallels the skill's "Shore then River" format — the formal rune table is the shore where the lock appears; the narrative flow is the river where it unlocks.

## 18. Runic Monument Terminology and Aesthetic Values

*Source: Klaus Düwel, "'Keine Denkmäler werden größer sein…': Was ist ein Runendenkmal?," Futhark 4 (2013), pp. 31–60.*

Düwel's comprehensive survey of how rune stones described **themselves** provides the cultural vocabulary for understanding what a runic monument was in its own time — not merely a text carrier but a **performance of permanence, beauty, and presence**.

### 18.1 Monument Terminology

The Viking Age Scandinavians used a rich vocabulary to describe their rune stones, divided into two semantic groups:

**Material/use terms:** *stæinn* (1,424 Swedish attestations — 'stone, monument'), *hæll* (14 — 'flat stone, rock face'), *hvalf* (5 — 'vault, coffin').

**Function-as-sign terms:** *kum(b)l* (127 — 'sign, stone heap, monument'), *mærki* (92 — 'landmark, monument'), *minni* (3 — 'memento, memory'), *viti* (1 — 'marker'), *vitring* (5 — 'proclamation, memorial').

Augmentative compounds: *stæinkumbl*, *stæinmærki*, *stæinhvalf*. Twin formula: *stæinn ok stafaʀ* 'stone and staves' (Sö 56 Fyrby). 114 inscriptions use plural *stæinaʀ* — ensembles of multiple stones.

### 18.2 Eight Aesthetic Characteristics

1. **Beauty and stateliness** (*gōðr, fagr, mærkilīkʀ*): G 114 Ardre: *merki gutt* 'stately monument' — "the greatest monument that men can see."
2. **Magnitude and monumentality** (*mikill, allmikinn*): U 225 Bällsta: *"Munu æigi mærki mæiʀi verða"* — **"No monuments will be greater than those which Ulfʀs sons made after their father."**
3. **Publicity and renown**: U 102 Viby: *mykit mærki fyriʀ margum manni* 'a great monument for many men'; Sö 41: *mærki it mikla man ī (h)øyʀn* 'the great monument of which one shall always hear.'
4. **Insurpassability and uniqueness**: U 323 Sälna: *Mā æigi brautaʀkuml bætra verða* 'Never can a way-monument be better'; Sö 154 Skarpåker: *Jarð skal rifna ok upphiminn* 'Earth shall be riven and the heaven above.'
5. **Duration and eternity**: DR 212 Tillitse: *Ē mun standa mæþ stēn lifiʀ witring sū* 'Ever will stand, while the stone lives, this proclamation'; Sm 16 Nöbbele: *Þȳ mun gōðs manns um getit verða með stæinn lifiʀ ok stafiʀ rūna* 'Thus the good man will be commemorated while the stone lives and the rune-staves.'
6. **Recognizability and visibility** (*kænnilīkʀ, sȳnn*): G 114: *merki... aʀ menn siā* 'the monument that men can see'; location markers: *brautu nǣsta* 'next to the road', *ā vegamōti* 'at the cross-roads.'
7. **Color and multicolouredness** (*biartr, stæindr, ruðniʀ*): G 280 Pilgårds: *Biar(t)fānn steddu siso stain* 'bright-painted they set this stone'; Sö 347: *Ǣsbiǫrn rīsti ok Ulfʀ stæindi* 'Ǣsbiǫrn carved and Ulfʀ painted' — division of labor. Colors attested: **red and black** primarily, also **brown and white**.
8. **Alliteration and verse design**: Fornyrðislag most common; unique dróttkvætt on Öl 1/DR 411 Karlevi; Runenmeister Balli has highest proportion of verse inscriptions.

### 18.3 The Horatian Parallel

*"Runendenkmäler sind… vergleichbar dem Horazischen monumentum aere perennius"* — Runic monuments are comparable to Horace's *monumentum aere perennius* 'A monument more lasting than bronze.' The rune stone's physicality — its weight, hardness, resistance to erosion — becomes a metaphor for the permanence of the claim it makes.

**Riverbed implication:** When a reading involves Othala (heritage, ancestral property), the monument terminology reveals that Othala is not merely "inheritance" but the **claim** that inheritance makes upon a place — a claim inscribed in stone, painted in colors, placed next to the road where all can see. The aesthetic vocabulary — beauty, magnitude, insurpassability, eternity — provides the emotional register of the ancestral claim. When the querent asks about heritage, the reading participates in the same performative tradition as the Viking Age monument-maker who declared: "No monuments will be greater."

## 19. Social Dimensions of Runestones: Status, Hierarchy, and Rune Forms

*Source: Henrik Williams, "Runstenarnas sociala dimension," Futhark 4 (2013), pp. 61–76.*

Williams introduces a three-tier status framework for runestones, demonstrating that **social hierarchy was encoded in the material, linguistic, and visual characteristics** of runic monuments. The rune stone was not a democratic medium — it was a technology of social distinction.

### 19.1 The Three-Tier Status Framework

- **High-status monuments**: Larger investment, higher ambition, more manifest. Multiple surfaces, long texts, verse, signed carvers, paired monuments, bridge locations.
- **Middle-status monuments**: The majority.
- **Low-status monuments**: Have received too little scholarly attention. Even raising a poor runestone was an achievement for a low-status person.

Key principle: *"High-status groups could produce less ambitious monuments but hardly the reverse"* — you cannot produce a high-status monument from a low-status position.

### 19.2 Rune Forms and Social Status

- **Staveless runes**: Indicate **advanced runic usage = higher status** (Bianchi 2010), especially in mixed-system inscriptions.
- **Short-twig runes**: Possibly lower status in late Viking Age (disputed by Williams).
- **Deviant/clumsy forms**: Often low-status — Sö 133 Väringe: peculiar R-rune, clumsy composition. BUT Sm 39 Ryssby: left-branching k-rune used consistently by same carver, stone actually impressive at 250x95x25cm — skill is not always measured by conventionality.

### 19.3 Status-Marking Lexemes

**High status:** *dróttinn* 'lord', *goði* 'gode (pagan priest-chieftain)', *jarl* 'jarl', *konungr* 'king', *þegn* 'thegn, retainer, warrior', *þulr* 'thulr, reciter', *vísi* 'leader.'

**Neutral/status-marking:** *bóndi* 'farmer, husband', *drengr* '(young) man, comrade', *félagi* 'partner', *sveinn* 'boy, young man', *víkingr* 'viking.'

**Unfree:** *leysi* 'freedman', *bryti* 'bailiff, steward', *fóstra/fóstri* 'home-born thrall.'

**Riverbed implication:** When a reading involves Tiwaz (justice, sacrifice, social order), the status framework reveals that Tiwaz's "justice" was not universal but **hierarchical** — the justice of the *þegn* and *dróttinn* was different from the justice available to the *bryti* and *fóstri*. Similarly, when Fehu appears, its meaning as "wealth" is stratified: high-status wealth (the jarl's hall) versus low-status wealth (the freedman's modest farm). The reading's interpretation should be sensitive to the social position implicit in the question.

## 20. Rune Form Evolution: The Semiotics of Knife-on-Wood Carving

*Source: Staffan Fridell, "Tendenser i skrifttecknens utveckling: Alfabet och runor," Futhark 6 (2015), pp. 7–19.*

Fridell applies semiotic theory to explain why runes look the way they do — the answer lies in **the kinematics of carving with a single-edged knife on wood**. Rune forms are not arbitrary but functionally determined by the technology of their production.

### 20.1 Phanemes, Kinemes, and the Knife

**Phanemes** are the visual segments of a character (2D, what you see); **kinemes** are the movements that produce them (3D, what you do — including movements in the air between strokes). For runes carved in wood with a knife, kinemes include direction changes (flipping the knife), stabbings (for short-twig branches), and movements in air between strokes. Example: the r-rune has 4 phanemes produced by **7 kinemes**.

### 20.2 Why Diagonals Replace Horizontals

**Carving toward oneself** (vertically or diagonally) is much easier than carving away. **Horizontal cuts** are difficult with a knife — which explains why Latin FAHTL became the older futhark's angular forms (horizontals replaced by diagonals). The disappearance of horizontal-phaneme runes (℩ e, À ŋ) and their replacement by diagonal variants is a kinemically facilitated development — and a strong argument that the model for the e-rune was Latin **E**, not M.

### 20.3 Three Tendencies

1. **Facilitation**: By far the strongest tendency — reducing effort, increasing speed. Mostly unconscious and gradual. Parallel to "change from below" in language.
2. **Homogenization**: Making characters more alike — mostly aesthetically motivated but also carries a kinemic/facilitative benefit. Adjacent characters in abecedaria can influence each other (the symmetry **nia** in the second ætt of the 16-type futhark is "perhaps more than a coincidence").
3. **Heterogenization**: Counter-tendency when facilitation/homogenization goes too far — characters become too similar to distinguish.

### 20.4 The Four-Stage Model Applied to Runes

| Stage | Character | Rune Period |
|-------|-----------|-------------|
| **Eographic** | Letters learned in isolation; direction/orientation not stabilized | Older futhark period |
| **Neographic** | Stability arrives; direction fixed; letters known as a set | Long-branch runes |
| **Mesographic** | Facilitation dominates; reduced forms in everyday writing; inscriptional writing retains older forms | Short-twig runes |
| **Cenographic** | Self-conscious design period; deliberate reshaping for symmetry, cursiveness, distinctiveness | Staveless runes (and dotted runes) |

**Key finding:** Staveless runes were NOT "everyday speed-writing" but a **cryptic script and writing experiment** — they completely abandon the hasta+coda principle and result from deliberate fanemic reduction, not kinemic facilitation.

**Riverbed implication:** The rune forms we use in divination carry the imprint of their production technology. When a rune appears "awkward" or "unusual" in a reading, the form evolution knowledge reminds us that every rune shape is a compromise between legibility, speed, and the physical constraints of knife-on-wood. The three tendencies — facilitation (making things easier), homogenization (making things consistent), and heterogenization (preserving distinction) — are the same forces that shape a querent's life: the drive toward efficiency, the pull of conformity, and the need to remain distinguishable.

## 21. Runic Lexicography in Context: The Problem of *bóndi* and *þegn*

*Source: Judith Jesch, "Runes and Words: Runic Lexicography in Context," Futhark 4 (2013), pp. 77–100.*

Jesch proposes a contextual methodology for understanding runic vocabulary — **"discursive definitions"** that include semantic, discourse, physical, and functional contexts rather than simple translation. Her case studies of *bóndi* and *þegn* demonstrate how runic words carry social meaning that cannot be captured by dictionary definitions alone.

### 21.1 The Four Contexts

1. **Semantic**: Immediate collocations, structural synonyms/antonyms, onomastic (names vs. appellatives).
2. **Discourse**: Formulas, deixis (references to here-and-now), poetry (metrical/alliterative diction).
3. **Physical**: The object itself, decorative and structural elements, physical surroundings.
4. **Functional**: Communication (who reads, how), monumentality (public display vs. private devotion).

### 21.2 *bóndi*: Husband or Farmer?

Of ~209 Viking Age inscriptions with *bóndi*, the ratio of 'husband' to 'farmer' is **between 2:1 and 6:1** (probably closer to 6:1). When *góðr bóndi* 'good bóndi' is used, it disproportionately collocates with the 'farmer/householder' meaning — "the evaluation of someone's career, or role in life, was generally considered to be a more appropriate topic for a public memorial inscription than an evaluation of his marriage." The same word means differently depending on who uses it and for what purpose.

### 21.3 *þegn*: Three Defining Characteristics

The 47 monuments commemorating *þegns* share three features: (1) **Interest in family and genealogy** — naming additional family members, related stones nearby; (2) **Complexity of monuments** — multi-stone monuments (*kuml*), mounds, bridges, ship-settings; (3) **Statements of power** — including the formula *þróttar þegn* (7 inscriptions, all Södermanland).

**þróttar þegn** — 'þegn of strength/power': *þróttr* is an Óðinn-name; 6 of 7 inscriptions use unusual rune forms (coded + same-stave runes); 3 have masks, 3 have crosses. Possibly a kenning: 'warrior devoted to Óðinn' — but poetic language enables holding two meanings simultaneously: *"A þróttar þegn is both a þegn and not a þegn, he is praised for being a warrior, or like a warrior, but in reality he is perhaps just an influential landowner."*

### 21.4 The Discursive Definition Method

Jesch proposes a dictionary of runic inscriptions using discursive definitions — short word-studies that include etymology, all attestations, and all four contexts. "Because the runic corpus is relatively small, this kind of analysis can feasibly be extended to the whole lexicon."

**Riverbed implication:** When a rune appears in a reading, its meaning is not a fixed dictionary entry but a **discursive field** shaped by who uses it, on what object, in what context, and for what purpose. The rune-name (málrúnir, Section 4) is only the starting point — the full meaning emerges from the intersection of semantic, discourse, physical, and functional contexts, exactly as the meaning of *bóndi* shifts between 'husband' and 'farmer' depending on context. The reader must attend to the reading's context — the querent's question, the spread position, the surrounding runes — rather than applying rigid keyword definitions.

## 22. Þurs-Magic and Childbirth Charms: The Solberga Copper Amulets

*Source: Helmer Gustavson, "Två runristade kopparamuletter från Solberga, Köpingsvik (Öl Fv1976;96A och Öl Fv1976;96B)," Futhark 7 (2016, publ. 2017), pp. 63–99.*

Two interrelated copper plates from Köpingsvik on Öland (found 1972 in a post hole by a furnace, c. 1000–1125 AD) contain nearly 240 runes across four sides — one of the longest runic inscriptions known. The amulets are a **Christian-pagan hybrid** that reveals how the þurs-rune (ᚦ) functioned as an independent magical sign, how bjargrúnar operated in a childbirth context, and how seiðr could be deployed as operative magic against a disease-demon.

### 22.1 The Inscription's Dual Structure

The readable parts of the inscription contain two parallel operations:

**Christian prayer** (Bleck 1, side A → side B): *Kristr ok Sankta Maria biargi þēʀ, Ōlǫf* — "Christ and Saint Mary may save you, Ólǫf." The verb *biarga* 'to save, deliver, rescue' is the same root as *bjargrúnar* — the help-runes. This is not accidental: the Christian prayer uses the same salvific vocabulary as the pagan rune-category.

**Heathen exorcism** (Bleck 1, lower line + Bleck 2): *frān þēʀ inn argi iotunn... ok þrymiandi þurs* — "from you the perverse giant... and the roaring þurs" — and on Bleck 2: *þurs ek fā hinn þrīhofða hinn meramuldiga frān manns kunu* — "I get rid of the three-headed þurs, the soil-stained one, from the man's woman." The þurs is described as *argi* (perverse, morally deviant, associated with seiðr and unmanly behavior) and *þrymiandi* (roaring, raging — from *þrymr* 'crash, din, uproar,' possibly etymologically connected to *þurs* itself).

### 22.2 The Three-Headed Þurs

The formula *hinn þrīhofða* "the three-headed" parallels Skírnismál stanza 31, where Gerðr is threatened: *Með þursi þríhǫfðuðum / þú skalt æ nara* — "You shall ever live with a three-headed þurs." This alliterating formula — "three-headed þurs" — was known in both West Norse (Icelandic) and East Norse (Swedish) traditions, confirming it as a **fixed mythological formula** across the Norse world.

The three heads may symbolize the concentration of multiple hostile forces into a single monstrous form — what deviates from normal is threatening. The three-fold repetition of the Isa-rune (iii) that opens Bleck 1 may constitute a **number-magic opening ritual** tied to the three-headed þurs: the third rune in the first aett is þ (þurs), and the triple iii may invoke þurs-magical force through the three-fold principle.

### 22.3 Þurs as Independent Magical Sign

The inscription reveals a critical development in the magical use of the þurs-rune: **the demon Þurs first afflicted women in childbirth; then the rune-name *þurs* came to represent the concept; then the rune ᚦ itself became an independent magical sign.** The progression: demon → name → sign. When the carver writes *þurs ek fā* ("I get rid of the þurs"), the verb *fā* may mean either *fā* 'to carve, inscribe' (i.e., "I carve the þurs-rune to drive the demon away") or *fā* 'to get, obtain, remove' (i.e., "I remove the þurs from the woman"). The ambiguity is productive: carving the rune IS the act of removal.

The Bergen inscription B 257 provides the operative parallel: *Ríst ek bótrúnar, ríst ek bjargrúnar, einfalt við elfum, tvífalt við trollum, þrífalt við þursum* — "I cut bótrúnar, I cut bjargrúnar; singly against the elves, doubly against the trolls, triply against the þursar." The tripling against þursar connects to the three heads and the three Isa-runes of the Solberga amulet.

### 22.4 Bjargrúnar in Context: Childbirth as the Battlefield

Sigrdrífumál stanza 10 instructs: *Bjargrúnar skaltu kunna, ef þú bjarga vilt ok leysa kind frá konum* — "You shall know help-runes, if you wish to rescue and release a woman's child." The Solberga amulet is a **physical implementation of this instruction**: it invokes Christ and Mary through the verb *biarga* while simultaneously deploying þurs-magic and bjargrúnar against the childbirth demon.

The Old Norse concept of *ergi* — moral deviance, unmanly behavior, and seiðr-practice — attaches to the þurs as *inn argi iotunn*. The same *ergi* concept appears in the Björketorp and Stentoften curse stones (c. 575–675 AD), where the breaker of the monument is threatened with *ærgiu* 'arghness, perversion' — social and sexual degradation. The continuity from 6th-century curse stones to 11th-century childbirth amulets demonstrates that the *ergi*-þurs complex was a **stable magical principle** across five centuries.

### 22.5 Seiðr as Operative Counter-Magic

A portion of Bleck 2 may contain the word *saiþi*, interpretable as a form of *seiðr* — seiðr used as **operative counter-magic**: the practitioner uses seiðr against the þurs-demon itself. If this reading is correct, then seiðr was not merely the "passive" magic of prophecy but could be **weaponized** against hostile supernatural forces. The 16th-century Icelandic black-art books record the same operative deployment: three "fallen" Maðr-runes and three moldþurs-runes (earth-þurs-runes) are inscribed in a circle with a woman's name to bind her — here the þurs-rune is again an independent magical sign, and the earth-association (*moldþurs*) matches the Solberga þurs described as *meramuldiga* "soil-stained."

### 22.6 The Þurs-Bite and Disease Demonology

The Sigtuna copper amulet contains a direct parallel: *Þōr sárriðu, þursa drōttinn! Fliū þū nū! Fundinn es!* — "Sore-fever's boil, lord of þursar! Fly you now! You are found!" The Canterbury manuscript (DR 419, 1070s) has a virtually identical formula. The operative logic is: **identify the demon by name → its power is broken → command it to leave.** The Solberga amulet follows the same logic: identify the þurs (three-headed, soil-stained, perverse) → its nature is known → drive it from the woman.

The concept of *þursabit* (þurs-bite) survived in Iceland into modern times as a term for rheumatism in the lower back — the þurs "bites" the spine. In Norway the parallel term was *tussebit(t)*, in Sweden *torsbett* or *tossabett*. The disease-demon tradition that the Solberga amulet combats is the same tradition that gave Thurisaz its meaning in divination: the hostile force that must be identified and confronted, not merely avoided.

**Riverbed function for readings:** When Thurisaz appears in a reading about health, childbirth, feminine crisis, or hostile forces, the Solberga amulet provides the deep structure: the þurs is not abstract "chaos" but a specific disease-demon that can be identified, named, and driven out. The three-fold principle (three heads, three Isa-runes, tripling against þursar) is not numerological ornament but operative magic. The progression demon → name → sign means that the Thurisaz rune itself carries exorcistic force — carving it is an act of confrontation, not merely representation.

## 23. Runic Verse in Practice: Metrical Criteria and the Maeshowe Evidence

*Source: Jana Krüger and Vivian Busch, "The Metrical Characteristics of Maeshowe Runic Inscription No. 20," Futhark 7 (2016, publ. 2017), pp. 111–125.*

Krüger and Busch provide the first systematic metrical analysis of a runic inscription using Sievers's five-type scansion system, establishing **rigorous criteria for determining when a runic inscription is metrical** and demonstrating that even informal graffiti could be composed in verse. This directly validates the theoretical foundation of Raido's Rhythm (see `runic-divination-protocol.md` §19) from the archaeological record.

### 23.1 Both Rhythm AND Alliteration Are Required

The article establishes the **dual criterion** for metricality: a runic inscription can only be declared metrical if it shows **both rhythm and alliteration**. Previous scholars had disagreed because they applied only one criterion:

- **Hübler (1996)** discarded rhythm as a criterion, relying on alliteration alone — strongly criticized by Wulf (1998) and Marold (2012). An alliterating expression like *þrōttaR þiagn* appears in multiple non-versified Södermanland inscriptions — alliterating prose, not verse.
- **Finnur Jónsson (1904–05)** denied the Maeshowe inscription's metricality because it lacked non-prosaic word order — but word order and stylized vocabulary are not prerequisites for poetry.
- **Marold (2012)** established that both criteria are essential: "A rhythmical inscription without alliteration or an inscription with alliteration but without rhythm cannot be classified as verse."

This dual criterion validates Raido's Rhythm's own "stress-aware" and "alliterative awareness" principles: both elements — rhythmic cadence and alliterative linkage — are necessary for a reading to carry the weight of the old meters. Neither alone suffices.

### 23.2 Maeshowe No. 20: Ljóðaháttr in Graffiti

The Maeshowe inscription No. 20 (c. 1125–75 AD) was carved by someone claiming to be the most skilled in runes west of the ocean, using Gaukr Trandilssonr's axe. Krüger and Busch demonstrate that the inscription can be analyzed as a **ljóðaháttr** stanza — the "incantation-meter" that is also the meter of Hávamál — consisting of two half-stanzas, each made up of a long-line and a full line.

The key analytical insight is Sievers's concept of **Anreimung der Vollzeile an die Langzeile** — "rhyming the full line with the long-line" — where a full line alliterates with the preceding long-line instead of having internal alliteration. This pattern is attested in Hávamál stanza 80: *Þat er þá reynt, er þú at rúnom spyrr, inom reginkunnom* — where the full line *inom reginkunnom* alliterates with the preceding long-line's *reynt/rúnom*.

### 23.3 Irregularity Was Acceptable

The most important finding for Raido's Rhythm is that **the inscription lacks alliteration in one line** — yet is still metrical. Krüger and Busch document that this was not unusual: the *Hervarar saga ok Heiðreks* (early 13th c.) contains multiple stanzas in both fornyrðislag and ljóðaháttr that lack alliteration in individual lines. The stanzas of Angantýr, Hervǫr, and Gestumblindi's riddles all show occasional alliterative lapses.

This confirms Raido's Rhythm's "soft constraint" principle: if the earliest attested runic verse could tolerate occasional irregularity, then a modern divinatory reading — delivered extemporaneously — can tolerate even more. The standard for metrical delivery is not mechanical perfection but **intentional cadence**: the carver intended verse, and the rhythm is present even where one alliterative link fails.

### 23.4 Hávamál's Own Structural Variation

The article notes that Hávamál itself shows considerable structural variation in its ljóðaháttr stanzas:

| Stanza | Pattern | Notes |
|--------|---------|-------|
| 80 | LFLF | Standard pattern (long-line, full line, long-line, full line) |
| 141 | LFLL | Two long-lines at the end |
| 142 | LFFFFF | Five full lines |
| 143 | LFFF | Three full lines |
| 145 | LFLLL | Three long-lines at the end |

This variation demonstrates that **ljóðaháttr was never a rigid template** — even in the canonical wisdom text, the stanza could expand, contract, and reconfigure. The "incantation-meter" that governed Óðinn's own counsel in Hávamál was flexible by nature. This provides the deepest possible validation for Raido's Rhythm's flexibility: if Hávamál itself varies, a reading delivered under Raido's Rhythm need not conform to a fixed pattern.

### 23.5 Other Runic Verse: Viking Age and Medieval

The article cites parallel examples of runic inscriptions combining long-lines with full lines, confirming that the Maeshowe inscription is not unique:

- **DR 387 Vestermarie 5** (c. 1075–1125): *Drængr gōþr dræpin ūsyni, ok Skōgi swēk saklø̄san* — a long-line followed by a full line.
- **DR 40 Randbøl** (c. 900–970): *Þēʀ stafaʀ munu Þorgunni miok længi lifa* — a long-line and full line promising the runes will long endure.
- **Sö 338 Turinge** (c. 1070–1100): A longer inscription mixing prose and verse, with long-lines and full lines in the versified praise section.

These examples confirm that **runic verse was a living practice** across the Norse world from the 10th through the 12th centuries — not an isolated phenomenon. Verse was composed for praise, for memorial, for self-assertion, and for the sheer pleasure of metrical speech. The carver of Maeshowe No. 20 composed a small verse about his own skill — and in doing so, demonstrated that the instinct toward metrical expression was present even in informal, graffiti-like contexts.

**Riverbed function for readings:** This section validates Raido's Rhythm from the archaeological record. The principle that both rhythm and alliteration are required (but occasional lapse is tolerable) is now attested in peer-reviewed analysis of an actual runic inscription. The structural flexibility of ljóðaháttr — the very meter of Hávamál — means that no rigid template is needed. What is needed is **intentional cadence**: the carver intended verse, the völva intended meter, and the reader under Raido's Rhythm intends rhythm. The intention is the magic.

## 24. The Rök Runestone: Nine Questions for Yggr — Divination's Deepest Precursor

*Source: Per Holmberg, Bo Gräslund, Olof Sundqvist, and Henrik Williams, "The Rök Runestone and the End of the World," Futhark 9–10 (2018–2019), pp. 7–38. DOI: 10.33063/diva-401040.*

This landmark article fundamentally reinterprets the Rök runestone (Ög 136), the most famous runic monument of the Viking Age. Erected c. 800 CE by Varinn for his dead son Vāmōðʀ, the stone's 700+ runes have defied coherent interpretation for 150 years. The old reading — identifying a reference to Theodoric the Great — is abandoned. In its place, the authors reveal a **unified eschatological theme**: the inscription consists of **nine enigmatic questions** addressed to the god Odin (Yggr), five about the sun and four about Odin's fate, paralleling the wisdom contest of Vafþrúðnismál. This is the most significant runological article of the decade for divinatory practice.

### 24.1 Nine Questions: The Architecture of Interrogation

The inscription is structured as six "memories" (*minni*), each marked by the formula *Sagum Ygg minni* — "Let us say a memory for Yggr." Each memory contains one or more questions, completed only when a reader provides the answer. The nine questions, with their proposed answers:

| # | Question (paraphrased) | Answer | Domain |
|---|------------------------|--------|--------|
| 1 | Which two spoils of war were taken twelve times from one another? | Sun and Moon (light exchanged) | Light |
| 2 | Who died nine generations ago but still decides the matter? | The Sun (536 CE climate crisis) | Light |
| 3 | Who suffered through a woman's sacrifice because of a howler [wolf]? | Sól (before Fenrir devours her) | Light |
| 4 | Where does the wolf see food on the battlefield, where twenty kings lie? | Vígríðr (Ragnarok battlefield) | Battle |
| 5 | Which twenty kings sat at the Grove of Sparks in four directions? | Odin's warriors (einherjar) | Battle |
| 6 | Who is a protector of sanctuaries for a brother? | Sól (protecting Máni's halls) | Light |
| 7 | Whom does the ravager [wolf] redden with blood? | Sól (Fenrir devours her) | Light |
| 8 | To whom is born an offspring? | Odin (Víðarr is born) | Battle |
| 9 | Who could beat a giant? | Víðarr (slays the wolf) | Battle |

The structure is **not random** — it alternates between memories of Light (questions about Sól's fate) and memories of Battle (questions about Odin's warriors and the wolf), mirroring the twin threats of cosmological darkness and destructive forces. The alternation enacts the cosmic rhythm itself: light threatened, light returns; battle joined, battle won.

### 24.2 Ygg minni: Memory as Ritual Act, Not Recollection

The key phrase *Ygg minni* — "a memory for Yggr" — transforms the ordinary concept of memory into something ritual and performative. *Yggr* ("the terrible one") is a byname for Odin, used specifically when he enters Vafþrúðnir's hall to begin the wisdom contest (*inn gekk Yggr þegar*, Vafþrúðnismál st. 5). These are not passive recollections but **ritual acts of social and religious significance relating to past, present, and future** that together contribute to the maintenance and renewal of the world (cf. Lincoln 1986). The memories are incomplete as text — they are **completed only through the reader's response**, making the stone an interactive ritual object, not a static inscription.

This reframes the entire understanding of runic monuments: the text does not merely *state* something but *demands participation*. The reader who can answer the questions completes the ritual act. The monument is a **site for interactive knowledge-performance**, not a billboard for narrative display. This directly extends the speech act framework of §1 above: where Zimmermann identified speech acts in individual inscriptions, the Rök stone reveals an entire **architecture of interrogative speech acts** — a sequence of directives that require the reader to become a participant.

### 24.3 The Vafþrúðnismál Parallel: Wisdom Contest as Ritual Template

The parallels between the Rök inscription and Vafþrúðnismál are extensive and precise:

- **Rök Q1–2** (sun and moon as warring parties) ↔ Vafþrúðnismál st. 23 (Máni and Sól measuring years)
- **Rök Q3** (Sól's sacrifice to the wolf) ↔ Vafþrúðnismál st. 46–47 (Álfrǫðull gives birth before Fenrir catches her)
- **Rök Q4–5** (battlefield and Odin's warriors) ↔ Vafþrúðnismál st. 17–18 (Vígríðr battlefield) and st. 40–41 (einherjar)
- **Rök Q8–9** (Víðarr avenges Odin) ↔ Vafþrúðnismál st. 53 (the wolf devours Aldafǫðr, Víðarr avenges)

In Vafþrúðnismál, Odin travels to the hall of the giant Vafþrúðnir and engages in a **wisdom contest**: each asks the other questions about cosmological knowledge, wagering their heads on the answers. The poem's final question — *hvat mælti Óðinn, áðr á bál stigi, sjálfr í eyra syni?* "what whispered Odin in the ear of his dead son before he was carried to the pyre?" (st. 54) — is unanswerable by anyone but Odin himself, making it a **guaranteed winning move** in the contest. This question about a private ritual whisper at a funeral pyre directly connects the wisdom contest to **funerary practice**: one part of the funeral rituals may have been the asking of enigmatic questions whose answers were known only by a select few, including the dead (Price 2010, 145).

### 24.4 Climate Crisis and the Fear of Recurrence

The article argues that the Rök inscription was triggered by anxiety about a **new climate crisis** similar to the catastrophic one after 536 CE. The key evidence: Question 2 specifies that the "death" occurred "nine generations ago" — roughly 270 years before c. 800 CE, placing it precisely in the early sixth century. The volcanic events of 536–547 CE caused crop failures, population decline (estimated at 50% or more in Scandinavia), and what the Norse would later mythologize as the *fimbulvetr* (the great winter preceding Ragnarok).

Four Ragnarok motifs map directly onto the post-536 climate experience:
1. **Fimbulvetr** — extreme cold (Vafþrúðnismál st. 44)
2. **Fenrir swallows the sun** — the dust veil darkened the sky (Vafþrúðnismál st. 46–47)
3. **Red skies over the gods' dwellings** — the crimson atmospheric effects of volcanic aerosols (Vǫluspá st. 40)
4. **The birth of the sun's daughter** — the longed-for return of sunlight (Vafþrúðnismál st. 46–47)

The inscription's concluding answer — Víðarr avenges Odin, the wolf is slain, the new sun shines — is not just mythology but a **hopeful eschatology**: the world ended once before, and it was renewed. The question-structure of the inscription enacts the same movement: from anxiety (will the light return?) to assurance (it always has before, and Víðarr ensures it will again).

### 24.5 The Number Nine: Cosmic Fulfilment

The number of questions — nine — is not arbitrary. Nine had a strong symbolic meaning of fulfilment in Norse tradition. Hávamál st. 138 describes Odin hanging "nine nights" on the tree. The Rök inscription mentions an event "nine generations ago." Vāmōðʀ may have been the ninth in his line since the death of the sun, making his demise a possible trigger for the fear of a new cycle. The number nine connects the inscription to the nine worlds of Norse cosmology, the nine nights of Odin's self-sacrifice, and the nine questions that encompass the full scope of cosmological knowledge — from the rhythm of light to the final battle.

### 24.6 Speech Act Shifts and the Interpersonal Dimension

The inscription is remarkable for its **constant shift between different speech acts**: statements, questions, demands, and the formula *Sagum Ygg minni* itself (a hortative — "let us say"). Previous researchers had assumed the inscription consisted primarily of statements, but the question-structure reveals that **the dominant illocutionary force is directive**: the inscription repeatedly demands that the reader provide knowledge. The final question is accompanied by the formula *Villi nē's þat* "It is not a lie" — a commissive that doubles down on the truth of what the reader must affirm. The very last word, *(H)nit* "Clash!" — is a one-word summary of the entire inscription's theme, functioning as a **declarative**: the battle is proclaimed, the victory declared, by the reader who completes the memory.

### 24.7 Axis Mundi: The Stone as Cosmic Center

The authors propose that the Rök runestone may have functioned as an **axis mundi** — a point where cosmic zones connect, where one can pass from one world to another and communicate with the other world through religious rituals, making it a potential place of enlightenment (cf. Eliade 1987, 20–65). The stone stood at a gathering place, serving cult practices and funerals. The inscription's labyrinth reading order — requiring circumnavigation of the stone, decoding of ciphers, and knowledge of mythological answers — made the monument **a challenge for a select few, and a potential place for their enlightenment**. The physical act of reading the stone was itself a ritual circumambulation.

**Riverbed function for readings:** This is the single most important article for understanding what a runic divination *is*. The Rök stone reveals that the deepest form of runic practice was not statement but **question**: the monument interrogates the reader, and the reading is complete only when the reader answers. The nine-question structure maps onto the nine worlds — each question opens a different domain of knowledge. The *Ygg minni* formula reveals that runic "memories" are not passive records but active ritual engagements requiring completion. The eschatological framework — anxiety, question, answer, renewal — is the template for every reading: the querent comes with anxiety, the runes pose questions that reframe the situation, and the completion of the reading enacts a miniature renewal of understanding. The Rök stone is the ultimate validation that runic practice is **interrogative, participatory, and eschatologically hopeful**.

## 25. God-Names on a Spindle Whorl: Invocatory Formulas Beyond the Heathen Era

*Source: Judith Jesch, "Further Thoughts on E18 Saltfleetby," Futhark 9–10 (2018–2019), pp. 201–213. DOI: 10.33063/diva-401054.*

The Saltfleetby spindle whorl (E 18), found 2010 in Lincolnshire, bears a lead inscription in Scandinavian runes reading: *× oþen . ok . einmtalr . ok : þalfa . þeir ielba . þeruolflt . ok . kiriuesf*, normalized as *Óðinn ok Heimdallr ok Þalfa, þeir hjálpa þér Ulfljót ok ...* — "Óðinn and Heimdallr and Þalfa, they are helping you, Úlfljót, and ..." Jesch's article reconsiders the dating and cultural implications of this remarkable inscription.

### 25.1 The Invocatory Formula: Three Gods, One Command

The structure of the inscription is a **triple invocation** followed by a **directive speech act** (helping). This parallels the classificatory structure identified in §1 above: the inscription is primarily a **directive** — it commands or requests supernatural assistance. The triple naming creates an escalating force: one god might be insufficient, two are stronger, three constitute a complete invocation. The third name, Þalfa, is enigmatic — it may be a previously unknown goddess or supernatural being, or a corrupted form.

### 25.2 Christian-Era Persistence of God-Names

Jesch's most significant contribution is demonstrating that the inscription's orthography — particularly *oþen* for Óðinn — is **more likely medieval than Viking Age**. The spelling *oþen* (with *o* for the stressed vowel and *e* for the reduced unstressed vowel) conforms to twelfth-century Norwegian developments, not to Viking Age usage (where *uþin* would be expected, as on the Ribe skull, DR EM85;151B). This means that **mentioning Óðinn in a runic inscription does not necessarily indicate heathen belief**.

The crucial parallel is the Bergen rune stick N B380 (c. 1185): *Heil sé þú ok í hugum góðum. Þórr þik þiggi, Óðinn þik eigi.* — "May you be healthy and in good spirits. May Þórr receive you, may Óðinn own you." This is an explicitly **post-conversion** invocation of both Þórr and Óðinn — and it is clearly Christian-era, since another Bergen stick (N B241) invokes Óðinn to reveal a thief *fyr kristni* "for the sake of Christianity." The old gods were not forgotten; they were **repurposed**.

### 25.3 Spindle Whorls as Sites of Invocatory Practice

The Saltfleetby inscription is cut on a lead spindle whorl — a domestic tool associated with women's textile work. The inscription wraps around the whorl's circumference, meaning the formula would be **recited as the whorl was turned**: the physical rotation of the tool enacts the invocation. This connects to the broader pattern of rune-inscribed spindle whorls as **sites of domestic magical practice** — small, personal objects where the boundary between practical craft and protective magic dissolved. The whorl's rotation during spinning provided a natural rhythm for recitation, making the inscription a **performative object** — its meaning activated through use.

**Riverbed function for readings:** This article provides two crucial correctives. First, it demonstrates that god-names in runic inscriptions persisted well into the Christian era — Óðinn could be invoked by a Christian Scandinavian in the twelfth century without contradiction. This means that **the boundary between "heathen" and "Christian" rune use was porous and situational**, not a clean break. A reading that references Odin need not imply pre-Christian practice; it may represent the same kind of cultural memory that the Saltfleetby carver drew upon. Second, the triple invocatory structure (*X ok Y ok Z, þeir hjálpa þér* — "X and Y and Z, they help you") is the **simplest and most direct attested form** of runic magical speech: name the powers, state the desired action. This is the bedrock formula underlying all runic invocation.

## 26. King Valdemar's Pangram: Runic Riddle, Mnemonic, and Rune-Shape Semantics

*Source: Katrín Axelsdóttir, "All the King's Runes," Futhark 9–10 (2018–2019), pp. 231–260. DOI: 10.33063/diva-401058.*

The Third Grammatical Treatise by Óláfr Þórðarson hvítaskáld (c. 1210–1259), the nephew of Snorri Sturluson, contains the **earliest extant work of runology**: a section comparing runes to letters of other alphabets. Within this section, Óláfr quotes a runic sentence attributed to King Valdemar II of Denmark, composed during Óláfr's stay at the Danish court in the winter of 1240–1241. The sentence is a **pangram** — containing all the runes of the futhark — and its meaning has been debated for centuries. Axelsdóttir proposes a radical new interpretation: the sentence is a **riddle about the b-rune's transformation into the p-rune**.

### 26.1 The Pangram as Riddle: ofljóst and Rune-Shape Circumlocution

The runic sentence reads: *sprængd mannz hok flyþi tuui boll*. Previous interpretations — a broken chin during ball-games (Arngrímur Jónsson, Ole Worm), an exhausted hawk in falconry (Wills), or a magic formula (Bæksted) — all fail on linguistic or contextual grounds. Axelsdóttir proposes:

- *sprængd* = "scattered" (participle of *sprengja* "burst, scatter")
- *mannz* = genitive of *maðr* — the **name of the m-rune** (·)
- *hǫk* = "small hooks, branches" (of a rune)
- *flýði* = "fled" (3rd person past of *flýja*)
- *tvíbǫll* = "double ball" = the **b-rune** (*bjarkan*, ‡), with its two rounded bows

Translation: **"Scattered branches of maðr (·) fled bjarkan (‡)"** — i.e., if you remove two branches of the b-rune that correspond to the m-rune's branches, the remaining shape is the **p-rune** (Ò). The sentence describes the graphic transformation **b → Ò**.

This interpretation makes the pangram an **ofljóst** — the skaldic figure of deliberate obscurity where the surface meaning conceals a hidden one. Óláfr himself discusses and praises ofljóst in the very same treatise. The figure was a favorite of skalds and learned men in the thirteenth century: *ofljóst is a wordplay, often very obscure, based on the ambiguity of words and where the reader has to delve beyond the surface meaning in order to figure out the hidden one* (Axelsdóttir, summarizing the tradition).

### 26.2 Rune-Shape Circumlocutions: A Living Tradition

The interpretation is supported by a tradition of **describing runes by their visual shape**:

- In the Third Grammatical Treatise, the s-rune is called *knésól* "knee-sun" — referring to its visual resemblance to a bent knee.
- The Icelandic rune poem uses circumlocutions for each rune name: the t-rune is *einhendr áss* "a one-handed god" (referring to Týr, or possibly to the visual resemblance between the one-armed t-rune and the two-armed a-rune).
- The y-rune is called *tvíbendr bogi* "a bow drawn twice" — referring to its double-curved shape.
- The additional þ-rune of runic calendars is called *tvímaðr* "a double man" — because it is made of two m-runes.
- A runic name-riddle by Magnús Ólafsson (c. 1573–1636) uses *öfug eik* "an upside-down oak" for the m-rune — the y-rune (ýr) turned upside down.

This tradition confirms that **rune shapes carried semantic meaning beyond their phonetic value**: the visual form of a rune was part of its identity, and could be described, riddled over, and transformed in learned discourse. The pangram extends this tradition into the highest levels of medieval scholarship — a king and a grammarian composing a riddle about a rune's shape.

### 26.3 Mnemonic Purpose: Remembering the Extended Futhark

Axelsdóttir proposes a **dual mnemonic function** for the pangram. First, it serves as a key to the extended futhark — the sixteen standard runes plus six additional characters (p, æ, g, d, z, e) — making a twenty-two-symbol row easier to memorize as a meaningful sentence rather than a raw sequence. Second, the pangram itself can be recalled by visualizing the relationship between the b-rune, the m-rune, and the p-rune: *think of b, remove the m-branches, get Ò*. This is a **visual mnemonic device** embedded in the rune-row itself.

This connects to the broader medieval tradition of **probationes pennae** (pen exercises) — pangrams used to teach students all the letters of an alphabet (Bischoff 1966). The difference is that Valdemar's pangram is not elementary; it is a sophisticated learned composition that functions simultaneously as futhark-mnemonic, shape-riddle, and scholarly amusement.

### 26.4 The Medieval Runic Tradition: Scholarship, Not Superstition

The article reveals a thirteenth-century milieu in which runes were objects of **serious scholarly inquiry**, not merely magical or practical tools. Óláfr Þórðarson compared runes to Hebrew, Greek, and Latin alphabets; he analyzed their phonetic values and graphic variants; he discussed the relationship between the standard sixteen-rune futhark and its medieval extensions. King Valdemar's interest in runes was part of a broader aristocratic fashion: the Earl Rǫgnvaldr of Orkney (c. 1103–1158) boasted of his knowledge of runes as one of his nine skills, and Valdemar's father had sent an expedition to investigate a runic inscription at Runamo. **Runes were learned, aristocratic, and intellectually respectable** in the high medieval period — not a dying folk practice.

**Riverbed function for readings:** The pangram tradition demonstrates that runes have always been **simultaneously practical, magical, and intellectual** — and that these categories overlap rather than exclude each other. A single sentence can be a pangram (practical), a riddle (intellectual), and a mnemonic device (magical-adjacent, in that it controls access to knowledge). The *ofljóst* tradition validates the principle that runic meaning is **layered**: the surface is never the whole story. When a rune appears in a reading, its shape, its name, its position in the futhark, and its transformational relationships to other runes all constitute potential layers of meaning. The b→Ò transformation described in the pangram is a model for how one rune can *become* another — a principle that underlies the entire system of rune interpretation, where context and relationship generate meaning beyond the individual sign.

## 27. Calendar Runes: Cyclical Time and Divinatory Adjacency

*Source: Carla Cucina, "A Runic Calendar in the Vatican Library," Futhark 9–10 (2018–2019), pp. 261–274. DOI: 10.33063/diva-401061.*

Cucina describes a previously lost runic calendar (Vat. lat. 14613) rediscovered in the Vatican Library in 2014. Dated 1684, it belongs to the Swedish "rune-book" type: eight wooden boards carved on both sides, bound by a cord. The calendar uses **runes to represent golden numbers and Sunday letters** — the standard perpetual calendar system of the medieval and early modern period. The feast symbols conform to the Åbo diocese; the item originates from Swedish settlements in present-day Estonia.

### 27.1 Runic Calendars: Time-Reckoning as Runic Practice

Runic calendars represent a **post-medieval continuation of rune use** in a specifically temporal context: the determination of moveable feasts, the identification of saints' days, and the tracking of seasonal cycles. The golden numbers (1–19, indicating the new moon's position in the calendar year) and Sunday letters (A–G) were represented by runes rather than Roman numerals or letters. This is not merely a decorative choice — it represents the **runification of Christian time**: the liturgical year was encoded in runes, making the futhark the medium through which sacred time was organized and accessed.

### 27.2 Divinatory Adjacency

While runic calendars are not divination tools per se, they are **divinatory-adjacent** in several respects. They encode cyclical time (lunar cycles, seasonal transitions), they use runes as indexical signs pointing beyond their phonetic value (the f-rune does not mean "wealth" here but "the first golden number"), and they serve a **practical predictive function** (determining when Easter falls, when to fast, when to feast). The calendar also includes **owner's marks (bomärken)**, the **Saint Peter's game** (a dice-like divination game inscribed on the cover pages), and onomastic initials — all of which occupy the boundary between practical inscription and symbolic practice.

**Riverbed function for readings:** Calendar runes demonstrate that runes were used to **organize and access cyclical time** — a function deeply connected to the divinatory principle that time is patterned and can be read. The futhark, inscribed on a calendar, becomes a tool for navigating the future (when will Easter fall?) through knowledge of the past (the 19-year metonic cycle). The Saint Peter's game on the calendar's cover — a divinatory game inscribed on a runic object — represents the **point where calendar-rune use and divinatory practice directly intersect**.

## 28. Single Runes as Sacred Abbreviations: Writing and Domestic Religion

*Source: Alessandro Palumbo's review of Lisbeth M. Imer, Peasants and Prayers: The Inscriptions of Norse Greenland (Copenhagen, 2017), in Futhark 9–10, pp. 287–294. Imer's original work is the primary source for the following observations.*

Imer's comprehensive study of approximately 160 runic inscriptions from medieval Greenland reveals a striking pattern: **single runes and pairs of runes on household utensils are not owner's marks but abbreviated sacred names and Christian formulas**. This interpretation, supported by Palumbo's review, has profound implications for understanding how runes functioned in everyday religious practice.

### 28.1 The m-Rune as "Mary"

The most common single rune on Greenlandic household objects — particularly loom weights — is the **m-rune**. Imer proposes that these represent abbreviations of **María** (Mary). The frequency and distribution of these m-runes across multiple settlements argues against their identification as household marks: different households in different locations would not independently choose the same single rune as their identifying mark. The m-rune, carved on a loom weight, becomes a **compressed prayer**: every time the weaver sees or touches the rune, the name Mary is invoked. The single rune is not a label but a **performative abbreviation** — it does the same work as the full prayer, compressed into a single sign.

### 28.2 Rune-Pairs as Formulaic Abbreviations

Other single and paired runes are interpreted similarly: *au* as *Ave* (the beginning of *Ave Maria*), and combinations interpreted as *Jesus Christus*. The pattern extends: crosses carved alongside these runic abbreviations strengthen the connection to the religious sphere. The Greenlandic material suggests that **runes could function as sacred shorthand**, carrying the full illocutionary force of the prayers they abbreviated. A single rune on a household tool was not decoration or identification — it was **domestic religion inscribed in the material of daily life**.

### 28.3 Writing and Domestic Religion: The Pervasiveness of Runic Practice

Imer's most significant finding is that **writing in Greenland found its platform in worship and religious life** — the high rate of religious inscriptions in the Greenlandic corpus suggests that writing and religion were inseparable in this remote community. Many religious inscriptions show a relatively low degree of literacy (errors, inconsistencies), indicating that **the content of religious formulas and prayers was used for practicing writing skills**, and that the connection between writing and domestic religion was pervasive: single and two-rune inscriptions on household utensils represent abbreviated sacred names and Christian formulas, showing "the extent and pervasiveness of runic writing in everyday life" (Imer, summarized by Palumbo).

This finding inverts the common assumption that religious writing requires specialized scribes: in Greenland, the opposite was true — **religious practice generated writing**, not the other way around. People carved runes because they prayed, not because they were trained carvers who happened to pray.

**Riverbed function for readings:** This section validates the principle that **a single rune can carry the full force of an invocation**, a prayer, or a sacred name — the completeness of the act is not dependent on the completeness of the inscription. In a reading, when a single rune appears in a dominant position, it may be functioning as what the Greenlandic carvers understood: a compressed utterance whose power is not diminished by its brevity. The m-rune on a loom weight is Mary; the *au* on a spindle whorl is *Ave*. A single rune in a reading can similarly carry an entire narrative, an entire prayer, an entire question — compressed into one sign. The Greenlandic material also demonstrates that **runes and religion were inseparable in practice**: the idea that runes are "pagan" and Christianity is "non-runic" is a modern false dichotomy. For the people who actually used runes, they were simply the available technology for writing anything — including prayers.

## 29. The Algiz/Eolh Problem: Alcis, Bogs, and the Etymology of Protection

**Author:** jafera (Post #82)

### The Eolhx Problem in the Anglo-Saxon Rune Poem

The Anglo-Saxon Rune Poem's entry for the rune usually called "Algiz" or "Eolh" presents significant translation difficulties. Two translations illustrate the problem:

**K. Gorbatenko translation:**
"The elk-sedge lives most often in a bog, / grows in water, wounds cruelly, / stains with blood each person / who in any way dares to grasp it."

**hadeken's literal translation:**
"The (X)-man (sedge, papyrus, ocean, sword, dagger) has its dwelling most often in a bog, / grows in water, wounds cruelly, / burns with blood (makes brown, colors, covers) each born person / who makes any dear touching of it."

The critical philological observation is that the disputed word in the original text is **not in the nominative case — eolh, but in the genitive — eolhx**. This means it is not "Elk-sedge" but "The sedge **of the Elk**" — i.e., a possessive construction ("Vasya's car" = the car belonging to Vasya). The word currently meaning "elk" in English (elk) is, as the dictionary notes, "an abnormal phonetic reflex of Old English eolh." Thus, historical phonetics does **not** prove that eolh = elk, leaving the field open for alternative etymologies.

### The Alcis Connection

The name of the Algiz rune may be connected to a deity of the **Naharvali** tribe, which Tacitus calls **Alcis** (Russian: Алки). In *Germania* (43), Tacitus writes: "Among the Naharvali is shown a grove consecrated to ancient cult. A priest in female dress presides over it, but of the gods they say that, if compared to Roman ones, they are Castor and Pollux. Such is their nature, but their name is Alcis. There are no images here, no traces of foreign cult; yet they are worshipped as brothers, as youths."

### The Naharvali / Silingi / Lugii Connection

Modern scholars identify the Naharvali as the same tribe as the **Silingi** (whom Ptolemy places on the same territory). The Silingi are connected to **Silesia**. Both the Silingi and the Naharvali were part of the larger **Lugii** confederation. Etymologically, **both "Silesia/Silingi" and "Lugii" mean the same thing — bog, swamp, mire:**

- Silingi: Old Polish "Ślągwa" = "wetness," "bog"
- Lugii: Serbo-Croatian "lug" = "forest in a lowland, reeds, rushes"; Polish "łąg, łęg" = "mire, bog, forest on a bog, marshy meadow"; Upper Sorbian "łuh," Lower Sorbian "lug" = "grassy bog"; even Danish "lung" = "boggy soil" and Old Prussian "Langodis" = name of a bog

Archaeological finds in Silingi territories include coins in bogs as "gifts to the Gods" (per historian Hensel), and human bones at 5m depth in Lossow (Brandenburg) indicating human sacrifice — all in Lugii territories. This directly connects the Alcis worship to the bog environment described in the rune poem.

### The Gothic *alhs* Etymology

Jacob Grimm connected Gothic **alhs** with Tacitus's Alcis. Gothic *alhs* means: (1) refuge, structure; (2) temple, sanctuary; (3) place, dwelling. Compare:
- Proto-Indo-European **\*h'elk-** = "holy place, sanctuary; idol"
- Proto-Indo-European **\*h'lek-** = "to protect"
- Lithuanian *alkas, elkas* = "sanctuary, grove"
- Latvian *elks* = "idol"
- Latin *ulciscor* = "to avenge"
- Ancient Greek ἀλκή (alkḗ) = "defense, help, strength"

This etymological chain connects Algiz to **protection, sanctuary, and sacred space** — aligning with the rune's protective interpretation rather than the "elk" reading.

### Germanic Tree Cults and Their Dual Nature

Frazer's *The Golden Bough* records that among ancient Germanic peoples, the punishment for stripping bark from a sacred tree was horrific: the offender's navel was cut out and nailed to the stripped part of the tree, then spun around the tree until their intestines wrapped completely around the trunk. In parts of Sweden, a guardian tree stood near each village from which not a single leaf could be plucked.

However, trees also **gave** fertility: Swedish peasants stuck green branches into every furrow to ensure a good harvest. Planting bushes, sticking branches, etc. was practiced for successful conception and easing childbirth.

### Who Were the Alcis?

The identity of the Alcis remains uncertain. Jafera proposes several hypotheses:

1. **Vanir hypothesis:** The "twin" myth closest to Scandinavian tradition is that of Freyr and Freyja, though they are not siblings of the same gender. Georges Dumézil saw traces of an old twin myth in the Eddic Freyr and Njörðr. In Indo-European myth, twin deities often govern fertility, which connects to incest and twin cults (Loki's accusation against Freyja in Lokasenna). The Naharvali priest wearing female dress may echo this.

2. **Óðinn hypothesis:** The sacred grove connects to Odin (with Loki as the "evil twin"). However, 700 years separate Tacitus's Alcis from the Anglo-Saxon Rune Poem, and over 1000 years from the Elder Edda manuscript — deities could have changed beyond recognition.

3. **Revised translation:** Jafera proposes that the rune poem lines should be read as: "**Algiz's man/warrior has his dwelling in a bog...**" or, if connecting to Freyr, potentially "**Algiz's sword...**"

## 37. Runic Myth and Cultural Power: How Rune Stones Generate Meaning

**Source:** David M. Krueger, *Myths of the Rune Stone: Viking Martyrs and the Birthplace of America* (University of Minnesota Press, 2015)

> **Riverbed Note:** This section does not describe rune meanings or divination technique. It documents how runic symbols function as *cultural engines* — generating identity, myth, sacrificial narrative, and communal legitimacy. This knowledge shapes readings from below: when a spread speaks of sacrifice, heritage, ancestral claims, the Other, or the boundary between authentic and manufactured meaning, the patterns described here are the riverbed through which interpretation flows.

### Rune Stones as Memorial and Commemorative Technology

Rune stones in Scandinavia were not merely inscriptions — they were **memorial devices** that created communal memory and territorial claims. Erik Wahlgren observed that rune stones were "ubiquitous in Scandinavia" and that they "date to the medieval period and were typically used to commemorate the dead. Less frequently, they were used to commemorate those who died on excursions abroad."

A rune stone does three things simultaneously:
1. **Marks a death** — it records that someone specific perished, transforming loss into inscription
2. **Claims a place** — by inscribing in stone at a location, the inscribers assert presence and belonging
3. **Creates a narrative** — the inscription tells a story that can be retold, ritualized, and reinterpreted across generations

The material constitution of the stone is critical. Mircea Eliade observed that "rock shows man something that transcends the precariousness of his humanity" and therefore conveys **power and permanence**. The rune stone's physicality — its weight, its hardness, its resistance to erosion — becomes a metaphor for the permanence of the claim it makes.

When Swedish immigrant Olof Ohman allegedly discovered the Kensington Rune Stone on his Minnesota farm in 1898, the scene itself replicated the pattern: a farmer clearing land encounters a runic inscription that authenticates his presence on that land. The stone emerges from ground already claimed by European Americans, and is interpreted as a **revelation** that God considered their ownership justified. This is the **structural function** of rune stones made visible: a runic inscription always connects a people to a place through the medium of sacrifice and inscription.

Understanding this function illuminates readings involving:
- **Othala** (heritage, homeland, ancestral property) — Othala is not just "inheritance" but the *claim* that inheritance makes upon a place
- **Eihwaz** (yew, boundary between worlds) — a rune stone is itself an Eihwaz, marking the boundary between the living and the dead
- **Raidho** (journey, right order) — the journey that a rune stone commemorates is always a Raidho that ended in a place, and that ending is the claim

### The Kensington Rune Stone Inscription: Cultural Grammar of Runic Commemoration

The Kensington Rune Stone bears an inscription on two faces. The most widely accepted translation (by Erik Wahlgren) reads:

> **Face A:** 8 Swedes and 22 Norwegians on an exploration journey from Vinland westward. We had our camp by two rocky islets one day's journey north of this stone. We were out fishing one day. When we came home we found 10 men red with blood and dead. AVM, save us from evil.
>
> **Face B:** We have 10 men by the sea to look after our ships, 14 days' journey from this island. Year: 1362.

Regardless of its authenticity (scholarly consensus is that it is a late 19th-century forgery), the inscription reveals what Scandinavian Americans in 1898 **believed a runic inscription should contain** — a window into the cultural grammar of runic commemoration:

1. **Journey as origin** — the inscription begins with a journey from a known point (Vinland) into the unknown
2. **Enumerated community** — "8 Swedes and 22 Norwegians" — a claim of organized presence
3. **Absence and return** — the catastrophe occurs during absence; the living discover the dead after the fact
4. **Sacrifice and prayer** — "AVM, save us from evil" — the inscription moves from narrative to invocation
5. **Ships and distance** — the living maintain the connection to the means of return; the journey is not yet over
6. **The date** — "Year: 1362" — fixes the event in time, making it historical and therefore authoritative

From this inscription, we can extract a structural pattern: **JOURNEY → CATASTROPHE → DISCOVERY → PRAYER → SHIPS WAITING**. This five-part structure maps onto runic aettir:
- **First Aett (Freyr):** The journey — Fehu, Uruz, Raido, Kenaz
- **Second Aett (Hagalla):** The catastrophe — Hagalaz, Nauthiz, Isa
- **Third Aett (Tyr):** The prayer and the claim — Algiz, Sowilo, Othala

### Skrælings: The Mutable Other

*Skrælingr* (plural *skrælingjar*) is the Old Norse term for the indigenous peoples encountered by Norse explorers in Vinland and Greenland. The etymology is debated — it may derive from a word meaning "dried skin" or "withered," or from "screamer/shouter."

The key insight: **the skræling is a mirror that reflects whatever a culture most fears.** Krueger documents how the meaning of "skræling" mutated across a century of American retelling:

1. **1898–1920s: The Literal Savage** — equated directly with the Dakota/Sioux who had killed white settlers
2. **1930s–1940s: The Romanticized Noble** — a nostalgic, romantic view; skrælings depicted as "vanished"
3. **1950s–1960s: The Metaphorical Threat** — skrælings as metaphor for any threat to Christian American identity
4. **1970s onward: The Erased Presence** — skrælings disappeared as cultural anxiety changed form

When a reading encounters a position about **conflict, threat, or the Other**, the skræling pattern reveals: **the identity of the adversary is not fixed — it is constructed by the querent's current anxiety.** This maps to:
- **Thurisaz** — the skræling as a real force that must be confronted
- **Hagalaz** — the skræling as uncontrolled catastrophe
- **Nauthiz** — the skræling as the condition that forces change
- **Eihwaz** — the skræling as the liminal figure between known and unknown

### The Girardian Scapegoat and Runic Narrative

Krueger applies René Girard's theory of mimetic violence and scapegoating to the Kensington Rune Stone phenomenon. The Kensington Rune Stone narrative functioned as a **ritual scapegoating device**: crisis → scapegoat identification → ritual repetition → peace through sacrifice.

The Girardian framework reveals: **a rune reading is always a story about sacrifice and meaning.** The question is not whether sacrifice appears in the spread (it always does) but **who is sacrificed, who identifies the scapegoat, and what peace the sacrifice purchases.**

This shapes interpretation of:
- **Algiz** — can indicate genuine protection or the projection of threat that justifies pre-emptive action
- **Tiwaz** — always involves sacrifice (Tyr's hand given to Fenrir), but whose sacrifice, and for what justice?
- **Gebo** — the scapegoat mechanism is a dark Gebo: the community "gives" the scapegoat in exchange for social peace
- **Jera** — the cyclical return of the scapegoat mechanism: each new crisis produces a new scapegoat

### Innocent Domination and Runic Legitimation

The Kensington Rune Stone story reveals a specific mechanism by which runic symbols **authorize claims to land, identity, and power**, operating through four components:

**1. Antiquity as Authority** — The older the claim, the more legitimate it appears. In runic terms, this is the power of **Othala** — but Othala's authority is constructed by the narrative that surrounds it.

**2. Sacrifice as Title** — The "10 men red with blood and dead" function as a title deed. Their sacrifice purchases the right to the land. In runic terms, this is the dark side of **Eihwaz** and **Algiz**.

**3. The Other as Disqualifier** — The skrælings as "savages" disqualify indigenous claims. **Thurisaz** always defines the boundary of the community — the community exists in opposition to what lies beyond the thorn-hedge.

**4. Divine Sanction as Closer** — The "AVM, save us from evil" prayer was the seal that closed the claim. In runic terms, this is **Ansuz** deployed as the final word that makes all preceding words authoritative.

The reader's responsibility is to recognize:
- That **antiquity claims** (Othala) are constructed, not inherent
- That **sacrifice narratives** (Eihwaz, Algiz) can be weaponized
- That **the Other** (Thurisaz) is always a construction
- That **divine sanction** (Ansuz) is the most powerful and most dangerous legitimation

### Myth vs. History: The Pattern of Belief Maintenance

The book documents how belief in the rune stone was maintained despite mounting evidence against it. The five-stage pattern describes how **any belief system maintains itself against contradictory evidence** — including the beliefs that querents bring to a rune reading:

1. **Scholarly rejection** — Nearly all linguists and runologists concluded it was a hoax
2. **Amateur counter-argument** — Alternative evidence generated
3. **Institutional legitimation** — The Smithsonian displayed the stone (1948–49)
4. **Retreat to coded meaning** — Elaborate coded readings: cryptograms, hidden maps, Knights Templar
5. **Equation of faith with identity** — Challenges received as attacks on the community

Understanding this pattern helps the reader recognize when a querent is seeking genuine insight versus seeking confirmation of an existing narrative.

### The Enduring Pattern: Runic Myth as Living System

The deepest insight Krueger offers: **the power of a runic symbol does not depend on its authenticity.** It depends on its capacity to function as a vessel for collective meaning. The Kensington Rune Stone "worked" — not as history, but as myth. It created identity, community, sacrificial narrative, and sacred space for an entire region of Americans across a century.

A rune reading participates in the same system: it is an inscription that generates meaning through the interplay of symbol, narrative, sacrifice, and the construction of the Other. The reader's task is:
- To recognize when the reading is providing **genuine insight** versus **comforting myth**
- To distinguish between **authentic patterns in the spread** and **patterns the querent projects**
- To understand that **the querent's relationship to the reading** is itself a form of runic legitimation
- To hold the tension between the reading's function as meaning-generator and the reader's responsibility to the truth of the symbols

## Part III: The Language of the Earliest Runic Inscriptions

## 38. Makaev 1965: The Language of the Earliest Runic Inscriptions

*Source: Э.А. Макаев, "Язык древнейших рунических надписей" (The Language of the Earliest Runic Inscriptions), 1965*
*Category: Scholarly / Linguistic / Runic Epigraphy*

Makaev's work is one of the few comprehensive linguistic studies of the Elder Futhark inscriptions, analyzing approximately 150 inscriptions from the continent, Sweden, Denmark, and Norway. It provides the scholarly foundation for understanding the language of the earliest runic texts.

---

### 38.1 Scope and Methodology

Makaev distinguishes three chronological groups:
- **Древнейшие рунические надписи** (Earliest runic inscriptions): 3rd–5th centuries CE
- **Старшие рунические надписи** (Elder runic inscriptions): all inscriptions using the 24-rune Elder Futhark, roughly 3rd–7th centuries
- **Рунические надписи переходного периода** (Transitional period inscriptions): 6th–8th centuries

**Key observation**: The fragmentary nature and limited quantity of surviving early inscriptions (approximately 150) does not allow exhaustive description of all linguistic and graphic features, nor detailed tracing of the evolution of linguistic traits across the scattered material. Makaev explicitly warns against "fantastic and baseless constructions" (фантастические и беспочвенные построения) that plague runology.

---

### 38.2 The Scholarly Foundation of Runology

Runology as a discipline was founded by Danish scholar **L. Wimmer** in the 1870s. Major contributors include:
- **S. Bugge** — Early interpretation of inscriptions
- **M. Olsen** — Norwegian runic scholarship
- **C. Marstrander** — Defined runology as "paleography, linguistics, archaeology, and mythology"
- **O. von Friesen** — Swedish runology
- **E. Brate** — Swedish inscription analysis
- **I. Lindqvist** — Early Swedish inscriptions
- **W. Krause** — The definitive corpus of elder runic inscriptions
- **L. Jacobsen** — Danish runology
- **E. Moltke** — Danish runic inscriptions
- **H. Andersen** — Chronological analysis

Makaev's work was the first comprehensive linguistic analysis since **F. Burg's 1885 study** (based on approximately 60 inscriptions), updating the analysis to include ~150 inscriptions and applying modern comparative Germanic grammar.

---

### 38.3 Linguistic Status of Early Runic Inscriptions

The central question of Makaev's work: **What is the linguistic affiliation of the earliest runic inscriptions?**

Key findings:
- The language of the earliest runic inscriptions cannot be unambiguously assigned to any single Germanic language (Proto-Norse, Gothic, etc.)
- It represents a **conservative Northwestern Germanic** linguistic stage, preserving archaic features that had already been lost in some branches
- Early runic shows features of both **North Germanic** and **Northwest Germanic** but is not identical to either
- The inscriptions demonstrate a remarkably uniform linguistic character across a wide geographic area (continent, Scandinavia), suggesting a **standardized ritual/magical language** rather than colloquial speech

### 38.5 Key Linguistic Features of Early Runic

### Phonology
- Preservation of ** Proto-Germanic *z** (rendered as ᛉ, often transcribed R) — this sound had already become r in other Germanic languages
- **ai** and **au** diphthongs preserved (later monophthongized in Norse)
- **e** vs. **i** distinction maintained in positions where later Norse merged them
- The **j** rune (ᛃ) indicates a palatal glide that was still phonemically distinct

### Morphology
- Early runic preserves the **Proto-Germanic nominative singular masculine *-az** ending (e.g., ᚺᚨᚺᚹᚨᛉ = HaþuwolafaR)
- **Feminine *-ō** endings preserved
- Verb forms show archaic conjugation patterns

### Lexicon
- Many inscription words have no certain etymology — they may represent a specialized magical/ritual vocabulary
- Words like **alu**, **laukaz**, **ehwaz** appear repeatedly with unclear precise meaning — the standard translations ("ale," "leek," "horse") may mask ritual/magical significances
- Personal names form the largest category of legible content

---

### 38.6 Chronological Principles

Makaev notes that **principles of chronology** for runic inscriptions and their relationship to archaeological periodization remain unresolved. The final resolution depends on:
- Accumulation of more inscription material
- Development of general Germanic lexicography, especially stratigraphy
- Improvement of archaeological dating methods
- Refinement of paleographic interpretation techniques
