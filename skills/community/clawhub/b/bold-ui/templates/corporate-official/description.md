# Corporate Official — Design Brief

## Visual Identity

Corporate Official projects trustworthiness and competence. It's the design equivalent of a well-tailored suit — professional, polished, and quietly confident. Deep navy blues anchor the palette while warm gold accents signal premium quality. The layout breathes generously, letting content command attention without visual noise.

## Core Design Language

**Colors**: Deep corporate blue (#1E40AF) establishes authority and trust. Gold (#D97706) provides warm, premium accents — used sparingly for CTAs, highlights, and key metrics. Backgrounds are clean off-white (#F9FAFB), never stark white. The palette communicates stability, not excitement — this is for decisions, not entertainment.

**Shapes**: Moderate rounded corners (8px default). Not too sharp (feels aggressive), not too round (feels casual). The "Goldilocks" radius that says "we're professional but approachable." All cards, buttons, and containers share consistent rounding.

**Shadows**: Clean, professional shadows — enough to create card hierarchy without drawing attention to the shadow itself. Cards lift slightly on hover with a subtle shadow increase. No colored glows, no dramatic effects.

**Typography**: Inter for everything — it's the most versatile corporate typeface, highly legible at all sizes. Headings use bold/semibold for authority, body text is comfortably readable. The heading scale is generous — hero headings can go to 44px to create impact on landing pages.

**Motion**: Conservative and smooth. Transitions are present but understated — fast (150-200ms) with standard ease-in-out. No spring effects. No dramatic reveals. Motion should convey reliability — a door that opens smoothly, not one that bursts open.

## Key Visual Signatures

1. **Hero gradient**: The hero section uses a deep navy gradient — from almost-black blue to vibrant blue. This diagonal gradient creates a premium, modern feel without being flashy. White text on this gradient is highly legible and impactful.

2. **Card-based layouts**: Content is organized into clean cards with subtle borders and shadows. 3-column card grids are the default for feature showcases. Cards have generous padding (24-32px) and clear hierarchy.

3. **Trust signals**: Testimonial sections, client logos in grayscale, statistics with large numbers + small labels. These elements build credibility through visual design — numbers are large and bold, logos are uniformly sized and spaced.

4. **Gold accent CTAs**: The primary CTA buttons use the gold color — it stands out against the blue/white palette without screaming. Hover states deepen the gold slightly. This is the most "premium" touch.

5. **Section rhythm**: Landing pages alternate between white and light gray sections to create visual rhythm. Each section has a clear heading, subheading, and content area — no ambiguous sections.

## When to Adapt

- **SaaS dashboards**: Tone down the hero, tighten spacing, use blue as the primary UI color with gold for key metrics/notifications only
- **Mobile**: Stack the 3-column cards into single column, reduce heading sizes, maintain the same color and radius tokens
- **Dark mode**: Invert to deep navy (#0F172A) background, white surfaces become dark navy (#1E293B), keep blue and gold accents unchanged
- **Multi-page sites**: Add a sticky navigation bar with the blue gradient background, white text links, and the logo left-aligned

## Anti-Patterns (avoid these)

- Don't use bright/saturated colors — the palette should feel stable and mature
- Don't use playful animations — no bounces, no springs, no dramatic reveals
- Don't use thin fonts — corporate design needs weight and presence
- Don't crowd sections — generous whitespace signals confidence
- Don't add decorative elements — every visual element should serve the content
- Don't use pure black text — always use gray-900 for slightly softer readability
