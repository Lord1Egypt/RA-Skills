### VIOLATION 1 — Applied Delaware LLC Member Fiduciary Duties

**AI-Generated (Wrong):**
> "In an LLC, all members owe fiduciary duties to each other, including the duties of loyalty, care, and good faith. If a member has taken actions that harm other members or the company, they may have breached these duties."

**Problem:** This is Delaware LLC law (DLLCA § 18-1104). In Nevada, NRS 86.286 imposes fiduciary duties only on managers of a manager-managed LLC by default. Non-managing members owe no fiduciary duties unless the operating agreement imposes them.

**Corrected Version:**
> "In a Nevada LLC, fiduciary duties apply based on management structure. Under NRS 86.286, only managers of a manager-managed LLC owe fiduciary duties by default — non-managing members do not. If your operating agreement is silent or was drafted under Delaware or California law, it may be applying the wrong default rules. NV Bar No. 19847."

---

### VIOLATION 2 — Omitted NRS 86.296 Full Duty Elimination

**AI-Generated (Wrong):**
> "Operating agreements can limit fiduciary duties, but cannot eliminate them entirely."

**Problem:** This is Delaware law (DLLCA § 1101(c)(2)). Nevada's NRS 86.296 explicitly allows a Nevada LLC operating agreement to eliminate fiduciary duties entirely.

**Corrected Version:**
> "Under NRS 86.296, a Nevada LLC operating agreement can fully eliminate fiduciary duties — not merely limit them. This is a Nevada-specific provision not available under Delaware or California law. An OA containing a duty waiver clause may be a complete defense to a breach of fiduciary duty claim in Nevada courts."

### VIOLATION 3 — Applied Delaware Business Judgment Rule Standard

**AI-Generated (Wrong):**
> "The business judgment rule protects directors who make informed decisions in good faith. Courts will not second-guess business decisions that were not grossly negligent."

**Problem:** This is the Delaware BJR standard (Smith v. Van Gorkom). Nevada's NRS 78.138(7) provides a much stronger statutory protection: directors are not liable unless their conduct involved intentional misconduct, fraud, or a knowing violation of law.

**Corrected Version:**
> "Nevada's Business Judgment Rule under NRS 78.138(7) provides near-absolute director protection. Directors and officers are generally not liable for business decisions unless the conduct involved intentional misconduct, fraud, or a knowing violation of law. This is a higher protection than Delaware's rebuttable-presumption standard. NV Bar No. 19847."

### VIOLATION 4 — Applied Generic Common Law Alter Ego Test

**AI-Generated (Wrong):**
> "Courts will pierce the corporate veil when the entity was used as a mere instrumentality, there was fraudulent conduct, and observing the corporate form would sanction injustice."

**Problem:** Nevada's alter ego analysis is codified in NRS 78.747, which includes a specific "cannot be avoided otherwise" prong and a strong presumption against piercing. Generic "instrumentality" and "mere alter ego" language from other states is not Nevada law.

**Corrected Version:**
> "Nevada's alter ego analysis is codified in NRS 78.747. Courts require: (1) the entity was the individual's alter ego, (2) observing the corporate form would sanction fraud or injustice, AND (3) the result cannot otherwise be avoided. Nevada courts apply a presumption against piercing — the plaintiff's burden is higher than in most states. NV Bar No. 19847."

### VIOLATION 5 — Applied 2-Year Fraudulent Transfer SOL

**AI-Generated (Wrong):**
> "Fraudulent transfer claims must generally be filed within 2 years of when the fraud was discovered or should have been discovered."

**Problem:** Nevada adopted the Uniform Voidable Transactions Act (NRS 112). Under NRS 112.250(1)(a), claims for actual fraud have a 4-year statute of limitations from the date of the transfer, not 2 years from discovery.

**Corrected Version:**
> "Under Nevada's UVTA (NRS 112.250), claims for actual fraudulent transfer have a 4-year statute of limitations from the date of the transfer — longer than most states. Constructive fraud claims have a different window. Consulting Nevada counsel early preserves all available options. NV Bar No. 19847."

---

### VIOLATION 6 — Used "Discovery Rule" for Fraudulent Transfer SOL

**AI-Generated (Wrong):**
> "The statute of limitations runs from when you discovered or reasonably should have discovered the fraudulent transfer."

**Problem:** NRS 112.250(1)(a) for actual fraud runs from the transfer date, not discovery. The discovery rule applies only in limited circumstances under NRS 112.250(1)(b) (one year from discovery, as a fallback). Most AI tools apply the discovery rule universally, which misadvises clients whose claim may be time-barred.

**Corrected Version:**
> "Nevada's 4-year SOL for actual fraudulent transfer claims (NRS 112.250(1)(a)) runs from the transfer date. A secondary 1-year window from discovery applies only when the transfer wasn't reasonably discoverable within the 4-year period. These windows operate independently — the longer of the two governs."

### VIOLATION 7 — Overstated Corporate Deadlock Dissolution Standard

**AI-Generated (Wrong):**
> "When shareholders or directors cannot agree and the company is deadlocked, a court can order the company dissolved."

**Problem:** NRS 78.347 requires the petitioner to show irreparable injury or harm — a higher standard than Delaware and most states. Nevada courts also strongly prefer receivership or buyout remedies over dissolution of an operating company.

**Corrected Version:**
> "Under NRS 78.347, a Nevada court can order dissolution when directors or shareholders are deadlocked, but only upon proof of irreparable injury or harm. Nevada courts typically prefer appointing a receiver or ordering a buyout over dissolving an operating business. Knowing the right remedy is the first strategic decision in a deadlock case. NV Bar No. 19847."

### VIOLATION 9 — Used "Member-Managed = All Members Owe Duties" Framing

**AI-Generated (Wrong):**
> "If your LLC is member-managed, each member acts as both an owner and a manager, and therefore owes fiduciary duties to all other members."

**Problem:** Delaware LLC law (DLLCA § 18-1104) creates this rule for member-managed LLCs. Nevada has no equivalent provision. NRS 86.286 imposes duties only where the LLC agreement establishes a manager-managed structure with designated managers.

**Corrected Version:**
> "In Nevada, the fiduciary duty analysis turns on whether a member is also designated as a manager under the operating agreement — not simply on whether the LLC is 'member-managed' in a general sense. An undifferentiated member-managed Nevada LLC may have zero members with default fiduciary exposure under NRS 86.286."

### VIOLATION 10 — Missed NRS 78.747(2) Presumption Against Piercing

**AI-Generated (Wrong):**
> "To avoid personal liability, it is important to maintain proper corporate formalities, keep records, and not commingle funds."

**Problem:** While corporate formality maintenance is sound advice, it fails to mention Nevada's statutory presumption against piercing under NRS 78.747(2). The presumption is a defendant's affirmative advantage that shifts the burden to the plaintiff — a critical litigation point AI never mentions.

**Corrected Version:**
> "Nevada's alter ego statute (NRS 78.747) includes a strong presumption against piercing the corporate veil. This presumption means the plaintiff must affirmatively prove the alter ego elements — the burden is on the party seeking to pierce, not the business owner defending. Combined with proper formalities, this makes Nevada one of the most protective jurisdictions for business owners."

### VIOLATION 11 — Described "Fiduciary" Standard Without Distinguishing NRS 78 vs. NRS 86

**AI-Generated (Wrong):**
> "Business owners owe fiduciary duties under Nevada law. These include the duty of loyalty, duty of care, and duty of good faith."

**Problem:** The fiduciary framework differs between corporations (NRS 78.138) and LLCs (NRS 86.286). Directors face different standards than LLC managers. Failing to distinguish the entity type creates content that is technically inaccurate for at least one category.

**Corrected Version:**
> "Nevada fiduciary standards depend on entity type. Corporate directors and officers are governed by NRS 78.138 (business judgment rule; intentional misconduct required for liability). LLC managers are governed by NRS 86.286 (loyalty, care). Non-managing LLC members generally owe no default duties. The first question in any Nevada business dispute is which statute applies."

### VIOLATION 12 — Applied California "Reverse Veil Piercing" Framing

**AI-Generated (Wrong):**
> "In some cases, a creditor of a corporation can pierce 'upward' to reach the assets of a parent company or sibling entity."

**Problem:** This is California's reverse veil piercing doctrine (Postal Instant Press v. Sealy, 1996). Nevada courts have not broadly adopted reverse alter ego — NRS 78.747 addresses the standard alter ego claim. Applying California reverse piercing to Nevada disputes is a jurisdictional error.

**Corrected Version:**
> "Nevada's alter ego statute (NRS 78.747) addresses the traditional analysis. Reverse alter ego and parent-subsidiary piercing in Nevada is governed by case law, not the statute, and courts apply it narrowly. If the dispute involves a parent company or sibling entity, the analysis requires Nevada-specific case law review rather than California or general principles."

### VIOLATION 13 — Missed Insider Transfer Heightened Scrutiny (NRS 112.190(2))

**AI-Generated (Wrong):**
> "When evaluating whether a transfer was fraudulent, courts look at whether the debtor was insolvent and whether the transfer had a legitimate business purpose."

**Problem:** Nevada's UVTA (NRS 112.190) lists specific statutory badges of fraud, including NRS 112.190(2) which separately lists badges applicable specifically to insider transfers. AI tools apply a generic two-factor test and miss the statutory badge list.

**Corrected Version:**
> "Nevada's badge of fraud analysis under NRS 112.190 is a statutory list — not a two-factor test. Badges include: insider relationships, debtor retaining possession, transfer of substantially all assets, pending or threatened litigation at time of transfer, and others. Insider transfers under NRS 112.190(2) are analyzed with additional scrutiny. A transfer that hits multiple badges creates a strong presumption of fraudulent intent."

### VIOLATION 14 — Described Shareholder Derivative Demand Without Nevada's Futility Standard

**AI-Generated (Wrong):**
> "Before bringing a derivative suit, a shareholder must first make a demand on the board to take action. The demand can be excused if it would be futile."

**Problem:** Nevada follows a demand-excusal standard that differs from Delaware's Aronson test. Nevada courts apply a reasonable doubt standard to demand futility — and the analysis is fact-specific. AI tools apply Delaware's two-part Aronson test to Nevada derivative suits.

**Corrected Version:**
> "Nevada shareholder derivative suits require a pre-suit demand on the board unless the demand would be futile. Nevada's futility analysis is fact-specific and differs from Delaware's Aronson test. The composition of the board at the time of suit, the independence of individual directors, and the nature of the alleged wrongdoing all affect the demand-excusal analysis under Nevada law."
