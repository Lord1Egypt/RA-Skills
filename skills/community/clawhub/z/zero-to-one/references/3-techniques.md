# Techniques: The Seven Questions and Building a Great Company

## The Seven Questions Framework

Every startup must answer all seven questions. A "no" on any one is fatal.

| # | Question | What it tests |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Engineering | Can you create a breakthrough technology, not just incremental improvement? |
| 2 | Timing | Is now the right time to start this business? |
| 3 | Monopoly | Are you starting with a big share of a small market? |
| 4 | People | Do you have the right team? |
| 5 | Distribution | Do you have a way to deliver your product, not just build it? |
| 6 | Durability | Will your market position be defensible 10-20 years from now? |
| 7 | Secret | Have you identified a unique opportunity that others don't see? |

**How to use it**: Write a one-paragraph answer for each question. If you can't answer any of them confidently, that area needs more work.

> **Case: Tesla's 7 for 7** (Chapter 13): "Tesla answered all seven questions. Engineering: electric powertrain breakthrough. Timing: started during the cleantech boom when battery tech was improving. Monopoly: started with the high-end Roadster (tiny market, dominant share). People: Elon Musk and a world-class engineering team. Distribution: owned stores, no dealerships. Durability: brand and technology moat grew with each model. Secret: cleantech wasn't just about being green — it was about building cars people WANTED to drive, even if they didn't care about the environment."

## The Power Law in Action

Venture capital follows a power law: a small number of investments generate almost all returns. Apply the same logic to your own business.

**Practical application:**
- Focus on the 1-2 activities that drive 80%+ of your results
- Cut the rest ruthlessly
- When evaluating opportunities, ask: "Is this a power-law bet or a linear bet?"

> **Case: The venture fund** (Chapter 10): "Most venture funds make 30-50 investments knowing that 2-3 will generate all returns. The best VCs know this and focus their time on the outliers. Thiel's Founders Fund applies the same logic: invest in the few companies that could change the world."

## The Founding Team Checklist

When building a founding team, check:

- [ ] Do the co-founders know each other well before starting? (Existing relationships > founder dating)
- [ ] Do they share a clear, contrarian vision?
- [ ] Is equity split fairly? (Equal splits avoid early resentment)
- [ ] Do they complement each other's skills?
- [ ] Do early employees share the mission, not just the salary?

## The Distribution Ladder

Not all distribution is equal. Match your sales model to your product:

| Product Price | Sales Model | Example |
|---|---|---|
| $1M+ | Complex sales (CEO-to-CEO) | Palantir's government contracts |
| $10K-$100K | Direct sales team | Enterprise software |
| $1K-$10K | Sales support + self-service | Mailchimp |
| $1-$100 | Viral marketing / freemium | Dropbox, WhatsApp |

> **Case: Palantir's complex sales** (Chapter 11): "Palantir sells data analysis software to intelligence agencies and large corporations. A single sale can take 18 months and involve the CEO personally. This is not scalable in the traditional sense, but it is the only model that works for complex, high-stakes products. The key insight: choose your distribution model before you build your product, because your product's design must be consistent with how you sell it."
