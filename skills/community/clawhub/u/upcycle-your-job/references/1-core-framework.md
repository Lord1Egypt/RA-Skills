## The PROPEL Model — 6-Step Framework

PROPEL is a research-based coaching model for working mothers to upcycle their corporate careers instead of quitting. Each step builds on the previous one.

### Step P: Preferences — Who Are You?

Before you change anything, understand how you naturally manage the work-life interface.

**The Three Types (Ellen Ernst Kossek's framework):**
- **Separators** — Keep work and home psychologically and physically separate. Focus on work at work, home at home. Sub-types: Work Firsters (prioritize work even at home) vs Family Firsters.
- **Integrators** — Blend work and personal life. Comfortable checking emails after dinner. The "multitasking myth" was born from working mothers forced to integrate.
- **Vollyers** — Switch between strategies based on circumstances. E.g., integrate during school terms, separate during holidays. The most flexible — and most demanding — approach.

**Warning signs your preference is being violated:**
- **Reactor** (forced Integrator): You want separation but feel pushed to integrate — working on your day off, taking calls during family dinner. This is the most common trap for working mothers.
- **Captive** (forced Separator): You'd prefer integration but your culture demands physical presence — can't WFH even occasionally, expected to be in office 9-6.
- **Job Warrior** (forced Vollyer): Travel or client demands force you to cycle between strategies against your will.

**Essential diagnostic tool:** Where on the Separator↔Integrator continuum do you naturally sit? Where are you being forced? The gap between preference and reality = your stress.

> **Case: Laura's 7pm boundary** (Chapter 3): Laura was the higher earner in her marriage with a long London commute. She told her boss she'd be unavailable after 7pm until 9am — no emails, no texts. She was surprised by his positive response. She then used her commute home for finishing work tasks, and took "a few mindful minutes" at her front door to switch into parent mode before entering. Result: she felt more present with family and discovered new energy for work.
> **Key takeaway:** You can set boundaries even in demanding senior roles. The act of asking often reveals more flexibility than assumed.

> **Case: Justine's Family Friday** (Chapter 3): Justine designated Friday evenings as "Family Friday" — no technology, just chatting and watching a film. Then "Super Saturday" with board games, and "Strolling Sunday" for family walks. Her quote: "Because there is a focus it stops drift…I'm now spending real quality time with [my children]."
> **Key takeaway:** Forced boundaries (not passive intentions) are what create separation. Name the time, protect it.

### Step R: Roles — Rewrite Your Scripts

We play multiple roles (parent, employee, partner, friend, carer) — each with a "script" we learned unconsciously.

**Donald Super's Life Career Rainbow:**
- Nine universal roles: child, student, leisureite, citizen, worker, spouse/partner, homemaker, parent, pensioner
- Each role has **temporal importance** (time commitment) and **emotional involvement** (energy/feeling commitment)
- The rainbow metaphor: roles wax and wane through life stages. Too many "vibrant colors" at once = burnout.

**Script Rewriting Technique:**
1. Identify the script: "Where did I learn what a 'good mother' looks like? What about 'good employee'?"
2. Check if it's current: "Does this script still serve my life today?"
3. Try on new behavior: Professor Herminia Ibarra suggests "trying on a new persona" lightly — see how it fits before committing
4. Renegotiate with stakeholders: Changes in your role impact others — talk to them

**Role Conflict vs Role Enrichment:**
- **Conflict** (time-based, behavior-based, or strain-based) — depletes energy, creates guilt, lowers performance
- **Enrichment** — roles act as "psychological buffers." Success at work → more relaxed parenting. Loving family → better workplace resilience.
- **Your mindset matters:** "I've got too much to do and it's exhausting" = conflict frame. "These roles enrich the totality of my life" = enrichment frame.

> **Case: Emma's promotion timing** (Chapter 4): Emma had wanted a promotion for years but doubted she could manage the balance. After coaching she felt ready — except her son was about to change schools. She recognized the emotional load would spike during his integration, so she deliberately deferred the promotion for one year and used that time to research job-share possibilities. The rainbow: her "mother" role needed more intensity temporarily; she chose not to clash it with "worker" intensity.
> **Key takeaway:** Timing matters. The Life Career Rainbow helps you see when to push and when to wait.

### Step O: Options — Know What's Possible

Audit your employer's culture, policies, and practice to identify realistic entry points.

**The First Law of Cybernetics:** "The unit within the system with the most behavioral responses available to it controls the system." The more options you generate, the more control you have in negotiation.

**Strategy:**
1. **Survey the landscape** — What flexible working arrangements already exist? Who's using them? (Often "under the radar" — invisible success stories)
2. **Find the chinks in the cultural armor** — Which managers already flexible? Which policies exist but aren't used? Which competitors are leading?
3. **Build a three-level business case:**
   - **Personal:** What's in it for your manager? Retention, performance, reduced recruitment cost
   - **Team:** How will this arrangement improve team outcomes?
   - **Organizational:** How does this align with stated DEI, wellbeing, or productivity goals?

> **Case: The hidden expertise in the manager survey** (Chapter 5): When consulting, Meller's first step is a manager survey on flexible working experience. The amount of "corporate expertise" that emerges is often surprising — managers who've successfully managed flexible teams in previous roles. These are the allies who can help shift culture.
> **Key takeaway:** Don't assume nobody's done it before. Find the hidden success stories.

### Step P: Possibilities — Job Craft Your Way Forward

This is the practical redesign step. Turn your flexible working request into a concrete job proposal.

**Techniques:**
1. **Work Out process** (adapted from GE): List all your tasks. Identify redundant/low-value tasks. Discard or delegate them. Focus on high-value outputs.
2. **Outputs-based framing:** Instead of "I want to work 4 days," propose "I will deliver X, Y, and Z within these parameters." Shift from hours to deliverables.
3. **Job Crafting (Wrzesniewski & Dutton):** Employees naturally make small adjustments to their jobs — changing physical, relational, or cognitive boundaries. Formalize this.
4. **Possibility generation:** Create 3 distinct options before you negotiate (part-time, compressed hours, job-share, term-time, WFH, output-based contract). More options = more control.

> **Case: The social workers' 15-minute shift** (Chapter 4): A group of local authority social workers were required in the office by 9am, which conflicted with safely dropping children at school. After group coaching, they negotiated a single adjustment: starting 15 minutes later. This small change resolved the conflict.
> **Key takeaway:** Don't over-engineer. Sometimes the smallest adjustment is the breakthrough.

### Step E: Essential Skills — The Upcycled Skill Set

Meller identifies 7 skills that need upcycling for flexible senior role success. These are NOT new skills — they're existing management skills applied in a new context:

1. **Boundary management** — Knowing when to separate and when to integrate; setting and communicating boundaries clearly
2. **Negotiation** — Framing requests as business propositions, not personal concessions
3. **Delegation** — Letting go of low-value tasks; trusting others; managing perfectionism
4. **Time management for outputs** — Shifting from hour-counting to deliverable-focus
5. **Communication** — Being explicit about availability, expectations, and boundaries without apologizing
6. **Resilience** — Managing the emotional load of pioneering; dealing with visibility and potential criticism
7. **Self-coaching** — Using the PROPEL model periodically to reassess and adjust as life changes

### Step L: Leadership — The Balanced Leader Model

The final reframe: your flexible working arrangement is not a concession — it's an act of leadership.

**Professor Herminia Ibarra's insight:** To grow into the leader you want to become, act like that leader first. You don't think your way into a new way of acting — you act your way into a new way of thinking.

**Professor Stew Friedman's Total Leadership (Four-Way Wins):**
Work × Home × Community × Self — aim for excellent performance in all four domains. Get clear on what you want from each and what you can contribute. Experiment with small changes. Manage boundaries intelligently.

**The Balanced Leader identity:**
- You're a visible role model for others
- Your pioneering arrangement creates proof that senior flexible roles work
- Your courage in asking changes the conversation
- You are leading the shift from "Always On" to "Balance Is Possible"
