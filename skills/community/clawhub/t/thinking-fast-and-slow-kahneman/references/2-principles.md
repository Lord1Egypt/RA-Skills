> Source: Thinking, Fast and Slow by Daniel Kahneman, Parts 2-4

# Key Principles

## Principle 1: Cognitive ease is a warning sign

When something feels easy to think about (because it's familiar, clear, or emotionally resonant), System 1 embraces it. But ease is not a signal of truth. It is a signal of processing fluency.

> **Case: The font experiment** (Part 2, Chapter 5): Participants were asked to judge the truth of statements. Some were printed in a clear, easy-to-read font; others in a faint, hard-to-read font. People judged the easy-to-read statements as more likely to be true — even when the content was the same. Cognitive ease = belief. Cognitive strain = skepticism.
> **Key takeaway**: If a decision feels too easy, force System 2 to engage. Slow down. Question the fluency.

## Principle 2: Anchoring is everywhere

The first number you encounter exerts a gravitational pull on all subsequent judgments. This is true whether the anchor is relevant or not — and even when you know it's a bad anchor.

> **Case: The real estate experiment** (Part 2, Chapter 10): Experienced real estate agents were shown a house and asked to estimate its value. They were also shown a "listing price" (the anchor). Even though the agents insisted the listing price didn't influence them, estimates moved 30-40% toward the anchor. Expert knowledge did not protect against anchoring.

## Principle 3: Losses loom larger than gains

Prospect theory's core insight: the pain of a loss is about 2-2.5 times the pleasure of an equivalent gain. This asymmetry explains why people make seemingly irrational financial decisions — they are more motivated by avoiding loss than by achieving gain.

> **Case: The coin toss experiment** (Part 4, Chapter 25): Kahneman offers participants a gamble: a 50% chance to win $200 and a 50% chance to lose $100. The expected value is positive ($50). Most people refuse. Why? Because the pain of losing $100 outweighs the pleasure of gaining $200. The asymmetry is built into our emotional wiring.

## Principle 4: The outside view is the antidote to overconfidence

When planning, the inside view focuses on your specific situation. The outside view looks at the base rate: what happens to people in similar situations? The outside view is almost always more accurate.

> **Case: The curriculum planning team** (Part 3, Chapter 23): A team of experts spent a year planning a new curriculum. They estimated completion in 18 months. Kahneman asked: "What is the base rate for similar curriculum development projects?" The answer: 7-10 years, and 40% fail entirely. The team's inside view was wildly optimistic. The outside view was sobering.

## Principle 5: The remembering self makes decisions; the experiencing self lives them

We experience life moment by moment, but we make decisions based on stories constructed from peak moments and endings. This "peak-end rule" means that a longer painful experience can be remembered as less painful if the ending is improved.

> **Case: The cold water experiment** (Part 5, Chapter 35): Participants held their hand in painfully cold water for 60 seconds, then again for 90 seconds (with the last 30 seconds slightly warmer). When asked which they'd repeat, the majority chose the longer 90-second trial — because the improved ending created a better memory, even though the experience was objectively worse.
> **Key takeaway**: Life decisions are driven by the remembering self, not the experiencing self. To make better decisions, you must understand which self is in control.
