# Competitor Analysis Methodology

## Core definition

Competitor analysis is not about reading a lot. It is about using others' experience to solve our problem.

Minimal formula:
1. Clarify our problem
2. Find the right analysis objects
3. Break down why they succeeded or failed
4. Derive conclusions that are useful to us

Question to keep asking:
> What does this actually help us decide?

## Six-word method

**Information → Analysis → Conclusion**

Rules:
- Information must be relevant to the problem
- Analysis must target the essence, not the surface
- Conclusion must be actionable

## Five review questions

1. What problem are we solving?
2. Which information is truly useful for solving it?
3. Where did the information come from?
4. Is the analysis subjective or objective; surface-level or root-cause level?
5. What concrete help does the conclusion provide for this project?

If these cannot be answered, the analysis is weak.

## Competitor role map

### Core competitor
Highly overlaps with us in target user, positioning, category, product, or spend.
Use for direct comparison and gap diagnosis.

### Benchmark competitor
Bigger / stronger / more established.
Use to learn direction, standard, system capability, and category-leading logic.

### Potential competitor
Not as large, but strategically sharp.
Use to identify emerging tactics or underpriced opportunities.

### Substitute competitor
Different category, same higher-order job-to-be-done.
Use when competition comes from demand substitution, not direct category overlap.

### Avoidance competitor
A negative case showing what not to do.
Use to clarify risk boundaries and non-replicable mistakes.

## Three-layer decomposition

### Fact layer
Ask:
- What exactly did they do?
- What was the sequence?
- How did they allocate channels, budget, people, or product effort?

### Motive layer
Ask:
- Why did they do it?
- What background made this necessary?
- Why now?
- What was the strategic path?

### Effect layer
Ask:
- What results did they get?
- What changed in user cognition or business performance?
- What factor really drove success / failure?

## What strong conclusions look like

Strong conclusions answer all three:
- What should we borrow?
- What should we avoid?
- What should we do next given our own context?

## Recommended report structure

1. Project problem statement
2. Competitor selection logic
3. Competitor role map
4. Cross-case comparison table or bullets
5. Deep dives on the most relevant cases
6. Root insight / decisive variables
7. Action recommendations
