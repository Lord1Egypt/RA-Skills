# Legal Precedents in Construction Claims

Legal precedents and case law can influence how construction claims are interpreted and decided. Their importance and application vary significantly between legal systems.

> **Important caution:** Specific legal precedents are highly jurisdiction-specific. Always seek advice from qualified legal counsel familiar with the governing law of the contract.

---

## Common Law vs Civil Law Approach

| Aspect | Common Law | Civil Law |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| **Source of law** | Judicial precedents are usually an important source | Codified statutes and general principles are central |
| **Previous decisions** | Binding or highly persuasive | Persuasive but not applied in the same binding way |
| **Focus** | Wording of the contract, previous case interpretation | Wording of civil code, fairness, contractual intention |
| **Good faith** | Developing but not uniformly applied | Central principle in most civil law systems |

---

## Key Areas Where Precedents Commonly Influence Claims

- **Notice requirements and time bars** — what constitutes valid notice and whether strict time bars are enforceable
- **Prevention Principle** and related good faith concepts
- **Concurrent delay** — dominant cause, apportionment, and first-in-time arguments
- **Quantum methods** — acceptance of Eichleay, Hudson, Emden, actual cost, and total cost approaches
- **Global or total cost claims** — often treated cautiously if poorly substantiated
- **Mitigation duty** — extent to which a party must take reasonable steps to reduce loss

---

## The Prevention Principle — Practical Explanation

The Prevention Principle is generally understood as a doctrine that prevents a party from taking advantage of its own wrongdoing or breach to the detriment of the other party.

### In Common Law

Classic examples include situations where the Employer causes delay and then seeks to enforce the original contractual completion date or liquidated damages for the affected period. The principle often interacts with notice requirements and extension of time clauses.

### In Civil Law

Equivalent protections may arise through duties of good faith and fairness. The exact approach depends on the governing law and project-specific contract terms.

### Practical Implications for Claims

- If claiming an Extension of Time due to Employer-caused events, prevention or good faith arguments may support the primary contractual entitlement
- Such arguments may assist where the Employer's own conduct made strict compliance difficult
- They should **not** be used as a substitute for notice compliance where notice was reasonably possible
- Always verify the current status and relevance of any case or precedent relied upon
