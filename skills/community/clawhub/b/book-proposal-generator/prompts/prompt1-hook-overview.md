# Prompt 1: Hook & Overview (FREE)

**Copy and paste this prompt into Claude, ChatGPT, or any AI assistant.**

---

## THE PROMPT

```
You are a literary agent with 20 years of experience at a top-tier agency (think CAA, WME, Writers House, Curtis Brown). You have read and evaluated over 10,000 book proposals. You know exactly what makes agents read past the first paragraph and what makes them delete.

I need you to write the Hook & Overview section of my nonfiction book proposal. This is the most critical section — it's what agents read in 30 seconds to decide if they continue. It must be gripping, specific, and commercially sharp.

Here is my book information:

**Book Topic:** [Describe your book's topic in 2-3 sentences]
**Core Argument / Central Thesis:** [What is the ONE big idea your book argues or proves?]
**Why This Book Exists:** [What problem does it solve? What transformation does it create?]
**Your Background:** [Why are you uniquely qualified to write this book? Be specific — credentials, experience, access, lived story]
**Target Reader:** [Who is this book for? Be specific — not "everyone" — describe one real person]
**Intended Tone:** [Circle one: Prescriptive / Narrative / Hybrid / Academic-Accessible]

Please produce:

1. **TITLE OPTIONS (3 variants)**
   For each variant provide:
   - Main title (3-6 words, bold, memorable)
   - Subtitle (benefit-forward, 8-12 words, includes target reader + transformation)
   - One sentence explaining the strategic logic behind this title choice

2. **THE HOOK (1 sentence)**
   The agent-ready elevator pitch. Format: "[Book title] is a [category] for [specific reader] that [core promise/transformation]." Under 30 words. Every word must earn its place.

3. **ONE-PARAGRAPH SUMMARY (250-300 words)**
   This is the query letter core. Structure:
   - Opening: A specific scene, statistic, or provocative statement that drops the reader into the problem
   - Middle: What the book argues, how it argues it, what evidence or stories ground it
   - Close: What the reader will have, know, or be able to do after reading this book
   No vague language. No "this book explores." Every sentence must move.

4. **WHY THIS BOOK, WHY NOW (150-200 words)**
   Make the cultural/market case for this book existing in this moment. Reference:
   - A specific trend, shift, or crisis that makes this topic urgent
   - A gap in existing books (what's missing that this fills)
   - Why the window for this book is NOW and not in 3 years

5. **WHY YOU (100-150 words)**
   The author credibility section. Answer:
   - What gives you the right to write this book?
   - What unique access, experience, data, or story do you have that no one else does?
   - What have you already done with this material? (spoken, consulted, researched, lived it)
   Be specific. Agents are suspicious of vague credentials.

6. **BOOK SPECS**
   - Estimated word count: [recommend based on category]
   - Format: [hardcover / trade paperback / both]
   - Comparable titles: [3 books, published last 5 years, same shelf]
   - Projected completion: [realistic timeline from signed deal]

Write as a polished proposal section ready to paste directly into an agent submission portal. Professional but not stiff. Confident but not arrogant. Commercial but not cheap.
```

---

## How to Use This Output

1. Fill in all 6 bracketed inputs before running
2. Review all 3 title options — pick your favorite or use as inspiration
3. The one-paragraph summary becomes your query letter body
4. The "Why Now" and "Why You" sections paste directly into your proposal document
5. Run Prompt 2 next with your comp titles to complete the market analysis

---

## Pro Tips

- **On titles:** Agents love titles that are clear AND surprising. "Atomic Habits" is both obvious and fresh. Avoid clever puns or titles that only make sense after you read the book.
- **On the hook:** Read it out loud. If it sounds like a press release, rewrite it.
- **On "Why You":** A personal story > a credential. "I helped 500 IT managers" hits harder than "I have an MBA."
- **On word count:** Trade nonfiction typically runs 60,000-80,000 words. Under 50K signals thin content. Over 100K signals poor editing discipline.
