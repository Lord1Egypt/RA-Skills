## Author Voice & Personality

### David Hogg's Voice
- **Self-aware narcissist turned activist:** He openly admits he was "a jerk," "pretentious," "arrogant" before Parkland. His voice is raw, self-deprecating, brutally honest.
- **Born contrarian:** "I am a born contrarian. The good part of that is stepping back from normal-kid life allowed me to find my own way."
- **Speed + momentum obsessed:** His personal motto was "push like hell to get it moving, then it takes on its own momentum."
- **Key phrase:** "Action is therapeutic."

### Lauren Hogg's Voice
- **Emotional, vulnerable, precise:** Her descriptions of hiding during the shooting are visceral — "the look in those kids' eyes," the "subhuman" sound she made when she saw her friends' faces on TV.
- **No-nonsense clarity:** When she joined the movement, she didn't ask permission. She walked into Cameron's house and said, "I need to do something."
- **Key phrase:** "We do not need comfort, we need change."

### Emma González's Implied Voice
- Described by others as "our Buddha," "peaceful radiance," "unflappable and eloquent."
- Silent protest at the March for Our Lives — stood without speaking for 6 minutes and 20 seconds, the length of the shooting.

### Cameron Kasky's Voice
- "Power source." Fast-talking, improvisational, brave. Climbed on tables to calm a riot four days after the shooting.
- Key line on Bill Maher: "We don't respect you just because you have 'senator' in front of your name."

## 5 Common Scenarios

### Scenario 1: First 48 Hours After a Traumatic Event
**What to do:**
1. Survive first. Get to safety. Breathe.
2. The person who can act should act. The person who needs to grieve should grieve. Both are valid.
3. Record what happened if you can — photos, videos, notes. Not for trauma-porn. For evidence and storytelling.
4. Find 3-5 people who feel the same urgency. Go to someone's house. Talk.
5. Do NOT wait for a perfect plan. Do one thing tomorrow.
6. Prepare for trolls immediately. They will come within hours.

### Scenario 2: The First Media Interview
**What to do:**
1. Have one message. Say it three times.
2. Be boring on their terms, explosive on yours.
3. Wear something distinctive.
4. Ignore the host's framing. Answer the question you want.
5. After the interview: post the clip on your own channels immediately.

### Scenario 3: Your Movement Is Being Attacked Online
**What to do:**
1. Determine: is this a troll (ignore), a media figure (clap back with facts), or a politician (name and shame)?
2. If you must respond: one line. Sharp. Shareable. Never defensive.
3. Tag their advertisers if they cross the line.
4. Do NOT write a long thread. Long threads = losing.
5. Laugh at the absurd ones. Let the internet laugh with you.

### Scenario 4: Internal Conflict or Burnout
**What to do:**
1. Name it: "This is entropy. The universe wants us to fall apart. Let's not give it to them."
2. Have the loudest person in the room (Cameron/David equivalent) call a reset.
3. Remind everyone why they're together. Read the names of who you're fighting for.
4. Let people take breaks. The movement must survive its members.
5. If someone is toxic, have the hard conversation — or assign them to a solo task.

### Scenario 5: Lobbying or Meeting a Politician
**What to do:**
1. Know their voting record. Cite specific bills.
2. Bring a prop or visual (March for Our Lives attendees wore $1.05 price tags — Rubio's per-student NRA contribution).
3. Ask a direct, yes/no question. Do not let them filibuster.
4. If they dodge: ask again. On camera.
5. If they attack you afterward: you've won. Use the clip.
