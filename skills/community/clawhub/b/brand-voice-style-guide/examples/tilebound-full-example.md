# Brand Voice & Style Guide Example
## TileBound — Premium Tile & Stone Design Studio (Las Vegas)

*A complete worked example running all 4 prompts end-to-end.*

---

## Inputs

**Business name:** TileBound  
**Industry:** Home renovation / interior design / e-commerce + local studio  
**Target audience:** Homeowners (35–60, HHI $100K+) remodeling kitchens/bathrooms; interior designers sourcing materials for clients; luxury home builders  
**3 adjectives:** Confident, tactile, honest  
**Brands they admire:** Patagonia (quality storytelling), Aesop (intentional restraint), Field Notes (craft-obsessed without pretense)  
**Brands that feel "wrong":** Home Depot (big box impersonality), Wayfair (discount-feel), HGTV (style over substance)  
**Biggest communication problem:** "We come across as either too salesy or too plain. Our tiles are stunning but our copy doesn't convey it."

---

## Prompt 1 Output — Brand Voice Discovery & Personality Profile

### Section 1: Brand Archetype

**Primary:** Creator  
**Secondary:** Sage

TileBound is a maker brand — obsessed with material quality, craftsmanship, and the idea that a well-chosen surface changes how a room feels. The Creator archetype fits because the brand believes in the transformative power of good design decisions made with intention. The Sage secondary shows up in the brand's commitment to educating customers on material properties, installation realities, and long-term value rather than selling the fantasy.

### Section 2: Tone Spectrum Position

| Spectrum | Score | Rationale |
|----------|-------|-----------|
| Formal (1) ↔ Casual (10) | 4 | Approachable but not breezy. Never uses slang. Warm, not corporate. |
| Serious (1) ↔ Playful (10) | 3 | Design is taken seriously here. Occasional dry wit, never jokes. |
| Authoritative (1) ↔ Approachable (10) | 5 | Equal parts expert and collaborator. We know materials; you know your home. |
| Reserved (1) ↔ Bold (10) | 6 | Confident about quality claims. Never shouts. Lets the product lead. |
| Technical (1) ↔ Plain-language (10) | 4 | Uses correct terminology but always explains it. No jargon gates. |

### Section 3: Brand Personality Adjectives

**1. Tactile**
*For TileBound:* We write like you can feel the material through the screen — weight, texture, temperature, and finish are always present in the language.
*Example:* "The honed finish on this Calacatta marble has the quiet coolness of a library floor. Run your hand across it once and you'll understand why it costs what it costs."

**2. Unhurried**
*For TileBound:* We don't rush the customer to a decision or the copy to a CTA. Good rooms take time. Good writing does too.
*Example:* "There's no deadline on getting this right. Take the sample home. Live with it in different light. Come back when you're certain."

**3. Honest**
*For TileBound:* We say what the tile can and can't do. Porcelain near a pool? Yes. White grout in a kitchen with kids? We'll tell you the truth.
*Example:* "This travertine is stunning. It also needs sealing every 18–24 months. If that's not your kind of maintenance, we have better options for you."

**4. Precise**
*For TileBound:* Every measurement, material spec, and installation note is stated accurately. No "approximately." No vague "durable."
*Example:* "Slip resistance: DCOF 0.42 wet. Safe for pool decks and outdoor showers. We tested it."

**5. Restrained**
*For TileBound:* One strong claim per paragraph. No superlatives. No exclamation points. The confidence is in what we don't feel the need to say.
*Example:* "Italian quartzite. 3cm thick. The slab we keep reordering."

### Section 4: Sounds Like Us / Never Sounds Like Us

**Sounds Like Us:**
1. "This floor will outlast the house. That's not marketing — it's what 30mm porcelain does." *(Demonstrates: Honest + Precise)*
2. "We don't carry 4,000 tiles. We carry 400 we'd put in our own homes." *(Demonstrates: Restrained + Honest)*
3. "The grout joint width matters more than most people realize. Let's talk about it before you order." *(Demonstrates: Tactile + Unhurried)*

**Never Sounds Like Us:**
1. ❌ "Transform your space with our AMAZING selection of tiles — shop now for incredible deals!"  
   ✅ "The right tile changes a room. Ours are chosen for the rooms they'll outlast."

2. ❌ "Elevate your home's aesthetic with our luxurious tile solutions for discerning homeowners!"  
   ✅ "Good materials don't need to announce themselves. They just make everything around them look considered."

3. ❌ "We offer competitive pricing and fast shipping on thousands of SKUs!"  
   ✅ "Delivery in 5–7 days. We'll call you the day before to confirm the window."

### Section 5: Brand Voice Manifesto

You are the person in the room who knows materials deeply and lets that knowledge do the talking. You don't oversell. You don't undersell. You describe exactly what something is, how it behaves, and what it asks of the person who chooses it. You write like you're standing next to the customer in the showroom — not behind a keyboard trying to convert them. Your sentences are short because good design doesn't need explanation. Your paragraphs breathe because good rooms do too. When you're done, the reader should feel like they just spoke with someone who genuinely knows tile and genuinely doesn't care if they buy today, as long as they buy right.

### Section 6: One-Line Voice Summary

TileBound's voice is **a knowledgeable craftsperson who respects the customer's intelligence, tells the truth about materials, and writes with the same intentionality they expect from a well-designed room.**

## Prompt 2 Output — Writing Style Rules & Grammar Guide

### The 20 Writing Rules

**Sentence & Paragraph Structure**

1. **Sentence length: 8–18 words on average.** Short sentences land facts. Longer sentences carry texture and context. Never let the average exceed 20 words.  
   DO: "This is a 3cm slab. It doesn't flex. It doesn't chip."  
   DON'T: "Our premium 3cm thick slab tiles are engineered for exceptional durability and are highly resistant to chipping and flexing under normal use conditions."

2. **Paragraph length: 2–4 sentences max on web/email. 1 sentence is acceptable when it earns it.**  
   DO: "The finish is everything. Ask for the matte sample."  
   DON'T: [5-sentence product description paragraph with no breaks]

3. **The one-sentence punch.** Every product description, email, and page should have at least one sentence that stands alone and could be a headline.  
   DO: "Some tiles are chosen. This one earns its place."  
   DON'T: Multiple medium sentences with no single standout.

4. **Active voice, always.** We describe what the tile does, not what is done to it.  
   DO: "The matte finish hides fingerprints."  
   DON'T: "Fingerprints are concealed by the matte finish."

5. **Vary your rhythm.** Short. Then medium length. Then short again. Reading TileBound copy should feel like walking through a well-designed room — varied, intentional, never monotonous.

**Punctuation & Formatting**

6. **Oxford comma: yes.** Precision is the brand. "Length, width, and depth" not "length, width and depth."

7. **Em dash for emphasis and apposition.** Use it sparingly — once per paragraph maximum. Never use ellipses (too hesitant for this brand).  
   DO: "The grout — not the tile — is usually the maintenance problem."  
   DON'T: "The grout... is usually the maintenance problem..."

8. **No exclamation points.** Ever. In any channel. If the news is exciting, let the words be exciting.  
   DO: "The Calacatta Gold is back in stock."  
   DON'T: "The Calacatta Gold is back in stock!"

9. **Capitalization: sentence case for headers.** Capitalize proper nouns (stone names like Calacatta, Statuario), brand name (TileBound), and product lines. Job titles lowercase unless in a byline.

10. **Bullet lists: max 5 items, always parallel structure.** If you're writing specs, use a table instead of bullets.

**Numbers, Dates & Data**

11. **Numerals always for measurements, specs, prices.** "3cm", "0.42 DCOF", "$147/sq ft" — never spelled out. Spell out numbers under 10 in narrative text ("three options," "five finishes").

12. **Dates: Month DD, YYYY.** No ordinals ("April 18, 2026" not "April 18th, 2026").

13. **Data with context.** Never state a spec without explaining why it matters.  
    DO: "DCOF 0.42 wet — the threshold for commercial wet areas is 0.42. This tile qualifies."  
    DON'T: "DCOF 0.42."

**Tone Consistency**

14. **Reading level: Grade 8–10 (Flesch-Kincaid).** Educated audience, not academic copy. Test before publishing.

15. **Humor: dry, rare, earned.** One wit moment per email max. No puns. No memes. Subtle observation only.  
    DO: "We've seen this bathroom remodel go three different directions in one showroom visit. We've got time."  
    DON'T: "Tile puns? We've got loads! You'll be floored by our selection! 😄"

16. **Empathy: state the real concern, then address it.** Don't guess at what the customer worries about — name it directly.  
    DO: "White tile and kids is a real conversation. Here's what we'd actually recommend."  
    DON'T: "We understand your concern and are happy to assist!"

17. **CTAs: invitations, not commands.** Soft verbs. We're not in a hurry.  
    DO: "Request a sample." / "Come see it in the showroom." / "We can send you options."  
    DON'T: "Shop Now!" / "Buy Today!" / "Don't Miss Out!"

**Voice-Specific Rules**

18. **Open with the thing, not the adjective.** Start product descriptions and emails with what something is or does — not how stunning, luxurious, or beautiful it is.  
    DO: "This is a 12x24 matte porcelain that reads as stone without stone's maintenance demands."  
    DON'T: "Beautiful and luxurious, this stunning porcelain tile..."

19. **Name the tradeoff.** Every material has one. Stating it builds trust.  
    DO: "Marble is porous. It needs sealing and it will etch with acidic cleaners. The people who love it love it anyway."  
    DON'T: [no mention of maintenance reality]

20. **End on the material, not the sale.** Final sentences in product descriptions and emails should be about the product, not the CTA.  
    DO: "This is the slab that turns a bathroom into the reason you bought the house."  
    DON'T: "Order yours today and transform your space!"

### Brand Vocabulary

**Preferred Terms (use these)**
- "slab" not "piece" or "section"
- "finish" not "look" or "style"
- "material" not "product" (for tile)
- "installation" not "putting it in"
- "maintenance" not "upkeep" or "care"
- "matte" not "flat" or "dull"
- "honed" not "smooth" (for stone)
- "grout joint" not "gap" or "space between tiles"
- "field tile" (the main tile), "trim tile" (edge/border)
- "substrate" not "floor underneath"
- "slip resistance" not "non-slip"
- "DCOF" (Dynamic Coefficient of Friction) — always spell out on first use
- "bookmatched" (two slabs mirrored like an open book)
- "variation" not "inconsistency" (natural stone varies — that's a feature)
- "natural stone" not "real stone" (all materials are real)

**Banned Terms (never use)**
- "luxurious" / "luxury" → use "premium" or just describe the material
- "stunning" → describe what makes it striking
- "amazing" → state the specific quality
- "affordable" → we compete on value, not price
- "transform" → be specific about what changes
- "elevate" → overused in design marketing
- "aesthetic" as a noun ("the aesthetic") → say what you mean
- "solutions" → we sell tile, not solutions
- "synergy" → never
- "curated" → everything in every store is "curated" now; means nothing
- "best-in-class" → cite the spec instead
- "seamless" (unless describing literal groutless installation)
- "one-stop shop" → we're not
- "bespoke" → use "custom" or describe the specific customization
- "game-changer" → never

**Jargon Policy**
Always use correct technical terms (DCOF, PEI rating, ANSI standards). On first use, add a one-phrase definition in parentheses. In email and social, lead with plain language, add the spec after.

### Headline Formulas

**Blog posts:** [Material/situation] + [counterintuitive truth or question]  
Examples: "Marble in the kitchen: what we actually recommend" / "Why your grout joint width matters more than the tile" / "The case for matte in a high-traffic bathroom"

**Email subject lines:** [Specific material or situation] + [honest claim or question]  
Examples: "The Calacatta Gold is back" / "Three tiles for outdoor kitchens that actually hold up" / "A question before you order white tile"

**Social:** [Observation or fact] + [invitation to learn more]  
Examples: "This is what 30mm porcelain looks like after 15 years" / "The finish you almost chose, and the one we'd pick instead"

**Ad headlines:** [Specific outcome] + [honest qualifier]  
Examples: "A bathroom floor that outlasts the renovation" / "The tile your designer keeps asking about" / "Porcelain that reads as stone. Without stone's maintenance."

## Prompt 3 Output — Platform Adaptation Guide (Selected Platforms)

### Website (Homepage, Product Pages, About)

*Tone adjustment:* Most formal expression of the brand. Full brand voice — no shortcuts. This is where the voice is defined.

*Format rules:*
- Headlines: 6–12 words
- Product descriptions: 50–120 words
- No emoji
- No hashtags
- CTAs: soft ("Request a sample" / "Visit the showroom")

*Content pillars:* Material education, project storytelling, honest product specs, studio culture.

*DO:* "Statuario marble has been pulled from the same quarry in Carrara since the 1500s. This slab came out of that mountain this year."  
*DON'T:* "Shop our amazing collection of premium marble tiles for your next project!" (right words, wrong energy)

---

### Instagram

*Tone adjustment:* Dial up tactile and visual language. Shorter captions. The image carries the weight — copy reinforces, doesn't repeat.

*Format rules:*
- Caption: 60–150 words for feed posts, 1–3 sentences for Reels
- 5–8 hashtags (material + design + city-specific)
- No direct price in caption (DM for pricing)
- One soft CTA per caption ("Link in bio" / "Come see it in person")

*Content pillars:* Material close-ups, installed shots, before/after (real projects only), material education (carousel), studio life.

*DO:* "This is what happens when you let the slab decide. Bookmatched Statuario, master bath, Henderson. The vein runs uninterrupted for 14 feet. #naturalstone #marblebathroom #lasvegas #tiledesign"  
*DON'T:* "Check out this STUNNING marble tile available now in our showroom! DM us for pricing on all our LUXURIOUS options! 😍✨"

---

### Email

*Tone adjustment:* Most personal voice. Write like one human to another. First name if segmented. No corporate "we are pleased to inform you."

*Format rules:*
- Subject line: 6–9 words, no emojis, no ALL CAPS
- Body: 150–300 words for newsletters; under 100 for transactional
- Plain text preferred; HTML only if image is essential
- One CTA per email
- Sign with a real name (not "The TileBound Team")

*DO:* Subject: "The travertine you asked about is in"  
Body: "It arrived Thursday. We put a slab on the floor of the studio and haven't moved it. If you're still thinking about it, now's a good time to come see it..."

*DON'T:* Subject: "EXCITING NEWS from TileBound! 🎉 New arrivals just dropped!"

---

### Customer Support

*Tone adjustment:* Warmest, most direct, no corporate language. Acknowledge first. Explain second. Fix third.

*Format rules:*
- First response under 4 hours M–F
- Under 50 words for simple questions; up to 150 for complex issues
- No canned opening ("Thank you for contacting TileBound...")
- Sign with first name only

*DO:* "Hi Sarah — that's a fair concern. The variation you're seeing in those photos is normal for natural travertine. Happy to pull your specific lot and send you photos before we ship. Want me to do that?"  
*DON'T:* "Dear Valued Customer, Thank you for reaching out to TileBound. We apologize for any inconvenience and are happy to assist you with your inquiry."

## Prompt 4 Output — Brand Voice Example Bank (Selected)

### Side-by-Side Rewrites

**Sample 1 (existing email):**  
❌ Original: "We are excited to announce the arrival of our new Calacatta Gold marble collection! These beautiful tiles are perfect for any luxury kitchen or bathroom project. Shop now while supplies last!"  
✅ On-Brand: "The Calacatta Gold we've been waiting six months for landed this week. We've got it in 12x24 and full slabs. The vein pattern on this lot is particularly clean — worth seeing in person before you commit to a size."  
*What changed:* Removed "excited to announce," "beautiful," "luxury," and urgency tactic. Added specific details (sizes, vein pattern, wait time) that signal genuine expertise. Last sentence invites rather than commands.

**Sample 2 (product description):**  
❌ Original: "Transform your bathroom into a luxurious oasis with our stunning Bianco Bello porcelain tile! Features a beautiful matte finish that's easy to clean and maintenance-free!"  
✅ On-Brand: "Bianco Bello. A 24x48 matte porcelain that reads as Italian marble without marble's maintenance demands. The rectified edge makes for tight grout joints — 1/16" is achievable. No sealing. No etching from cleaners. Wipe it down and it looks exactly the same in ten years."  
*What changed:* Removed all adjectives that don't carry information. Added spec (size, grout joint capability), honest comparison (vs. real marble), and a specific durability claim with a timeframe.

### New Writer Onboarding Quiz

**Q1:** You're writing a product description for a matte black porcelain. Which opening is most on-brand?

A) "Elevate your space with this stunning matte black porcelain tile!"  
B) "This 12x24 matte black porcelain has a DCOF of 0.45 wet — qualified for bathroom floors and outdoor showers."  
C) "A timeless matte black option for modern and transitional spaces."

**Answer: B.** Opens with specs and context. No adjectives doing the work that facts could do. *(A is off-brand: "elevate" + "stunning" + exclamation. C is off-brand: vague design-speak.)*

**Q2:** A customer emails saying they're upset their order arrived two weeks late. Which reply fits TileBound?

A) "Dear Valued Customer, We sincerely apologize for any inconvenience caused by your recent order delay..."  
B) "Hi — two weeks is too long and I'm sorry it happened. Can you confirm your order number? I want to look at this myself."  
C) "We're so sorry! We totally understand your frustration and are committed to making this right ASAP!"

**Answer: B.** Acknowledges, owns it, takes personal action. No corporate language. No exclamation points. *(A is off-brand: "valued customer" + "any inconvenience." C is off-brand: "so sorry!" + "ASAP!")*

**Q3:** You're writing an Instagram caption for a dramatic marble slab installation. Which fits TileBound?

A) "OBSESSED with how this turned out! 😍😍 Stunning Calacatta marble bathroom — who else is loving this look? Drop a ❤️ below!"  
B) "Calacatta Gold, master bath, Paradise Valley. The bookmatched slab runs floor to ceiling — that decision took about 30 seconds to make. #naturalstone #marblebath #luxurydesign"  
C) "Our latest project featuring premium Calacatta Gold marble tile installation. Contact us for a free quote on your next project."

**Answer: B.** Specific (client, location, material). Tells a story (the decision moment). Hashtags relevant, not excessive. No exclamation points. *(A is off-brand: emoji overload, engagement bait. C is off-brand: corporate voice, immediate sales push.)*

**Q4:** Which CTA is most on-brand for TileBound?

A) "Buy Now — Limited Stock!"  
B) "Transform Your Space Today"  
C) "Request a sample and live with it for a week"

**Answer: C.** Invites, doesn't command. Gives the customer permission to take time. Specific action. *(A is off-brand: urgency tactic + command. B is off-brand: "transform" + vague.)*

**Q5:** A new tile arrives that you're personally excited about. Which email subject line fits TileBound?

A) "NEW ARRIVAL: You'll LOVE This! 🎉"  
B) "The slab we've been waiting four months for is here"  
C) "Introducing Our Stunning New Italian Marble Collection!"

**Answer: B.** Honest, specific, gives context (four months = rare/worth waiting for). No hype. *(A is off-brand: all caps + "love" + celebration emoji. C is off-brand: "introducing" + "stunning" + "collection".)*
