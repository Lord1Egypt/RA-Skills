## Pre-Trial Preparation

### Document Checklist

| Category | Documents | Quantity |
|----------|-----------|:--------:|
| Identity | Lawyer's license (original + copy) | 2 copies |
| Authority | Power of attorney, law firm letter | 2 each |
| Case materials | Complaint/appeal/answer | 1 each |
| Evidence | Evidence index + evidence book (one per defendant + third party) | N+1 sets |
| Legal authorities | Compiled relevant statutes (print key provisions) | 1 set |
| Trial outline | Questioning outline + cross-examination outline + debate outline | 1 set |
| Comparable cases | Favorable precedents (print judgments) | 1 set |
| Recording tools | Laptop / pen and paper | 1 each |

### Predicting Disputed Issues

Before trial, assess:
1. Judge's likely focus areas (based on case type)
2. Opponent's likely defenses
3. Weaknesses in opponent's evidence (3-dimension test)
4. Favorable legal application interpretations
5. Procedural issues the judge may raise

### Questioning Outline Preparation

**Questioning your own client/witness:**
- Guide the party to state favorable facts
- Avoid open-ended questions (prefer yes/no questions)

**Questioning the opponent/witness:**
- Design logical trap questions
- Closed questions primarily (yes/no only)
- One fact per question, avoid compound questions

**Questioning experts:**
- Confirm professional qualifications
- Question basis and methodology of opinions
- Question procedural compliance
- Question logical reasoning from facts to conclusions

## Trial Procedure Breakdown

### 1. Session Opening
- Verify party identities
- Inform parties of procedural rights and obligations
- Ask about recusal applications
- Announce collegial panel composition

### 2. Court Investigation (Core Stage)

**Plaintiff/Prosecution Statement:**
- Claims → Facts & reasons → Evidence index
- Note: Be concise, not a full reading
- Emphasize key facts and disputed issues

**Defendant/Defense Statement:**
- Defense position → Defense facts & reasons
- Response to plaintiff's claims (accept fully/partially/reject fully)

**Evidence Presentation and Cross-Examination:**

Presentation method:
- General: one by one or by group
- Complex cases: grouped by disputed issue
- Cross-examine immediately after each group

Cross-examination order:
- Opponent presents evidence → Opponent states evidence name + source + purpose
- Your cross-examination opinion → address three dimensions sequentially

**Judge's Questions:**
- Focus on disputed issues, directed at both parties
- Answer concisely and directly
- If unsure, "I will verify after trial" is a legitimate response

### 3. Court Debate

First round:
- Focus on disputed issues identified by the court
- Factual argument first, then legal application
- Complete logic chain: major premise (law) → minor premise (facts) → conclusion

Second round (if any):
- Address opponent's core arguments from first round
- Do not repeat first round content
- Focus on rebutting opponent's key errors

**Basic Debate Structure:**
```
State position → Factual support (cite evidence) → Legal analysis (cite statutes) → Rebut opponent → Reiterate conclusion
```

### 4. Final Statement

- Brief and focused, 30 seconds to 1 minute
- Summarize core position
- Civil: "Request the court to support plaintiff's claims" / "Request the court to dismiss plaintiff's claims"
- Criminal: "Request leniency/mitigated punishment" or "Request a not guilty verdict"

### 5. Mediation (Civil Cases)
- Prepare mediation bottom line
- Assess opponent's willingness
- Weigh judgment risk vs. mediation outcome

## Trial Defense Strategies

### Common Defense Types

| Type | Applicable Scenario | Core Strategy |
|------|-------------------|--------------|
| Complete denial | All opposing facts are false | Rebut each point, provide counter-evidence |
| Partial admission | Some facts true, some denied | Clearly state admission scope, focus on disputes |
| Legal application defense | Facts admitted but legal characterization disputed | Cite correct statutes and comparable cases |
| Procedural defense | Jurisdiction/statute of limitations/standing | Address procedure first, do not enter substantive debate |
| Counterclaim strategy | Opponent also breached/tort | File counterclaim, shift offense-defense dynamic |

### Offense-Defense Principles

**Offense (Plaintiff/Prosecution):**
1. Burden of proof is on you → Complete evidence chain with logical closure
2. Anticipate opponent's defenses → Prepare rebuttals in advance
3. Highlight contradictions in opponent's evidence → Undermine their proof system

**Defense (Defendant/Defense):**
1. Undermine opponent's evidence three-dimension test → Show evidence is unreliable
2. Establish your own version of facts → Provide counter-evidence
3. Legal application rebuttal → "Even if facts exist, they do not constitute..."

### Cross-Examination Techniques

**Attacking Authenticity:**
- Evidence is not original without valid reason
- Signature/seal is questionable
- Creation time is inconsistent
- Content has internal contradictions

**Attacking Legality:**
- Collection subject lacks authority (e.g., unauthorized entity collected evidence)
- Collection procedure unlawful (torture/illegal search/illegal recording)
- Evidence form fails to meet statutory requirements

**Attacking Relevance:**
- Evidence is unrelated to the facts to be proved
- Evidence only proves secondary facts, not core facts
- Evidence is too indirect, insufficient probative value

**Attacking Probative Value:**
- Single evidence insufficient to establish a fact
- Evidence from interested persons less credible than neutral witnesses
- Audio-visual/electronic data integrity is questionable

## Courtroom Questioning Techniques

### Questioning Opposing Party/Witness

1. **One-Fact Principle**: Ask only one fact per question
   - ❌ "Did you go to the scene that day AND see Zhang San?" (compound)
   - ✅ "Did you go to the scene that day?" → "Did you see Zhang San?"

2. **Primarily Closed Questions** (yes/no format)
   - "Had you met the plaintiff before?" (leading to "no")
   - "Did you sign the contract?"

3. **Control Response Space**
   - Don't give the opponent room for lengthy explanations
   - Interrupt irrelevant answers: "Please answer yes or no directly"

4. **Set Traps**
   - First confirm basic facts → Naturally lead to unfavorable conclusion
   - Example: "You said you were there at 6pm, correct?" → "But surveillance shows you weren't"

### Questioning Your Own Client/Witness

1. **Open + Guided Questions**
   - "Please tell the court what happened that day"
   - Interrupt when appropriate: "You mentioned X, that's important... could you elaborate?"

2. **Avoid Leading Questions**
   - ❌ "You saw Zhang San hit someone, right?"
   - ✅ "What did you see?"

## Emergency Handling During Trial

| Situation | Response Strategy |
|-----------|-----------------|
| Opponent presents new evidence at trial | Request adjournment to prepare cross-examination, or give preliminary opinion |
| Judge's summary of disputed issues differs from your prediction | Acknowledge judge's summary, request supplement/correction if needed |
| Your client changes testimony during trial | Request recess to confer with client, adjust strategy |
| Your witness recants testimony | Request recess, reassess evidence system |
| Opponent uses threatening/insulting/aggressive language | Request judge to intervene, have it recorded |
| Judge appears obviously biased toward opponent | Stay calm, persuade through facts and law, avoid emotional confrontation |
| Discover inadequate preparation/missing evidence | Request recess to supplement, or request adjournment |

## Post-Trial Work

### Supplementary Submissions
- Submit complete representation opinion/defense statement within 3-7 days after trial
- Supplement arguments not fully developed at trial
- Complete legal citations and comparable case references
- Address questions raised by the judge during trial

### Matters to Verify After Trial
- Facts the judge requested be verified after trial
- New evidence opponent submitted at trial
- Gather additional evidence favorable to your case

### Trial Debriefing
- Record judge's focus areas and questions
- Analyze your strengths and mistakes
- Predict judgment direction
- Prepare for next steps (mediation/appeal/enforcement)
