# Clayton Christensen — Tech Forecasting

Harvard Business School; Christensen Institute. Primary sources: *The Innovator's
Dilemma* (1997); *The Innovator's Solution* (2003, with Raynor); *Seeing What's
Next* (2004, with Anthony & Roth); Christensen Institute six-question diagnostic
(2023).

**Role in the tech stack:** *Who wins industry shifts* — disruption vs.
sustaining, signals of change, incumbent response.

---

## Disruption vs. sustaining (don't conflate)

**Disruptive innovation** (Christensen definition): starts **simple, affordable,
convenient** — often worse on **historical performance metrics** — and serves
**nonconsumers** or **overserved** customers; improves via a trajectory that
eventually meets mainstream needs.

**Not disruption:** breakthrough technology that makes good products **better**
for existing best customers (sustaining innovation).

**Forecast error to avoid:** labeling every new tech "disruptive." Test with the
six questions below.

---

## Signals of change (*Seeing What's Next*)

Scan for customers incumbents **mis-serve**:

| Signal | Who | Forecast implication |
|--------|-----|----------------------|
| **Nonconsumers** | Want job done but lack skill, wealth, or access | New-market disruption foothold |
| **Undershot customers** | Need more than current solutions deliver | Sustaining or new-market opening |
| **Overshot customers** | Pay for performance they don't use | Low-end or modular disruption entry |

Also track **nonmarket** forces: regulation, standards, subsidies, trade policy.

**Emerging-market / fringe indicators:** hackers, students, SMBs, developing
markets tolerating "good enough" — high growth + acceleration in a small segment
often precedes mainstream shift.

## Three diagnostics (run in order)

### 1. Customer diagnostic

Find **disruptable** segments: overserved or nonconsumers with unmet jobs.

**Ask:** Who can't access the incumbent solution? Who is overpaying for unused
features? What job are they hiring a product to do?

### 2. Portfolio diagnostic

Can a current or potential innovation serve that segment with a **simpler,
cheaper, more convenient** offer — not just better specs?

**Check:** technology enabler for upmarket climb; **business model** that works
at low margins / different distribution.

### 3. Competitor diagnostic

Will incumbents **ignore** the threat (rational motivation) or feel compelled
to respond? If they respond, can they **succeed**?

Use **Resources–Processes–Values (RPV)** from *Innovator's Solution*:

- **Resources** — people, cash, IP (most flexible).
- **Processes** — how work gets done (hard to change).
- **Values** — profit formula, margin floors, customer-size thresholds (most
  rigid).

Incumbents often **cannot** pursue disruption without a **separate unit** with
different values — acquisition without RPV fit usually fails.

**Asymmetries of motivation:** disruptors win when leaders rationally defend
most profitable customers and **defer** low-end/nonconsumer segments.

## Six questions — is it (really) disruptive?

Christensen Institute (2023); each "yes" supports disruption relative to an
incumbent:

1. Targets **nonconsumers** or **overserved** by the incumbent?
2. **Not as good** on historical performance measures?
3. **Simpler, more convenient, or more affordable**?
4. Has a **technological enabler** that can carry it upmarket over time?
5. Paired with a **sustainable business model** innovation?
6. Are existing providers **motivated to ignore** it at the outset?

**Scoring:** more yeses → higher probability of disruptive path; zero on (1) and
(6) → likely sustaining battle, different forecast.

---

## Improvement trajectory (leading indicator)

Don't ask only "Is it as good as the leader today?"

Ask: **Is it improving faster than the market's performance bar is rising?**

A foothold that looks irrelevant can cross into the core market on a **process**,
not a single launch event.
