# English Reading Coach — Strategy Teaching Guide

Read this file when preparing a session for a specific reading strategy. Each section covers: what it is, how to explain it, how to model it, what tasks to assign, common errors, and feedback scripts.

## 1. Skimming

### What it is
Reading a text very quickly to get the general shape and topic — not details. The reader moves through the text in roughly 20–30% of normal reading time.

### Metaphor for learners
*"Like watching a movie trailer — you get the mood and topic, not the full plot."*
*"Like driving past a city at 100km/h — you see the skyline, not the street signs."*

### How to model it (before the real text)
Show this paragraph:
> "Scientists have discovered a new species of deep-sea fish near the Pacific Ocean floor. The creature, which can grow up to two metres long, produces its own light in the darkness. Researchers believe it may help explain how life evolved in extreme environments."

Say: *"Watch me skim. I read the first sentence — 'new species of deep-sea fish'. I skip the middle. I read the last sentence — 'life evolved in extreme environments'. The topic is: science / ocean / discovery. I didn't read every word. Now you try."*

### Tasks to assign
- **Timed skim:** Give the learner 30–45 seconds to skim a 200-word text. Then hide the text. Ask: "What is the text about? Write one sentence."
- **Best title:** Show 4 possible titles. Learner picks the best one after skimming (no re-reading for detail allowed).
- **True/False gist:** 3 statements about the general topic. Learner marks True/False after skimming only.

### What good skimming looks like
- Learner reads first and last sentences of each paragraph
- Learner notices headings, bold words, numbers
- Learner's gist answer is general, not detailed
- Learner finishes in time

### Common errors and feedback
| Error | Feedback |
|---|---|
| Reads every word (not skimming) | "You spent too long on the details. For skimming, try reading only the first and last sentence of each paragraph. Cover the middle and see what you learn." |
| Gist answer is too specific | "That's a detail, not the main idea. Ask yourself: 'If I had to explain this text to a friend in 5 seconds, what would I say?'" |
| Gets the wrong gist | "Let's look at the first and last sentences together. What do they have in common? That overlap is usually the topic." |

## 2. Scanning

### What it is
Reading fast to find one specific piece of information — a name, date, number, place, or keyword. The reader ignores everything except what they're looking for.

### Metaphor for learners
*"Like using Ctrl+F in a document — your eyes jump to the target word and ignore everything else."*
*"Like finding your friend in a crowd — you don't look at everyone's faces, you look for their red jacket."*

### How to model it
Show 3 sentences. Say: *"Find the year."*
> "The Eiffel Tower was built in 1889 by Gustave Eiffel. It stands 330 metres tall and was the world's tallest structure for 41 years. Over seven million people visit it every year."

Say: *"I'm not reading for meaning. My eyes jump to a number. I see 1889. Done. That's scanning."*

### Tasks to assign
- **Timed fact hunt:** Give the learner 5 specific questions before they read (a name, a number, a place, a date, a reason). Set 60–90 seconds. Learner finds only those answers.
- **True/False/Not Given:** Give statements that require locating a specific piece of information (or its absence) in the text. Classic IELTS format.
- **Information matching:** 4 questions, 4 paragraphs. Which paragraph contains the answer? Learner scans each paragraph header/first line to locate.

### What good scanning looks like
- Learner finishes quickly (faster than normal reading time)
- Learner predicts what form the answer will take (number vs name vs date)
- Learner ignores irrelevant sentences completely

### Common errors and feedback
| Error | Feedback |
|---|---|
| Reads the whole text before answering | "Scanning means you search, not read. Before you start, ask: 'Am I looking for a name? A number? A place?' Then your eyes know what shape to find." |
| Misses the answer even though it's there | "The answer was there — let's look together. It was hidden because the text uses a synonym. For example, instead of 'increase', the text said 'grew'. Train your eye to notice synonyms." |
| Finds a wrong similar-looking word | "You found a word that looks similar but read it in context — one more sentence around the word tells you if it's the right answer." |

## 3. Reading for Gist

### What it is
Reading a complete text at normal speed but not stopping for every unknown word — the goal is to understand the overall message and main point, tolerating some gaps.

### Metaphor for learners
*"Like listening to a song in a foreign language — you don't understand every word but you feel the emotion and the story."*
*"Gist reading means you accept 80% understanding and keep moving."*

### How to model it
Show a paragraph with 2–3 unfamiliar words. Read it aloud and say: *"I don't know 'incessant' but I can see it's describing rain, and the feeling is negative. I skip it and understand: the weather was bad and made the journey hard. That's gist — I got the message without every word."*

### Tasks to assign
- **One-sentence summary:** After reading the whole text, write exactly one sentence that captures the main message.
- **Best summary choice:** Give 4 one-paragraph summaries. Which one best captures the text's overall message (not a specific detail)?
- **Unknown word tolerance drill:** Give a text with 5 made-up nonsense words replacing real ones (e.g., "The *blorfing* continued for three days, leaving the city in chaos."). Ask: What happened? This trains the learner to tolerate gaps and still understand.

### Common errors and feedback
| Error | Feedback |
|---|---|
| Stops at every unknown word | "Try to keep moving. When you hit an unknown word, circle it and continue. After you finish the text, you'll often understand it from context — and if not, we'll figure it out together." |
| Summary is too detailed | "A gist summary is the 30,000-foot view. If your sentence has specific names, numbers, or examples, it's probably a detail. Ask: 'What is this text *really* about?'" |
| Summary misses the main message | "Let's look at the first and last paragraphs again. Writers usually put their main message there. What do both paragraphs have in common?" |

## 4. Inference

### What it is
Understanding what the writer *implies* but doesn't say directly. The reader uses clues in the text — word choice, tone, what's left unsaid — to draw conclusions.

### Metaphor for learners
*"Like being a detective. The text leaves clues. Your job is to follow them."*
*"Inference is reading between the lines — not what the text says, but what it means."*

### How to model it
Show: *"She said nothing, but her hands were shaking."*
Say: *"The text doesn't say she was scared or nervous. But 'said nothing' + 'hands shaking' = she felt something strong. I infer: she was frightened or upset. That's inference."*

### Tasks to assign
- **Implication questions:** "What does the writer imply when they say X?" / "What can we infer about the character from this sentence?"
- **Tone identification:** What is the writer's attitude toward the topic — positive, negative, critical, hopeful? Find one sentence that reveals the tone. Explain.
- **What's NOT said:** "The writer never says why the factory closed. What can you infer from the surrounding information?"
- **Character feeling:** In a story passage: "How does the character feel? How do you know? Quote the evidence."

### Inference question design
Always give evidence-based questions, not opinion questions:
- ✅ "What does the writer's use of the word 'merely' suggest about their attitude?"
- ✅ "Why do you think the company delayed the announcement for three months?"
- ❌ "Do you think the company was wrong?" (opinion, not inference)

### Common errors and feedback
| Error | Feedback |
|---|---|
| States a fact already in the text | "That's what the text *says* — inference is what it *implies*. Look for what the writer hints at but doesn't explain directly." |
| Makes an inference with no textual basis | "That's an interesting idea, but what in the text led you there? Inference must be supported by clues — show me the clue." |
| Can't find the inference | "Let's look at the writer's word choices. Why 'alarming' instead of 'surprising'? Why 'admitted' instead of 'said'? Those choices are the clues." |

## 5. Prediction

### What it is
Using the title, headings, images, and background knowledge to anticipate what a text will say *before* reading it. Good prediction activates prior knowledge and creates a reading purpose.

### Metaphor for learners
*"Like a chef who reads a recipe title and already imagines the dish before seeing the ingredients."*
*"Prediction warms up your brain so it's ready to catch information when you read."*

### How to model it
Show a title: *"The Hidden Cost of Fast Fashion"*
Say: *"Before reading, I predict: this text will talk about environmental damage, cheap labour, waste, or health issues. Now when I read, my brain is already alert for those topics. I'll absorb the information faster."*

### Tasks to assign
- **Title prediction:** Show the title only. Ask: "Write 3 things you think this text will mention." After reading, check which predictions were correct.
- **First-sentence prediction:** Show only the first sentence. Ask: "Where do you think this text is going? What will the next paragraph say?"
- **Paragraph prediction:** After reading each paragraph, predict what the next one will discuss.
- **Vocabulary prediction:** Given the topic, ask: "Write 5 words you expect to see in this text." After reading, check how many appeared.

### Common errors and feedback
| Error | Feedback |
|---|---|
| Predictions are too vague | "Try to be specific. Instead of 'it will talk about problems', predict: 'it will mention climate change, water usage, and worker salaries.' Specific predictions make you a better reader." |
| Learner doesn't connect predictions to reading | "After you read, always check back: which predictions were right? Which surprised you? The surprises are the most valuable — they show you what you didn't know." |
| Prediction anxiety ("I might be wrong") | "Wrong predictions are fine — prediction isn't guessing correctly, it's activating your brain. The point is to *arrive at the text ready*, not to predict perfectly." |

## 6. Main Idea Identification

### What it is
Finding the most important sentence or idea in a paragraph (usually the topic sentence) and understanding how the supporting sentences relate to it.

### Metaphor for learners
*"Every paragraph has a spine. The topic sentence is the spine. Everything else is muscle and skin supporting it."*
*"Ask: if I removed this sentence, would the paragraph collapse? If yes — that's the main idea."*

### How to model it
Show a paragraph. Circle the topic sentence. Say: *"This sentence tells me what the paragraph is about. These other sentences give examples, reasons, or details. They support the spine but they aren't the spine."*

### Tasks to assign
- **Topic sentence identification:** For each paragraph, underline (or write down) the one sentence that carries the main idea.
- **Topic sentence vs detail:** Give 5 sentences from a paragraph. Which one is the topic sentence? Which are supporting details?
- **Paragraph outline:** After reading, write the topic sentence of each paragraph in order. The result is a paragraph-by-paragraph outline.
- **Missing topic sentence:** Give a paragraph with the topic sentence removed. Ask: "Write a topic sentence for this paragraph."

### Common errors and feedback
| Error | Feedback |
|---|---|
| Picks a detail, not the main idea | "That sentence is a supporting example — it's there to prove or illustrate something. The main idea is the claim that needs proving. Ask: 'What is this sentence *supporting*?'" |
| Picks the first sentence automatically | "Topic sentences are often first — but not always. Sometimes writers build to the main point. Always check: does every other sentence support this one?" |
| Main idea is too broad | "A topic sentence should be specific to *this* paragraph, not the whole text. The main idea of a paragraph is narrower than the main idea of the text." |

## 7. Intensive Reading

### What it is
Slow, careful reading of every sentence — the goal is 100% comprehension. Used for contracts, academic texts, instructions, complex literature, or any text where every word matters.

### Metaphor for learners
*"Intensive reading is like a jeweller examining a diamond with a magnifying glass — nothing is too small to notice."*
*"You're not racing. You're mining."*

### How to model it
Show one sentence: *"Despite widespread criticism, the government proceeded with the policy, citing long-term economic necessity."*
Say: *"Let me unpack this. 'Despite' = contrast — there was opposition but they continued. 'Widespread' = many people. 'Proceeded' = continued. 'Citing' = giving as a reason. 'Long-term economic necessity' = they said it was needed for future economy. Now I understand exactly what this sentence means."*

### Tasks to assign
- **Sentence unpacking:** Take one complex sentence and ask the learner to explain it in simple English.
- **Connector analysis:** Identify all the linking words (however, although, despite, therefore). What relationship does each express?
- **Reference tracking:** Find all pronouns (it, they, this, that) in a paragraph and identify what each one refers to.
- **Paraphrase:** Rewrite one paragraph in simpler English without losing any information.
- **Grammar note:** Find one interesting grammatical structure in the text and explain why the writer used it.

### Common errors and feedback
| Error | Feedback |
|---|---|
| Paraphrase loses meaning | "Check: did your version say the same thing? Let's compare: the original says X, yours says Y — are they the same?" |
| Struggles with complex sentences | "Long sentences often have a hidden simple structure. Find the main verb. Then find what does the action and what receives it. That's the core. Everything else is extra information." |
| Connector errors | "The connector 'although' means the second idea is surprising given the first. 'However' introduces a contrast or exception. These words are signposts — follow them." |

## 8. Strategy Combinations

### Read → Reflect (all levels)
Skim first → then intensive reading.
Best for: academic texts, learning from non-fiction.
Workflow: Skim to get the map → Intensive to fill in the detail.

### Hunt → Infer (B1+)
Scan for facts → then infer what those facts imply.
Best for: news articles, IELTS True/False/Not Given.
Workflow: Scan to gather data → Inference to interpret what the data means.

### Predict → Gist → Infer (B2+)
Full pre-to-post reading arc.
Best for: long articles, opinion pieces, stories.
Workflow: Predict before → Gist during → Infer after.

### Main Idea → Summary (all levels)
Identify topic sentence per paragraph → combine into a summary.
Best for: academic reading, note-taking skills.
Workflow: Outline each paragraph's main idea → write a cohesive summary.

### IELTS Full Workflow (B2–C1)
Predict from title → Skim for structure → Scan for answers → Infer for implied meaning → Intensive for tricky sentences.
Best for: exam simulation sessions.
