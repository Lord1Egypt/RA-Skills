# Customer-Facing Warranty Copy Patterns

The warranty page is a conversion asset. Write it for the buyer reading before purchase, not for the lawyer reviewing after a claim.

## The three-line summary

Top of the warranty page, also used as PDP reassurance copy:

> **[Brand] stands behind this product. If it fails from a defect in materials or workmanship within [length], we'll repair, replace, or refund it. Here's how.**

This should fit in a single card on mobile. Link to the full policy underneath.

## PDP trust badge

Single line, top of PDP, near the price:

> ✓ Backed by our [length] guarantee — [link: See what's covered]

Use the checkmark and keep it short. Don't promise what the full policy doesn't deliver.

## Checkout reassurance

Below the payment button:

> Every order includes our [length] [limited/full] warranty against defects. [link to policy]

## Post-purchase email

Subject line: "Your [Brand] is covered."

Body (first 80 chars): "Good news — your order is backed by our [length] warranty. Here's what that means…"

Link to a clean one-page policy, not the legal PDF.

## Claim page copy

Title: **Start a claim** (not "File a warranty dispute" or "Return request").

Line 1: "Sorry you're having trouble — let's sort this out fast."

Line 2: "Three things we need: your order number, a photo of the issue, and a sentence about what happened."

Decision SLA visible: "We respond within 48 hours. Most claims are approved."

## Language patterns to use

- "We'll repair, replace, or refund" — gives the brand the remedy choice.
- "Covers defects in materials and workmanship" — industry-standard phrase.
- "Does not cover normal wear, misuse, or damage from use outside the product's intended purpose" — three exclusions in one sentence.
- "In addition to your rights under applicable consumer law" — EU/UK/AU-safe phrasing.
- "From the original date of purchase" — avoids disputes about when the clock started.

## Language patterns to avoid

- "Lifetime warranty" without defining whose lifetime.
- "No questions asked" — invites abuse and contradicts any exclusions.
- "100% satisfaction guaranteed" — this is a returns promise, not a warranty. Don't conflate.
- "Hassle-free" — customers interpret this as "no questions, no photos," then get frustrated when you ask.
- "Unconditional" — legally risky and financially reckless.

## Tone

Direct, warm, factual. Write like a small business that takes responsibility but has limits, not like a luxury brand performing noblesse oblige or a big-box retailer hiding behind fine print.

## Accessibility and formatting

- Warranty page should be plain HTML, not a PDF embed.
- Heading structure: H1 policy name, H2 sections.
- Key numbers (length, threshold, SLA) in bold.
- Mobile: cards, not tables.
- Translatable strings in a dedicated JSON/CMS field — don't hard-code.

## Review cadence

Review all customer-facing warranty copy quarterly. Triggers for immediate update:

- Policy change (length, scope, remedy).
- Law change in a supported market.
- Claim rate shift outside ±20%.
- Support team reports confusion about a specific phrase (add clarification).
- Competitive benchmarking shows you've fallen behind on any axis.
