# Simulation Guide — Building Worlds That Reveal Truth

The simulation is not creative writing. It is a structured method for discovering what analysis alone cannot reveal.

---

## Why Simulation Works

Analysis tells you what *should* happen based on logic.
Simulation shows you what *would* happen when real humans with conflicting interests, emotions, and blind spots collide.

The gap between those two is where the most valuable insights live.

---

## The Four Simulation Principles

### 1. Ground in Reality
Build from real data gathered in the Intelligence phase. If your research found that 70% of similar ventures fail in year two — your simulation must reflect that pressure. Characters who ignore base rates are not realistic characters.

### 2. Characters Over Archetypes
The most common failure: creating characters that are positions, not people.

❌ Weak: "The Skeptic" who opposes everything.
✅ Strong: "Layla, 44, CFO who survived a failed pivot in 2019 and has been quietly building a personal reserve fund ever since. She doesn't oppose this plan — she just needs to see the downside protection first."

Characters need:
- A specific name and background
- A concrete stake in the outcome
- A fear that shapes their behavior
- An internal contradiction (something they want but won't admit)

### 3. The Unexpected Variable Is Mandatory
Every simulation must include one event that nobody in the scenario anticipated. This is not for drama — it is because real decisions are made in unstable environments.

**Sources for unexpected variables:**
- Marginal data from your web research (things mentioned once, not twice)
- Historical analogues where a similar situation was disrupted by an external shock
- Second-order effects from Lens 6 that arrive faster or slower than expected

### 4. The Simulation Must Be Allowed to Fail
If the idea you are analyzing is bad — the simulation should show it failing. Do not protect the user's preferred outcome. The value of the simulation is that it is honest.

## Character Construction Template

```
Name: [First name + age]
Role: [Their position or relationship to the topic]
Stake: [What they stand to gain or lose]
Fear: [What they are afraid of — specific, not generic]
Blind spot: [What they cannot see about themselves or the situation]
Voice: [One sentence that captures how they speak and think]
```

**Example:**
```
Name: Marcus, 51
Role: Institutional investor evaluating the proposal
Stake: His fund's next raise depends on at least one "AI play" showing returns by Q4
Fear: That he is already two years too late and the obvious deals are gone
Blind spot: He evaluates novelty as a proxy for quality because he missed GPT
Voice: "I don't need it to be perfect. I need it to be defensible in a board meeting."
```

## Simulation Structure

### Act 1 — Setup (brief)
Establish the world. Who is here? What decision or event is in motion? What does each character want?

### Act 2 — Collision
Let the characters interact with the idea and with each other. Show:
- The moment someone's stated position conflicts with their behavior
- The first sign that something is not going as planned
- A decision point where characters choose differently than logic predicts

### Act 3 — The Unexpected Variable
Introduce the disruption. Show how each character responds. This is where the simulation earns its value.

### Act 4 — Resolution (open, not neat)
Show where things land — not a clean ending, but a honest state. Some things worked, some did not, something was revealed that no one expected.

---

## What the Simulation Should Reveal

At minimum, the simulation must surface at least one of these:

1. **A decision that looks rational in analysis but fails in practice** because of how humans actually behave
2. **An unexpected winner or loser** — someone who benefits or suffers in a way the analysis did not predict
3. **The assumption that breaks** — the thing the original framing took for granted that turned out to be fragile
4. **A path forward that the analysis missed** — sometimes the simulation reveals an option that was not visible before running it

## Narrative Tone

Match the seriousness of the topic:

| Topic type | Tone |
|------------|------|
| Business strategy | Grounded, specific, industry-accurate |
| Political / social | Careful, factual framing, avoid sensationalism |
| Personal decision | Warm, human, psychologically honest |
| Technical / scientific | Precise, acknowledge uncertainty |
| Historical | Respectful of real events, clear about fictional elements |

For simulations involving real named public figures: clearly label all dialogue and inferred behavior as fictional reconstruction. Do not present it as factual.

## Common Simulation Failures

| Failure | What it looks like | Fix |
|---------|--------------------|-----|
| Characters agree too easily | No real conflict, everyone comes around | Give each character a genuine reason to resist |
| Unexpected variable is irrelevant | The disruption doesn't change anything | The disruption must threaten the core assumption |
| Resolution is too neat | Everything works out or fails cleanly | Real outcomes are partial and ambiguous |
| Characters speak the same voice | All dialogue sounds like the analyst | Write each character's voice before writing their lines |
| Simulation confirms the analysis | The world just illustrates the conclusions | Push harder — look for where they diverge |
