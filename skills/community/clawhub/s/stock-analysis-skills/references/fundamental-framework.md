# Fundamental Analysis Framework — Reference Guide

Methodology for Module 3 (Company Competency) and Module 5 (Earnings & Analyst Forecast).

---

## Module 3: Company Competency Analysis

### 1. Revenue Growth Trajectory

**What to evaluate:**
- 3-year and 5-year compound annual growth rate (CAGR)
- Most recent quarter's YoY growth rate and acceleration/deceleration trend
- Organic growth vs. growth through M&A (organic is generally higher quality)
- Revenue mix: product vs. service; recurring vs. one-time (subscriptions/recurring are typically higher quality)
- Geographic breakdown: contribution share from high-growth markets vs. mature markets?

**Revenue Quality Framework:**
| Revenue Type | Quality |
|--------------|---------|
| Subscription / recurring | Highest — predictable, compounding |
| Long-term contracts | High — strong visibility, low churn risk |
| Transactional / volume-based | Medium — more cyclical, higher volatility |
| One-time / project-based | Lower — lumpy, harder to forecast |

**Red flags:**
- Company growth decelerating while competitors accelerate
- Over-reliance on a single customer (single customer contributing >10–15% = concentration risk)
- Channel stuffing / revenue pulling forward (rising channel inventory days, distributor inventory builds)

### 2. Gross Margin & Operating Margin Analysis

**Gross Margin = (Revenue – COGS) / Revenue**
- Reflects pricing power and the basic economics of core products/services
- Software/SaaS: typically 70–85% (significant variation by business model)
- Hardware: typically 30–50%
- Retail: typically 20–40%
- Services: typically 40–70%

**Operating Margin = Operating Income / Revenue**
- Reflects operating efficiency after SG&A, R&D, and general & administrative expenses
- Trends often matter more than absolute levels — is the company becoming more efficient as it scales?
- Operating leverage: when revenue growth outpaces opex growth, margins expand — usually a very positive signal

**Key questions:**
- Is gross margin expanding (pricing power / mix improvement) or contracting (competitive pressure / cost inflation)?
- Is the company sacrificing near-term operating margins to invest in R&D and S&M for growth? Are these investments "working" (driving revenue and market share gains, product strength)?

### 3. R&D Investment

**Why it matters:**
R&D is the "seed" for future competitive advantages. Under-investment relative to peers may signal a thinning moat; over-investment without product/revenue conversion may signal capital misallocation.

**Metrics:**
- R&D as % of revenue vs. industry median
- R&D productivity: contribution of new products/businesses to revenue
- Patent portfolio size and growth rate
- Pipeline quality: for biotech/pharma, track clinical stage distribution and progress

**R&D/Revenue Reference Ranges:**
| Sector | Typical R&D/Revenue |
|--------|---------------------|
| Biotech / Pharma | 15–25%+ |
| Software / SaaS | 10–20% |
| Semiconductors | 10–15% |
| Industrials | 3–6% |
| Consumer Staples | 1–3% |
| Financials | N/A |

### 4. Management Quality & Capital Allocation

**Management quality indicators:**
- CEO and CFO tenure and track record
- Management / key employee ownership and insider buying/selling activity (moderate ownership aligns incentives; large-scale net selling warrants caution)
- Consistency in delivering on guidance and market expectations over time (manageable beats are acceptable, but chronic credibility gaps are not)
- Communication quality: candor about risks and challenges, specificity in guidance
- Proxy indicators for culture and organizational health: employee turnover, Glassdoor ratings, employer branding (use Glassdoor, Blind, and other public reviews, adjusting for sample bias)

**Capital allocation priorities (ranked from most to least shareholder-value-optimizing):**
1. Reinvest in high-ROIC organic growth (typically the best option when returns are high)
2. Accretive M&A with clear strategic rationale and verifiable integration track record
3. Deleveraging when balance sheet is stressed
4. Buybacks at reasonable valuations (buybacks at inflated valuations can destroy value; assess source of funds, retirement vs. dilution offset mechanics)
5. Dividends (typically signals maturity; may not be optimal if high-return growth opportunities exist)

**Capital allocation red flags:**
- High-premium acquisitions (e.g., excessive revenue multiples) without clear synergy and integration path
- Borrowing to fund buybacks (especially at cycle peaks), or revolving buyback/financing "loops"
- Frequent equity issuance leading to persistent dilution
- Executive compensation disconnected from performance / shareholder returns

### 5. Competitive Moat Assessment

Five moat types (per Morningstar framework):

| Moat Type | Definition | Examples |
|-----------|------------|----------|
| **Network Effects** | Value increases with user base | Visa, Meta, Airbnb, LinkedIn |
| **Switching Costs** | High cost to change providers | Salesforce, Oracle, SAP, Adobe, ServiceNow |
| **Cost Advantage** | Structural low-cost position | Amazon (scale), Walmart (distribution), Costco (membership model) |
| **Intangible Assets** | Brands, patents, regulatory licenses | Coca-Cola, LVMH, Pfizer, Disney |
| **Efficient Scale** | Market naturally supports few players | Waste Management, Union Pacific, airports |

**Moat width:**
- **Wide moat**: sustainable competitive advantage expected to persist 20+ years
- **Narrow moat**: competitive advantage may last 10–20 years
- **No moat**: highly competitive, commoditized — price becomes the primary differentiator

### 6. Operational Efficiency Metrics

| Metric | Formula | What it measures |
|--------|---------|------------------|
| ROE (Return on Equity) | Net Income / Shareholders' Equity | Profitability of equity capital |
| ROIC (Return on Invested Capital) | NOPAT / Invested Capital | Efficiency of all invested capital |
| Asset Turnover | Revenue / Total Assets | Efficiency of generating revenue from assets |
| Free Cash Flow Margin | FCF / Revenue | Cash generation capability |
| FCF Conversion | FCF / Net Income | Earnings quality (>80% is typically strong) |
| Cash Return on Capital | FCF / Total Capital | More "capital allocation-neutral" measure |

**ROIC vs. WACC is the key test:** ROIC > WACC means value creation; ROIC < WACC means value destruction even if profits are growing.

## Module 5: Earnings & Analyst Forecast

### Earnings Quality Checklist

High-quality earnings typically exhibit:
- [ ] EPS growth driven by revenue and operating improvement, not primarily financial engineering (buybacks, accounting changes, etc.)
- [ ] Gross and operating margins expanding in tandem with revenue growth
- [ ] Free cash flow conversion (FCF conversion) > 80% of net income (lower may signal earnings quality issues)
- [ ] Accounts receivable growth below revenue growth (faster AR growth may signal aggressive revenue recognition)
- [ ] Inventory levels within reasonable range (abnormal inventory build may signal impairment or future markdown risk)

### Beat/Miss Analysis

**Consistently beating estimates is positive**, but dig deeper:
- Is the company deliberately guiding conservatively to engineer beats ("beat-and-raise")?
- Does the beat come from the top line (higher quality) or mainly from expense cuts / one-time items?
- Is the beat magnitude narrowing over time while consensus hasn't changed much? This may signal growth deceleration.

**Earnings revision momentum:**
- Sustained upward revisions = strengthening momentum, often creates institutional "tailwind"
- Sustained downward revisions = weakening momentum, often leads to further valuation and sentiment pressure

### Consensus vs. Management Divergence

| Scenario | Implication |
|----------|-------------|
| Management guidance above consensus | High confidence, typically bullish |
| Management guidance roughly in line with consensus | Neutral, no "expectations gap" |
| Management guidance below consensus | Bearish — even strong current results may be met with selling |
| Wide analyst estimate dispersion | High uncertainty, larger moves around earnings likely |

### Key Financial Ratios for Earnings Module

| Ratio | Formula | Benchmark |
|-------|---------|-----------|
| YoY EPS Growth | (Current EPS – Prior EPS) / Prior EPS | Growth stocks >15% typically strong (adjust for base effect and sector) |
| Revenue Beat % | (Actual – Estimate) / Estimate × 100 | Sustained >1–2% is typically positive (adjust for sector volatility) |
| Forward EPS Growth | Next FY EPS vs. Current FY EPS | Growth >15%; mature >5–8% (reference only) |
| PEG Ratio | P/E / EPS Growth Rate | <1.0 may indicate undervaluation (validate growth quality) |
