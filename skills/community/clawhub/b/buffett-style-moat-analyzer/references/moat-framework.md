# Moat Framework

Use this reference to score business quality.

## Moat Strength

Strong moats usually come from:

- switching costs
- network effects
- cost advantage
- distribution advantage
- regulated or licensed position
- trusted brand that changes buying behavior
- data advantage that improves the product
- ecosystem lock-in
- habit or workflow captivity

Weak moats usually rely on:

- being first
- nice branding without behavior change
- generic software features
- paid ads only
- founder charisma
- temporary trend demand
- underpricing competitors

## Durability

Ask:

- Will customers still need this in 10 years?
- Does the business become stronger as it grows?
- Can competitors copy the product but not the economics?
- Does technology help or threaten the business?
- Is demand recurring, habitual, or mission-critical?

## Pricing Power

High pricing power:

- customers complain but stay
- product is a small cost relative to value
- switching is painful
- price increases do not materially reduce demand
- buyer cannot easily compare alternatives

Low pricing power:

- customers compare mainly on price
- product is easy to replace
- competitors can undercut quickly
- contracts renew only with discounts
- no differentiated outcome

## Customer Captivity

Score high when:

- customer data, workflow, training, integrations, habit, trust, or compliance keeps buyers attached
- the business becomes embedded in daily operations
- leaving creates real risk, work, or lost revenue

Score low when:

- the buyer can replace it in a day
- there is no data/history/workflow lock-in
- users only stay because it is cheap

## Cashflow Quality

High quality:

- recurring or repeat revenue
- high gross margins
- low maintenance capex
- low working capital strain
- positive unit economics
- growth can be funded internally

Low quality:

- revenue growth consumes cash
- heavy capex
- high inventory risk
- long receivables
- low margins
- high refunds/churn
- constant capital raises

## Capital Intensity

Asset-light businesses can compound more easily if they also have durable demand.

Capital-heavy businesses can still be strong, but require more proof:

- high returns on invested capital
- disciplined reinvestment
- predictable demand
- conservative balance sheet

## Management / Operator Quality

Use evidence, not vibes:

- capital allocation history
- owner mentality
- honest communication
- sensible incentives
- durable culture
- operational discipline
- willingness to say no

Red flags:

- promotional language
- serial dilution
- poor acquisitions
- adjusted metrics that hide reality
- changing story every year
- aggressive related-party deals

## Reinvestment Runway

A business compounds when it can reinvest cash at high rates for a long time.

Score high when:

- large market remains
- unit economics stay attractive with scale
- expansion uses existing strengths
- new locations/products/channels reinforce the moat

Score low when:

- growth requires unrelated expansion
- core market is saturated
- each new sale gets harder
- growth depends on financial engineering
