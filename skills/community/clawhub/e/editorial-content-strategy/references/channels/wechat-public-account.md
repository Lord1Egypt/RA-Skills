# Channel Strategy: WeChat Public Account

## 1. Channel nature

WeChat public account writing is not engineering documentation.
It must be readable on mobile, quickly understandable, and worth saving or sharing.

## 2. Strategic objective on this channel

A strong WeChat piece should do four things:
- stop the scroll quickly
- establish relevance in the first screen
- deliver at least one reusable judgment or framework
- leave the reader with a clear sense that the article was worth their time

## 3. Writing strategy

### 3.1 Hook strategy
Open fast.
Use a pain point, contradiction, or sharp diagnosis in the first screen.
Do not waste the opening on slow setup or decorative language.

Good opening directions:
- a familiar work pain
- a wrong assumption most readers make
- a high-value contrast
- a sharp consequence of getting it wrong

### 3.2 Paragraph rhythm
- keep paragraphs short
- use clear section breaks
- let key judgments stand alone
- avoid large dense walls of text
- use breathing room after major conclusions

### 3.3 Argument strategy
Prioritize:
- why the issue matters
- what mistake most people make
- what practical framework solves it
- what the reader should do differently

### 3.4 Technical-depth rule
Do not turn the article into a product manual or developer doc.
Keep enough technical detail for trust, but optimize for practical value and reader progression.

### 3.5 Save/share strategy
The article should earn retention by containing one or more of these:
- a reusable framework
- a crisp contrast
- a practical checklist
- a non-obvious judgment
- a useful naming of a common failure

### 3.6 Obsidian title rule
If the Obsidian article already uses frontmatter or metadata to define the title, do not repeat the title as an H1 at the start of the body.

Reason:
When the piece is submitted to WeChat public account workflows, repeating the title in the body creates a duplicated title effect.

Rule of thumb:
- keep the title in frontmatter/metadata
- start the body with the opening hook, not a repeated H1
- check the staged article before submission to ensure the title appears only once in the final publishing flow

### 3.7 Visual strategy
Use visuals to:
- reduce abstraction
- clarify contrasts
- support key transitions
- improve mobile reading flow

Images must be actually embedded in the final staged article.

### 3.8 Ending strategy
Do not end by fading out.
End with one of these:
- a sharper restatement of the core judgment
- a next-step implication
- a strong transition to the next related topic

## 4. Common failure modes

### 4.1 Documentation smell
The article reads like internal notes or technical documentation.

### 4.2 Weak hook
The opening takes too long to say why the reader should care.

### 4.3 Overlong paragraphs
The piece becomes tiring on mobile.

### 4.4 Too much mechanism, too little consequence
The article explains how things work without showing why they matter.

### 4.5 No retention reason
The article is readable but leaves no memorable framework, judgment, or takeaway worth saving.

## 5. Acceptance checklist

### 5.1 First-screen test
Can the reader understand the pain point or value promise quickly?

### 5.2 Mobile reading test
Are paragraphs and transitions comfortable on a phone screen?

### 5.3 Save/share test
Does the article contain clear judgments, frameworks, or examples worth keeping?

### 5.4 Ending test
Does the ending land with a judgment, implication, or continuation?

### 5.5 Title-duplication test
If Obsidian/frontmatter already carries the title, is there no duplicated H1 title at the start of the body?

### 5.6 Staging test
Is the final article staged in Obsidian with image embeds before publication?
