# English Writing Coach — Genre Teaching Guide

Full teaching guide for each writing type. Read the relevant section when designing a session. Each entry covers: purpose, audience, structure, model text, key language, common errors, and feedback focus.

---

## 1. Professional Email

### Purpose

To communicate clearly, efficiently, and professionally in a workplace context. Every email has ONE primary purpose — state it early.

### Audience

Colleagues, managers, clients, or external partners. Tone adjusts based on relationship (formal/semi-formal/internal).

### Structure

```
Subject line: [Specific, actionable, clear — max 8 words]
Opening: [Context or purpose — never "I hope this email finds you well"]
Body: [1–3 short paragraphs, one idea per paragraph]
Call to action: [What do you want the reader to do?]
Closing: [Professional sign-off]
```

### Model (Request Email — Semi-formal)

> **Subject:** Request for Feedback on Q3 Report
>
> Hi Sarah,
>
> I've just finished the first draft of the Q3 performance report and would appreciate your feedback before I send it to the wider team.
>
> Would you be available to review it by Thursday? I've attached the draft for your reference.
>
> Thanks in advance for your time.
>
> Best regards,
> Minh

### Key Language

- **Opening alternatives**: "I am writing to...", "I wanted to follow up on...", "Further to our conversation..."
- **Making requests**: "Would it be possible to...", "I was wondering if you could...", "Could you please..."
- **Closing**: "Please let me know if you have any questions.", "I look forward to hearing from you.", "Thank you for your time."
- **Sign-offs**: "Best regards" (semi-formal), "Kind regards" (formal), "Thanks" (internal/informal)

### Common Errors

- Subject line too vague: "Update" → "Update on Project X Deadline"
- Opening too stiff: "I am in receipt of your esteemed correspondence" → "Thank you for your email"
- No call to action: reader doesn't know what to do after reading
- Mixing formal and informal register in the same email
- Using contractions in formal emails ("can't" → "cannot")

### Feedback Focus

- Is the purpose clear in the first 2 sentences?
- Is the tone appropriate for the relationship?
- Is there a clear call to action?
- Are subject line and sign-off matched to the register?

### Email Sub-types and Their Specific Conventions

**Apology Email:** State the problem → take responsibility → explain briefly → offer a solution → thank them for understanding. Never over-explain or blame others.

**Complaint Email:** State the problem clearly → give evidence → state the impact → request a specific remedy → maintain professional tone throughout. Avoid emotional language.

**Follow-up Email:** Reference the previous interaction → state why you're following up → ask a specific question or restate the request → keep it brief (max 3 sentences in the body).

**Decline Email:** Thank them → decline clearly (don't be vague) → brief reason (optional) → offer an alternative if possible → close warmly. Never say "I'm afraid I cannot help you" — say "Unfortunately, I'm unable to..."

## 2. Formal Letter

### Purpose

Official communication: applications, complaints, requests to organizations, cover letters.

### Audience

HR departments, companies, government offices, institutions. Always formal.

### Structure

```
[Your address — top right or top left depending on convention]
[Date]
[Recipient's name and address]

Dear [Mr/Ms Last Name] / Dear Sir or Madam (if unknown),

Opening paragraph: State purpose clearly
Body paragraphs: Develop each point (1 point per paragraph)
Closing paragraph: State what you want to happen next

Yours sincerely, [if you used a name] / Yours faithfully, [if you used Sir/Madam]
[Full name]
```

### Key Language

- "I am writing to express my concern regarding..."
- "I would be grateful if you could..."
- "I look forward to receiving your response."
- "Please do not hesitate to contact me should you require further information."

### Common Errors

- Confusing "Yours sincerely" and "Yours faithfully"
- Using contractions in a formal letter
- No clear request at the end
- Over-long sentences trying to sound formal

## 3. Academic Essay

### Purpose

To argue a position, discuss multiple perspectives, or analyze a topic using logical reasoning and evidence.

### Audience

Academic readers — teachers, professors, examiners. They expect structure, precision, formality, and evidence.

### Structure (Standard 4-paragraph IELTS/Academic format)

```
Introduction (2–3 sentences)
→ General statement (hook)
→ Paraphrase of the question
→ Thesis (your position or essay map)

Body Paragraph 1 (5–7 sentences)
→ Topic sentence
→ Explanation
→ Example/Evidence
→ Link to thesis

Body Paragraph 2 (5–7 sentences)
→ Topic sentence
→ Explanation
→ Example/Evidence
→ Link to thesis

Conclusion (2–3 sentences)
→ Restate thesis (different words)
→ Summarize key points
→ Closing thought
```

### Model Introduction (IELTS style)

> In recent years, the question of whether technology improves or hinders human communication has become increasingly debated. While some argue that digital tools have made interaction more efficient, others believe they have weakened the depth of personal relationships. This essay will argue that although technology offers clear benefits for communication, its overuse poses significant risks to meaningful human connection.

### Key Language

- **Thesis starters**: "This essay will argue that...", "It is contended that...", "While X is true, this essay maintains that..."
- **Body paragraph openers**: "One significant reason for this is...", "A key argument in favour of... is..."
- **Exemplifying**: "For example, a study by...", "To illustrate, consider..."
- **Concluding**: "In conclusion, it is clear that...", "To summarize, while both perspectives have merit..."

### Common Errors

- Thesis that is a fact, not an argument ("Technology is used by many people")
- Body paragraph with no topic sentence
- Examples without explanation ("For example, smartphones. This shows technology is important.")
- Changing opinion mid-essay
- Beginning a sentence with "And", "But", "Because" in academic writing
- Copying the question wording in the introduction

### Feedback Focus

- Does the thesis take a clear, arguable position?
- Does each body paragraph have a topic sentence?
- Are examples explained (not just listed)?
- Is cohesion achieved through connectors AND logical flow?

## 4. IELTS Academic Task 1 — Chart/Graph Description

### Purpose

Describe and summarize visual data (bar chart, line graph, pie chart, table, process diagram, map). Do NOT give opinions.

### Key Principles

1. **Overview first** — identify the main trend before any detail
2. **Group and compare** — don't describe every data point individually
3. **Use data language accurately** — numbers, percentages, approximations
4. **No opinion** — "This shows that society is wasting energy" is wrong; "The data shows a significant increase" is correct

### Structure

```
Introduction (1–2 sentences): Paraphrase the question + time period/location
Overview (1–2 sentences): The most significant trend(s) — no data yet
Body Paragraph 1: Key comparisons or main trends with specific data
Body Paragraph 2: Secondary trends or notable exceptions with specific data
```

### Model Overview Sentence (Bar Chart)

> Overall, it is clear that Country A consistently consumed more energy than Countries B and C throughout the period, while all three nations showed a general upward trend.

### Key Language — Trends

- **Increase**: rose, increased, grew, climbed, surged (sharply/gradually/steadily)
- **Decrease**: fell, declined, dropped, decreased, plummeted (dramatically/slightly)
- **Stable**: remained stable, levelled off, plateaued, showed little change
- **Approximate**: approximately, roughly, around, just over/under, nearly
- **Comparison**: while, whereas, in contrast, compared to, significantly more/less than

### Common Errors

- No overview paragraph (most common reason for Band 5 or below)
- Describing every single data point instead of trends
- Copying numbers without units ("increased to 50" → "increased to 50 million")
- Using opinion or recommendation language
- Writing less than 150 words

### Feedback Focus

- Is there a clear overview of the main trend?
- Are data points grouped, not listed one by one?
- Is data language varied (not just "increased" every time)?
- Are numbers referenced with units and approximate language?

## 5. IELTS Task 2 / Academic Essay (Exam Format)

### Essay Types and Their Approaches

**Opinion Essay (Agree/Disagree)**

- Take a clear position: "I completely agree/disagree" OR "I partially agree"
- Don't discuss the other side equally — your essay should favor your position
- Structure: Intro (thesis) → Body 1 (reason 1) → Body 2 (reason 2) → Conclusion

**Discussion Essay (Discuss Both Views)**

- Present BOTH sides fairly — then give your opinion in conclusion or as part of the introduction
- Structure: Intro → Body 1 (View A) → Body 2 (View B) → Conclusion (your view)

**Problem-Solution Essay**

- Identify the problem clearly → explain cause → propose specific solutions
- Structure: Intro → Body 1 (problem/cause) → Body 2 (solutions) → Conclusion

**Advantages-Disadvantages Essay**

- Balance both sides unless the question asks "do advantages outweigh disadvantages?" — then you must take a position
- Structure: Intro → Body 1 (advantages) → Body 2 (disadvantages) → Conclusion

**Two-Part Question Essay**

- Answer BOTH questions, one per body paragraph
- Structure: Intro → Body 1 (Q1) → Body 2 (Q2) → Conclusion

### IELTS Band Descriptors (simplified)

| Band | Task Response                   | Coherence             | Vocabulary                    | Grammar                       |
| ---- | ------------------------------- | --------------------- | ----------------------------- | ----------------------------- |
| 5    | Addresses task but incompletely | Some organization     | Limited range                 | Many errors                   |
| 6    | Addresses all parts             | Arranged coherently   | Adequate range                | Mix of accurate/inaccurate    |
| 7    | Covers all requirements         | Logical progression   | Sufficient range, some errors | Mostly accurate, some errors  |
| 8    | Fully developed position        | Skillful paragraphing | Wide range, rare errors       | Wide range, occasional errors |
| 9    | Fully relevant, fully developed | Cohesion unnoticeable | Natural, precise              | Full accuracy and flexibility |

### Timing Guide

- 5 min: Read question + plan (thesis + 2 body paragraph topics)
- 30 min: Write (intro + 2 body paragraphs + conclusion)
- 5 min: Check for errors

## 6. Report Writing

### Purpose

Present findings, analyze data, and make recommendations to a specific audience for a professional or academic purpose.

### Audience

Management, colleagues, clients, or academic supervisors. Expect objectivity and structured presentation.

### Structure

```
Title / Heading
Introduction: Purpose, scope, and method
Findings: What you discovered (organized by theme)
Conclusion: Summary of key findings
Recommendations: What should happen next (clear, specific, actionable)
```

### Key Features

- **Headings and subheadings** — standard in reports, unlike essays
- **Objective tone** — no "I think"; instead: "It is recommended that...", "The data suggests..."
- **Passive voice** used more than in casual writing: "Participants were surveyed", "Data was collected"
- **Bullet points** acceptable (unlike essays)

### Key Language

- "The purpose of this report is to..."
- "The findings indicate that..."
- "It is recommended that the company..."
- "Based on the data collected..."
- "In light of these findings..."

### Common Errors

- Writing a report like an essay (no headings, no recommendations section)
- Using "I think" or "In my opinion" (too informal/subjective)
- Recommendations that are vague: "They should improve" → "The company should implement a monthly feedback survey by Q3"

## 7. Summary and Paraphrase

### Purpose

Condense a source text or restate its meaning in your own words. Essential for academic writing and avoiding plagiarism.

### Key Rules

1. **Don't copy**. Change both the vocabulary AND the sentence structure.
2. **Keep the meaning**. Don't change what the original says.
3. **Be shorter** (summary) or **same length** (paraphrase).
4. **Attribute the source**: "According to...", "The author argues that..."

### Paraphrase Technique

Step 1: Read the original. Put it face-down.
Step 2: Write what it says from memory.
Step 3: Check: same meaning? different words? different structure?

### Model

**Original:** "The global rise in smartphone usage has fundamentally changed how people consume news, with many now preferring short social media posts over long-form journalism."

**Weak paraphrase (too close):** "The worldwide increase in smartphone use has fundamentally altered how individuals consume news, with many now preferring brief social media posts over lengthy journalism."

**Strong paraphrase:** "As smartphones have become increasingly prevalent worldwide, news consumption habits have shifted significantly — short-form social media content is now often preferred over traditional in-depth reporting."

### Common Errors

- Changing only a few words (synonym swapping is NOT paraphrasing)
- Losing meaning by paraphrasing too freely
- Forgetting to attribute the source

## 8. Creative Writing

### Purpose

Express ideas, emotions, or stories in an engaging, imaginative way. The goal is to make the reader feel something.

### Key Skills

- **Show, don't tell**: "She was nervous" (telling) → "Her hands wouldn't stop shaking" (showing)
- **Sensory detail**: Engage sight, sound, smell, taste, touch
- **Varied sentence length**: Short sentences for impact. Longer sentences build atmosphere and create a sense of momentum and rhythm.
- **Dialogue**: Make it natural — people don't speak in complete formal sentences
- **Specific over general**: "a bird" → "a small brown sparrow"

### Structure (Short Story)

```
Opening: Hook the reader immediately (action, dialogue, or striking image)
Rising action: Introduce the conflict or tension
Climax: The turning point
Resolution: How it ends (doesn't have to be happy)
```

### Common Errors

- Starting with "Once upon a time" or "In this story, I will..."
- Telling emotions instead of showing them
- No sensory detail — purely abstract
- All sentences the same length
- Too much plot summary, not enough scene

### Feedback Focus

- Does the opening hook you immediately?
- Is there at least one "show don't tell" moment?
- Is there sensory detail?
- Is there sentence variety?

## 9. Social / Everyday Writing

### Purpose

Informal messages, social media posts, WhatsApp, short notes between friends. The goal is naturalness, not formality.

### Key Features

- Contractions are normal: "I'm", "can't", "it's"
- Incomplete sentences are acceptable in messages: "Sounds good!", "On my way."
- Emojis and exclamation marks are contextually appropriate
- Direct and brief is better than long and formal

### Common Errors (for ESL learners)

- Being too formal in informal contexts: "I would like to inform you that I will be arriving at 7pm" → "I'll be there at 7!"
- Over-politeness that sounds unnatural: "Would it be possible if you could perhaps..." → "Could you...?"
- Translating idioms literally from L1

### Register Awareness (key skill)

Teach the difference between:

- **Formal**: Academic, official, business correspondence → no contractions, full sentences, precise vocabulary
- **Semi-formal**: Professional emails to colleagues, LinkedIn messages → contractions OK, conversational but professional
- **Informal**: Friends, family, social media → natural, brief, emojis OK
