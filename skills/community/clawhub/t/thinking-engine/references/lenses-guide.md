# The 11 Lenses — Deep Reference Guide

This guide explains each lens in depth, with examples of what to look for and common mistakes to avoid.

---

## How to Use This Guide

Read this when you need to go deeper on a specific lens. For most analyses, the SKILL.md instructions are sufficient. Come here when:
- A lens feels thin or generic
- The topic is unusually complex in one dimension
- You want to avoid common blind spots per lens

---

## 🔭 Lens 1 — Philosophical / Epistemological

**Core question:** What do we actually know? What can be known? What are the foundational assumptions?

**What to look for:**
- What truth claims are being made, explicitly or implicitly?
- What worldview underlies the conventional framing?
- Is this an empirical question, a normative question, or both?
- Are there competing philosophical traditions that would frame this completely differently?

**Common blind spots:**
- Treating empirical questions as settled when they're contested
- Missing the normative assumptions hidden inside "neutral" framing
- Ignoring non-Western philosophical traditions that offer different starting points

**Example probe:** "Is economic growth good?" — the philosophical lens asks: good according to whom? What theory of value is assumed? Is GDP a valid proxy for wellbeing?

---

## ⚖️ Lens 2 — Ethical / Moral

**Core question:** Who benefits? Who is harmed? What duties, rights, or virtues are at stake?

**Frameworks to apply (minimum two):**
- **Consequentialist:** What outcomes does this produce? For whom?
- **Deontological:** What rules, duties, or rights does this respect or violate?
- **Virtue ethics:** What kind of person/organization does this reflect?
- **Care ethics:** How does this affect relationships and dependencies?

**Common blind spots:**
- Only applying one framework and missing the others
- Ignoring harms to non-human stakeholders (nature, future generations)
- Papering over genuine moral tension with false consensus

---

## 🔧 Lens 3 — Practical / Real-World

**Core question:** Does this actually work? Where does theory break down?

**What to look for:**
- What resources, skills, time, and conditions are required?
- What typically goes wrong in implementation?
- Who has tried this before, and what happened?
- What is the minimum viable version vs. the ideal version?

**Common blind spots:**
- Assuming motivated, competent actors throughout
- Underestimating coordination costs
- Ignoring the gap between pilot success and full-scale deployment

---

## 📜 Lens 4 — Historical / Contextual

**Core question:** Has this happened before? What patterns repeat?

**What to look for:**
- Historical analogues — similar decisions, technologies, movements
- What did they reveal about human behavior under similar conditions?
- What patterns tend to repeat regardless of context?
- What is genuinely new this time (vs. what just feels new)?

**Common blind spots:**
- Selecting only confirming historical analogues
- Ignoring non-Western historical examples
- Assuming "this time is different" without strong evidence

---

## 🧠 Lens 5 — Psychological / Human Behavior

**Core question:** How do real humans actually respond to this?

**What to look for:**
- Relevant cognitive biases (loss aversion, status quo bias, social proof, etc.)
- Emotional drivers that override rational decision-making
- The gap between stated preferences and revealed preferences
- Psychological resistance or adoption drivers

**Common blind spots:**
- Assuming rational actors
- Ignoring emotional contagion and social dynamics
- Missing the psychological impact on the people implementing the decision, not just those affected by it

---

## 🕸️ Lens 6 — Systems / Second-Order Effects

**Core question:** What happens downstream? What feedback loops are created?

**What to look for:**
- Direct effects (first order)
- Reactions to those effects (second order)
- Reactions to reactions (third order)
- Feedback loops: reinforcing (accelerating) vs. balancing (stabilizing)
- Where are the leverage points? Where is the system fragile?

**Common blind spots:**
- Stopping at first-order effects
- Missing time delays between cause and effect
- Ignoring emergent properties that only appear at scale

---

## 💰 Lens 7 — Financial / Economic

**Core question:** Who gains? Who loses? What are the incentive structures?

**What to look for:**
- Direct costs and benefits
- Indirect and opportunity costs
- Who has financial incentives to maintain the status quo?
- What behaviors do the incentive structures encourage or discourage?
- Where do stated goals and financial incentives misalign?

**Common blind spots:**
- Focusing only on visible costs and ignoring externalities
- Missing how financial incentives distort stated motivations
- Ignoring distributional effects (who bears costs vs. who receives benefits)

---

## ⚙️ Lens 8 — Technological / Feasibility

**Core question:** Is the technology ready? What are the constraints and dependencies?

**What to look for:**
- Technology Readiness Level (TRL) of key components
- Infrastructure dependencies
- What happens at scale that doesn't happen in pilots?
- What technological shift could change this picture dramatically?

**Common blind spots:**
- Confusing prototype success with deployment readiness
- Ignoring the human/organizational side of technology adoption
- Missing interdependencies between technologies

---

## ♟️ Lens 9 — Competitive / Adversarial

**Core question:** Who opposes this? How could this be exploited?

**What to look for:**
- Who is threatened by this decision or idea?
- How will competitors, bad actors, or resistant parties respond?
- What are the attack surfaces — ways this could be gamed, undermined, or weaponized?
- What does the most sophisticated adversary do?

**Common blind spots:**
- Assuming good-faith actors throughout
- Ignoring regulatory or political resistance
- Missing how success itself creates new adversarial pressure

---

## 🌍 Lens 10 — Environmental / Societal

**Core question:** What is the effect on society, communities, and the natural world?

**What to look for:**
- Who is affected beyond direct stakeholders?
- Long-term cultural and community effects
- Environmental costs and benefits (direct and indirect)
- Effects on vulnerable or marginalized groups

**Common blind spots:**
- Treating society as a monolith
- Ignoring environmental costs as "externalities"
- Missing how cultural context shapes what effects are even visible

---

## 🔀 Lens 11 — Counterfactual / Alternative Scenarios

**Core question:** What if the unexpected happens? What are we not imagining?

**Three scenarios to generate:**
1. **Optimistic surprise:** What if something goes unexpectedly better than assumed? What would that require?
2. **Pessimistic surprise:** What if a key assumption turns out to be wrong? Which assumption is most fragile?
3. **Wild card:** What entirely left-field event could reshape the picture? (Technology shift, political shock, natural event, cultural change)

**Common blind spots:**
- Only imagining failures as "things go worse than expected" — missing structural surprises
- Ignoring positive black swans
- Failing to identify which assumption, if wrong, changes everything

---

## Cross-Lens Interactions

Some of the most valuable insights come from **lens conflicts**:

| Conflict | What it reveals |
|----------|-----------------|
| Ethical says ✓, Economic says ✗ | Hidden tradeoff between values and incentives |
| Practical says ✓, Systems says ✗ | Works locally, fails at scale |
| Historical says ✓, Psychological says ✗ | Pattern exists but humans resist it |
| Technical says ✓, Adversarial says ✗ | Vulnerable to exploitation despite capability |

When two lenses point in opposite directions — that's where the real decision lives.
