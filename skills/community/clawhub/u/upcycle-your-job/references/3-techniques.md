## The Flexible Working Negotiation Toolkit

### 1. The Preferences Diagnostic (Before You Negotiate)

**Ask yourself:**
- When work intrudes on home (email at 8pm, weekend call), how do I feel?
- When I can't answer a work email for 12 hours, how do I feel?
- Do I feel more productive in the office or at home?
- Does my partner have the same preference, and have we talked about it?

**The result:** If you're a Separator, negotiate for protected non-work time (e.g., no emails 7pm-7am). If you're an Integrator, negotiate for trust-based flexibility (e.g., work anywhere, manage your own hours).

> **Case: Laura's boundary negotiation** (Chapter 3): Laura told her boss she'd be unavailable after 7pm. She was surprised by his positive response. Her mistake: she'd assumed it would be rejected, so she never asked. The boundary wasn't the barrier — her fear was. **Key takeaway:** Ask first. The answer might surprise you.

### 2. The Business Case Framework (Three Levels)

When asking for flexible working, frame it as a business proposition:

**Level 1 — Personal (your manager's interest):**
- You stay productive and healthy → better performance
- You stay with the company → no recruitment/replacement cost
- Reduced sick leave from burnout prevention

**Level 2 — Team:**
- Your arrangement could model flexible working for others
- If other team members have similar needs, you're proof of concept
- A balanced manager models healthy boundaries, which reduces team burnout

**Level 3 — Organizational:**
- Aligns with stated DEI, wellbeing, or flexible working policies
- Tap into the hidden talent pool (returners, parents, carers)
- Competitive advantage in talent retention

### 3. The Three-Option Rule

Before you enter the negotiation, prepare three distinct options. Use the framework:

| Option | Arrangement | Why it works for employer | Why it works for me |
|---|---|---|---|
| A | 4 days/week, compressed | Same hours, fewer days — full coverage | One full day with family |
| B | 5 days/week, 10-3 in office + evening work from home | Core hours coverage, client meetings held | School pickup possible |
| C | Job-share with [colleague name] | Full role coverage, built-in backup | 2.5 days work, continuity |

> **Case: The Power Part-Timers evidence** (Chapter 6): The social enterprise Timewise publishes an annual list of "Power Part Timers" — senior leaders working reduced hours successfully. This is concrete evidence that senior flexible roles exist. When your boss says "senior roles can't be flexible," you can say: "Actually, here's a list of people doing it right now."
> **Key takeaway:** Have evidence ready. Your personal request is reinforced by external proof.

### 4. Outputs-First Framing

**Don't say:** "I want to reduce my hours to 4 days a week."
**Instead say:** "I've analyzed my role and identified the highest-value outputs I deliver. Here's a proposal for restructuring my work around those outputs, which would let me deliver the same results in a compressed schedule."

**The Work Out exercise (adapted from GE):**
1. List every task you currently do
2. Mark each as: High Value / Medium / Low Value / Redundant
3. For Low Value and Redundant tasks: who can take these on? What would need to happen to stop doing them?
4. Redesign your role around the High Value outputs only

### 5. Managing the Flexibility Stigma

**Anticipate the assumptions:**
- "She's less committed now" → Counter with output metrics
- "She's not available for the big opportunities" → Counter with visible availability during core hours
- "She's putting her family first" → Counter with: "I'm putting my productivity first. A balanced employee is a better employee."

**Visibility tactics:**
- Be more visible in your reduced hours, not less
- Schedule key stakeholder touchpoints intentionally
- Document and communicate your outputs clearly
- Don't apologize for your arrangement — own it as a conscious choice

### 6. The "Concession to Entitlement" Reframe

Many women approach flexible working as asking for a favor. The book reframes it: flexible working is not a concession for "deserving groups" (like new mothers). It's a sound business practice that improves productivity, retention, and diversity.

**When your manager treats it as a concession:**
- Redirect: "This isn't about making my life easier. This is about structuring work for maximum output."
- Name the evidence: "Research shows output-based flexible working increases productivity and engagement."
- Offer a trial: "Let's try this for 3 months with clear metrics. If outputs drop, we revisit."

### 7. Spillover Management for Dual-Career Couples

If your partner also works, manage spillover together:

**Warning signs of negative spillover:**
- Partner "off-loads" work stress to you daily
- You feel responsible for absorbing their exhaustion
- Your family time is dominated by complaint loops about work

**Fix:** Set "no work talk" boundaries. Use Professor Kinman's "corridor" technique — after work, have a small transition activity (run, yoga, call a friend, cook) before engaging with family. Being fully present in the unwinding activity restores energy.
