## Prompt 1 Output: Signature Offer Framework

### Methodology Name Options

**Option A: The Executive Ascent Framework™**
*Why it works:* "Ascent" is aspirational and directional. "Framework" signals structure and repeatability. Targets ambitious professionals who want a system, not just pep talks. Appeals to analytical thinkers (engineers, PMs, ops leaders) who are the ICP.

**Option B: The C-Suite Code™**
*Why it works:* "Code" implies unlocking a secret that not everyone knows — triggers curiosity. Pairs perfectly with insider positioning ("I know how promotions are actually decided because I made them"). Shorter, more memorable for social media.

**Option C: The Visibility Architecture Method™**
*Why it works:* "Architecture" = strategic and structured (appeals to type-A achievers). "Visibility" names the actual problem — most stalled managers are invisible to decision-makers, not unqualified. Differentiates from generic "leadership development" positioning.

**Recommended: The Executive Ascent Framework™** — most Google-searchable, cleanest trademark, works in every format.

### Ideal Client Profile

Maria is a Director of Product at a 5,000-person tech company. She's 36, earns $175K base, and has 10 years at the company. She's consistently rated "Exceeds Expectations," gets pulled into every major initiative, and trains the junior PMs — but was passed over for VP twice in the last 18 months. She tells herself she's not political enough, not visible enough, or maybe just not "VP material." She's read every leadership book, done her 360, asked for feedback (and gotten generic answers). Her fear is she'll plateau here and have to start over somewhere else. Her buying trigger: she just found out a peer with less experience was just promoted over her. Something clicked. She's ready to invest in figuring this out.

**Red flags:** Coaches looking for someone to do the work for them, individuals who believe their organization is irredeemably broken, people in toxic environments where no amount of coaching will change the structural dynamics.

**Phase 1 — Executive Audit (Weeks 1-4)**

*Deliverable:* Personalized Executive Audit Report — 360-degree perception gap analysis, stakeholder map, 3 blind spots to address, 3 strengths to amplify.

**Phase 2 — Influence Architecture (Weeks 5-16)**
*What shifts:* Client moves from being excellent at tasks to being excellent at impact. They change the rooms they're in, how they speak in those rooms, and who is advocating for them.

## Prompt 2 Output: Enrollment Conversation (Key Excerpt)

### Objection Handler — "I Need to Think About It"

*Script:*
"Of course. I'd never want you to make a decision this important before you're ready. Can I ask — when you say you need to think about it, is it more about making sure the timing is right, or is there something specific about the program you're not sure about?"

*[Pause and listen]*

If timing: "What would need to be true for the timing to feel right?"

If program concern: "I appreciate you being direct. What's the piece you're uncertain about? Let's talk through it."

If money: "Is the investment the piece you're thinking about?"

*[After they answer:]*
"Here's what I've seen with clients who've been in your position: the people who say 'I need to think about it' and come back in 3 months are usually thinking about the same things. The difference is that in those 3 months, one of two things happens: either nothing changes — and the cost of waiting becomes clearer — or the peer who was on your same track gets promoted, and the window gets harder. I'm not saying that to pressure you. I'm saying it because I watched it happen 12 times before I understood it as a pattern. What would it take for you to make a decision today?"

## Prompt 4 Output: Case Study — Maria's Promotion Story

### Full Case Study

**The Before**

Maria had spent eight years building products at one of the world's most competitive tech companies. Her team loved her. Her projects shipped on time. She'd trained three junior PMs who were now her peers — one of them had just been promoted to VP.

At her most recent performance review, her manager said she was "almost there." It was the third time she'd heard those exact words. That night, she sat in her car in the parking garage for 20 minutes before she could drive home.

"I kept thinking, 'What is 'almost there' even supposed to mean? I'm doing the VP's job. I've been doing it for two years.'" The cost wasn't just the salary gap — it was the creeping doubt that maybe she was wrong about herself.

**The Turning Point**

Six weeks later, she found out a peer with less experience and a shorter tenure had been promoted over her. She didn't feel angry. She felt done. Done waiting, done hoping the organization would notice on its own.

She booked a discovery call that evening.

**The Journey**

In Phase 1 of the Executive Ascent Framework, we ran a 360-degree perception audit. What Maria discovered surprised her: her peers and stakeholders didn't see her as "almost VP." They saw her as the best person at execution they'd ever worked with — indispensable as a Director and, in their minds, exactly where she belonged.

She was being perceived as a superstar at the wrong level.

Phase 2 was the hardest and most valuable. Maria shifted from running her team to shaping her organization. She stopped accepting every cross-functional task that kept her in execution mode. She started requesting (and getting) seats in strategic planning sessions. She built sponsorship relationships with two SVPs who hadn't known her name 6 months before.

The breakthrough moment came in Month 9, in a leadership alignment meeting where Maria — for the first time — challenged a business direction in front of 15 executives, with data and a clear recommendation. Her manager told her afterward it was the moment he knew she was ready.

**The Result**

Fourteen months after Maria's first coaching session, she was offered a VP of Product role. Not at a new company. At the one that had passed her over twice.

The offer included a $42,000 base salary increase and a team of 28. She negotiated an additional $15,000 in signing bonus using the language we'd rehearsed.

**The New Life**

Maria now leads one of the company's three core product lines. She mentors two of the Directors who were her peers. Last month, she was asked to present to the board.

"I spent three years thinking I wasn't ready," she said. "Sarah helped me understand I'd been ready — I just didn't know how to show it. The promotions don't find you. You have to walk into the room where they're decided."
