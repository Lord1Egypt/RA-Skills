# Anti-Patterns: Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

## Anti-Pattern 1: Goal Fixation

**The mistake:** Believing that setting a big goal is enough. "I want to lose 20 pounds" → nothing changes.

**The correction:** Goals are useful for setting direction, but systems are useful for making progress. Winners and losers share the same goals. Stop obsessing over the outcome. Design the process.

> **Case: The entrepreneur who wanted to build a million-dollar business** (Chapter 1): He set the goal, but had no system for daily customer acquisition. He knew where he wanted to go but didn't have a way to get there. The goal was direction; the system was transportation.

## Anti-Pattern 2: Willpower Dependency

**The mistake:** Believing that motivation and willpower are reliable. "I just need to try harder." But willpower is finite and depletable.

**The correction:** People with the best self-control rarely use it. They design their lives to avoid temptation. Environment design beats willpower every time.

> **Case: The cookie experiment** (Chapter 6): People who sat in front of a plate of cookies and told not to eat them had significantly less willpower for subsequent tasks than those who couldn't see the cookies. The visible temptation drained willpower — not through consumption, but through resistance.

## Anti-Pattern 3: All-or-Nothing Thinking

**The mistake:** One missed workout ruins the whole week. "I already failed, might as well give up."

**The correction:** Never miss twice. Missing once is an accident. Missing twice is the start of a new (bad) habit. Get back on track immediately. Perfection is not the goal — consistency is.

> **Case: The gym study** (Chapter 4): People who missed a workout were compared. Those who said "I'll start again tomorrow" were far more likely to stick long-term than those who said "I've blown it." The second group spiraled.

## Anti-Pattern 4: Identity Mismatch

**The mistake:** Trying to adopt habits that conflict with your current identity. "I'm not a morning person" → no morning routine will stick.

**The correction:** Align your habits with the identity you want, not the identity you have. Start by believing "I'm the kind of person who..." and then prove it with one small action.

> **Case: The voting study** (Chapter 3): People who said "I'm a voter" were more likely to vote than those who said "voting is important." The identity statement ("I'm a voter") was a stronger predictor than the values statement.

## Anti-Pattern 5: Chasing Motivation

**The mistake:** Waiting for the "right mood" to act. "I'll exercise when I feel like it."

**The correction:** Action creates motivation, not the other way around. Start small, build momentum. The motivation to continue appears after you start, not before.

> **Case: The writer who wrote every day** (Chapter 1): He said "Writing is hard. But starting is easy." The motivation to write a chapter came from the act of writing the first sentence. Action precedes motivation — not the reverse.
