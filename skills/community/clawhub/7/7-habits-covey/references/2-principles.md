> Source: The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People by Stephen R. Covey, Chapters 2, 4, 7

# Key Principles

## Principle 1: Paradigm shifts change everything

The way you see the problem IS the problem. A paradigm shift — seeing the world differently — changes your behavior more than any technique.

> **Case: The subway story** (Chapter 2): A father sat on a subway with his children, who were running wild and screaming. Covey asked if he could help. The father said, "We just came from the hospital. Their mother died an hour ago. I don't know what to think." Covey's paradigm instantly shifted from annoyance to compassion. Nothing changed except his frame.
> **Key takeaway**: Before trying to fix your behavior, examine the lens through which you see the situation.

## Principle 2: Character precedes competence

The personality ethic (smile, image, positive attitude) is superficial without character integrity. Trust is built on character — on who you are, not how you appear.

> **Case: The 19th century self-help literature** (Chapter 1): Covey studied 200 years of American self-help writing. The first 150 years were focused on character (integrity, humility, courage). Only the last 50 years shifted to personality (image, technique, positive thinking). The personality ethic fails because it's built on a foundation of sand.
> **Key takeaway**: Before working on your image, work on your character. Effectiveness built on technique without character is fragile.

## Principle 3: Proactivity is a choice, not a feeling

You are free to choose your response, regardless of the stimulus. Mood doesn't drive action — values do.

> **Case: The reactive employee** (Chapter 1): An employee complained, "My wife is so critical. I can't be happy at home." Covey asked: "Who chooses your response?" The employee realized he had given his wife the power to determine his happiness. The proactive response: "I choose to be loving regardless of her behavior."

## Principle 4: Understanding before being understood

The four biggest communication problems are: judging, advising, probing, and interpreting. Most people listen with the intent to reply, not with the intent to understand.

> **Case: The teenage son** (Chapter 9): A father tried to lecture his son about responsibility, but the son withdrew. Covey advised the father to spend a week just listening — to "seek first to understand" — without giving any advice. By the end of the week, the son was asking his father for guidance.
> **Key takeaway**: The deepest need of the human soul is to be understood. Diagnose before you prescribe. Listen before you advise.

## Principle 5: Synergy comes from valuing differences

Synergy is not compromise. It's not 1+1=2. It's 1+1=3 or more. The key is to genuinely value the differences between people.

> **Case: The failed business partnership** (Chapter 10): Two partners had completely different styles — one analytical, one creative. They were constantly in conflict. When they learned to synergize by combining their strengths instead of fighting over whose approach was better, their business grew 10x.
> **Key takeaway**: The best solutions come from combining different perspectives. Don't try to make everyone the same.

## Principle 6: Renewal is not optional

The four dimensions of renewal (physical, mental, social/emotional, spiritual) are not luxuries. They are the maintenance that keeps your effectiveness engine running.

> **Case: The manager who had no time** (Chapter 13): A manager complained she had no time to exercise or read. Covey asked: "How long have you felt this way?" "Five years." Covey's point: five years of not sharpening the saw had dulled every aspect of her life. Short-term "savings" had cost her long-term effectiveness.
> **Key takeaway**: You are the most valuable asset you have. Invest in your renewal. An hour of sharpening the saw saves hours of dull effort.
