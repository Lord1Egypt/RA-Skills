> Source: The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People by Stephen R. Covey, Chapters 1-4

# Core Framework: Private Victory → Public Victory → Renewal

## The Maturity Continuum

Covey's central model: effectiveness grows through three stages:

**Dependence → Independence → Interdependence**

| Stage | Meaning | Habits |
|---|---|---|
| Dependence | You need others to take care of you | (starting point) |
| Independence | You can take care of yourself | Habits 1-3 (Private Victory) |
| Interdependence | You can work effectively with others | Habits 4-6 (Public Victory) |

> **Case: The manager who couldn't delegate** (Chapter 1): A highly skilled engineer was promoted to manager but couldn't let go of doing the technical work himself. He was stuck in independence — great at doing, terrible at leading. Only when he learned to Think Win-Win and Synergize did he become an effective leader.
> **Key takeaway**: Independence is necessary but not sufficient. The highest level of effectiveness is interdependence.

## Habit 1: Be Proactive — The Power of Choice

Between stimulus and response, there is a space. In that space lies our freedom to choose.

**Circle of Influence** — things you can control (your behavior, your response, your choices).
**Circle of Concern** — things you worry about but can't control (weather, other people's opinions, the economy).

> **Case: Viktor Frankl** (Chapter 1): A psychiatrist imprisoned in Nazi concentration camps, stripped of everything — family, possessions, dignity. Yet he discovered that even in those conditions, he could choose his attitude. He wrote: "Between stimulus and response, there is a space. In that space is our power to choose our response. In our response lies our growth and our freedom."
> **Key takeaway**: The proactive approach is to focus your energy on your Circle of Influence. Reactive people focus on their Circle of Concern, blaming others and external circumstances.

## Habit 2: Begin with the End in Mind — Mental Creation

All things are created twice: first mentally, then physically. A personal mission statement is the highest form of mental creation.

> **Case: The funeral visualization** (Chapter 3): Covey asks readers to imagine their own funeral and listen to what people would say about them. Would they speak about your achievements or your character? The gap between what you want them to say and what they would actually say reveals the misalignment in your life.
> **Key takeaway**: If you don't design your own life plan, chances are you'll fall into someone else's plan. Write a personal mission statement.

## Habit 3: Put First Things First — The Time Management Matrix

| | Urgent | Not Urgent |
|---|---|---|
| **Important** | Quadrant I: Crises, deadlines (necessary but don't live here) | **Quadrant II**: Planning, prevention, relationship building, exercise (THE most important quadrant) |
| **Not Important** | Quadrant III: Interruptions, some calls/meetings (deceive us into thinking they're important) | Quadrant IV: Trivia, time wasters |

> **Case: The overwhelmed executive** (Chapter 5): An executive spent 90% of his time in Quadrant I (urgent/important) and felt like a firefighter. Covey had him spend just one hour per day in Quadrant II — planning, relationship building, learning — and within weeks, the fires in Quadrant I began to diminish.
> **Key takeaway**: Spend more time in Quadrant II. The urgent things will never stop, but they will shrink if you invest in prevention.

## The P/PC Balance — Production vs Production Capability

A farmer who kills the goose that lays the golden eggs (Aesop's fable) destroys future production for present gain. P (production) must be balanced with PC (production capability).

> **Case: The worn-out machine** (Chapter 8): A maintenance manager found that his team was so focused on meeting production quotas that they never stopped to maintain the machines. Eventually, the machines broke down, and they lost far more production time than they would have spent on maintenance.
> **Key takeaway**: Sharpen the Saw (Habit 7) is PC. Without it, P declines. The most effective people invest in their capacity to produce.
