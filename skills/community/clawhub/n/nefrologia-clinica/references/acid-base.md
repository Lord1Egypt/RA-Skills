# Acid-base and electrolytes

Use this reference for metabolic acidosis, metabolic alkalosis, respiratory disorders, mixed disorders, sodium, potassium, calcium, phosphate, magnesium, osmolarity, and urine electrolytes.

## Core approach

1. Confirm units and sample type: venous vs arterial gas, serum chemistry timing, albumin, lactate, ketones, renal function, and medications.
2. Identify the primary disorder from pH, bicarbonate, and pCO2.
3. Check expected compensation before naming a mixed disorder.
4. Calculate anion gap and correct it for albumin when albumin is available.
5. If relevant, calculate delta ratio, osmolar gap, urine anion gap, urine osmolarity, urine sodium, and transtubular potassium gradient only when clinically appropriate.
6. Tie the interpretation to kidney physiology: ammoniagenesis, distal acidification, proximal bicarbonate handling, aldosterone axis, and volume status.

## Red flags

- Severe hyperkalemia, ECG changes, severe acidemia, altered mental status, seizures, severe hyponatremia, pulmonary edema, shock, toxic alcohol suspicion, and rapidly worsening kidney function.

## Sources

Prefer nephrology texts, KDIGO context when relevant, critical care reviews, and primary literature for controversial thresholds.
