> Source: Thinking, Fast and Slow by Daniel Kahneman, Parts 2-4

# Anti-Patterns: Common Cognitive Errors

## Anti-Pattern 1: Overconfidence

**The mistake:** Believing you know more than you do. Overconfidence is not limited to arrogant people — it affects everyone, especially experts.

**The correction:** Use the outside view. Before making a confident prediction, ask: "What is the base rate for similar predictions by similar people?" Experts are often less accurate than they think they are.

> **Case: The stock pickers** (Part 3, Chapter 20): Kahneman presents the evidence on financial advisors: their stock picks don't outperform random selection, yet they remain supremely confident. The illusion of skill persists because our memory selectively recalls our wins and forgets our losses.

## Anti-Pattern 2: The Planning Fallacy

**The mistake:** Underestimating time, cost, and risk while overestimating benefits — systematically, not occasionally.

**The correction:** Use the outside view. Before approving any plan, ask: "What is the base rate for similar projects?" If 40% of similar projects fail, yours probably has a 40% chance of failing too.

> **Case: The Sydney Opera House** (Part 3, Chapter 23): Originally estimated to cost $7 million and be completed in 1963. It opened in 1973 at a cost of $102 million. 15x the estimate. The planners knew every detail of the design but ignored the base rate for iconic architectural projects.

## Anti-Pattern 3: Hindsight Bias

**The mistake:** After an event occurs, believing that you "knew it all along." This creates a false sense of certainty about the past and makes you overconfident about predicting the future.

**The correction:** Before learning the outcome of a decision, write down your prediction and your confidence level. After the outcome, compare. This is the only way to calibrate your judgment.

> **Case: The Pearl Harbor intelligence** (Part 3, Chapter 22): After Pearl Harbor, intelligence analysts insisted they had known an attack was imminent. In fact, the signals were ambiguous and many analysts had predicted a different target. Hindsight bias erased the uncertainty that existed before the event.

## Anti-Pattern 4: Confirmation Bias

**The mistake:** Seeking evidence that confirms what you already believe and ignoring evidence that contradicts it.

**The correction:** Actively seek disconfirming evidence. Ask: "What would cause my belief to be wrong?" If you can't think of anything, you're not trying hard enough.

## Anti-Pattern 5: Base Rate Neglect

**The mistake:** Focusing on the individual case (the vivid story) while ignoring the statistical reality (the base rate).

**The correction:** Always consider the base rate first, then adjust for specific case evidence. The vivid story of a startup founder succeeding should not outweigh the fact that 90% of startups fail.

> **Case: The lawyer and the cab driver** (Part 2, Chapter 14): Kahneman describes a man who is shy, organized, and detail-oriented. People predict he is a librarian (the representative individual) even though there are far more cab drivers than librarians. The base rate (more cab drivers exist) is ignored in favor of representativeness.

## Anti-Pattern 6: The Affect Heuristic

**The mistake:** Letting your emotional reaction to something determine your judgment of its risks and benefits.

**The correction:** Separate the question "How do I feel about this?" from "What is the evidence?" If you feel enthusiastic about an opportunity, you will underestimate its risks. If you feel afraid, you will overestimate them. Neither is a reliable guide.
