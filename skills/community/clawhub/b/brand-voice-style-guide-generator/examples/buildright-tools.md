# Example: BuildRight Tools

**Company:** BuildRight Tools  
**Industry:** B2B SaaS — project management for construction companies  
**ICP:** Operations managers and project leads at mid-size general contractors (25–200 employees), 35–55, desktop-heavy, skeptical of tech  
**Competitors:** Procore, Buildertrend, CoConstruct  
**Mission:** "We make the paperwork as solid as the builds."

---

## Prompt 1 Output: Brand Voice Audit + Personality Profile

### Voice Gap Analysis

**Sample 1 (Homepage hero):** "BuildRight Tools empowers construction teams with innovative project management solutions designed to streamline workflows and enhance operational efficiency."

Diagnosis: Sounds like every B2B SaaS company from 2015. "Empowers," "innovative," and "streamline" are the most over-used words in the industry. The target customer — a 50-year-old site foreman who's been managing projects on a yellow legal pad for 30 years — will scroll right past this.

Should sound like: Gruff, practical, direct. Talks about the job, not about "solutions." Uses the customer's actual language (RFIs, punch lists, daily logs, change orders — not "workflow optimization").

Gap: Zero specificity. You could swap "construction" for any industry and this copy still works — which means it works for nobody.

**Sample 2 (Email nurture):** "Hi [First Name], I wanted to reach out because I believe BuildRight could be a great fit for your team..."

Diagnosis: Passive and apologetic. "I wanted to reach out" is hedging language — it signals the sender isn't confident in what they're selling. Construction people respect confidence. They make hard calls under pressure. They don't want a vendor who "believes" something might work.

Should sound like: Lead with the result. "Your daily reports are taking 2 hours. BuildRight cuts it to 20 minutes."

Gap: Lacks the directness that earns trust with a blue-collar buyer who's already wary of software salespeople.

**Sample 3 (Social post):** "Excited to share our latest feature update! 🎉 Check out our new dashboard improvements that will help your team stay connected and aligned. #ConstructionTech #Projectmanagement"

Diagnosis: Generic, emoji-driven, and says nothing. "Stay connected and aligned" means nothing to someone juggling 6 subcontractors and a surprise soil test. The hashtags are industry defaults.

Should sound like: "New in BuildRight: your daily log, RFI status, and punch list — one screen, no toggling. Site supervisors in our beta cut their morning admin time by 40%. Here's how."

Gap: No specificity, no proof, no personality. Sounds like a startup intern wrote it, not a tool built for people who work with their hands.

**Overall gap:** BuildRight is using corporate SaaS copy to sell to non-corporate, hands-on buyers. The gap isn't just tone — it's worldview. The company is talking about software. Its customers are thinking about deadlines, weather delays, and getting home before dark.

### Brand Archetype

**Primary: The Everyman**  
BuildRight's buyer is not looking for a hero brand or a sage brand. They want a tool that works like they work — no nonsense, no training manual, no IT ticket. The Everyman archetype says: "We're one of you. We built this because we've been on the job site too." That's the trust unlock for this market.

**Secondary: The Hero**  
Surfaces in case studies and testimonials — the customer is the hero who finished the project on time despite budget cuts. BuildRight is the tool that made it possible. Never position BuildRight itself as the hero.

**Archetypes to avoid:**  
- **The Sage:** Sounds preachy and academic. This customer doesn't want insights — they want their punch list done.
- **The Ruler:** Sounds corporate and top-down. The ICP has been burned by expensive enterprise software before.

---

### Voice Analogies

1. **Celebrity:** Mike Rowe (Dirty Jobs) — straight talk, genuine respect for tradespeople, no pretension
2. **Brand:** DeWalt — built tough, no fluff, claims backed by specs not adjectives
3. **Media:** For Construction Pros magazine — written by people in the industry for people in the industry; assumes competence, skips the hand-holding

---

### Five Voice Pillars

1. **Straight** — We say what we mean, first sentence. No throat-clearing, no disclaimers, no "I wanted to reach out."
2. **Proven** — Every claim has a number or a name behind it. "Faster" is meaningless. "40% less admin time" is a reason to buy.
3. **Grounded** — We use job site language, not software language. RFIs, not "tickets." Punch lists, not "task tracking."
4. **Durable** — Our copy feels like it was built to last, not churned out for clicks. Dense, specific, worth reading twice.
5. **Respectful** — We never talk down to someone who can read a blueprint. We assume they're smart and busy.

## Prompt 2 Output: Messaging Framework + Tone Guide

### Taglines

1. **Short & punchy:** "Built for the build." — Mirrors the brand name, doubles as a quality signal
2. **Benefit-led:** "Project management that works as hard as your crew." — Speaks the customer's language
3. **Question format:** "Still running your projects on spreadsheets?" — Calls out the status quo the ICP knows
4. **Personality-led:** "No fluff. No training manual. Just the job, managed." — Everyman archetype in action
5. **Long-form:** "BuildRight gives construction teams the daily logs, RFI tracking, and punch lists they need to finish on time — without the software learning curve." — For About pages and email footers

**Recommended:** #2 ("Project management that works as hard as your crew") — directly mirrors the ICP's identity as a hard-working professional without being patronizing.

---

### Elevator Pitches

**15-second pitch:** BuildRight is project management built specifically for construction teams — daily logs, RFIs, punch lists, and change orders, all in one place. No training. Just open it and go.

**60-second pitch:** Most project management software is built for tech companies. Construction project managers end up using spreadsheets and WhatsApp because the tools don't fit the work. BuildRight is different. We built it on real job sites, with real project managers, specifically for how construction work actually runs — daily logs, RFI tracking, punch list management, change order approvals. Our customers at firms like [CUSTOMER NAME] cut their daily admin time from 2 hours to under 20 minutes in their first week. No IT department required. No week-long onboarding. You open it, it makes sense, and you get back to the job.

**LinkedIn Summary (company page):** Construction runs on tight deadlines, thin margins, and people who don't have time for software that needs a manual. We built BuildRight for them. Daily logs, RFIs, punch lists, change orders — everything a project manager needs to keep a job site moving, in one tool that actually works the way construction does. No enterprise contracts. No IT ticket to get set up. Just open it and run your project. Used by 1,200+ general contractors across North America. Built on job sites, not in a conference room.

## Prompt 3 Output: Content Rules + Examples Library

### Vocabulary List

**USE:**
- "Job site" (not "worksite" or "work environment")
- "Punch list" (not "task list" or "to-do list")
- "RFI" (spell out Request for Information only on first use for external audiences)
- "Change order" (not "scope change" or "modification request")
- "Daily log" (not "progress report" or "status update")
- "Subcontractor" (not "vendor" or "partner")
- "Foreman" / "superintendent" / "PM" (use role-specific titles, not generic "user" or "team member")
- "Deadline" (not "target date" or "milestone")
- "On time, on budget" (the universal north star)
- "Built for" (positions vs. generic tools)
- Numbers with units ("2 hours saved," "40% faster," "8 minutes to log")
- "Here's how" (leads into practical content)
- "No [pain]" (e.g., "No spreadsheets," "No training manual")
- "Works" / "Runs" / "Gets the job done"
- "Grounded" / "Solid" / "Reliable"
- "Crew" (not "workforce" or "resources")
- "The build" (not "the project")
- "Real" (as in "real job sites," "real construction teams")
- "Simple" (only when something actually is simple — never as marketing fluff)

**AVOID:**
- "Empower" — overused SaaS jargon, means nothing to a foreman
- "Innovative" — every company says this; it signals you have nothing real to say
- "Solution" — vague, corporate, doesn't describe anything
- "Streamline" — same problem as "empower"
- "Robust" — lazy adjective, no specificity
- "World-class" — unverifiable, sounds like puffery
- "Best-in-class" — same
- "Leverage" — corporate-speak; say "use"
- "Synergy" / "alignment" — irrelevant to the ICP's actual work
- "Stakeholders" — call them what they are (clients, GC, subcontractors)
- "Utilize" — use "use"
- "Pain points" — say the specific pain (daily logs taking 2 hours)
- "Game-changer" — overused, underproven
- "Excited to share" — passive, nervous, not on-brand
- "I believe" / "I think" / "I hope" — hedging language; lead with claims, back with proof
- "Stay connected" — meaningless in construction context
- "Holistic" — out of place in this industry
- "Journey" (the customer's) — not the language of construction
- "Transparency" as a value prop — too vague to mean anything
- "Disrupt" — construction industry bristles at this framing

### Do/Don't Table

| Rule | DON'T | DO |
|------|-------|-----|
| Lead with the result | "BuildRight is a project management platform that helps construction teams manage their daily operations more effectively." | "BuildRight cuts daily log time from 2 hours to 20 minutes. That's it." |
| Use job site language | "Enhance operational efficiency across your project lifecycle." | "Finish your punch list before lunch." |
| Claims need proof | "BuildRight is faster and easier than anything else on the market." | "BuildRight users log their dailies in 8 minutes. Industry average: 47." |
| Never apologize for reaching out | "I just wanted to reach out to see if BuildRight might be a potential fit..." | "Your daily logs are taking too long. BuildRight fixes that. Want to see?" |
| Features are useless without outcomes | "New: improved RFI dashboard with enhanced sorting and filtering options." | "New: find any RFI in under 10 seconds. No more digging through email threads." |
| Support sounds human | "Thank you for reaching out. We apologize for any inconvenience. Your issue has been escalated to our team." | "On it. Here's what's happening and how we're fixing it: [explanation]. ETA: 2 hours." |
| Headlines are earned, not decorated | "🚀 Exciting news! We're thrilled to announce our biggest feature drop yet! 🎉" | "BuildRight now syncs your RFIs directly with Procore. Took 6 months. Worth it." |
| Specificity > generality | "Our customers love BuildRight and see great results." | "Jensen Construction reduced change order disputes by 60% in their first 3 months." |
| Confidence, not aggression | "Unlike outdated competitors, BuildRight is built for the modern job site." | "BuildRight is built for how construction actually works. Procore is built for enterprise. Different tools for different jobs." |
| Respect the reader's time | "In this comprehensive guide, we'll be walking you through everything you need to know about..." | "Here's the short version. [3 bullet points.] Full breakdown below if you want it." |

### Before/After Rewrites

**1. Homepage Hero Headline**
- Before: "Empower Your Construction Team with Innovative Project Management"
- After: "Your daily logs. Your RFIs. Your punch lists. One tool. No training."
- What changed: Replaced abstract promise with specific deliverables. "One tool. No training." directly addresses the two biggest ICP objections (fragmented tools, software learning curve).

**2. LinkedIn Post Opening Line**
- Before: "Excited to share some insights about project management best practices for construction teams!"
- After: "The average GC project manager spends 2.3 hours a day on admin. Here's exactly where it goes — and how to cut it in half."
- What changed: Leads with a specific, surprising data point instead of a preamble. Signals expertise before asking for attention.

**3. Email Subject Line**
- Before: "Check out what's new at BuildRight!"
- After: "Your punch list is in 3 places right now (fix this today)"
- What changed: Opens with the reader's problem, not the sender's news. Parenthetical adds urgency without using "urgent."

**4. Product Feature Description**
- Before: "BuildRight's advanced reporting module delivers comprehensive insights into your project's performance metrics, enabling data-driven decision making across your entire organization."
- After: "See every job's status at a glance — budget, schedule, open RFIs, and punch list progress — in one dashboard that updates in real time. No more calling the foreman for a status update."
- What changed: Replaced abstract capability ("comprehensive insights") with specific use case (calling the foreman). The last sentence is the real value — removing a daily friction point.

**5. Customer Support Reply** (billing dispute)
- Before: "Thank you for contacting BuildRight Support. We apologize for any inconvenience this may have caused. Your inquiry has been received and will be reviewed by our billing team within 3-5 business days."
- After: "Got your message — I can see exactly what happened here. Your card was charged twice on March 12 because of a sync issue on our end. That's our mistake, not yours. I'm reversing the duplicate charge now. You'll see it back in 3–5 business days. Sorry for the hassle."
- What changed: Opens with action (I can see), explains what happened (builds trust), owns the error (no passive voice), gives a concrete timeline, and closes like a human.

## Prompt 4 Output: Team Training Kit (excerpt)

### Brand Voice Quiz (Sample Questions)

**Q1: Which of these is the most on-brand homepage subheadline for BuildRight?**

A) "Leveraging cutting-edge technology to empower construction professionals with innovative solutions"  
B) "RFI tracking, daily logs, punch lists — without the learning curve"  
C) "The comprehensive platform that streamlines your construction workflow end to end"  
D) "We're excited to help your team achieve its full potential"

**Answer: B** — Uses specific job site vocabulary (RFIs, daily logs, punch lists), leads with deliverables, and addresses the #1 ICP objection (software learning curve) in 10 words.

**Q3: A customer tweets that BuildRight "saved their project." Which reply is most on-brand?**

A) "Thanks so much! We're so happy to hear that! 🙌 Love hearing from our amazing customers! 😊"  
B) "Great to hear, [name]. Which part — the RFI tracking or the daily logs? Asking for our next changelog."  
C) "We appreciate your feedback and are thrilled that BuildRight has added value to your project management experience."  
D) "Appreciate it — what project? We might want to feature you in a case study."

**Answer: B** — On-brand reply: specific (asks which feature), Grounded (job site language), shows active product interest. Confident without being sycophantic.

### "Is This On-Brand?" Exercise (Sample)

**Exercise 3: LinkedIn post about a new feature**

*"We're thrilled to announce a major platform update that enhances BuildRight's document management capabilities, enabling construction teams to organize, store, and retrieve project documentation with unprecedented ease and efficiency. This feature is part of our ongoing commitment to innovation and delivering best-in-class solutions for the construction industry. Learn more on our website! #ConstructionTech #Innovation #Projectmanagement"*

A) On-brand — embodies *Proven* and uses the right channel tone for LinkedIn  
B) Partially on-brand — good intent but too many corporate buzzwords and no proof  
C) Off-brand — violates the *Straight* and *Proven* pillars; should be rewritten

**Answer: C**  
This post violates every core rule. "Unprecedented ease," "best-in-class," "commitment to innovation" — these are exactly the off-brand words on the avoidance list. There's no proof (no time saved, no specific workflow described), no job site language, and it ends with a non-specific CTA and generic hashtags. A rewrite: "New in BuildRight: find any document on any job in under 10 seconds. We indexed 2M+ project files in our beta. Here's how the search works."
