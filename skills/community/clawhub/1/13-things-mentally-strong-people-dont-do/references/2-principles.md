> Source: 13 Things Mentally Strong People Don't Do by Amy Morin, Chapters 1-4, 10

# Key Principles

## Principle 1: Self-pity is a choice

When something bad happens, it is natural to feel sad, angry, or scared. Self-pity is not the same as sadness. Self-pity is the belief that you are uniquely unlucky and that the world owes you something. It is a choice to stay stuck.

> **Case: The widow who rebuilt her life** (Chapter 1): After Morin's husband died, she faced a choice. "I could either feel sorry for myself and stay in bed, or I could take tiny steps forward. I chose to take a motorcycle safety course and buy a motorcycle. It wasn't about the motorcycle. It was about proving to myself that I could still learn new things and experience joy."

## Principle 2: No one can make you feel anything without your permission

When you say "He made me angry" or "She made me feel guilty," you are giving away your power. The truth is: you choose how to respond. Taking back that choice is the foundation of mental strength.

> **Case: The daughter controlled by guilt** (Chapter 2): Morin's client was a grown woman whose mother controlled her through guilt. Every time the mother said "I guess I'm just a terrible mother," the client would cave. Morin helped her realize: "Your mother's words are not commands. They are words. You can choose how to respond." The client learned to say: "I love you, but I can't do what you're asking."

## Principle 3: The opposite of dwelling is learning

Reflecting on the past can be productive. Dwelling on the past is never productive. The difference is simple: are you learning and moving forward, or are you replaying the pain?

> **Case: The man stuck in his divorce** (Chapter 7): A man spent five years replaying the details of his divorce, asking "What if?" and "Why?" He was stuck. Morin's intervention: "For every hour you spend thinking about the past, spend one hour taking action toward a future goal." Within weeks, he started a new business and began dating again.

## Principle 4: Discipline the exchange, not the elimination

You cannot simply stop a bad habit. You must exchange it for a good one. Every bad habit meets a need (comfort, escape, validation). Find a healthier way to meet that need.

> **Case: The former smoker** (Chapter 8): "I once worked with a woman who had quit smoking three times and relapsed each time. We discovered she used smoking as a way to take breaks from her stressful job. Once she replaced smoking with a five-minute walk outside, she never relapsed. The exchange, not the elimination, is what made it stick."

## Principle 5: Reward courage, not outcomes

Mentally strong people do not fear failure. They fear not trying. The goal is not to avoid mistakes — it is to try new things, learn, and grow stronger from the experience.

> **Case: The mother who praised effort** (Chapter 10): A mother wanted her son to be more resilient. Morin advised her to stop praising his grades and start praising his effort. "You studied so hard for that test" rather than "You got an A." When the boy failed a test, he was able to say: "I studied hard. It didn't work out. I'll try a different approach next time."

## Principle 6: Resentment is like drinking poison and expecting the other person to die

When you hold a grudge, the person you resent may not even know about it. You are the one suffering. Letting go is not for them — it is for you.

> **Case: The betrayed business partner** (Chapter 9): A businessman was consumed by resentment toward his former partner who had cheated him. The partner had moved on and was thriving. Morin asked: "Who is suffering from your resentment?" He realized he was punishing himself, not his former partner. Letting go freed him to start a new, more successful venture.

## Principle 7: Mental strength compounds

Just like physical strength, mental strength grows through consistent practice. Each time you choose a healthy response instead of an unhealthy one, you build a little more muscle. Over time, the small choices add up.

> **Case: The step-by-step recovery** (Introduction): "After my husband died, I didn't try to fix everything at once. I made small choices each day. Some days I chose to get out of bed. Other days I chose to call a friend instead of isolating myself. Over a year, those small choices added up to a completely new life. That's how mental strength works — one choice at a time."
