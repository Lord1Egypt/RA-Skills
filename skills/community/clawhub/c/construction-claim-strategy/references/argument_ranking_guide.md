# Argument Ranking — Detailed Guide

*Construction Claim Strategy Framework — Fully Generic*

---

## Why the Ranking Exists

Not all arguments carry the same weight. Stronger arguments are harder to rebut and more likely to be accepted by tribunals, adjudicators, and courts. Weaker arguments are easier to challenge.

Always start from the top of the ranking. Only move down when stronger options are unavailable.

---

## Rank 1: Textual Interpretation (Strongest)

**What it is:**
Argue directly from the actual words used in the Contract clause.

**Why it is strongest:**
- Based on express terms the parties agreed to.
- Most objective and difficult for the other side to dismiss.
- Tribunals and courts prefer to give effect to clear wording.

**When to use:** Whenever the wording of the clause supports your position.

**Example structure:**
> The Contract provides that the Contractor shall be entitled to [entitlement] where the Authority issues an instruction which changes the scope of the Works.
> On [date], the Authority issued Instruction No. X which required the Contractor to [specific change]. This instruction directly changed the scope.
> Accordingly, the Contractor is entitled to [relief] pursuant to the clear wording of the clause.

**Recommended disclosure level:** State Precisely — you can be confident and specific.

---

## Rank 2: Factual Distinction (Strong)

**What it is:**
Accept that the clause exists, but argue that the facts of your case fall outside the situation the clause was intended to cover.

**Why it is strong:**
Stays close to the Contract wording while showing why the clause does not apply to the current situation.

**When to use:** When the clause wording is not clearly in your favour, but the facts are materially different from what the clause contemplates.

**Example structure:**
> While the Contract contains provisions regarding [subject], those provisions are predicated on the assumption that [normal situation].
> In the present case, the situation is materially different because [key factual difference].
> Accordingly, the clause does not operate to [deny the claim / allocate risk in the way suggested].

**Recommended disclosure level:** State Precisely or Keep General — be precise on facts that support the distinction.

---

## Rank 3: Concede-then-Distinguish (Moderate-Strong)

**What it is:**
Concede that the clause would normally apply, then explain why the current situation is extraordinary or materially different.

**Why it is moderate-strong:**
Shows reasonableness (not denying the clause entirely), while still protecting your position.

**When to use:** When the clause appears to go against you at first glance, but there are good reasons why it should not apply in this specific case.

**Example structure:**
> The Contractor acknowledges that, in normal circumstances, [risk] would be allocated to the Contractor under the Contract.
> However, the present situation is materially different. The Authority [did X / failed to do Y / provided incorrect information], which fundamentally changed the nature of the risk.
> In these exceptional circumstances, it would be inconsistent with the Contract for the risk to remain with the Contractor.
> Accordingly, the Contractor is entitled to [relief].

**Recommended disclosure level:** Keep General — be careful not to over-disclose internal thinking.

---

## Rank 4: Implied Terms / Prevention Principle (Weakest)

**What it is:**
Argue that a term should be implied into the Contract, or that the Authority's actions prevented you from performing.

**Why it is weakest:**
- Implied terms are harder to prove.
- Prevention principle is heavily contested.
- Moves away from express Contract words.
- Easier for the other side to challenge.

**When to use:** Only as a last resort, when none of the stronger arguments are available.

**Example structure:**
> It is a well-established principle that a party to a contract must not do anything to prevent the other party from performing its obligations.
> In this case, the Authority's [action / inaction / instruction] had the effect of preventing the Contractor from [completing the works / mitigating losses].
> As a result, the Contractor is entitled to [relief].

**Note:** More commonly used in Common Law jurisdictions. In Civil Law systems, similar concepts exist under good faith (*bonne foi* / *Treu und Glauben*).

**Recommended disclosure level:** Keep General — these arguments are more vulnerable.

---

## How to Choose

| Rank | Argument Type | Strength | When to Use | Recommendation |
|------|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Textual Interpretation | Strongest | Clause wording supports your position | Use first |
| 2 | Factual Distinction | Strong | Facts are materially different | Strong backup |
| 3 | Concede-then-Distinguish | Moderate-Strong | Clause appears unfavourable but facts are exceptional | Use carefully |
| 4 | Implied Terms / Prevention | Weakest | No better argument available | Last resort |

---

## Combining Arguments (Primary + Fallback)

You can and often should use a primary + fallback approach in the same response.

**Recommended structure:**
1. Lead with the strongest available argument.
2. Clearly signal the fallback.
3. Use clear paragraph breaks or numbering.

**Example — Textual Interpretation + Factual Distinction:**

> **Primary Position — Textual Interpretation**
> The Contract states that the Contractor is entitled to additional time and cost where the Authority issues an instruction which varies the Works. The Authority's Instruction dated [X] required the Contractor to [change]. This falls squarely within the wording of the clause.
>
> **Alternative Position — Factual Distinction**
> Without prejudice to the above, even if the clause were interpreted more restrictively, the facts of this case are materially different from the normal situation contemplated by the Contract. At the time of the instruction, [key fact that makes it different]. Accordingly, the clause does not operate to exclude the Contractor's entitlement.

## Argument Strength ↔ Disclosure Control Interaction

| Argument Type | Strength | Disclosure Level | Reason |
|---|---|---|---|
| Textual Interpretation | Strongest | State Precisely | Position is robust; confidence is high |
| Factual Distinction | Strong | State Precisely / Keep General | Precise on supporting facts |
| Concede-then-Distinguish | Moderate-Strong | Keep General | Partial concession; don't over-disclose |
| Implied Terms / Prevention | Weakest | Keep General | Vulnerable; avoid giving material to attack |

**Rule:** Stronger arguments → more precise disclosure. Weaker arguments → more general disclosure. Always apply scope control regardless.
