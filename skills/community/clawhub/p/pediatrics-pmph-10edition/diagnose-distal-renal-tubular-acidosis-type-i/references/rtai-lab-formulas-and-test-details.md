# Detailed Laboratory Formulas and Test Protocols for RTA-I

## FE HCO₃⁻ Calculation
- **Formula**: FE HCO₃⁻ (%) = (Urine HCO₃⁻ / Serum HCO₃⁻) ÷ (Urine Creatinine / Serum Creatinine) × 100%
- **Units**: All concentrations in mmol/L
- **Interpretation**:
  - < 3%: Strongly supports RTA-I
  - 3–5%: Borderline; consider clinical context
  - ≥ 5%: Suggests proximal RTA (Type II) or other causes

## NH₄Cl Loading Test Protocol
- **Dose**: 0.1 g NH₄Cl per kg body weight, divided TID for 3 days OR single dose under monitoring
- **Monitoring**: Check venous blood gas and electrolytes every 6–12 hours
- **Endpoint**: Serum HCO₃⁻ < 20 mmol/L
- **Urine collection**: Midstream sample at endpoint for pH measurement
- **Safety**: Stop immediately if pH < 7.2, K⁺ < 2.5 mmol/L, or severe symptoms develop

## Urine Ammonia Assessment
- Semi-quantitative methods (e.g., colorimetric strips) are acceptable in clinical practice
- Markedly reduced ammonia excretion is characteristic of RTA-I due to impaired distal H⁺ secretion

## Differential Diagnosis Clues
| Feature               | RTA-I (Distal)       | RTA-II (Proximal)     |
|-----------------------|----------------------|------------------------|
| Urine pH (acidotic)   | >5.5                 | <5.5 (can acidify)     |
| FE HCO₃⁻              | <5%                  | >15%                   |
| Hypokalemia           | Severe               | Moderate               |
| Nephrolithiasis       | Common               | Rare                   |
| Bone disease          | Common               | Less common            |
