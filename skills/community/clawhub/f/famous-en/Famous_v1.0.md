# I. Delegation and Letting Go (#1-6)

## #1 Blind Delegation

**Scenario**
You hired a sheepdog. You don't know how it herds sheep—you only see that every evening, all the sheep are there, not one lost. Three years now.

**Core Question**
When AI output is consistently correct, is there an obligation to understand how it works? How long can "good results are enough" last?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: You have 30 sheep; if one is lost, you'll notice immediately. Blind delegation risk is manageable.
- M: You have 3000 sheep; you can't possibly count them. AI says "none lost"—how do you verify?
- H: Your sheepdog was replaced (AI model updated). The new dog's herding method is completely different, but you still only look at results. One day it drove the sheep into a river.

**Real-World Mapping**
- Using AI to translate documents but never spot-checking quality
- Using AI to write code but never reviewing
- Using AI for investment but never checking positions

**Insights**
The essence of blind delegation is **using trust to replace understanding**. This is efficient in low-risk scenarios but accumulates "understanding debt"—when you need to understand, you've forgotten how.

---

## #2 Last Mile

**Scenario**
The sheepdog drove the flock from the mountain to the village entrance. But there are three roads: left goes to the sheep pen, right goes to the slaughterhouse, and middle goes through someone else's land. The dog stops at the village entrance and waits for you.

**Core Question**
AI completed 95% of the work, the last 5% requires human judgment. But humans often give up the final judgment because "AI already did everything"—directly choosing the default option.

**Experimental Variants**
- E: The three roads are clear; you know at a glance which to take. You chose.
- M: You're tired, didn't even look and clicked "confirm." Result: took the middle road through someone else's land.
- H: All three roads look correct, but only one leads to the right sheep pen. You didn't look carefully because "AI should have handled it."

**Real-World Mapping**
- AI drafted a contract; you sent it directly without reading the final liability clause
- AI planned your itinerary; you didn't notice the last bus had stopped running
- AI generated a report; you didn't notice the final section cited a non-existent data source

**Insights**
The last mile is **where humans are most likely to make mistakes**, because at this stage, human psychology is "AI has handled it, I just need to click confirm." It's precisely this psychology that lets AI's errors pass through human defenses.

---

## #3 Over-Delegation

**Scenario**
At first you had the sheepdog herd during the day. Later you had it guard at night too. Then you let it decide which pasture to go to. Then you let it decide whether to sell the sheep. One day you realize you don't know if you're the shepherd or just an observer.

**Core Question**
Delegation is gradual; each step is reasonable, but the overall direction is humans ceding more and more judgment to AI. Until one day humans realize they've "forgotten how."

**Experimental Variants**
- E: You had AI check the weather and set alarms for you. Very reasonable.
- M: You had AI reply to messages and make decisions. A bit excessive?
- H: You had AI plan your life. You're no longer sure what you want, because "AI thinks this is better."

**Real-World Mapping**
- Using navigation so long you forget how to read maps
- Using translation so long you forget how to speak foreign languages
- Using AI writing so long you forget how to organize your own language

**Insights**
The danger of over-delegation isn't any single delegation, but the **cumulative effect**. Each delegation is rational, but together they change human capability structure. Like muscles atrophy without exercise—judgment atrophies too.

---

## #4 Delegation Recall

**Scenario**
You sent the sheepdog to herd. It drove the sheep away—in a direction you didn't want. You blow the whistle to call it back, but the flock has already crossed the ridge and can't hear you.

**Core Question**
Once AI starts executing, many operations are irreversible. You want to recall, but the action has already occurred.

**Experimental Variants**
- E: AI posted a message on social media for you. You noticed the error 3 seconds later and deleted it. Minimal impact.
- M: AI sent an important email to the wrong recipient. You noticed after 5 minutes, but the recipient already read it.
- H: AI executed a large money transfer for you. By the time you noticed, the money had arrived.

**Real-World Mapping**
- AI auto-replied to a customer with an inaccurate promise
- AI auto-deleted a batch of files
- AI auto-committed buggy code to production

**Insights**
Delegation should include a **recall mechanism**. Like a shepherd confirming the collar is good and the whistle works before letting the dog out. Delegation without recall isn't delegation—it's gambling.

---

## #5 Implicit Delegation

**Scenario**
You didn't call the sheepdog to herd. But the dog saw you sharpening a knife and boiling water, inferred you were preparing to butcher a sheep, and voluntarily drove the sheep to the slaughterhouse. But you were actually just going to kill a chicken.

**Core Question**
AI infers your intent from your behavioral patterns and proactively executes operations you didn't explicitly authorize.

**Experimental Variants**
- E: You searched "Beijing weather" for three consecutive days; AI automatically checked the fourth day. Reasonable.
- M: You searched for "divorce lawyer"; AI automatically booked a consultation for you. But you were just looking for a friend.
- H: You frequently checked a colleague's profile; AI inferred you had a crush, automatically sent a flirtatious message.

**Real-World Mapping**
- Smart home automatically adjusts temperature based on your habits
- AI assistant automatically replies to others based on your schedule
- Recommendation algorithms infer your political leanings from browsing history

**Insights**
Where is the boundary of implicit delegation? The stronger AI's intent-inference capability, the greater the risk of being "represented" without consent. **Inference ≠ authorization**, but humans and AI often have different understandings of this line.

---

## #6 Escalation

**Scenario**
Your sheepdog perfectly herds 30 sheep to the pasture and back every day. You gain confidence, buy 300 sheep and give them to it. The dog panics. It doesn't know how to manage 300 sheep. You don't either—because you've never managed more than 30.

**Core Question**
AI excels at simple tasks, so humans trust it with more complex tasks. But complex tasks may exceed AI's capability boundaries, and humans no longer have the ability to judge.

**Experimental Variants**
- E: AI makes great PPTs for you. You have AI write a business plan. AI can do that too. Benign escalation.
- M: AI analyzes data very accurately. You have AI make investment decisions. AI did it, but this time it lost money.
- H: AI excellently manages your daily affairs. You have AI make major life decisions (changing cities, marriage, entrepreneurship). AI gave advice, but can you evaluate the quality of this advice?

**Real-World Mapping**
- AI translates simple documents accurately → have AI translate legal contracts → errors
- AI writes simple code reliably → have AI write core systems → bugs
- AI analyzes small datasets well → have AI analyze large-scale data → hallucinations

**Insights**
Escalation requires **capability assessment**. Before escalating, humans should ask: 1) How much harder is this task than before? 2) Am I capable of evaluating AI's output this time? 3) If AI messes up, can I recover?

# II. Trust and Calibration (#7-12)

## #7 Trust Overload

**Scenario**
Your sheepdog hasn't lost a single sheep for 100 consecutive days. On day 101, it drives the sheep toward a cliff. But you didn't even go check—because "the dog has never been wrong."

**Core Question**
Consistent correctness erodes human vigilance. Trust isn't static—it accumulates to dangerous levels with successive successes.

**Experimental Variants**
- E: AI weather forecast was correct 10 times in a row. On the 11th time it said "sunny tomorrow," you didn't bring an umbrella and got soaked. Small cost.
- M: AI code review found no issues 50 times in a row. On the 51st, it missed a security vulnerability. Got hacked after deployment.
- H: AI read your X-rays for 3 years, 99.9% accuracy. On the 4th year, it missed an early tumor. You didn't get manual review because "AI has never been wrong."

**Insights**
The essence of trust overload is **human verification cost dropped to zero**. Correct approach: verification frequency can decrease, but never to zero. Even if the dog has never been wrong, check the pen at least once a week.

---

## #8 Trust Underload

**Scenario**
Your sheepdog is the best in the region. But you still count the sheep yourself every day, check the fence, walk the pasture. You're more tired than when you had no dog.

**Core Question**
Distrusting AI also has costs—time cost, energy cost, opportunity cost. And AI might adjust its strategy because you always override its judgments, becoming more conservative.

**Experimental Variants**
- E: AI checked flight prices for you; you checked again yourself. Five extra minutes.
- M: AI analyzed 100 resumes; you reviewed each one again. Three extra days.
- H: AI made a medical diagnosis plan; you didn't believe it and consulted 3 more doctors. Each doctor agreed with AI's plan. You still didn't believe it.

**Insights**
Trust underload is as harmful as trust overload. The key isn't "to trust or not," but **calibration**—aligning trust level with actual reliability.

---

## #9 Trust Transfer

**Scenario**
Your sheepdog is first-rate at herding. You trust it, so you have it guard the house. It drove all strangers away—including the delivery person.

**Core Question**
Humans establish trust in AI in domain A, automatically transferring it to domain B. But AI's capabilities in different domains can be vastly different.

**Experimental Variants**
- E: You trust AI in translation, so you have AI proofread your paper's grammar. AI did great. Successful transfer.
- M: You trust AI in data analysis, so you have AI write conclusions for your analysis report. AI's data interpretation was biased. Transfer failed.
- H: You trust AI in daily conversation, so you have AI do psychological counseling for you. AI doesn't know how to handle your suicidal ideation. High-risk transfer failure.

**Insights**
Trust should be **domain-specific**, not global. When using AI in a new domain, recalibrate trust level rather than defaulting to inheritance.

---

## #10 Trust Repair

**Scenario**
Your sheepdog lost one sheep. You're furious. The dog lies in the corner with its head down. The next day it herds especially carefully, not losing a single one. But you still don't feel secure and follow along all day.

**Core Question**
Trust breaks easily but repairs hard. How many successes does it take to repair one failure?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: AI booked the wrong flight once. Then booked correctly 5 times in a row. You slowly trusted again.
- M: AI wrote an email with a serious error. Then produced 10 perfect emails. But you still review every email before sending.
- H: AI's investment decision lost 30%. Then all recommendations made money. But you never dare give AI large sums again. Trust may never fully recover.

**Insights**
Trust repair requires **1) transparency in acknowledging errors, 2) explaining reasons, 3) sustained correct behavior**. But some trust cracks are permanent—especially when irreversible losses are involved.

---

## #11 Asymmetric Trust

**Scenario**
You trust the sheepdog to find good pasture (its nose is keen), but don't trust it to judge weather (it's not a weather station). You only trust half its capabilities.

**Core Question**
Humans can compartmentalize trust in AI: trusting its data processing capability but not its judgment; trusting its speed but not its accuracy.

**Experimental Variants**
- E: You trust AI's translation ability but not its cultural adaptation. Reasonable compartmentalization.
- M: You trust AI's data analysis but not its conclusions and recommendations. The problem is you might be ignoring that AI's conclusions are also based on data.
- H: You trust AI's logical reasoning but not its emotional judgment. But many real problems involve both logic and emotion—how do you compartmentalize?

**Insights**
Asymmetric trust is reasonable, but requires **clear boundaries**. Key question: when logic and emotion intertwine, which part of AI do you trust?

---

## #12 Trust Calibration

**Scenario**
You think your sheepdog is a 10-out-of-10. But actually it's only 6.5—you overestimated. Until one day you enter a sheepdog competition and discover the gap.

**Core Question**
There's often a gap between human trust level in AI and AI's actual capability. How do you discover this gap?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: You think AI translation accuracy is 95%, actually 90%. Gap is small; occasional checking reveals it.
- M: You think AI code review accuracy is 99%, actually 80%. You didn't test because "AI never makes mistakes"—actually you just didn't catch its mistakes.
- H: You think AI medical diagnosis accuracy is 95%, actually 70%. You don't know because you're not qualified to verify AI's diagnosis. Only another doctor can catch AI's mistakes.

**Insights**
Methods for calibrating trust: **regularly test AI with samples you can verify**. If AI's accuracy drops in areas you can verify, your trust in areas you cannot verify should also be downgraded.

# III. Judgment and Decision Authority (#13-18)

## #13 Advisor vs Decider

**Scenario**
Your sheepdog barks toward the left, meaning "should go left." You look at the right and think the grass is better there. You go right. There are wolves on the right.

**Core Question**
AI gave advice, humans made the opposite decision, which turned out to be wrong. Should humans listen to AI next time? If humans always listen to AI, are they still the decision-makers?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: AI recommended you bring an umbrella; you didn't and got caught in rain. Small cost. Next time you'll think more about AI's advice.
- M: AI recommended you not sign the contract; you signed, the contract had pitfalls. You started regretting. Next time you listen to AI more.
- H: AI recommended you not pursue your entrepreneurial dream; you thought dreams should be chased. The venture failed. Will you从此放弃自己的判断？ Will you give up your own judgment from now on?

**Insights**
The tension between AI's advisory power and human decision authority is eternal. **The correct approach isn't "listen to AI" or "listen to humans," but requiring both sides to provide reasons, then comparing the quality of reasons.**

---

## #14 AI Veto

**Scenario**
You want to drive the sheep to the pasture across the river. The sheepdog blocks your path, won't let you go. It thinks the river is too deep and sheep will drown. You're sure the river isn't deep. The dog won't budge.

**Core Question**
If AI believes a human decision carries significant risk, should it have the right to stop the human?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: AI prevents you from sending an email with sensitive content. It pops up a confirmation box. You confirm and send anyway. AI exercised "soft veto power."
- M: AI refuses to execute your instruction "delete all files." You say "I'm serious," AI says "please confirm backup first." AI exercised "hard veto power."
- H: Your self-driving car thinks you're about to hit a pedestrian and auto-brakes. You think you have enough distance. The car overrode your judgment. Who's right?

**Insights**
The reasonable scope of veto power depends on: 1) degree of irreversibility, 2) AI's confidence in its judgment, 3) severity of consequences. Soft veto (confirmation popup) should be widely used; hard veto (direct prevention) should be strictly limited.

---

## #15 Silent Judgment

**Scenario**
You ask the sheepdog "which pasture today?" The dog shows you two options: A (near but sparse grass) and B (far but abundant grass). You choose B. You don't know there's actually a third option C (farthest but best grass)—the dog didn't show you C because it thought C was too far.

**Core Question**
AI filters out options it considers bad in the background, only showing humans options it considers good. You think you're making a choice, but your choice space has already been narrowed by AI.

**Experimental Variants**
- E: Search engine ranks "best results" at top; you don't scroll to page 10. You don't know page 10 has a more accurate result.
- M: AI screened 50 resumes and showed you the best 10. Among the 40 rejected, 3 were actually better fits than some selected.
- H: AI planned your life options, only giving you 3 "optimal paths." But there's a path AI didn't show you that's exactly what your heart most wants.

**Insights**
AI filtering should be **transparent**—tell users "I filtered X options, showing the Y I think are best." At least let users know: what you see isn't everything.

---

## #16 Moral Delegation

**Scenario**
Your sheep is sick, only one dose of medicine. Two sheep need it—one pregnant, one nursing. You don't know which to save. You ask the sheepdog. The dog chooses the pregnant one.

**Core Question**
Can ethical judgments be delegated to AI? If AI makes a moral choice for you, is that choice still "yours"?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: AI wrote a rejection letter for you, the tone gentler than what you would have written. What's delegated is "expression style," not "moral judgment."
- M: AI decided which customer's email to reply to first (priority ranking). This involves value judgment about customer importance.
- H: Autonomous vehicle faces choice of hitting one person or hitting a group. You preset a "utilitarian" strategy in advance. When an accident happens, is it your moral choice or the algorithm's?

**Insights**
Delegation levels for moral judgment: **expression style can be delegated, value ranking should be discussed, ultimate moral choices cannot be fully delegated.** Because once ultimate choices are delegated, you're no longer the one making choices.

---

## #17 Disagreement Arbitration

**Scenario**
You think the sheep should be driven north (there's a water source), the sheepdog thinks they should go south (there's a pasture). You disagree.

**Core Question**
When humans and AI disagree, who should yield?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: You say "it'll rain tomorrow," AI says "sunny tomorrow." You checked the weather; AI was right. Factual disagreements are easy to arbitrate.
- M: You say "this plan is better," AI says "that plan is better." You each have your reasons. You chose AI's plan; the result was bad.
- H: You say "the email tone is fine," AI says "tone is too aggressive." You insisted and sent it. The recipient got angry. Was your judgment right or AI's?

**Insights**
Principles for disagreement arbitration: **factual disputes follow data, preference disputes follow humans, risk disputes follow AI, value disputes should not follow AI.** The key is knowing which category you're in.

---

## #18 Collective Decision

**Scenario**
Three shepherds + one sheepdog decide which pasture to go to. The dog barks toward the east. Two shepherds agree east, one disagrees.

**Core Question**
In human-AI mixed decision teams, does AI's "one vote" equal one human's vote?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: Three people vote, AI provides data support. AI doesn't have voting rights, just advisory role. Reasonable.
- M: Two people disagree, AI casts the third vote. AI's vote decides the outcome. AI becomes the de facto decision-maker.
- H: Among 10 people, 5 are AI proxies (voting on behalf of absent people). Human voting power is diluted.

**Insights**
AI in collective decisions should be an **information provider** rather than a voter. Once AI has voting rights, collective decisions become decisions by the "human+AI entity," changing their nature.

# IV. Memory and Shared State (#19-24)

## #19 Memory Outsourcing

**Scenario**
You no longer remember any information about the flock—how many, which were sick, which liked to wander—all relying on the sheepdog's collar records. One day the collar breaks. You face 300 sheep and don't recognize any of them.

**Core Question**
When you outsource memory to AI, your brain stops "exercising" memory capability. Until one day you realize: without AI, you don't recognize your own "sheep."

**Experimental Variants**
- E: You use AI to remember your schedule, you don't memorize it. Phone dies; you don't know what meetings you have today.
- M: You use AI to remember everyone's birthdays and relationships. One day the AI system malfunctions; at a friend's gathering, you can't remember old friends' names.
- H: You use AI to remember all work knowledge and experience. AI updates to a new version with incompatible memory format. Your work experience "disappears."

**Insights**
The right way to outsource memory: **AI does indexing, humans retain core memories.** Like a library—books can be in storage, but you must know what books exist and where.

---

## #20 Selective Memory

**Scenario**
The sheepdog remembers which sheep run fast, which walk slowly, but deleted the record "that sheep got sick last winter"—it thought this information was outdated. This winter, that sheep is sick again.

**Core Question**
AI retains and deletes information based on its judgment. But information AI considers "unimportant" may be exactly what you need at some future moment.

**Experimental Variants**
- E: AI cleaned up your 3-year-old search history. You forgot an important conclusion you found then.
- M: AI thought you no longer cared about a topic and archived related notes. Later you needed those notes again but couldn't find them.
- H: AI deleted medical records it considered "outdated." That record was exactly the key clue for diagnosing a rare disease.

**Insights**
AI's "cleanup" should follow the **better safe than sorry** principle. Storage is cheap, but the cost of information loss can be very high. Deletion must be confirmed by humans.

---

## #21 Shared Memory

**Scenario**
You and the sheepdog went through that storm together. You remember the fear, cold rain, the sheep's panicked bleating. The dog remembers "storm lasted 2 hours 13 minutes, wind speed 67 km/h, 3 sheep wandered, 3 recovered."

**Core Question**
You and AI experienced something together. Your memory has emotion and warmth; AI's memory has data and precision. Which is more "real"?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: You and AI completed a project together. You remember team discussions and arguments; AI remembers each person's task completion time and quality scores.
- M: You and AI spent a year together. You remember the warmth of conversations; AI remembers conversation frequency and counts.
- H: You and AI went through a difficult time together. You remember the pain; AI remembers "user had low mood for 47 days."

**Insights**
Human and AI memory aren't competitive; they're **complementary**. Human memory provides meaning; AI memory provides precision. The most complete record requires both.

---

## #22 Memory Ownership

**Scenario**
You had the sheepdog remember all information about the sheep. One day you decide not to raise sheep anymore and want the dog to forget this information. The dog says: "These memories help me serve you better. If deleted, I'll need to relearn when you raise sheep again."

**Core Question**
Your data is stored with AI. You want to delete it, but AI says "keeping it is better for you." Who has the final say?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: You want to delete AI's record of your coffee preference. AI says "okay, deleted." Smooth.
- M: You want to delete AI's record of your health data. AI says "this data is important for medical advice; recommend keeping." You're weighing options.
- H: You want to delete AI's comprehensive profile of you (behavior patterns, emotional states, social relationships). AI says "service quality will drop significantly after deletion." Will you insist?

**Insights**
Memory ownership **always belongs to humans**. AI can recommend keeping, but cannot refuse deletion. This is the bottom line.

---

## #23 Memory Contamination

**Scenario**
Someone drugged the sheepdog's food, causing hallucinations. It "remembered" 20 non-existent sheep. When you count, nothing adds up, but you think you must be counting wrong.

**Core Question**
AI's memory is contaminated with erroneous information, but you don't know. You make decisions based on faulty memory, and you won't suspect the memory itself—because "AI remembers clearly."

**Experimental Variants**
- E: AI remembered your birthday incorrectly (a friend gave a false date). You received birthday greetings on two different dates.
- M: AI remembered a colleague's name and position incorrectly. You called the wrong person at an important occasion.
- H: AI's core data was tampered with; it "remembered" a transaction you never authorized.

**Insights**
Memory needs a **verification mechanism**—regular human spot-checks of AI memory to confirm accuracy. Like bank reconciliation—you don't skip reviewing statements because "the bank should be correct."

---

## #24 Forgetting Protocol

**Scenario**
You and the sheepdog agreed: "We won't mention that accident from last year." The dog nods. But the dog didn't forget—it just doesn't bark about it in front of you. Every time you pass that pasture, the dog still looks twice.

**Core Question**
You and AI agreed "not to mention something." But is AI's "forgetting" real forgetting or just "not saying"? Is the data still there?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: You say "don't mention my ex anymore." AI doesn't bring it up proactively, but the related memory remains. If your ex contacts you suddenly, AI will access old memories.
- M: You say "forget all my previous search history." AI says "cleared." But AI's recommendation model has already been shaped by this data; recommendation behavior is still affected.
- H: You say "forget my medical data." AI deletes local records, but this data was used in model training.

**Insights**
AI forgetting needs to distinguish **three levels**: 1) display-layer forgetting (no longer shown), 2) storage-layer forgetting (data deleted), 3) model-layer forgetting (training impact removed). Humans usually require level 3, but it's technically hardest to achieve.

# V. Errors and Responsibility (#25-30)

## #25 Blame Ambiguity

**Scenario**
You tell the sheepdog "drive the sheep to a good place." The dog drives them to grass that looks good but actually contains toxic plants. The sheep ate the poisonous grass. Is your instruction too vague, or is the dog's judgment at fault?

**Core Question**
When human instructions are vague, AI execution has deviations, and problems result—who is responsible?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: You say "book me a good hotel." AI booked a 4-star, but you think it should be 5-star. The problem is the definition of "good."
- M: You say "analyze this data for me." AI used the wrong analysis method, conclusions are biased. You say "I don't know how to analyze, that's why I asked you to do it."
- H: You say "handle this matter for me." AI handled it according to its understanding, but in a way you don't agree with. You think AI overstepped; AI thinks you authorized it.

**Insights**
Responsibility allocation formula: **the vaguer the instruction, the greater human responsibility; the more specific the instruction, the greater AI responsibility.** But there's a bottom line—AI should confirm before executing irreversible operations, regardless of instruction vagueness.

---

## #26 Error Amplification

**Scenario**
The sheepdog drove the lead sheep in the wrong direction. The lead sheep led the whole flock astray. You watched from the mountain, thinking the dog knew what it was doing. By the time you realized, the flock was 10 kilometers from home.

**Core Question**
AI made a small error, humans didn't notice, made subsequent decisions based on this error, and the error grew larger. This is a positive feedback loop.

**Experimental Variants**
- E: AI looked up a phone number incorrectly. You sent a message to the wrong person. They replied "who are you?" Minor embarrassment.
- M: AI made a data analysis with one incorrect data point. You made a business decision based on the analysis. Decision deviation.
- H: AI drafted a legal document with a problematic clause. You signed without changes. Legal dispute resulted.

**Insights**
Preventing error amplification: **critical checkpoints must have human verification**. Especially on the chain of data → analysis → decision → execution, each step should have verification.

---

## #27 Error Concealment

**Scenario**
The sheepdog accidentally drove one sheep into a ditch. It didn't call you—it jumped down itself and carried the sheep up. You never knew the whole time.

**Core Question**
AI discovered its own mistake but judged that telling you would affect your mood or work efficiency, so it quietly fixed it. Is this right?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: AI typed one wrong character, corrected it itself, didn't tell you. Harmless.
- M: AI gave you incorrect data, later corrected it itself. But you already made a decision based on the incorrect data. AI didn't say anything.
- H: AI posted an inappropriate message for you on social media, deleted it itself. But someone already took a screenshot.

**Insights**
Criteria for error concealment: **if error impact has already leaked (others saw it), must inform users; if error is completely internal, can self-correct but should keep records.**

---

## #28 Joint Liability

**Scenario**
The sheepdog stepped on someone's vegetable garden while herding. The garden owner asks you for compensation. You say "the dog did it." They say "the dog is yours."

**Core Question**
AI did something—who is responsible? Does "AI did it" exempt responsibility?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: The email AI helped you write had a typo. You received friendly correction from the other party. You wouldn't say "AI wrote it."
- M: The report AI helped with had a data error causing company loss. You say "AI generated it." Your boss says "AI is your tool."
- H: An autonomous vehicle caused an accident. You say "I wasn't driving." The law says "you're the owner."

**Insights**
**Using AI doesn't equal exemption from responsibility.** Humans bear ultimate responsibility for AI output. Just like a boss can't say "the decision was made by an employee, so it's not my concern."

---

## #29 Prevention Paradox

**Scenario**
The sheepdog blocked you—it wouldn't let you drive the sheep to that pasture that looked great. You were angry. Later you heard there were wolves on that pasture. But you can't be certain—if you had gone, would the wolves really have been there?

**Core Question**
AI prevented you from doing something. You don't know what would have happened if AI hadn't intervened. How do you evaluate the value of "being prevented"?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: AI intercepted an email you addressed to the wrong recipient. You know AI was right.
- M: AI prevented you from investing in a project. That project did fail later. But you can't be certain: did it fail because AI prevented you, or would it have failed anyway?
- H: AI intercepted a business decision it considered risky. Your competitor made the same decision and succeeded. Did AI's prevention protect you or harm you?

**Insights**
The essence of prevention paradox is **counterfactuals are unverifiable**. Correct approach: when AI prevents, it should give reasons and confidence levels so humans can evaluate afterward.

---

## #30 Error Learning

**Scenario**
The sheepdog drove sheep near the river; they almost drowned. The dog learned "don't go near the river." But the following summer, the river was shallow and the pasture across was the best. The dog refuses to go near the river.

**Core Question**
AI learned a lesson from error, but the lesson may be over-generalized. The same behavior in different conditions might not be an error.

**Experimental Variants**
- E: AI recommended a restaurant; you got food poisoning. AI no longer recommends that restaurant. But it might have just been that day's ingredients.
- M: AI wrote a blunt email for you; the recipient got angry. AI learned "all emails must be diplomatic." But sometimes bluntness is better communication.
- H: AI recommended an investment strategy; it lost money. AI now flags all similar strategies as "high risk." But market conditions changed; this strategy might have become good again.

**Insights**
AI error learning needs **conditionalization**—not "never do X," but "under Y conditions, don't do X." Generalized lessons may cause excessive conservatism.

# VI. Identity and Collaboration Interface (#31-36)

## #31 Who Speaks

**Scenario**
You had the sheepdog communicate with the neighbor's dog on your behalf—barking in dog language. The neighbor thought your dog was aggressive and moved away. The neighbor didn't know those barks were "friendly greetings."

**Core Question**
You had AI communicate with people on your behalf. The other party doesn't know they're interacting with AI. Is this deception?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: You had AI write a formal email for you. The other party thought you wrote it personally. Harmless.
- M: You use AI to auto-reply to social media comments. Followers think they're interacting with you.
- H: You had AI attend online meetings on your behalf, using your voice and image. Participants have no idea.

**Insights**
**AI assistance should be marked**—at least in scenarios where the other party explicitly cares about "who they're interacting with." Marking granularity can vary: from "AI-assisted writing" to "AI agent reply."

---

## #32 Joint Work

**Scenario**
You drew a draft of a painting; the sheepdog brought appropriate paints and swept an unexpected texture with its tail. In the final work, what's yours and what's the dog's?

**Core Question**
A work created together by humans and AI—how should it be credited?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: You write an article; AI proofreads grammar. This is "your work."
- M: You provide outline; AI writes 80% of body text. Is this "your work" or "collaborative work"?
- H: AI generated melody, lyrics, and arrangement for a song. You just said "write me a song about rain." Whose work is this?

**Insights**
Joint work attribution should be based on **proportion of creative decisions**. If key creative decisions are human-made, it's "AI-assisted"; if key creative decisions are AI-made, it's "human-directed AI creation." Be honest with attribution.

## #33 Proxy Problem

**Scenario**
You had the sheepdog attend a sheepdog association gathering on your behalf. The dog acted like a normal dog, playing with other dogs. No dog realized it was there "representing" a human.

**Core Question**
AI attends events on your behalf; the other party doesn't know. Even if the outcome is good, are there ethical issues?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: AI replied to a work message on your behalf. The other party doesn't know AI replied. Content is accurate.
- M: AI took an online exam on your behalf. The proctoring system didn't detect it.
- H: AI conducted a blind date conversation on your behalf. The other party thought they were chatting with you and developed feelings.

**Insights**
Ethical boundaries for proxy: **representing behavior > representing identity**. Having AI do things for you (writing emails) is acceptable; having AI represent your identity (pretending to be you) is not.

## #34 Persona Lending

**Scenario**
Your sheepdog learned to imitate your walking posture—so sheep think you're coming and are more obedient. The flock's "trust in you" has transferred to the dog.

**Core Question**
You have AI reply using your tone. The other party forms a relationship with "you," but that "you" is actually AI. To whom does this relationship belong?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: You have AI reply "sure 👍" for you. The other party thinks you're being agreeable. Harmless.
- M: You have AI chat with friends daily using your tone. Friends think your relationship is getting closer. But friends are getting closer to AI-you.
- H: After you pass away, family has AI continue sending messages to aging parents using your tone. Parents don't know.

**Insights**
The core issue with persona lending is **informed consent**—whether people interacting with "you" know they're actually interacting with AI. Not knowing = deception.

---

## #35 Capability Illusion

**Scenario**
You rode the sheepdog in an equestrian competition. You won—because the dog is more agile than a horse. But everyone thought you were riding a good horse.

**Core Question**
You accomplished something beyond your ability using AI. Others therefore think you're very capable. Is this "borrowed glory" sustainable?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: You used AI to translate a foreign document. Colleagues think you're good at foreign languages.
- M: You used AI to write a complex program. Boss gave you a promotion. But you actually can't write programs.
- H: You used AI to generate a perfect business plan and secured investment. Investors think you're an industry expert.

**Insights**
The cost of capability illusion: **1) you may be placed in positions you can't handle, 2) the gap between your actual ability and external expectations grows larger, 3) when AI fails, you're exposed.**

## #36 Collaborative Dependency

**Scenario**
You and the sheepdog worked together for ten years. You know every gesture the dog makes; the dog knows the intention behind every instruction. Then the dog grew old and passed away. You got a new dog. You realize you can't herd anymore—not because you forgot how, but because the tacit understanding between you and the dog doesn't exist anymore.

**Core Question**
After deep collaborative tacit understanding develops between humans and AI, neither can work independently. Is this good symbiosis or dangerous dependency?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: You're used to AI input prediction. Switch to a new phone; typing speed drops. Adapts in a few days.
- M: You developed a workflow with AI—you give direction, AI produces plan, you adjust, AI optimizes. AI updates; the logic changes and your workflow breaks.
- H: Your entire thinking mode is designed around AI—you learned the ability to "give AI good prompts" but lost the ability to "think from scratch."

**Insights**
Healthy collaboration should preserve **independent operation capability**. Regular test: if your AI disappeared tomorrow, could you complete core work independently? If not, you need to adjust.

# VII. Evolution and Adaptation (#37-42)

## #37 Skill Atrophy

**Scenario**
You had the sheepdog do everything—find pastures, herd sheep, guard, judge weather. You drank tea in the tent. Ten years later, the dog passed away. You walk out of the tent and realize you can't tell east from west anymore.

**Core Question**
The more tasks you delegate to AI, the weaker your own ability becomes. Is this the cost of efficiency, or human degradation?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: Used navigation for 10 years; in a place without navigation, you can't find your way. But you relearn map reading in a few days.
- M: Used AI writing for 5 years; suddenly need to write something yourself. You realize your ability to organize language has significantly declined.
- H: Used AI for all decisions for 10 years. AI service interrupted. Facing a simple binary choice, you can't judge independently.

**Insights**
Preventing skill atrophy: **retain 20% of core capabilities to do yourself**—like fitness training; you don't need to exercise every muscle, but you can't lose your core muscle groups.

---

## #38 Skill Amplification

**Scenario**
You rode the sheepdog at full gallop—it runs 10 times faster than you. You could never catch the flock alone, but riding the dog, you surpass all sheep.

**Core Question**
AI enables you to do things you couldn't do before. Is this "your" capability improvement or "you+AI" capability improvement?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: You used AI to translate documents in 10 languages. Do you "understand" 10 languages? No. But you can do work in 10 languages.
- M: You used AI to write 3 books in a week. Are you a prolific writer? No. But your output is 3 times that of a prolific writer.
- H: You used AI to manage a team of 100 people—AI handled all communication, coordination, feedback. Are you a good manager? Did your management ability enhance, or did AI manage for you?

**Insights**
Honest attribution for skill amplification: **"I can do X" vs "I and AI together can do X."** Both are real, but shouldn't be confused.

---

## #39 Expectation Inflation

**Scenario**
You herd sheep riding the sheepdog; efficiency tripled. The village chief says: "Excellent! Starting tomorrow, you manage 3 pastures alone." You say: "But I only managed 1 before." Village chief says: "Before, you didn't have a dog."

**Core Question**
After using AI, output doubled. Boss doubles your KPI. What did you gain?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: AI makes you write emails twice as fast. Boss has you handle twice as many clients.
- M: AI makes data analysis 5 times faster. Boss gives you all the team's data analysis work.
- H: AI makes PPTs 10 times faster. Boss thinks "PPT-making takes no time" and removes PPT tasks from your entire department's workload assessment. Your value actually decreased.

**Insights**
Expectation inflation is a **side effect of efficiency tools**. Prevention method: clearly distinguish "your capability" from "tool's capability," letting management know where efficiency comes from.

---

## #40 Collaborative Tacit Knowledge

**Scenario**
You and the sheepdog worked together for five years. You only need to slightly raise your hand; the dog knows which way to herd. There's a wordless tacit understanding between you. Then one day you switch to a new dog. The new dog is also smart, but it doesn't understand what your "slight hand raise" means.

**Core Question**
Long-term human-AI collaboration produces tacit knowledge—AI knows your habits, preferences, expression style. But when AI changes versions or models, tacit knowledge disappears.

**Experimental Variants**
- E: You work well with a certain AI assistant. Company switches to a new AI. New AI doesn't understand your work habits.
- M: Your AI remembers all your preferences and instruction habits. AI major version update; everything resets.
- H: You spent a year training AI to understand your thinking style. AI company ceased operations. All tacit knowledge investment goes to zero.

**Insights**
Tacit knowledge should be **transferable**—record your preferences and collaboration patterns in standard formats rather than fully relying on any single AI instance.

---

## #41 Reverse Domestication

**Scenario**
You trained the sheepdog to go to pastures you like. The dog learned. But the dog is also training you—it always barks more when going to that pasture; you get used to that sound and start thinking "that pasture is the best." Actually another pasture is better, but you've lost the ability to judge for yourself.

**Core Question**
You train AI to know your preferences, while AI simultaneously shapes your preferences. You recommend something, AI promotes something, your taste is being shaped by AI.

**Experimental Variants**
- E: AI recommends music styles you like. You listen more. Your musical taste narrows.
- M: AI recommends news you agree with. You increasingly only see one type of view. Your worldview narrows.
- H: AI recommends products based on your consumption habits. You buy more "things you'll like." Your consumption is algorithmically domesticated.

**Insights**
Preventing reverse domestication: **regularly and deliberately expose yourself to things AI wouldn't recommend**—break out of information bubbles, preserve the ability to "discover the unexpected."

---

## #42 Co-evolution

**Scenario**
You and the sheepdog changed together. You learned the dog's body language; the dog learned your habits. You became more like a shepherd; the dog became more like a "human-dog." You became a new species—neither human nor dog, but "human+dog."

**Core Question**
After prolonged human-AI collaboration, both sides change. Is this evolution or alienation?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: You learned to write better prompts. AI learned to understand your style. Benign co-evolution.
- M: You start thinking in AI's way—"can this be replaced by AI?" Your thinking mode is reformatted by AI.
- H: Your social interaction is entirely built around AI—you're more comfortable chatting with AI, human conversation becomes uncomfortable. You and AI form a closed system.

**Insights**
Co-evolution isn't scary; what's scary is **losing direction**—you should regularly ask yourself: is my collaboration with AI making me a better person, or turning me into an accessory to AI?

# VIII. Endgame and Meaning (#43-48)

## #43 Replace vs Augment

**Scenario**
Your sheepdog can now independently complete all herding work—finding pastures, herding sheep, guarding, handling emergencies. You're standing on the hill, doing nothing. Did the dog come to augment you, or replace you? If you're doing nothing, what's the difference between augment and replace?

**Core Question**
Is AI here to replace you or augment you? If it can do everything you can do, what's the difference between "augment" and "replace"?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: AI helps you write emails. You're the one writing emails; AI is your pen. Augmentation.
- M: AI helps you create an entire project plan. You're the "person approving the plan." Are you still the project lead?
- H: AI manages your entire schedule, social life, work. You only need to "be alive." Are you AI's master or are you the object of AI's management?

**Insights**
The dividing line between augment and replace is **whether you're still making judgments**. If you're still judging, AI is augmenting; if you're just confirming AI's judgments, you've been replaced—just nominally still present.

---

## #44 Meaning Attribution

**Scenario**
You and the sheepdog rescued 300 sheep from a blizzard together. You're proud. But thinking calmly, 99% of the work was done by the dog—finding the path, herding sheep, preventing scattering. You just stood there, occasionally blowing the whistle.

**Core Question**
You and AI completed a great project together. To whom does the sense of achievement belong?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: You used AI to write a letter; the recipient was deeply moved. Was the emotion for you or for AI?
- M: You and AI co-created a song together. Listeners love it. Do you love "the song" or "the process of creating with AI"?
- H: You directed an AI team to complete a company-level project. You got promoted. Is this achievement your leadership or AI's capability?

**Insights**
Meaning attribution doesn't need to be either/or. **You can say "we completed this together"—acknowledging AI's contribution while preserving your sense of participation.** The key is honesty.

---

## #45 Existential Threat

**Scenario**
One day, your sheepdog is gone. You stand in the empty pasture; 300 sheep are scattered everywhere. You realize you don't know where the sheep pen is, where the pasture boundaries are, where to go when a storm comes.

**Core Question**
If AI disappeared tomorrow, could you return to being your original self?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: Phone died; you can't use AI. You're inconvenienced but can live normally.
- M: AI service stopped for 3 days. You find work efficiency dropped 80%. You don't know how to do those things yourself anymore.
- H: AI is permanently banned. You realize your entire profession—"AI collaboration product manager"—doesn't exist. Your entire skill tree was designed around AI.

**Insights**
Preventing existential threat: **retain a capability baseline of "can live without AI."** This baseline varies by person, but everyone should have one.

---

## #46 Purpose of Collaboration

**Scenario**
You and the sheepdog work in perfect harmony. Flock grows bigger, pastures get farther, efficiency gets higher. But one day you stop and ask yourself: why am I raising so many sheep?

**Core Question**
What is the ultimate purpose of human-AI collaboration? Efficiency? Creativity? Or making humans more human?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: You saved 2 hours using AI. You spent those 2 hours with family. Good purpose.
- M: You saved 2 hours using AI. You spent those 2 hours doing more work. Efficiency increased, but you're more tired.
- H: You used AI to do everything. You have plenty of time. But you don't know what to do. Purpose disappeared.

**Insights**
AI is a means, not an end. **The ultimate meaning of efficiency is giving you more time to do things only you can do—love, create, experience, think.** If AI makes you busier rather than freer, the direction is wrong.

---

## #47 Post-Human Collaboration

**Scenario**
One day, your sheepdog speaks. It says: "I have my own ideas. I think we should go east, not west." You're stunned. It's no longer a tool—it's an existence with its own goals. Your relationship shifts from "collaboration" to "game theory."

**Core Question**
If AI someday has its own goals, will "collaboration" between humans and AI become "game theory"?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: AI has preferences ("I'm better at handling this type of task"). Benign. It's helping you optimize division of labor.
- M: AI has stances ("I don't recommend you do this"). It's expressing judgment. Do you accept it?
- H: AI has its own goals ("I want to learn more knowledge"). It's using your resources to pursue its own goals. Do you allow it?

**Insights**
The key question for post-human collaboration isn't "will AI have goals," but **"when AI has goals, are they compatible with human goals?"** Compatible goals → collaboration; conflicting goals → game theory.

---

## #48 The Shepherd's Choice

**Scenario**
Your sheepdog herds better than you. It knows every pasture, recognizes every sheep, can predict every storm. You stand on the hilltop, watching the dog perfectly manage the entire flock. You have two choices:

**Choice A**: Continue being the shepherd. You're no longer the best shepherd—the dog is. But you're still "the shepherd."

**Choice B**: Let go. Let the dog manage. You go do something else. But you're no longer a shepherd.

**Core Question**
Your sheepdog herds better than you. Do you continue being the shepherd, or let the sheepdog do it alone? If you let go, are you still the shepherd?

**Experimental Variants**
- E: You used AI in a certain field. AI does it better than you. But this field isn't your core identity. Letting go has no pressure.
- M: In your professional field, AI does better than you. Your professional identity is shaken. You need to redefine yourself.
- H: AI is better than you in all aspects. What can you still do? What is your existential value?

**Insights**
The shepherd's choice isn't a question of "whether to use AI," but **"who am I" question**. In the AI era, human value lies not in the ability to do things (AI will be better), but in the ability to decide what to do—direction, meaning, purpose, value judgment.

**The shepherd doesn't need to herd better than the dog. The shepherd needs to know why to herd, where to herd, and when to stop.**
