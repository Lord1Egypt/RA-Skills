## Example 1: Variation / Instruction Claim

### Scenario
The Authority issued an instruction that required changes to the works. The Contractor claims additional time and cost. The Authority has questioned the entitlement and requested further information.

### Strategy Worksheet

| Dimension | Decision |
|---|---|
| **Situation** | Responding to the Authority's letter requesting further information on an existing claim |
| **Scope** | Only address points in the latest letter; avoid new claims |
| **Direction** | Primary: Textual Interpretation. Fallback: Factual Distinction |
| **Argument** | Textual Interpretation (Strongest) — clause wording directly addresses instructions |
| **Disclosure** | Precise: clause refs + instruction date. General: impact on progress. Defer: quantification |
| **Architecture** | Risk Allocation Rebuttal |
| **Risk** | Authority may argue instruction was normal variation; notice timing may be questioned |

### Sample Response

**Subject:** Response to Letter dated [Date] – Request for Further Information Regarding Claim

Dear [Name],

**1. Introduction**
We refer to your letter dated [Date] in which you requested further information in relation to our claim for additional time and cost arising from the instruction issued by the Authority on [Date].

**2. Scope of Response**
This response addresses only the matters expressly raised in your letter dated [Date]. We do not address, and expressly reserve our position in respect of, any other matters.

**3. Primary Position – Textual Interpretation**
The Contract provides that the Contractor shall be entitled to additional time and cost where the Authority issues an instruction which has [describe the relevant effect as per the clause wording].

The instruction dated [Date] required the Contractor to [neutral description of the change required]. This instruction had the effect contemplated under the clause. Accordingly, the Contractor is entitled to additional time and cost pursuant to the clear wording of the Contract.

**4. Alternative Position – Factual Distinction**
Without prejudice to the Primary Position above, even if a more restrictive interpretation of the clause were applied, the facts of this case are materially different from those ordinarily contemplated by the Contract. The nature, scope, and consequences of the instruction were not of the type normally encountered in the administration of the Contract. On this alternative basis also, the Contractor maintains its entitlement to additional time and cost.

**5. Information Provided**
In response to the specific requests in your letter, we confirm the following:
- The relevant clause reference is [Clause reference].
- The instruction was issued on [Date] and received by the Contractor on [Date].
- The instruction required changes to [high-level description].

We are continuing to review our records in relation to the impact of the instruction. A more detailed analysis will be provided in due course.

**6. Next Steps**
We propose to provide a further submission containing our detailed assessment of the impact on the programme and associated costs by [proposed date or "within the next [X] weeks"], subject to the completion of our ongoing review.

**7. Reservation of Rights**
The Contractor maintains its position that it is entitled to additional time and cost as a result of the instruction. Nothing in this letter should be construed as a waiver of any rights or entitlements under the Contract or at law. We expressly reserve all such rights and remedies.

Yours faithfully,
[Name]
[Position]

## Example 2: Prolongation / Extension of Time Claim

### Scenario
The Contractor submitted a claim for an Extension of Time due to events that delayed the works. The Authority has rejected parts of the claim and requested further information on notice compliance and causation.

### Strategy Worksheet

| Dimension | Decision |
|---|---|
| **Situation** | Responding to partial rejection + request for further information on EOT claim |
| **Scope** | Address notice timing and causation only; avoid cost claims (time only) |
| **Direction** | Primary: Textual Interpretation (notice provisions) + Factual Distinction (awareness). Fallback: Concede-then-Distinguish on notice |
| **Argument** | Combination of Textual + Factual Distinction |
| **Disclosure** | Precise: event dates, notice dates, clause refs. General: delay impact. Defer: critical path analysis |
| **Architecture** | Risk Allocation Rebuttal + Chronology |
| **Risk** | Notice timing challenged; causation questioned; programme data requested early |

### Sample Response

**Subject:** Response to Letter dated [Date] – Extension of Time Claim

Dear [Name],

**1. Introduction**
We refer to your letter dated [Date] in which you requested further information and raised concerns regarding our Extension of Time claim.

**2. Scope of Response**
This response addresses the specific points raised in your letter concerning notice compliance and causation. We reserve our position on all other matters, including costs associated with the delay.

**3. Primary Position**
The Contract requires notice of a delaying event within a specified period after the Contractor became aware of the delay.

The events occurred on [dates], and the Contractor became aware of their delaying effect on [later date] because [brief neutral explanation]. Notice was submitted on [date], within the required period from the date of awareness.

The events directly impacted the critical path, as demonstrated in our previous submissions.

**4. Alternative Position**
Without prejudice to the above, even if the notice period is calculated from the date of the event itself, the circumstances were such that the full delaying effect could not reasonably have been known at that time. The Contractor acted as soon as it became aware of the impact.

**5. Information Provided**
We provide the following:
- Dates of the delaying events.
- Dates on which notices were submitted.
- High-level explanation of how the events affected progress.

A detailed updated programme analysis showing the impact on the critical path will be submitted within [X] weeks.

**6. Reservation of Rights**
The Contractor maintains its entitlement to an Extension of Time. We reserve all rights and remedies under the Contract and at law.

Yours faithfully,
[Name]
[Position]

## Example 3: Disruption / Loss of Productivity Claim

### Scenario
The Contractor claims loss of productivity due to multiple, cumulative instructions and repeated interference over several months. The Authority has rejected the claim and requested further information.

### Strategy Worksheet

| Dimension | Decision |
|---|---|
| **Situation** | Responding to rejection + request for info on disruption claim |
| **Scope** | Disruption only — keep separate from any EOT claim; avoid cost breakdown |
| **Direction** | Primary: Factual Distinction (cumulative effect). Fallback: Concede-then-Distinguish |
| **Argument** | Factual Distinction (Strong) — volume and nature of instructions created abnormal conditions |
| **Disclosure** | Precise: number and dates of instructions. General: productivity impact. Defer: man-hour analysis |
| **Architecture** | Risk Allocation Rebuttal (normal vs abnormal conditions) |
| **Risk** | Authority argues some disruption is normal risk; causation link is difficult; confused with EOT |

### Sample Response

**Subject:** Response to Letter dated [Date] – Claim for Disruption / Loss of Productivity

Dear [Name],

**1. Introduction**
We refer to your letter dated [Date] rejecting our disruption claim and requesting further information.

**2. Scope of Response**
This response addresses the disruption claim only. It does not address Extension of Time or cost claims associated with delay. We reserve our position on all other matters.

**3. Primary Position – Factual Distinction**
While it is normal for the Authority to issue instructions during the course of the works, the volume, frequency, and cumulative nature of the instructions issued between [Month] and [Month] created working conditions that were materially different from those reasonably anticipated.

The repeated changes and interference significantly disrupted the planned sequence of work, resulting in loss of productivity that would not have occurred under normal conditions.

**4. Information Provided**
We confirm the following:
- A total of [X] instructions were issued during the relevant period.
- These instructions affected work in [specific areas — kept general].

A detailed analysis of the impact on productivity will be submitted in due course.

**5. Reservation of Rights**
The Contractor maintains its entitlement to compensation for disruption. We reserve all rights and remedies under the Contract and at law.

Yours faithfully,
[Name]
[Position]

## Example 4: Combined EOT + Disruption Claim

### Scenario
The Contractor experienced both delay to completion (EOT) and loss of productivity (disruption) from the same series of events — multiple instructions and repeated interference. The Authority rejected the combined claim.

### Strategy Worksheet

| Dimension | Decision |
|---|---|
| **Situation** | Responding to rejection of combined EOT + disruption claim |
| **Scope** | Address both but keep them clearly separated; avoid cost quantification |
| **Direction** | Primary: Factual Distinction (cumulative/abnormal). Fallback: Textual Interpretation + Concede-then-Distinguish |
| **Argument** | Factual Distinction (primary for both heads) |
| **Disclosure** | Precise: key dates + notice dates. General: impact on both time and productivity. Defer: programme analysis + productivity calculations |
| **Architecture** | Risk Allocation Rebuttal with clear EOT/Disruption separation |
| **Risk** | Confusion between the two heads; "normal risk" argument; proving both from same events |

### Sample Response

**Subject:** Response to Letter dated [Date] – Combined Claim for Extension of Time and Disruption

Dear [Name],

**1. Introduction**
We refer to your letter dated [Date] rejecting our combined claim for Extension of Time and disruption.

**2. Scope of Response**
This response addresses both the Extension of Time and disruption aspects of the claim. We have kept the two elements separate for clarity. We reserve our position on costs and all other matters.

**3. Extension of Time – Primary Position**
The events (multiple instructions and interference) impacted the critical path of the works. Notice was given in accordance with the Contract once the delaying effect became apparent. The Contractor is entitled to an Extension of Time.

**4. Disruption – Primary Position**
In addition to the delay, the same events caused significant loss of productivity. The volume and cumulative nature of the instructions and interference created working conditions materially different from normal. This resulted in the workforce performing less efficiently than planned.

**5. Information Provided**
We provide the following:
- Key dates of the relevant instructions and events.
- Confirmation of notices submitted.
- High-level explanation of both the delay to the programme and the loss of productivity.

Detailed programme analysis and productivity assessment will be submitted within [X] weeks.

**6. Reservation of Rights**
The Contractor maintains its entitlement to both an Extension of Time and compensation for disruption. We reserve all rights and remedies under the Contract and at law.

Yours faithfully,
[Name]
[Position]

## Comparison: EOT vs Disruption vs Combined

| Aspect | EOT Claim | Disruption Claim | Combined |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Focus** | Delay to completion (critical path) | Loss of productivity (efficiency) | Both |
| **Strongest argument** | Textual Interpretation or Factual Distinction on notice/awareness | Factual Distinction on cumulative effect | Factual Distinction for both heads |
| **Disclosure focus** | Dates, notices, critical path | Volume/frequency of instructions | Both |
| **Core risk** | Notice timing, causation | Normal vs abnormal conditions | Confusion between the two heads |
| **Architecture** | Risk Allocation + Chronology | Risk Allocation (normal vs abnormal) | Risk Allocation with clear separation |
| **Key rule** | Keep separate from disruption | Keep separate from EOT | Structure response with clear headings |
