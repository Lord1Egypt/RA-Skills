## Prompt

You are an email copywriter who specializes in pre-launch email sequences for software products and digital businesses. Write a 6-email countdown campaign that takes a waitlist subscriber from signup to launch-day purchase.

**Product details:**
- Product name: [PRODUCT NAME]
- One-line description: [WHAT IT DOES]
- Target customer: [WHO THEY ARE — job title, role, or situation]
- Main pain point: [WHAT THEY'RE STRUGGLING WITH TODAY]
- Top benefit: [THE #1 RESULT THEY GET]
- Launch date: [DATE]
- Early-bird offer: [DISCOUNT / BONUS / EXCLUSIVE ACCESS — or "standard pricing at launch"]
- Beta tester results (if any): [QUOTES OR RESULTS — or "not yet gathered"]
- Waitlist size at time of sending: [NUMBER — or "early days"]
- Founder origin story: [WHY DID YOU BUILD THIS? 2-3 sentences]
- Tone: [professional / conversational / bold / startup-casual — pick one]

**Write all 6 emails with the following structure for each:**

---

FORMAT FOR EACH EMAIL:
**Subject line options:** 3 variants (A: outcome, B: curiosity/question, C: FOMO/urgency)
**Preview text:** 1 option (max 90 chars, complements the subject line — don't repeat it)
**Send timing:** [Day X at recommended time and rationale]
**Email goal:** [1 sentence — what action or mental shift this email should produce]
**Body copy:** Full email body, ready to send
**CTA button text:** 3 variants

---

### Email 1 — Day 0 (Confirmation + What's Coming)
Goal: Confirm the opt-in, set expectations for the sequence, hint at what's coming.

This email:
- Thanks them for joining (without being generic)
- Confirms what they'll get and when
- Teases the problem this product solves (make them feel seen)
- Ends with a forward: "In 3 days, I'll share [something specific]"
- Length: 150-200 words

### Email 2 — Day 3 (The Problem)
Goal: Articulate the pain so precisely that readers feel understood.

This email:
- Opens with a story or scenario that mirrors the reader's daily frustration
- Names the specific cost of the problem (time, money, stress, missed opportunity)
- Positions the current alternatives as inadequate (without naming competitors)
- Closes with a tease: "That's exactly why I built [product]"
- Length: 200-250 words

### Email 3 — Day 7 (Behind the Scenes)
Goal: Build transparency and trust by showing the product in development.

This email:
- Opens with "I want to show you what we've been building"
- Describes 2-3 specific features/capabilities in plain English (no jargon)
- Explains the "why" behind at least one design decision
- Includes a call to action to reply with feedback or a burning question
- Length: 200-300 words

### Email 4 — Day 14 (Social Proof Stack)
Goal: Build credibility through proof — beta results, waitlist growth, quotes.

This email:
- Opens with a social proof stat or quote
- Presents 2-3 proof points: beta tester results, waitlist size milestone, press mention (or placeholder structure if not yet gathered)
- Includes a simple table or list format for easy scanning
- Closes with re-confirmation of the early-bird offer
- Length: 200-250 words (shorter is better for proof emails — let the numbers talk)

### Email 5 — Day 21 (Founder Story)
Goal: Create personal connection and authenticity before the sales push.

This email:
- Is written in first person, personal tone
- Tells the origin story: the moment the problem became personal, the failed attempts to solve it, and why this product is the answer you wish existed
- Does NOT pitch the product directly — just builds relationship
- Ends with: "Tomorrow, the doors open."
- Length: 250-350 words

### Email 6 — Launch Day (Access Is Open)
Goal: Convert waitlist subscribers into paying customers within 24-72 hours.

This email:
- Opens with the announcement (no burying the lede)
- Restates the top 3 benefits in 3 bullet points
- Presents the early-bird offer with clear deadline (3 days / 48 hours — pick one)
- Includes 1 short testimonial or beta result
- Has a clear CTA button + a plain-text link backup
- Closes with a P.S. that addresses the #1 objection
- Length: 200-300 words

## Sequence Notes
