> Source: Thinking, Fast and Slow by Daniel Kahneman, Parts 1-2

# Core Framework: System 1, System 2, and WYSIATI

## The Two Systems

Kahneman's central framework divides mental activity into two systems:

| | System 1 | System 2 |
|---|---|---|
| **Speed** | Fast, automatic | Slow, deliberate |
| **Effort** | Effortless | Requires attention and energy |
| **Operation** | Intuitive, associative | Analytical, rule-based |
| **Control** | Cannot be turned off | Can be engaged or disengaged |
| **Responsible for** | Impressions, feelings, habits | Reasoning, calculation, self-control |

> **Case: The ball and bat problem** (Introduction): A bat and ball cost $1.10. The bat costs $1.00 more than the ball. How much is the ball? System 1 instantly says 10 cents. System 2 should catch the error (the correct answer is 5 cents). Most people answer 10 cents because System 2 is lazy and endorses System 1's answer without checking.
> **Key takeaway**: System 2 is not automatically engaged. It must be actively called upon — and even then, it often defaults to System 1's answer because thinking is effortful.

## WYSIATI — What You See Is All There Is

The single most important concept in the book. System 1 constructs a coherent story from only the information available, and it has no awareness of what's missing.

> **Case: The love story** (Part 1, Chapter 7): Kahneman describes a story of a young woman who saves a drowning child, then falls in love with the child's older brother. The story is coherent and emotionally satisfying. But it leaves out the most relevant information: she had always wanted to meet someone. She had been lonely. The brother was available. The story is constructed from what you're told, and it feels complete — even though it's not.
> **Key takeaway**: Your mind will create a compelling narrative from limited information. The coherence of the story is not evidence of its truth.

## Heuristics and Biases

Three major heuristics drive most cognitive errors:

| Heuristic | What It Is | The Bias It Creates |
|---|---|---|
| **Anchoring** | Using an initial number as a reference point | Insufficient adjustment; over-reliance on first impression |
| **Availability** | Judging frequency by ease of recall | Overestimating vivid, recent, or dramatic events |
| **Representativeness** | Judging probability by similarity | Ignoring base rates; seeing patterns in randomness |

> **Case: The oil spill study** (Part 2, Chapter 12): Participants were asked their willingness to pay to save 2,000, 20,000, or 200,000 birds from oil spills. The average responses were $80, $78, and $88. The number of birds made almost no difference — people were reacting to the prototype (a single bird drowning in oil), not to the actual quantity. Availability at work.
> **Key takeaway**: Emotional reactions to vivid examples override statistical reasoning. The image of one bird drowning is more powerful than the logic of 200,000 birds.
