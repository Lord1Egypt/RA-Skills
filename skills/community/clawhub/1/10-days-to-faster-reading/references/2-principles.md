## Key Principles

### 1. Speed Improves Concentration
The biggest myth: "faster = less understanding." Actually, when you read at 100-150 wpm (talking speed), your brain has 250+ wpm of idle capacity — which it fills with daydreaming. Faster reading fills the gap, so your brain stays engaged.

### 2. You Can't Eliminate the Inner Voice — But You Can Turn It Down
Subvocalization is hardwired. The goal is not zero — it's reduction. Best strategy: read faster than you can talk. If you can't talk at that speed, you can't subvocalize at that speed.

### 3. Pacers Are Training Wheels That Work
Your eyes naturally follow movement. Using a hand, pen, or card as a pacer forces rhythmic, forward-only movement. Pros use pacers. They're not a crutch — they're a tool.

### 4. The White Card Goes ABOVE the Line
Counterintuitive but critical: place the card above the line you're reading, not below. This blocks what you've already read, preventing regression, and keeps your eyes moving forward to the unblocked text.

### 5. Previewing Is the Highest-Leverage Skill
Spending 2-3 minutes previewing a book before reading it can save hours and double comprehension. The mental map built during previewing makes the actual reading far more efficient.

### 6. Unlearning to Relearn Is Expected
When you first try a new reading technique, you'll feel slower and more confused. This is the "automatic to stick shift" transition. Push through — it lasts 3-7 days, then the new skill becomes automatic.

### 7. Match Your Rate to Your Purpose
Reading a novel for pleasure? Go moderate. Reading a technical manual? Go slow. Previewing a newspaper? Go fast. One speed does not fit all. Efficient readers know when to shift gears.

> **Case: The corporate class averages** (Day 1): Marks Beale tracked three years of 12-hour speed reading classes with corporate employees at all levels. Average starting WPM across all participants: 252. Most had not had any reading training since elementary school. By the end of the program, most doubled their speed. **Key takeaway:** Untrained readers cluster around the same average regardless of profession. Training is the differentiator, not intelligence or education.

> **Case: The slow reader who became a speed reader** (Foreword): Marks Beale shares that she herself was once a slow reader who procrastinated on school assignments and "rarely picked up a book for pleasure." After learning speed-reading techniques, she became the professional reading trainer she is today. **Key takeaway:** The person teaching speed reading was once a slow reader. If she can change, so can you.
