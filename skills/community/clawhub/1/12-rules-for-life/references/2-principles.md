> Source: 12 Rules for Life by Jordan B. Peterson, Rules 4, 6-8, 10

# Key Principles

## Principle 1: The only meaningful competition is with your former self

Comparing yourself to others is a guaranteed path to resentment or arrogance. The only comparison that matters is: are you better than you were yesterday?

> **Case: The Olympic athlete** (Rule 4, Chapter 4): "If you compare yourself to the world's best, you can easily be discouraged. The only reasonable goal is to improve slightly from where you were. That is the path to excellence."

## Principle 2: Set your house in perfect order before you criticize the world

Before you complain about society, politics, or other people — fix what is within your control. You will find that most of the chaos you complain about originates in your own life.

> **Case: The client who blamed everyone** (Rule 6, Chapter 6): Peterson describes a client who blamed his wife, his boss, the economy, and his parents for his unhappiness. After months of therapy, he finally agreed to do one thing: clean his room. It was the turning point. Once his room was clean, he started organizing his finances. Then his relationships. Then his career.

## Principle 3: Pursue meaning over expediency

The expedient choice is the easy one — the dopamine hit, the short term pleasure, the lie that makes things easier now. The meaningful choice is the hard one that builds a better future.

> **Case: The addict's choice** (Rule 7, Chapter 7): The drug addict faces a choice between the immediate relief of the drug (expedient) and the painful work of recovery (meaningful). The addict who chooses the drug gives up a decade of life for ten minutes of relief. The one who chooses recovery sacrifices ten minutes of relief for a decade of life.

## Principle 4: Tell the truth — or at least don't lie

Lies create a gap between your perception and reality. Over time, that gap widens until reality crashes through. The truth is always the path of least long-term resistance.

> **Case: The multiple layers of lying** (Rule 8, Chapter 8): Peterson distinguishes between lies of commission (things you say that aren't true) and lies of omission (things you don't say that need to be said). Both forms corrupt your perception of reality. "If you lie, you are too weak to bear the truth. And if you are too weak to bear the truth, how can you bear the responsibility of being alive?"

## Principle 5: Be precise in your speech

Chaos thrives in vagueness. When you name a problem precisely, you gain power over it. Vagueness allows problems to grow unnoticed.

> **Case: The couple who couldn't communicate** (Rule 10, Chapter 10): A couple comes to therapy frustrated. He says "She's always angry." She says "He never listens." Neither is precise. Through therapy, they discover the real problem: "When I forget to take out the trash, she feels I don't respect her contribution to the household." Precise speech reveals the real problem. Once named, it can be solved.

## Principle 6: Voluntary suffering is the source of strength

The question is not "How can I avoid suffering?" — it's "What suffering am I willing to accept?" The person who voluntarily takes on difficult responsibilities grows stronger. The person who avoids all suffering becomes fragile.

> **Case: The responsible parent vs the irresponsible adult** (Rule 5, Chapter 5): A parent who disciplines their child is voluntarily taking on short-term suffering (the child's anger, the effort of consistency) for long-term good (a well-adjusted adult). The parent who avoids the suffering creates long-term misery for everyone, including the child.
