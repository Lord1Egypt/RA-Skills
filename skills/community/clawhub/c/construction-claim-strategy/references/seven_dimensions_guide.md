# 7-Dimension Framework — Detailed Guide

*Complete explanation of each dimension with examples and practical tips.*

---

## Overview

The 7-Dimension Framework forces you to think strategically **before** drafting. The dimensions work in sequence:

- **Dimensions 1–3** → Define the battlefield (what, scope, direction)
- **Dimensions 4–5** → Decide *how* to argue and *how much* to reveal
- **Dimension 6** → Choose the most effective way to present it
- **Dimension 7** → Stress-test your position before sending

---

## Dimension 1: Situation Assessment

**What it is:** Understanding exactly what you are responding to and why.

**Why it matters:** Many responses go wrong because people don't fully clarify the trigger.

**Key questions:**
- What document or event triggered this response?
- What is the other party actually asking for or claiming?
- What is the background and context?

**Good vs poor assessment:**

| Scenario | Good | Poor |
|---|---|---|
| Authority sends letter requesting info | "Responding to the Authority's letter dated [X] which requests clarification on the basis of our claim for additional time arising from Instruction No. Y." | "We need to reply to the Authority about the claim." |
| Multiple letters exchanged | "This is the third exchange. The current letter raises three specific points we have not previously addressed." | "We have to respond again about the same issue." |

**Practical tip:** Be specific. Vague understanding at this stage leads to unfocused responses.

---

## Dimension 2: Scope Control

**What it is:** Deciding what you **must** address and what you should **avoid** volunteering.

**Why it matters:** One of the most common mistakes is volunteering information that was not requested. This can create new problems or weaken your position.

**Key questions:**
- What specific points must I reply to?
- What should I deliberately **not** mention?
- How can I preserve my rights on other matters?

**Good vs poor scope control:**

| Situation | Good | Poor |
|---|---|---|
| Authority asks about one instruction | Reply only addresses that instruction | Response also discusses two other potential claims |
| Authority questions notice timing | Clearly state awareness dates | Team explains entire project history and unrelated delays |
| Multiple topics in one letter | Number the response per points raised | Everything mixed together |

**Best practice phrase:**
> "This response is limited to the matters raised in your letter dated [Date]. We expressly reserve our position on all other issues."

**Practical tip:** Always define your "must avoid" items clearly. This is one of the highest-value parts of the framework.

---

## Dimension 3: Direction Decision

**What it is:** Choosing your **primary** argument, **fallback** position, and what to **avoid**.

**Why it matters:** Without clear direction, responses become confused or contradictory.

**Key questions:**
- What is my main argument?
- What is my backup if the primary is challenged?
- Which arguments or clauses should I avoid?

**Practical tip:** Always decide primary first, then identify a realistic fallback. This gives strength and flexibility.

---

## Dimension 4: Argument Strategy

**What it is:** Selecting the **type** of argument based on the strength ranking.

**The ranking:**
1. Textual Interpretation (Strongest)
2. Factual Distinction (Strong)
3. Concede-then-Distinguish (Moderate-Strong)
4. Implied Terms / Prevention Principle (Weakest)

**Why it matters:** Using a weak argument when a stronger one is available makes your position less credible.

**Deeper examples:**

| Argument Type | Situation | How to phrase it |
|---|---|---|
| Textual Interpretation | Clause clearly states entitlement when Authority issues an instruction with certain effects | "The clause states that the Contractor shall be entitled when the Authority issues an instruction which [effect]. The instruction issued had exactly that effect." |
| Factual Distinction | Clause requires notice within X days of becoming aware. Information was not available earlier. | "While the clause requires notice within X days of awareness, the full effect could not reasonably have been known until [later point] because [reason]." |
| Concede-then-Distinguish | Normal risk allocation places ground conditions risk on Contractor, but Authority provided incorrect information. | "We acknowledge that ground conditions risk would normally rest with the Contractor. However, the Authority provided specific information which proved materially incorrect, fundamentally changing the nature of the risk." |
| Implied Terms | Authority's delay in providing information prevented mitigation. | "It must be implied that the Authority would not hinder or prevent the Contractor from performing its obligations or mitigating its losses." |

**Practical tip:** Always try Textual Interpretation first. It is the cleanest and strongest.

## Dimension 5: Disclosure Control

**What it is:** Deciding **how much** information to reveal at this stage.

**The three levels:**
- **State Precisely** — Clause references, formal correspondence, key facts, legal reasoning
- **Keep General** — Operational context and high-level details
- **Defer** — Detailed quantification, delay analysis, cost breakdowns

**Deeper guidance:**

| Situation | Recommended Level | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Very strong textual argument | State Precisely | On solid ground — be clear and confident |
| Using Concede-then-Distinguish | Keep General on the distinction | Partial concession — don't over-explain |
| Still compiling records | Defer quantification | Avoid giving incomplete numbers |
| Other side is aggressive | Lean toward Keep General | Reduces material they can attack |
| Want to show cooperation | Mix of Precise + General | Builds credibility without over-disclosing |

**Golden rule:** Stronger argument → more precise disclosure. Weaker argument → more general.

**Practical tip:** Match disclosure level to argument strength.

---

## Dimension 6: Response Architecture

**What it is:** Choosing the overall **structure** of your reply.

**Common patterns:**

| Situation | Architecture | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Authority claims event falls under normal risk allocation | Risk Allocation Rebuttal | Directly addresses and distinguishes the risk argument |
| Complex sequence of events over months | Chronology-Based Response | Easier for the reader to follow |
| Responding to cost/variation questions | Cost / Quantum Claim Response | Structured around contractual basis and heads of claim |
| Simple request for clarification | Standard Query Response | Efficient and proportionate |

**Practical tip:** Risk Allocation Rebuttal is often powerful when the other side is pushing risk onto you.

---

## Dimension 7: Risk Assessment

**What it is:** Honestly identifying weaknesses, counter-arguments, and timing risks.

**Why it matters:** Ignoring risks doesn't make them disappear. Identifying them early allows better preparation.

**Practical examples:**

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Authority argues instruction was normal | High | High | Lead with Textual Interpretation + prepare Factual Distinction fallback |
| Notice was late | Medium | High | Use Factual Distinction to explain when awareness occurred |
| Incomplete records | High | Medium | Defer detailed quantification; commit to provide later |
| Response may damage relationship | Medium | Medium | Professional tone; "without prejudice" language |

**Good practice:** Always ask: "What is the weakest part of our position, and how might the other side attack it?"

**Practical tip:** Be honest here. The goal is not to look perfect, but to understand your position realistically.

---

## Summary

| Dimension | Main Purpose | Highest Value When... |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Situation Assessment | Clarity on what you're responding to | The trigger is complex |
| 2. Scope Control | Protect your position | Risk of volunteering too much |
| 3. Direction Decision | Clear strategic focus | Multiple possible arguments exist |
| 4. Argument Strategy | Use the strongest argument | You have a choice of arguments |
| 5. Disclosure Control | Control what you reveal | Matter is sensitive or high-value |
| 6. Response Architecture | Make the response persuasive | Reply needs to be structured |
| 7. Risk Assessment | Prepare for pushback | The other side is aggressive |
