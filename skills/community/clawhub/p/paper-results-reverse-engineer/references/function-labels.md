# Function Labels — Detailed Examples

Each label below is illustrated with real-ish academic English excerpts typical of psychology / cognitive neuroscience papers. Use these to calibrate annotation decisions.

## Label 1: Restate aim / question / hypothesis 重提研究目的/问题/假设

**Purpose:** Remind the reader what the study or analysis was designed to test. Common at the start of a subsection or before a complex analysis.

**Typical phrases:**
- "To test whether sleep enhances memory consolidation, we..."
- "We next asked whether the effect was specific to declarative memory."
- "Our primary hypothesis predicted that..."
- "The critical question was whether..."

**Example:**
> "To determine whether slow-wave sleep specifically benefits hippocampal-dependent memories, we compared retention across nap and wake conditions."

## Label 2: Restate key method 重提关键方法

**Purpose:** Brief method reminder, usually 5–15 words, to orient the reader without requiring them to flip back to the Methods section.

**Typical phrases:**
- "Using a modified serial reaction time task,"
- "After a 90-min retention interval filled with either sleep or quiet wake,"
- "In the fMRI analysis, we..."

**Example:**
> "After the 90-minute retention interval (either nap or wake), participants completed a surprise cued-recall test."

## Label 3: Overview result trend 总述结果趋势

**Purpose:** High-level summary before diving into specifics. Often the topic sentence of a paragraph. Sets expectations for what follows.

**Typical phrases:**
- "Overall, sleep benefitted memory performance."
- "Reaction times decreased across blocks in both conditions."
- "The pattern of results was consistent across all three experiments."

**Example:**
> "Overall, participants in the sleep group showed superior retention compared to the wake group, with the largest benefits emerging for emotionally negative stimuli."

## Label 4: Invite to view figure/table 邀请读者查看图表

**Purpose:** Direct the reader to a figure or table. Can be parenthetical or integrated into the sentence.

**Typical phrases:**
- "As shown in Figure 2,"
- "(see Table 1 for full model output)"
- "Figure 3A depicts the time course of..."
- "These results are summarized in Figure 4."

**Example:**
> "As illustrated in Figure 2A, recall accuracy was significantly higher in the sleep condition (M = 0.72, SD = 0.14) than in the wake condition (M = 0.58, SD = 0.16)."

## Label 5: Report specific result 报告具体结果

**Purpose:** Descriptive reporting of means, percentages, patterns, counts. The "what happened" without the inferential statistics.

**Typical phrases:**
- "Accuracy was 78% in the sleep condition and 64% in the wake condition."
- "Participants in the high-anxiety group reported more intrusive memories."
- "The ERP waveform showed a pronounced N400 effect."

**Example:**
> "Recall accuracy increased from pre-sleep (M = 0.45) to post-sleep (M = 0.72), whereas the wake group showed minimal change (pre: M = 0.46; post: M = 0.51)."

## Label 6: Report statistical evidence 报告统计证据

**Purpose:** Inferential statistics: t, F, χ², p, Bayes factors, confidence intervals, effect sizes (Cohen's d, η², etc.).

**Typical phrases:**
- "t(34) = 3.21, p = .003, d = 0.55"
- "The ANOVA revealed a significant Condition × Time interaction, F(1, 42) = 8.94, p = .005, ηp² = .18"
- "BF₁₀ = 4.67, providing moderate evidence for the alternative"

**Example:**
> "A 2 (Condition: sleep vs. wake) × 2 (Time: pre vs. post) mixed ANOVA on recall accuracy revealed a significant interaction, F(1, 38) = 12.47, p < .001, ηp² = .25."

## Label 7: Evaluative emphasis 评价性强调

**Purpose:** Words that tell the reader how to interpret the result — that it is important, surprising, robust, or notable. This is the author's interpretive voice.

**Typical phrases:**
- "Strikingly,"
- "Remarkably,"
- "Importantly,"
- "Of particular note,"
- "Consistent with this idea,"
- "Notably robust across all analyses,"

**Example:**
> "Strikingly, the sleep benefit emerged selectively for memories that were cued during slow-wave sleep, with no advantage for uncued memories."

## Label 8: Compare with prior work 与既有研究比较

**Purpose:** Position the current finding relative to published literature. Can be agreement or disagreement.

**Typical phrases:**
- "Consistent with Smith et al. (2020),"
- "This finding replicates and extends..."
- "In contrast to previous reports (Jones, 2019),"
- "These results align with a growing body of evidence suggesting..."

**Example:**
> "This pattern is consistent with prior work showing that slow-wave sleep preferentially consolidates amygdala-dependent memories (Payne & Kensinger, 2011)."

## Label 9: Compare with prediction/model/theory 与预测/模型/理论比较

**Purpose:** Explicitly evaluate results against the study's own hypotheses, computational models, or theoretical frameworks. Stronger than Label 8 because it tests a priori predictions.

**Typical phrases:**
- "As predicted,"
- "Contrary to our hypothesis,"
- "This finding is consistent with the synaptic homeostasis hypothesis."
- "The active systems consolidation model would predict..."

**Example:**
> "As predicted by the active systems consolidation framework, the sleep-dependent memory benefit was accompanied by increased hippocampal-cortical coupling."

## Label 10: Explain / interpret result 解释结果原因

**Purpose:** Offer a mechanism, reason, or interpretation for a result. Goes beyond describing what happened to suggesting why.

**Typical phrases:**
- "This may reflect..."
- "One possible explanation is..."
- "This pattern could arise because..."
- "The absence of an effect might be due to..."

**Example:**
> "This differential benefit may reflect the preferential reactivation of emotionally salient memories during REM sleep."

## Label 11: Note non-significant / inconsistent 指出不显著或不一致

**Purpose:** Report null results, non-significant tests, or results that go against the overall pattern. Honest reporting of what did NOT work.

**Typical phrases:**
- "However, this effect did not reach significance,"
- "No significant difference was observed..."
- "The interaction was not significant, F < 1."
- "Contrary to expectations,"

**Example:**
> "However, the Condition × Valence interaction did not reach significance, F(1, 42) = 0.87, p = .356."

## Label 12: Acknowledge limitation / anomaly 承认限制或异常

**Purpose:** Flag caveats, unexpected outliers, or limitations of the current analysis. More self-aware than Label 11.

**Typical phrases:**
- "We note that..."
- "Unexpectedly,..."
- "However, this interpretation is limited by..."
- "One caveat is that..."
- "Surprisingly,"

**Example:**
> "We note, however, that the sleep group also showed higher baseline performance, which may partially account for the observed benefit."

## Label 13: Hint at implication 提示结果意义

**Purpose:** Brief forward-looking statement about what the result means for the broader question. Not a full discussion — just a pointer.

**Typical phrases:**
- "This suggests that..."
- "These findings imply that..."
- "Together, these results point to..."
- "This raises the possibility that..."

**Example:**
> "These findings suggest that targeted memory reactivation during sleep may be a viable intervention for attenuating traumatic memories."

## Label 14: Transition to Discussion 过渡到 Discussion

**Purpose:** Signpost that the Results section is concluding and the Discussion follows. Usually appears in the last paragraph.

**Typical phrases:**
- "We return to this point in the Discussion."
- "The theoretical implications of these findings are considered in the Discussion."
- "These results, taken together, raise several questions that we address below."

**Example:**
> "Taken together, these findings demonstrate a selective role for REM sleep in emotional memory consolidation, a point we develop further in the Discussion."

## Annotation Guidelines

1. **One label per sentence (or clause if compound).** If a sentence does two things, pick the dominant function.
2. **Label the function, not the content.** "Recall was 72%" is Label 5 even if it appears in a paragraph about the hypothesis.
3. **Labels are not mutually exclusive in principle**, but assign only the *primary* function per sentence.
4. **Statistical reporting usually pairs with specific results.** If a sentence contains both descriptive and inferential stats, label it as 6 (statistical evidence) — it subsumes 5.
5. **"Significantly" is ambiguous.** If it means p < .05, it's Label 6. If it means "meaningfully / importantly", it's Label 7.
6. **When in doubt between 8 and 9:** Label 9 if the comparison is to the study's own prediction/model; Label 8 if comparing to a prior empirical study.
7. **Distinguish prediction from discovery.** If the paper uses words like "Intriguingly" or "Surprisingly" before a finding, it was likely not a pre-registered hypothesis. Do not label it as Label 9 (与预测/模型比较). Label the finding as Label 6 (统计证据) or Label 7 (评价性强调), and note that the theoretical interpretation was added post-hoc in Discussion.
8. **Do not rewrite null results as absolute negatives.** "Not differentially sensitive to spatial context alone" (Label 11) is not the same as "CA1 does not encode spatial information." Quote the paper's exact wording when reporting null results.
