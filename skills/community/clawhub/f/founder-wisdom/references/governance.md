# Boards & Governance

## Core axioms

**Your board is a tool, not an obligation.** Run good board meetings: pre-read sent 72 hours out, decisions documented, no surprises. The board should leave each meeting clearer about the business than they came in — that's the test of a well-run meeting.

**Never surprise your board.** Bad news early, often, with a plan attached. The investor who hears about a missed quarter for the first time in the board meeting loses confidence in you immediately. The investor who got the heads-up by email two weeks earlier with your proposed response becomes an ally.

**The board doesn't run the company — but they can fire you.** Maintain the relationship accordingly. Founders who treat the board as adversaries get fired during the first downturn. Founders who treat them as partners — sharing problems early, asking for help, taking feedback seriously — get supported through downturns.

**Independent directors matter more than you'd think.** They're the only board members without an agenda — no investment to protect, no LP money on the line. A strong independent director (usually an operator who's been a CEO) is often the most valuable voice in the room and the person who can mediate between founders and investors when conflicts arise. Add the first independent at Series A or B.

**Board composition is a multi-year game.** The board you have at Series A will be the core of the board at Series C. Optimize for partners you want to work with for 7 years, not just the ones offering the highest valuation today. Bad board members are very hard to remove.

**A 3-2-2 board is the goal at Series B.** Three founders/management, two investors, two independents. This composition keeps investor influence balanced with operating wisdom and gives founders/independents a majority. Founders who let investors get to majority before the company is mature lose strategic control.

**Pre-reads, not presentations.** The worst board meetings are PowerPoint walkthroughs. The best are 70% discussion and 30% review, because the pre-read covered the facts. If the board is hearing the facts for the first time in the meeting, you've wasted the most expensive 3 hours of the quarter.

**Board meetings are quarterly checkpoints; board members are weekly resources.** The 1:1 calls between meetings are where the real value comes from. Founders who only talk to board members in board meetings are getting maybe 10% of the available value. Schedule 30 minutes a month with each board member individually.

**The board pack should drive your operating cadence, not the other way around.** Design the pack around the metrics you actually run the company on. If you build a separate "board version" of metrics, you're either hiding something or running the company on the wrong numbers.

**Executive sessions are normal, not threatening.** When the board meets without management at the end of each meeting, this isn't a coup — it's healthy governance. Founders who feel threatened by executive sessions reveal a defensiveness that itself is a warning sign.

**Reid Hoffman's three-light framework for the board/CEO relationship.** (From the *High Growth Handbook* interview.) Boards and CEOs operate in one of three modes, and confusion about which mode you're in produces most of the friction:

- **Green light.** The board has confidence in the CEO. The CEO makes operational and strategic calls; the board is advisory. Even on big questions (selling the company, major pivots), the conversation is "we'd like to discuss it before you decide" — not "we vote." A board member who tries to direct a green-light CEO is overstepping.
- **Yellow light.** The board has specific concerns — traction, team composition, burn, execution velocity. The CEO is still the CEO, but oversight rises and the CEO must proactively communicate recovery plans. The path back to green light is the CEO closing the specific concerns; ignoring or denying them moves the company toward red light, not back to green.
- **Red light.** The board has lost confidence in the CEO's ability to execute. The CEO may still be in the seat, but the board is actively seeking a replacement. Once red light is reached, the CEO transition is usually a matter of months, not quarters.

The CEO's job is to read the light correctly. Most founders over-estimate how green their light is. The diagnostic: ask three different board members independently what they're worried about. If the lists are short and aligned with what you already know, you're green. If the lists are long, divergent, or contain things that surprise you, you're at least yellow.

**Founder-friendly is a strategy, not a virtue.** Some investors are founder-friendly because they believe founders make better long-term decisions. Others perform founder-friendliness because it's marketing. Reference-check this specifically: "When you've disagreed with this investor, how did it go?"

**The compensation committee is more important than founders realize.** Once you have multiple senior hires, executive comp becomes contested and political. A functioning comp committee with clear philosophy prevents the founders from having to negotiate every offer individually and from being accused of favoritism.

**The audit committee is required earlier than founders expect.** If you're heading toward an IPO, you need a functioning audit committee with a financial expert on it at least 2 years pre-IPO. Most founders treat this as a checkbox until the IPO process starts and discover that retrofitting governance is expensive and time-consuming.

**Document everything.** Board resolutions, written consents, option grants, conflict-of-interest disclosures. The paperwork seems excessive until you're in due diligence for an acquisition or IPO and a missing resolution from 2019 holds up the deal. Have great corporate counsel who keeps the records straight.

## Common founder mistakes

- Treating the board as an audience to be performed for rather than a resource to be deployed. The board can help with hiring, intros, customer leads, fundraising prep — but only if you ask.
- Letting one board member dominate. Strong personalities can monopolize meetings; the CEO's job is to manage airtime so all voices contribute.
- Avoiding the hard topics. The CEO who only brings good news to the board gets caught when bad news finally surfaces.
- Failing to use written consents between meetings. Many decisions can be handled by unanimous written consent without convening a meeting — much faster than waiting for the next quarterly.
- Forgetting that observer rights still create information liability. Observers see everything; act accordingly when granting observer seats.
