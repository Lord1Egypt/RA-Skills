> Source: 13 Things Mentally Strong People Don't Do by Amy Morin, Chapters 3, 7, 11-13

# Anti-Patterns: Common Mental Strength Mistakes

## Anti-Pattern 1: Resisting Change

**The mistake:** Clinging to the familiar because change is uncomfortable, even when the current situation is clearly not working.

**The correction:** Don't shy away from change. Change is a source of growth, not a threat. Mentally strong people embrace change as an opportunity to develop new skills and perspectives.

> **Case: The woman who stayed in a bad marriage** (Chapter 3): A client stayed in an emotionally draining marriage for years because she was terrified of being alone. Morin helped her see that staying in a bad situation was more painful than the discomfort of change. "The fear of being alone was worse than the loneliness she already felt. Once she acknowledged that, she could plan her exit."

## Anti-Pattern 2: Repeating the Same Mistakes

**The mistake:** Making the same error over and over while expecting different results. Refusing to reflect because reflection is painful.

**The correction:** After every failure or mistake, ask: "What can I learn from this? What will I do differently next time?" Write it down. The goal is not to avoid mistakes — it is to avoid making the same mistake twice.

> **Case: The serial entrepreneur** (Chapter 8): A businessman had started three companies. All three failed for the same reason: he hired people he liked rather than people who were qualified. After the third failure, he finally reflected on the pattern. "Three times I made the same mistake. I had to stop asking 'Why do my companies fail?' and start asking 'What am I doing that causes them to fail?'"

## Anti-Pattern 3: Fearing Alone Time

**The mistake:** Constantly surrounding yourself with noise, people, and distraction to avoid sitting with your own thoughts.

**The correction:** Don't fear alone time. Solitude is essential for building mental strength. It gives you space to reflect, process, and recharge. Schedule 15 minutes of alone time every day — no phone, no TV, no people.

> **Case: The workaholic** (Chapter 11): A client filled every waking moment with work, social plans, and media consumption. When he finally had quiet time, he felt anxious. "He was running from his thoughts. When we started with just five minutes of silence each day, he discovered that his thoughts weren't as scary as he imagined. Within a month, he looked forward to his quiet time."

## Anti-Pattern 4: Entitlement Thinking

**The mistake:** Believing that the world, your employer, your partner, or society owes you something. This leads to disappointment, resentment, and a lack of motivation.

**The correction:** Don't feel the world owes you anything. Take full responsibility for your life. You didn't choose your starting point, but you choose your direction. The more responsibility you take, the more control you have.

> **Case: The entitled graduate** (Chapter 12): A recent college graduate was angry that he couldn't find his dream job. "I have a degree. I deserve a good job." Morin's response: "The world doesn't owe you a job. You have to earn it." Once he accepted that, he took an internship, worked hard, and within a year had a promotion.

## Anti-Pattern 5: Expecting Immediate Results

**The mistake:** Expecting big changes to happen quickly. Giving up when results don't appear after a few attempts.

**The correction:** Don't expect immediate results. Mental strength grows slowly. So do all worthwhile outcomes. Commit to the process and be patient. The small daily choices add up over time.

> **Case: The dieter who quit** (Chapter 13): A client started a diet and exercise program, lost five pounds in two weeks, then nothing in week three. She quit. "I was looking at the scale every day. When I didn't see results, I felt like a failure." Morin helped her understand that plateaus are part of any growth process. She started again, this time measuring progress monthly instead of daily. A year later, she had lost 40 pounds.
