> Source: 13 Things Mentally Strong People Don't Do by Amy Morin, Introduction, Chapter 1

# Core Framework: The 13 Things and Mental Strength Definition

## What Mental Strength Is (and Is Not)

Amy Morin's central definition: mental strength is the ability to regulate your emotions, manage your thoughts, and behave productively despite your circumstances. It is not about being tough, invulnerable, or emotionless.

| Mental Strength Is | Mental Strength Is Not |
|---|---|
| Managing emotions productively | Suppressing or ignoring emotions |
| Choosing your response | Reacting automatically |
| Building healthy coping strategies | Avoiding discomfort |
| Taking calculated risks | Being fearless |
| Learning from failure | Never failing |

> **Case: Amy Morin's own story** (Introduction): Morin lost her mother suddenly to a brain aneurysm, then three years later lost her husband to a heart attack. She writes: "I had to allow myself time to experience the sorrow head-on. There was no ignoring it or pushing it away. I had to experience the pain while also proactively helping myself heal. I couldn't allow myself to stay stuck in my negative emotions." Her personal recovery became the foundation of her framework.
> **Key takeaway**: Mental strength is forged through adversity, not in spite of it. The key is to experience pain fully while choosing healthy coping strategies.

## The 13 Things Mentally Strong People Don't Do

The framework is structured as 13 behaviors to eliminate:

| # | They Don't | Instead They |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Waste time feeling sorry for themselves | Practice gratitude and take action |
| 2 | Give away their power | Own their emotional response |
| 3 | Shy away from change | Embrace change as growth |
| 4 | Waste energy on things they can't control | Focus on what they can influence |
| 5 | Worry about pleasing everyone | Set healthy boundaries |
| 6 | Fear taking calculated risks | Assess and act on smart risks |
| 7 | Dwell on the past | Learn from it and move forward |
| 8 | Make the same mistakes over and over | Reflect, adjust, and improve |
| 9 | Resent other people's success | Celebrate others, compete with themselves |
| 10 | Give up after failure | Treat failure as feedback |
| 11 | Fear alone time | Use solitude for reflection |
| 12 | Feel the world owes them anything | Take full responsibility |
| 13 | Expect immediate results | Commit to the process |

> **Case: The child who feared failure** (Chapter 10): Morin describes a young client who refused to try anything new because she was terrified of not being perfect. She wouldn't play a sport unless she knew she'd be the best. Morin helped her reframe failure as experimentation. "We worked on rewarding courage rather than outcomes. She learned to say, 'I tried something new today and that is a success.'"
> **Key takeaway**: Many of the 13 things are interconnected. The fear of failure often drives comparison, people-pleasing, and avoidance of risk.

## Emotional Energy as a Finite Resource

Morin's key insight: emotional energy is limited. Every time you engage in one of the 13 things — self-pity, resentment, comparison, dwelling — you drain your emotional bank account. Mental strength is about conserving your emotional energy for what matters.

> **Case: The woman who held grudges** (Chapter 9): A client spent hours each day replaying old arguments and imagining confrontations. She was exhausted. Morin asked: "How much emotional energy are you spending on people who don't even know they're on your mind?" The question helped her realize that resentment was a choice — and an expensive one.
> **Key takeaway**: The 13 things are not moral failings. They are energy drains. Identifying and eliminating them frees up emotional energy for growth, relationships, and purpose.
