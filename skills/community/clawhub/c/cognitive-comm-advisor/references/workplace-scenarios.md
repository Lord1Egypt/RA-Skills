# Workplace Scenarios Reference

Use this to clarify the relationship and the situation with low friction. Always provide
numbered choices so a mobile user can reply with `3` or `4b` instead of typing a paragraph.

## Relationship Types

Ask this when the relationship is not clear:

```markdown
What's the relationship?

1. Direct manager — your boss / person evaluating or directing your work
2. Skip-level / senior executive — higher-level leader, limited time, higher stakes
3. Client (external) — customer, account, vendor-side or service-side relationship
4. Key partner / stakeholder — influential internal partner whose buy-in matters
5. Close collaborator (daily work) — teammate or partner you work with constantly
6. Cross-functional peer — peer from another team/function with different priorities
7. Direct report — someone you manage, coach, or evaluate
```

## Scenario Examples by Relationship Type

When the user says "not sure" or gives only a relationship, offer examples from the relevant
section. Include both routine and tricky examples.

### 1. Direct Manager

Routine:

1. 1:1 — agenda, priorities, blockers, relationship maintenance
2. Project review — showing progress, tradeoffs, risks, decisions needed
3. Proposal discussion — pitching an idea, strategy, or resource request
4. Status update — keeping them informed without overexplaining
5. Feedback session — receiving or asking for feedback

Tricky:

6. Delivering bad news — miss, delay, failed experiment, risk escalation
7. Asking for promotion / scope / resources
8. Disagreeing with a decision without sounding defiant
9. Resetting expectations after misalignment
10. Navigating political tension around your work

### 2. Skip-Level / Senior Executive

Routine:

1. Intro / first formal interaction
2. Strategic update — concise context, business impact, decision needed
3. Project review — high-level outcomes and risk posture
4. Proposal discussion — why this matters now, what it unlocks
5. Career / visibility conversation

Tricky:

6. Escalating a blocked decision
7. Delivering bad news with limited time
8. Disagreeing with strategic direction
9. Asking for sponsorship or promotion support
10. Navigating sensitive org politics

### 3. Client (External)

Routine:

1. Status update — progress, timeline, next steps
2. Project review — deliverables, quality, open questions
3. Proposal / upsell discussion — new scope, renewal, expansion
4. Feedback session — learning what is or isn't working
5. Relationship check-in — trust maintenance

Tricky:

6. Client complaint handling
7. Delivering bad news — delay, bug, missed expectation, budget issue
8. Pushing back on unrealistic requests
9. Negotiating scope / timeline / price
10. Repairing trust after friction

### 4. Key Partner / Stakeholder

Routine:

1. Alignment meeting — goals, responsibilities, timeline
2. Proposal discussion — gaining buy-in before a decision
3. Project review — surfacing dependencies and risks
4. Status update — keeping them looped in enough, not too much
5. Feedback session — calibrating expectations

Tricky:

6. Disagreeing with their preferred direction
7. Asking for resources or prioritization
8. Handling competing priorities / turf tension
9. Navigating political tension between teams
10. Rebuilding alignment after a dropped ball

### 5. Close Collaborator (Daily Work)

Routine:

1. Daily/weekly sync — priorities, handoffs, blockers
2. Working session — solving a shared problem live
3. Project review — quality bar, decisions, next steps
4. Feedback session — improving the collaboration loop
5. Role split / ownership discussion

Tricky:

6. Naming recurring friction without damaging trust
7. Disagreeing on approach or quality bar
8. Giving direct feedback to someone you rely on daily
9. Recovering after a tense exchange
10. Handling unequal effort, missed commitments, or resentment

### 6. Cross-Functional Peer

Routine:

1. Kickoff / alignment — shared goal and working model
2. Status update — translating your team's work into their language
3. Project review — dependencies, risks, next steps
4. Proposal discussion — asking for support or input
5. Feedback session — improving cross-team collaboration

Tricky:

6. Disagreeing with a decision from their function
7. Negotiating priority when both teams are overloaded
8. Pushing back on requests that hurt your team's goals
9. Navigating political tension or hidden incentives
10. Handling blame / ambiguity when something slips

### 7. Direct Report

Routine:

1. 1:1 — coaching, priorities, blockers, motivation
2. Project review — quality, ownership, decision support
3. Status update — accountability without micromanaging
4. Feedback session — reinforcing strengths and correcting issues
5. Growth / career conversation

Tricky:

6. Delivering hard feedback or performance concerns
7. Handling defensiveness, disengagement, or low ownership
8. Disagreeing with their approach while preserving autonomy
9. Resetting expectations after missed commitments
10. Discussing promotion readiness honestly

## Scenario-Specific Playbook Structure

For each scenario, the advisor should produce:

### 1. Context Frame

- What's the power/relationship dynamic?
- What does each party's type prioritize in this dynamic?
- What is the likely hidden tension: trust, control, speed, status, clarity, autonomy, face-saving?

### 2. Preparation Checklist

- How to structure the message for their dominant function
- What information to lead with
- What emotional/logical tone to set
- What proof they need: data, precedent, vision, people impact, risk, options, ownership

### 3. In-Conversation Tactics

- Opening moves (first 30 seconds)
- Pacing guidance (their preferred rhythm)
- Signal reading (what to watch for mid-conversation)
- Pivot strategies (if conversation derails)

### 4. Common Pitfalls

- Type-pair specific mistakes
- Scenario-specific power mistakes
- Stress behavior to watch for (theirs and yours)
- Recovery moves if you trip a wire

### 5. Follow-Up

- Post-conversation reinforcement style
- Documentation preferences
- Relationship maintenance cadence

## Scenario Lens Quick Guide

| Relationship | Key Tension | What Matters Most |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Direct manager | Upward influence; evaluation risk | Framing, timing, alignment with their goals |
| Skip-level / senior executive | High stakes, low context, limited time | Concision, strategic relevance, decision clarity |
| Client | Trust, satisfaction, external accountability | Expectation management, ownership, proactive communication |
| Key stakeholder | Buy-in, dependencies, political incentives | Translation, coalition-building, making the ask easy |
| Close collaborator | Repeated exposure; micro-frictions compound | Repair speed, shared shorthand, explicit working agreements |
| Cross-functional peer | Different incentives and vocabularies | Bridging mental models, reducing coordination cost |
| Direct report | Trust, motivation, accountability | Adapting to reception style, clear standards, psychological safety |
