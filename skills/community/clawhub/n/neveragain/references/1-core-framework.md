## The Movement Engine — 5 Stages

The #NeverAgain movement didn't follow a pre-written playbook. It emerged from a specific sequence that any group can replicate. The authors didn't realize they were building a movement engine until they were already running it.

### Stage 1: Ignite (Trauma + Outrage → Fuel)

The movement began with raw emotion — Lauren's "subhuman" wailing, David's screaming "fuck" in the car, Emma González's silent fury. But what made this different from every other school shooting aftermath was **David's decision, within hours, to go back to the school and talk to reporters.** He had no media training. He had a camera and a voice.

**The ignition principle:** *Someone must turn the first wrench. Not everyone. Someone.*

> **Case: David's bike ride back to school** (Chapter 5): Two hours after the shooting, while Lauren was still hysterical at home, David got on his bike and rode back to Stoneman Douglas. He told his parents: "If they don't get any stories, this will just fade away. I have to make sure this stays in the news." His parents refused to drive him. He went anyway.
> **Key takeaway:** You don't need permission. You don't need a ride. You need a bike and a decision.

### Stage 2: Coalesce (Find Your Cameron's Couch)

Two days after the shooting, David went to Cameron Kasky's house. He didn't know Cameron well. But Cameron had gathered an "amazing collection of people" — Delaney Tarr, Ryan Deitsch, Jaclyn Corin, Sarah Chadwick, Alex Wind, Emma González. Drama kids. Debate nerds. TV production geeks. They didn't all like each other. They didn't agree on everything. **But they sat in the same room and talked.**

**The coalescence principle:** *Find 3-5 people who are as angry and determined as you are. They don't have to be your friends. They have to show up.*

> **Case: The first meeting** (Chapter 5): No agenda. No facilitator. They talked about what they wanted to accomplish, settled on background checks and raising the age limit for rifles, and Cameron came up with #neveragain as the hashtag. Most of the meeting was spent joking around. That "wasted time" built the trust that carried them through the next months.
> **Key takeaway:** Formal organizing kills momentum. Build family first, strategy second.

### Stage 3: Escalate (Media Blast + Viral Loop)

Within days, the #NeverAgain kids were doing live interviews at 1 AM for the BBC and 7 AM for CNN, sleeping on Cameron's couch between hits. They didn't have PR consultants — they had HBO's John Oliver and Vice News instincts absorbed through years of watching. David filmed himself in the classroom during the shooting. Lauren called out Melania Trump on cyberbullying. Sarah Chadwick tweeted "We should change the names of AR-15s to 'Marco Rubio' because they are so easy to buy."

**The escalation principle:** *Every interview is a chance to re-frame. Every attack is an opportunity for a viral counter-punch. Nothing goes to waste.*

### Stage 4: Institutionalize (Committees + 11-Point Strategy)

After the March for Our Lives, the group split into committees. Cameron became the "power source." Ryan became the "comedy sniper." Delaney became the analyst. Emma became the "Buddha." They created an 11-point strategy (see `references/2-principles.md`). They filed paperwork, raised money (including $4M from a GoFundMe), and turned a hashtag into an organization.

**The institutionalization principle:** *The anger that starts a movement won't sustain it. Structure does.*

### Stage 5: Sustain (Love, Memory, Legacy)

The final and most overlooked stage. The authors describe fusing "like different metals under heat" into "the strongest alloy in the natural world." They don't call it team-building. They call it "high-intensity do-it-yourself group therapy."

**The sustainability principle:** *If you don't love each other, you will burn out or tear each other apart. Make your movement a family, or it will die.*
