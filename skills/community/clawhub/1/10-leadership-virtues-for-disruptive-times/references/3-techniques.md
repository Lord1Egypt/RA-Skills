## Techniques and Practices

### 1. The Self-Control Pause

When emotions spike, your first instinct is to react. The Coach Leader's technique is the **Self-Control Pause** — a three-second internal check before responding.

**The Technique:**
1. **Stop** — Physically pause. Take a breath.
2. **Ask** — "Would a secure person say these things?" or "Is this about me or about their anxiety?"
3. **Respond** — Choose the action aligned with your purpose and values, not with your emotions.

**Practice:**
- Set a daily intention: "Today I will respond, not react"
- Keep a "react journal" — note each time you felt triggered and what you actually did
- Review weekly: are you catching yourself faster?

### 2. The Positive Energy Ritual

Positivity isn't a personality trait — it's a practice. Coach Leaders systematically inject energy into their teams.

**The Practice:**
- **Start meetings with a win** — Before discussing problems, ask "What went well this week?"
- **Celebrate small victories** — Public recognition for incremental progress
- **Align individual goals with company vision** — Connect daily work to the bigger picture so people feel purpose

### 3. The Perseverance Reframe

When things go wrong, the natural reaction is to see it as failure. The Coach Leader reframes obstacles as *pit stops* — places to refuel, gather data, and recalibrate.

**The Reframe Questions:**
- "What is this setback teaching me?"
- "What would I tell my best friend to do in this situation?"
- "What's the smallest next step I can take right now?"

### 4. Active Listening Protocol

Selfless leadership starts with truly hearing people. Not waiting for your turn to speak — actually listening.

**The Protocol:**
1. Give full attention — no phone, no laptop, no multi-tasking
2. Resist the urge to interrupt or offer solutions
3. Summarize back: "What I'm hearing is..."
4. Ask follow-up questions before offering advice

### 5. The Coach One-on-One

Replace status update meetings with coaching conversations.

**Structure:**
- Start: "How are you doing — really?"
- Middle: "What's one thing you're struggling with that I can help with?"
- End: "What are you excited to work on next?"

> **Case: The Heated Standoff** (Key Idea 5)
> A project manager was publicly criticized by a stakeholder. Instead of firing back, she paused, asked herself "Would a secure person say these things?", and calmly said "I hear your frustration. Let's talk about what's not working and find a solution together." The stakeholder's tone shifted immediately.
> **Key takeaway**: Self-control in high-stakes moments doesn't just save relationships — it earns lasting respect.

> **Case: The Failed Product Launch** (Key Idea 7)
> When a product launch failed, the CEO didn't blame anyone. She called an all-hands, said "We gathered valuable data on what doesn't work. Now let's figure out what does," and turned the failure into a team-wide learning sprint.
> **Key takeaway**: Perseverance isn't about toughing it out — it's about treating failure as information.
