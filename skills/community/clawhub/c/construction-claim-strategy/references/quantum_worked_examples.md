# Quantum Calculation Worked Examples

Below are practical worked examples using different quantum methods. Where possible, base quantum on actual costs supported by records.

---

## Method Overview

| Method | Description | Best For | Acceptance |
|--------|-------------|----------|------------|
| **Actual Cost** | Claim based on real additional costs incurred | All claims | High in both Common Law and Civil Law |
| **Measured Mile** | Compare productivity in unaffected vs affected period | Disruption | High in both systems |
| **Eichleay Formula** | Head office overheads allocable to a contract during delay | Prolongation | More accepted in Common Law |
| **Hudson Formula** | Uses tendered overhead & profit percentage | Prolongation | Used in some Common Law countries |
| **Emden Formula** | Uses actual overhead percentage | Prolongation | Accepted in Common Law |
| **Total Cost** | Difference between actual cost and tender allowance | Last resort | Low — viewed with caution |

---

## Example 1: Eichleay Formula — Prolongation Overhead

**Given:**
- Total Company Billings during delay period: **$50,000,000**
- Contract Billings during delay period: **$8,000,000**
- Total Company Overhead during delay period: **$4,000,000**
- Original Contract Period: **24 months**
- Period of Delay: **4 months**

**Calculation:**

1. Contract billings ratio = $8,000,000 ÷ $50,000,000 = **0.16**
2. Overhead allocable to contract = 0.16 × $4,000,000 = **$640,000**
3. Monthly overhead rate = $640,000 ÷ 24 months = **$26,666.67 per month**
4. Overhead for delay period = $26,666.67 × 4 months = **$106,667**

**Claimed Amount: $106,667**

## Example 2: Hudson Formula — Prolongation Overhead

**Given:**
- Contract Sum: **$10,000,000**
- Tendered Head Office Overhead & Profit: **10%**
- Original Contract Period: **730 days** (24 months)
- Period of Delay: **120 days**

**Calculation:**

1. Daily overhead & profit rate = ($10,000,000 × 10%) ÷ 730 = **$1,369.86 per day**
2. Overhead & profit for delay = $1,369.86 × 120 days = **$164,383**

**Claimed Amount: $164,383**

---

## Example 3: Emden Formula — Prolongation Overhead

**Given:**
- Contract Value: **$12,000,000**
- Actual Head Office Overhead: **8%**
- Original Contract Period: **600 days**
- Period of Delay: **90 days**

**Calculation:**

1. Daily overhead rate = ($12,000,000 × 8%) ÷ 600 = **$1,600 per day**
2. Overhead for delay = $1,600 × 90 days = **$144,000**

**Claimed Amount: $144,000**

> **Note:** The Emden Formula is generally considered more accurate than Hudson because it uses actual overheads rather than tendered percentages.

## Example 4: Measured Mile — Piling Works (Disruption)

**Given:**
- Measured Mile (Unaffected Period): 800 piles installed using 4,000 man-hours → **0.2 piles per man-hour**
- Disrupted Period: 1,200 piles installed using 9,000 man-hours → **0.133 piles per man-hour**

**Calculation:**

1. Productivity Loss: 0.2 − 0.133 = **0.067 piles per man-hour** (33.5% loss)
2. Hours at Measured Mile rate: 1,200 ÷ 0.2 = **6,000 man-hours**
3. Additional man-hours claimed: 9,000 − 6,000 = **3,000 man-hours**

**Claimed Amount:** 3,000 man-hours × applicable labour rate

## Example 5: Measured Mile — Formwork Installation (Disruption)

**Given:**
- Measured Mile (Normal Period): 2,500 m² installed using 5,000 man-hours → **0.5 m² per man-hour**
- Disrupted Period: 3,000 m² installed using 8,000 man-hours → **0.375 m² per man-hour**

**Calculation:**

1. Productivity Loss: 0.5 − 0.375 = **0.125 m² per man-hour** (25% loss)
2. Hours at Measured Mile rate: 3,000 ÷ 0.5 = **6,000 man-hours**
3. Additional man-hours: 8,000 − 6,000 = **2,000 man-hours**

**Claimed Amount:** 2,000 man-hours × applicable labour rate

## Example 7: Simple Productivity Loss Calculation

**Given:**
- Tender productivity for concrete works: **4 m³ per man-day**
- Actual average productivity during disruption: **2.8 m³ per man-day**
- Total volume placed during disrupted period: **1,400 m³**

**Calculation:**

1. Man-days at tender rate: 1,400 ÷ 4 = **350 man-days**
2. Actual man-days used: 1,400 ÷ 2.8 ≈ **500 man-days**
3. Additional man-days claimed: 500 − 350 = **150 man-days**

**Claimed Amount:** 150 man-days × applicable labour rate

## Example 8: Total Cost Method — Use with Strong Caution

**Given:**
- Tendered cost for the affected scope: **$2,500,000**
- Actual cost incurred: **$3,450,000**

**Claimed Amount:** $3,450,000 − $2,500,000 = **$950,000**

> **Important Warning:** The Total Cost Method is generally viewed with suspicion by tribunals and courts because it assumes that *all* cost overruns are the responsibility of the other party. It should only be used as a **last resort** when no better method is available, and even then, it requires very strong justification and supporting evidence.

## Recommendation by Claim Type

| Claim Type | Preferred Method | Fallback |
|-----------|-----------------|----------|
| **Variation** | Actual Cost | Rates-based valuation |
| **Disruption** | Measured Mile | Actual Cost comparison |
| **Prolongation** | Actual Cost | Eichleay or Emden |
| **Combined** | Keep EOT and Disruption quantum separate and clearly labelled | — |

---

## Civil Law vs Common Law Notes

- **Common Law:** More familiar with formula-based methods (Eichleay, Hudson, Emden); more developed case law
- **Civil Law:** Generally prefer claims based on **actual proven costs**; may require stronger justification for formulas
- **Best Practice:** Wherever possible, support quantum with **actual records**; use formulas only as secondary/alternative methods
